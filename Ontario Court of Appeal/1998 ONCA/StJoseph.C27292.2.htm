AUSTIN J.A.

[1]    These reasons supplement those of McKinlay J.A. released
today in this matter, and in a parallel appeal, C27291, involving
the same parties.  These reasons deal with the question of
interest, both pre- and post-judgment.


[2]    The result of the reasons of McKinlay J.A. is that the
refund of taxes ordered by Forget J. on August 18, 1994 in appeal
C27292, will be reduced to some extent, but there will be a
refund.  That being the case, the questions of pre-judgment
interest and post-judgment interest become relevant.


Pre-Judgment Interest


[3]    Forget J. dealt with pre-judgment interest in his reasons
as follows:


        applicant to be entilted to pre-judgment interest
	to be agreed to between the parties, failing which
	the parties may re-apply to the Court to determine
	same.
        

[4]    That ruling was carried forward into the formal order,
paragraph 5 of which reads as follows:


               This Court Orders pre-judgment
               interest in accordance with the
               Courts of Justice Act, R.S.O. 1990,
               c.C.43 as amended.






[5]    The Divisional Court dismissed the appeal from this order
and was silent on the subject of interest, no issue having been
raised in that regard.


[6]    Between the time of the decision of the Divisional Court
and the hearing of the appeal in this court, however, the
municipality raised and discussed with the applicant, the matter
of pre-judgment interest.  The municipality took the position
that no pre-judgment interest is payable on any refund of taxes
paid.  The position of the taxpayer was that refunds of taxes, in
the circumstances, were within the scope of s.128(1) of the
Courts of Justice Act and that pre-judgment interest is payable.


[7]    Section 128 reads, in part, as follows:


               (1)  A person who is entitled to an
               order for the payment of money is
               entitled to claim and have included
               in the order an award of interest
               therein....




               (4) Interest shall not be awarded
               under subsection (1)...


               (g)  where interest is payable by a
               right other than under this
               section.


[8]    The parties were unable to reach agreement on this issue.
The reasons of Forget J. invited counsel to confer with him in
the event of disagreement.  That invitation was not acted upon.
Instead, the taxpayer commenced a separate application to answer
the question whether pre-judgment interest was payable on refunds
of taxes paid.  That application is still pending in the General
Division.


[9]    The Commissioner then moved for the leave of this court to
file an amended notice of appeal to seek clarification of the
orders of the Divisional Court concerning the payment of pre-
judgment or post-judgment interest on any refunds of taxes which
may result from this court's orders and alternatively, for leave
to raise a ground of appeal not stated in the earlier notices of
appeal.


[10]   Because the two substantive appeals were already pending
in this court, because the question of pre- and post-judgment
interest will have to be resolved to bring finality to these
appeals, and because of the possible inhibition of the General
Division to deal with the application pending before it, leave
was granted and the matter argued at the same time as the
substantive appeals.


[11]   The position of the municipality is that the question has
been answered by this court in Re Zaidan Group Ltd. v. City of
London (1990) 71 O.R. (2d) 65 (C.A.), aff'd (1992), 5 O.R. (3d)
384 (S.C.C.).  That case holds that the Assessment Act, the
Municipal Act and the Municipal Interest and Discount Rates Act,
R.S.O. 1990, c.C.31, M.45 and M.58 respectively, together
constitute a complete statutory code dealing with the collection
and refund of taxes.  Under the last mentioned Act, the
individual municipality may decide whether to pay interest on
refunds and, if so, when, and at what rate.  Section 6(1) of that
Act is as follows:


               6(1)  A local municipality may pass
               by-laws to provide for paying to
               persons to whom overpayments are
               refunded under subsection 37 (6) of
               the Assessment Act, interest on the
               overpayments at such rate as the
               council may determine and different
               rates may be paid for different
               successive periods from the day the
               overpayments were made or such
               other day as may be set out in the
               by-law until the day they were
               refunded or such other day as may
               be set out in the by-law.






[12]   The municipality in question here, Cornwall, has not
passed any such bylaw.  Accordingly, no pre-judgment interest is
payable on any refund.


[13]   The taxpayer's position is that Zaidan applies to
situations where the taxes are lawfully raised, but where
assessments are ultimately found to be too high and as a result
refunds have to be paid to taxpayers.  Reliance is placed on the
decision in  The Queen in Right of Manitoba v. Air Canada (1978)
86 D.L.R. (3d) 631 (Man. C.A.); aff'd 111 D.L.R. (3d) 513
(S.C.C.) and [1980], 2 S.C.R. 303.  In that case, Manitoba had
levied taxes on food and liquor supplied by Air Canada to its
passengers when flying over that province.  The tax was declared
to be unconstitutional and thus illegal and Air Canada was
ordered to pay interest on the refund despite the lack of any
statutory authority for such a payment.


[14]   It is argued that the assessment of the lands of the
Religious Hospitallers is akin to the imposition of the tax by
Manitoba, i.e. unlawful, and that the refund of a tax unlawfully
collected is not within the ambit of s.6(1) of the Municipal
Interest and Discount Rates Act.  It is argued that this was not
an "overpayment of tax", to use the language of that act and of
s.37(6) of the Assessment Act, because no tax whatever is payable
if the property is exempt.


[15]   The Assessment Act, however, makes no distinction between
paying a tax in excess of what is properly due and paying tax
where none is due.  The relevant section is s.37 and the term
used in s.37(6) for all situations is "overpayment".


[16]   The taxpayer's real complaint here is with the process of
the Assessment Act which requires the taxpayer to pay the amount
assessed, whether or not the assessment is under appeal.  The
process also requires the taxpayer to wait until all appeals are
complete before any refund is payable.  Section 37(6).  The
process requires the taxpayer to pay in advance whether he or she
is right or wrong.  To avoid what the taxpayer sees as an
injustice, the process could be changed so as to require either
no payment or payment of only the amount both sides agree to be
appropriate, pending resolution of the dispute.  While the
individual taxpayer would undoubtedly regard such a change as
rendering the process fairer, it takes little imagination to
foresee the accounting chaos which might take place in such an
event.


[17]   It is argued that the assessment of the properties as not
exempt in the instant case is an error, not that the assessment
was illegal.  That being the case, there is no difference in
principle between the circumstances in this case and those in
Zaidan.  It follows therefore that this court is bound by the
decision of the Supreme Court of Canada in Zaidan, that s.6(1) of
the Municipal Interest and Discount Rates Act removes the matter
of the payment of pre-judgment interest on refunds of municipal
taxes from the ambit of s.128(1) of the Courts of Justice Act and
that pre-judgment interest is not payable on the tax refund in
this case.   I agree with that reasoning.


[18]   We were advised by counsel that the attention of Forget J.
was not drawn to the decision in Zaidan, nor was there any
argument before him on the subject of liability to pay pre-
judgment interest.


Post-Judgment Interest


[19]   The question of post-judgment interest was raised in the
notice of motion in this court.  It was also mentioned in the
facta but not really dealt with in oral argument.


[20]   The order of Forget J. of August 18, 1994 made no
provision for the payment of post-judgment interest.  The order
of the Divisional Court of November 19, 1996, however, provides
as follows:


               "This order bears interest at the
               rate of 6% per year commencing on
               November 21, 1996."




[21]   Section 37(6) of the Assessment Act provides that no
assessment shall be increased or reduced until all complaints,
appeals or proceedings concerning it "have been finally
determined and disposed of".  Then "the assessment shall be
adjusted accordingly and any overpayment resulting from the
adjustment shall be refunded by the municipality".


[22]   I take this to mean that in the instant circumstances, no
refund is due or payable until the completion of the litigation.
That is, until today, or, in the event the proceedings should go
further, until they come to an end.


[23]   It follows from this that, because there was an appeal, no
refund was payable until today.  The order of the Divisional
Court for the payment of post-judgment interest from November 21,
1996, should therefore be set aside.


[24]   That leaves the question whether an order should be made
for the payment of post-judgment interest from today's date.


[25]   The ordinary or basic rule that post-judgment interest is
payable is found in s. 129 of the Courts of Justice Act.  The
relevant parts are as follows:


               129(1)  Money owing under an order,
               including costs to be assessed, or
               costs fixed by the court, bears
               interest at the postjudgment
               interest rate, calculated from the
               date of the order.
               ...


                   (5)  Interest shall not be
               awarded under this section where
               interest is payable by a right
               other than under this section.






[26]   Section 6(1) of the Municipal Interest and Discount Rates
Act, set out earlier in these reasons, provides that a
municipality may pass by-laws to provide for the payment of
interest on refunds "until the day they were refunded or such
other day as may be set out in the by-law".


[27]   In other words, a municipality is given the power to
decide whether or not to pay interest on refunds, and if it does
decide to pay such interest, to determine the rate and the date
to which interest shall run.


[28]   The provisions of s.6(1) of the Municipal Interest and
Discount Rates Act are clearly more specific than those of s.129
of the Courts of Justice Act and in the circumstances must be
taken to displace the operation of the latter.


[29]   This, then, means that whether or not post-judgment
interest is to be paid is left in the hands of the individual
municipality.  This would be consistent with the "complete
statutory code" reasoning advanced in dissent in Zaidan in the
Divisional Court (1988), 64 O.R. (2d) 438 at p.444 and endorsed
by this court on appeal at (1990), 71 O.R. (2d) 65 at p.67.


[30]   There was no evidence before us that Cornwall had passed
any by-law to provide for the payment of any interest on refunds.
I conclude therefore that no order for the payment of post-
judgment interest should be included in the order of this court.


Conclusions and Disposition


[31]   I would therefore grant leave to the Commissioner to raise
a new ground of appeal, namely, the validity of the awards of pre-
and post-judgment interest.  The appeal in those regards should
be allowed and paragraph 5 of the order of Forget J. of August
18, 1994 and the post-judgment interest award in the order of the
Divisional Court of November 19, 1996 should be struck out.


[32]   Having regard to the nature of the issues and their
outcome, I would make no award of costs.
