COURT OF APPEAL FOR ONTARIO
RE:        HERMAN AIRST (Petitioner Husband / Appellant) and
           FAYE AIRST (Respondent Wife / Respondent)
BEFORE:    WEILER J.A. (In Chambers)

           S. Chhabra, for the petitioner husband, responding party
HEARD:     December 7, 1998

                      E N D O R S E M E N T
                                
[1]  This is a motion brought by Mrs. Airst to dismiss Mr.
Airst's appeal from the order of Wein J. wherein, among other
things, she ordered Mr. Airst to make an equalization payment to
his wife of an amount well in excess of $600,000. In the
alternative Mrs. Airst seeks security for costs of the appeal and
of trial. Two other proceedings between the parties which are the
subject of appeal, namely the appeal of Mr. Airst from the order
of costs made at trial and the appeal of Mr. Airst from the
finding of Wein J. that he was in contempt of her order, are not
affected by this motion.


[2]  The parties were married in 1952 and separated in 1982. The
trial respecting the amount of the wife's equalization payment
lasted twenty-three days and was completed in March 26,1998. Mr.
Airst makes his living buying, owning and selling real estate
properties from which he also derives income through a numbered
company of which he is the sole shareholder.


[3]  When Mr. Airst brought his appeal, Mrs. Airst brought a
motion to lift the stay and requested that a portion of the
equalization payment be paid to her. Doherty J.A. stated:

                    The Respondent concedes on appeal
          (contrary to the position that he took at
          trial ) that the Wife is entitled to an
          equalization payment of about $150,000. He
          contends, however, that it should be payable
          in instalments. Given that position, the
          Wife's circumstances, her need to make
          certain significant capital expenditures and
          the husband's determined resistance to
          anything even approaching a reasoned
          determination of these issues, I think the
          Applicant (Wife) is entitled to the benefit
          of at least part of her judgment immediately.
          The Respondent is a man of means and the
          documents suggest he can access significant
          funds on an as needed basis.


[4]  Doherty J.A. then ordered Mr. Airst to pay Mrs. Airst to pay
$50,000 no later than 4:00 p.m. on Monday, October 26, 1998 by
certified cheque delivered to the office of her counsel and a
further $50,000 payable in the same manner no later than Monday,
November 9, 1998.


[5]  When both amounts remained unpaid Mrs. Airst brought a
motion that Mr. Airst be found in contempt and in the alternative
that his appeal be dismissed unless he complied with the order of
Doherty J.A. within 24 hours. On the return of the motion,
counsel for Mr. Airst asked that the motion be adjourned to give
him an opportunity to cross-examine Margaret Ventura (a person
employed in the wife's counsel's firm) on her affidavit in
support of the motion. On November 5, 1998 Austin J.A. granted
Mr. Airst's request and adjourned the motion sine die to enable
the cross-examination to held in court before a judge disposing
of the motion as Austin J.A. had indicated this would be
preferable.


[6]  Mr. Airst also moved to extend the time for moving to vary
the order of Doherty J.A. and to vary that order by extending the
time within which to make the payments totalling $100,000. With
respect to that motion Austin J.A. held:

                    A judge in chambers has no general
          power to vary the order of another judge in
          chambers. The above mentioned Rules (37.14(6)
          and 59.06(2)(a)) and facts do not give me any
          such power. Accordingly, the motion is
          dismissed with costs fixed at $1500 payable
          forthwith.



[7]  On November 26, 1998, Feldman J. indicated that Mr. Chhabra,
counsel for Mr. Airst, was requesting an adjournment due to ill
health arising from epileptic seizures and was not in a position
to conduct a cross-examination or to argue the motion. Feldman J.A.
granted the adjournment to December 7, 1998 on the condition that
Mr. Airst deliver a certified cheque in the amount of $15,000 to
Mrs. Airst by 4 p.m. that day. In default the appeal was to be
struck. She further ordered that Mr. Chhabra retain an agent to
handle the matter and that the adjournment to Dec 7 was
peremptory against Mr. Airst. In default of this term the appeal
was to be struck. Costs fixed at $ 700 were made payable to Mrs.
Airst and were to be paid before December 7, 1998 as a condition
of proceeding on that date.


[8]  On December 7, 1998, Mr. Chhabra appeared and advised that
he had been unable to retain an agent and that as the matter was
peremptory to his client he would proceed with the cross-
examination and motion. At that time  he also tendered $700 in
cash to Mrs. Airst's counsel.


[9]  Mrs. Airst's counsel drew the court's attention to her
amended notice of motion, namely that she was now seeking
security for costs of the appeal and of trial, as alternative
relief to a dismissal of the husband's appeal for failure to
comply with the order of Doherty J.A. and, following cross-
examination of the witness by Mr. Chhabra, the motion then
proceeded.


[10] There is no dispute that Mr. Airst has not paid the amounts
he was ordered to pay by Doherty J.A. Mr. Airst's position is
that he ought not to have his appeal dismissed for failure to
make the $100,000 payment. His position is, essentially, that he
is unable to pay. He states that he does not have sufficient
liquid assets to make the payment.


[11] Pursuant to the order of Wein J., Mrs. Airst has a security
interest in Mr. Airst's real estate holdings and he is unable to
sell or mortgage them without her co-operation. She has refused
to co-operate. Mrs. Airst's position is that the husband would
have had sufficient liquid assets to make the payment in whole or
at least in part but, instead, he has dissipated his assets. In
addition, Mrs. Airst submits that, to the extent the properties
have increased in value since the date of separation, Mr. Airst
could mortgage the  increased equity in the property. Mrs. Airst
also points out that while claiming that he is unable to make the
payments ordered, Mr. Airst has steadfastly refused to provide a
recent audited financial statement for the company of which he is
the sole shareholder and which is the source of his rental income
which is in excess of $300,000 a year.


[12] From the information available to me I cannot say whether
the husband has sufficient liquid assets to meet the order of
Doherty J.A. I am informed that the security over the husband's
assets by Wein J. is in place. I do not have information as to
the actual value of the properties or encumbrances on them prior
to the wife's security being put in place. I would therefore
order that, if Mrs. Airst wishes to have the stay lifted, that
she postpone her security only to the extent of money actually
paid over to her counsel at that time.


[13] With respect to the motion for security for costs, it
appears to me that there is good reason security for costs should
be ordered pursuant to r. 61.06(1)(c). The trial judge gave full
and considered reasons as to the basis on which she accepted
expert evidence of the value of the properties in question. There
are some sixty grounds of appeal advanced and the date of
separation, which was not challenged at trial, is raised as a
ground of appeal. The appeal will be a long and expensive one.
The litigation has extended over many years. Over the course of
the proceedings Mrs. Airst brought motions or made requests at
case conferences on 16 occasions to compel Mr. Airst to produce
financial disclosure or to provide Mrs. Airst or her experts with
access to information or properties and fourteen orders or
agreements at case conferences were made for Mr. Airst to provide
such disclosure. Mr. Airst also changed counsel frequently. On
all of the evidence it appears to me that Mr. Airst should be
required to post security for the cost of this appeal and some
security for the costs of trial. I therefore order that the sum
of $40,000 be posted as security for costs of the appeal and that
$60,000 be posted as security for the costs of the trial for a
total of $100,000. Payment of that security is to be made by 4
p.m. on December 21. Mrs. Airst is to further postpone her
security only to the extent necessary to achieve this result.


[14] Costs of this motion are payable to Mrs. Airst forthwith
after assessment and in any event of the cause.
