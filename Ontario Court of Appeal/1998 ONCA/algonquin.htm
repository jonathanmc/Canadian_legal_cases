E N D O R S E M E N T

[1]              The principal issue in this appeal is whether the Divisional Court erred in declaring the 1996 Elk Lake, Upper Spanish and Temagami Forest Management Plans and associated work schedules void and of no force or effect.[1]

The Validity of the Plans

[2]              In concluding that impugned plans were invalid, the Divisional Court found that:

(a)       The statutory requirements of compliance with the Manual were mandatory and not directory;

(b)       The plans and work schedules were not saved by the deeming and transition provisions of s. 71 of the Crown Forest Sustainability Act, 1994, S.O. 1994, c. 25 (the “Act”), nor by the phase-in provisions of the Manual itself; and

(c)       The plans and work schedules did not comply substantially with the Manual, nor with the general principles of sustainability required by the Act.

[3]              We agree with the Divisional Court to the extent that it found that the 1996 plans failed to satisfy the standards of sustainability required by the Act and as such, they were invalid. In this respect, we adopt the following findings and conclusions of the Divisional Court:

(a)       the statutory requirements of compliance with the Manual are mandatory, not directory;

(b)       the 1996 plans could not be saved by the deeming provisions in s. 71 of the Act; and

(c)              the 1996 plans did not comply with the standards of sustainability required by the Act.

Is Appendix VIII ultra vires?

[4]              In arriving at its conclusion that the 1996 plans were invalid, the Divisional Court considered the phase-in provisions in Appendix VIII of the Manual and found them to be ultra vires. Accordingly, the court quashed the whole of Appendix VIII and declared that compliance with the full requirements of Part A of the Manual was necessary for the approval, amendment, or replacement of the 1996 plans in question.

[5]              In concluding that Appendix VIII of the Manual was ultra vires, the Divisional Court found, correctly in our view, that the statutory requirement of compliance with the standards of sustainability set out in the Manual is mandatory and that in order to approve the plans in question, the Minister had to be satisfied that the  plans complied with those standards.

[6]              In support of its contention that the 1996 plans were valid, one of the arguments advanced by the Minister was that in assessing whether the plans complied with the Manual, the Manual had to be considered as a whole, including the phase-in provisions contained in Appendix VIII. That being so, the Minister maintained that since the impugned plans did in fact comply with the phase-in provisions of s. 2.1 of Appendix VIII, the Minister was justified in approving them.

[7]              The Divisional Court disagreed with this submission. In its opinion, apart from the grandfathering provisions found in s. 71 of the Act, the statute was otherwise silent on the issue of transition and it did not provide the Minister with authority to phase-in the Manual. Accordingly, as the following passages at p. 186 of the judgment reveal, the Divisional Court viewed Appendix VIII as an unauthorized attempt by the Minister to amend s. 71 of the Act by extending the statutory transition period beyond that contemplated by s. 71:

The government by regulation through the manual took the phase-in enacted by the Legislative Assembly in s. 71 of the Act and purported, by deemed compliance provisions, to amend it to a much longer phase-in. Nothing in the statute even purported to delegate this power to the government. The effect of this regulation if permitted to stand, would be to suspend in part by executive action the application of a statute that is fully in force by operation of law.

The question is not whether or not it was wise or reasonable for the Ministry to choose to phase in the requirements of the manual. The question is whether it had the power, without any statutory authority, to delay the coming into force of the new forest sustainability provisions until a time later than the time prescribed by law.

Neither the government nor the officials of the Ministry have power to suspend even in part the application of a statute duly enacted by the Legislative Assembly that is fully in force by operation  of law.

The deeming and phase-in provisions of the manual, except in respect of the 1995 plans, are ultra vires the CFSA. Unauthorized by the statute, they have no legal force. They cannot save the impugned Elk Lake, Upper Spanish, and Temagami plans and work schedules.

[8]              With respect, we are not persuaded that the Divisional Court was correct in concluding that the Minister lacked the necessary authority under the statute to phase in the provisions of the Manual. Our  concern stems primarily from the Divisional Court’s mischaracterization of the nature and purpose of s. 71 of the Act.

[9]              The Divisional Court found that s. 71 of the Act occupied the field with respect to phasing in. We do not agree with this interpretation. Indeed, we do not read s. 71 as addressing the issue of phasing in at all.

[10]         Section 71 did not purport to validate forest management plans which failed to comply in every respect with the provisions of the Manual. Rather, it grandfathered certain plans which predated the Act, regardless of whether such plans conformed in whole, part or not at all with the provisions of the Manual.

[11]         Once this is understood, it becomes apparent that the Divisional Court acted upon a faulty premise in concluding that the Minister lacked authority under the statute to phase in the provisions of the Manual. Having concluded, incorrectly, that s. 71 constituted a complete code with respect to phasing in, the Divisional Court reasoned that Appendix VIII was in conflict with s. 71 and that absent statutory authority, the Minister had no warrant to extend the phase-in period beyond that contemplated by s. 71.

[12]         Given our conclusion that s. 71 does not address the issue of phase-in at all, we do not accept the Divisional Court’s finding that the phase-in provisions in Appendix VIII conflict with s. 71. Furthermore, we see nothing in the Act that would prevent the Minister from including an appendix in the Manual phasing in some of the sustainability directives contained in Part A of the Manual, so long as the phase-in provisions meet the sustainability requirements of the Act.

[13]         Section 68(2) of the Act required the Minister to ensure that every plan complied with the Manual. Under ss. 68(3) and (5), the Legislature mandated that the Manual contain certain sustainability provisions which every forest management plan must adhere to.

[14]         In our opinion, these sections do not preclude the Minister from phasing in portions of Part A of the Manual. They do, however, prevent the Minister from enacting phase-in provisions which fall below the standards of sustainability required by ss. 68(3) and (5) of the Act. In other words, the Minister could not circumvent those provisions by including in the appendix to the Manual, phase-in provisions which, if complied with, would result in forest management plans falling below the statutory standards of sustainability.

[15]         Based upon the record, we have no way of knowing whether Part A of the Manual simply reflected the standards of sustainability required by ss. 68(3) and (5) or whether it went further. To the extent, if any, that Part A went further, there would be nothing to prevent the Minister from phasing in only those portions of the Manual which went beyond the statutory sustainability requirements.

[16]         It follows, in our view, that the Divisional Court erred in principle in concluding that the Minister could not, due to want of authority, phase in the provisions of Part A of the Manual. Since that was the basis upon which the Divisional Court found Appendix VIII to be ultra vires, that finding cannot stand; nor can we sustain the related declaration that the full requirements of the Manual must be complied with for the approval, amendment or replacement of the impugned plans. The declarations in question are found in paragraphs 3, 4 and 5 of the judgment.  Those paragraphs cannot stand and an order will go deleting them from the judgment.

Are the Plans Saved by Appendix VIII?

[17]         The Divisional Court’s error in striking down Appendix VIII as ultra vires does not, in our view, affect its ultimate conclusion regarding the invalidity of the 1996 plans. In this respect, we agree with the reasons for judgment of the Divisional Court in which the court addressed an alternative argument put forward by the Minister, namely, that the plans, prepared in accordance with s. 2.1 of Appendix VIII,  were salvageable because they complied with the statutory principles of sustainability embodied in ss. 68(3) and (5) of the Act. While the court recognized that they could have been salvageable on that basis, it went on to find that in this case, they did not comply.

[18]         The Divisional Court found that the three impugned plans did not meet the statutory standards required by the Act. At p. 196, the court stated:

The things missing from the plans are things at the heart of the new concept of sustainability introduced by the new Act. At the heart of the new statute is the introduction of measurement indicators by which the sustainability of the forest can be determined openly and publicly and objectively.

[19]         The court addressed the standards as they are reflected in Part A of the Manual. That approach may well have been appropriate in this case because there was no suggestion that Part A of the Manual in its current format does contain requirements which go beyond the sustainability provisions of ss. 68(3) and (5) of the Act. If it had been otherwise, then the court would have been required to look directly at those sections, presumably with the assistance of expert evidence, in order to determine if the plans, prepared in accordance with the phase-in provisions, did in fact comply with the s. 68 requirements.

[20]         We agree with the Divisional Court’s conclusion that the plans in question, prepared in accordance with s. 2.1 of Appendix VIII, clearly fell short of the sustainability standards required by the Act. In this regard, we have no reason to question the findings of the Divisional Court that the impugned plans failed to address issues such as Crown forest diversity objectives, landscape patterns, habitat for animal life and social and economic objectives, all of which were necessary to the requirements of s. 68(5) of the Act.

[21]         Three further matters remain to be addressed.

            (i)        EAB Conditions

[22]         The Divisional Court found that the 1996 plans failed to comply with certain conditions contained in the October 1995 decision of the Environmental Assessment Board. Although raised as a ground of appeal, in oral argument, counsel for the Minister did not press this issue.

[23]         Bearing in mind the concession made on behalf of the Minister that compliance with these conditions does not pose a problem in rectifying the plans in issue and having regard to the fact that the Divisional Court did not rely upon the alleged breaches as a basis for declaring the impugned plans invalid, we see no need to resolve this issue.

            (ii)       Grace period

[24]         The appellant submitted that the Divisional Court erred in imposing too short a grace period. In oral argument, counsel for the Minister candidly admitted that the impugned plans could and would be rectified within the grace period allotted by the Divisional Court. At the same time, it was made clear that the grace period was not sufficient to rectify other 1996 plans which the Minister had approved and which were presently operational.

[25]         Accordingly, we were asked to extend the grace period to avoid any potential problems that might arise with respect to outstanding 1996 plans that, to date, have not been challenged.

[26]         We decline to do so. In our view, if, as and when the remaining 1996 plans are challenged, the question of the grace period, if any, and its length, is best left to the court hearing the application.

            (iii)     Costs

[27]         The Minister contended that the Divisional Court erred in awarding solicitor and client costs against the Minister and in awarding costs to the junior solicitors  for the respondents. We see no merit in either submission.

[28]         In our view, it was open to the Divisional Court to make the award of costs it did and we have not been persuaded that the court erred in the exercise of its discretion.

CONCLUSION

[29]         In the result, an order will go vacating paragraphs 3, 4 and 5 of the judgment. In all other respects, the appeal is dismissed.

[30]         In view of the partial success achieved by the appellants, the respondents should have their costs of the appeal on a party and party basis payable by the Minister.


[1]               The judgment is reported at (1998), 26 C.E.L.R. (N.S.) 163.
