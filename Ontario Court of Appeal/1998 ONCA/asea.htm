                          ENDORSEMENT

[1]   This  is  an  appeal of a judgment of  The  Honourable  Mr.
Justice  Sheard, dated June 27, 1996, in the amount  of  $900,220
plus pre-judgment interest, for a total of $l,342,183.63.


[2]  The appellant contracted with the respondent for repair of a
transformer  from  the  Hydro Ontario  Darlington  Nuclear  Power
Station.  It  had  originally  been  supplied  to  Hydro  by  the
appellant  Westinghouse  Canada  Inc.  under  warranty.  When  it
suffered electrical and mechanical failure on February 17,  1989,
Hydro had it delivered to Westinghouse in
  Hamilton, Ontario for repair. Because the transformer was still
under warranty, there was a substantial dispute between Hydro and
Westinghouse as to which of them was responsible for the costs of
repair.


[3]   Westinghouse  contracted with the  respondent,  Asea  Brown
Boveri, to perform the repairs. The case was argued before Sheard
J.  as  a  bailment case. However, although there may  have  been
aspects  of  bailment involved, this was actually  a  case  of  a
simple  contract between the respondent and Westinghouse, and  it
was argued before us as such.


[4]   After  the  respondent had performed  the  repairs  on  the
transformer and loaded it on a rail car for delivery to Hydro, it
received a letter from Westinghouse instructing it not to deliver
the  transformer to Hydro. By the time it received  that  letter,
some  of  the  accessories to the transformer  had  already  been
shipped  to  Hydro. The respondent was aware that the transformer
was   for   the  Darlington  plant,  and  it  received   shipping
instructions from Westinghouse, in December of 1989 stating  that
the transformer was to be "returned to site".


[5]   The letter of instruction not to return the transformer  to
Hydro  was  not sent by Westinghouse until all of the contractual
obligations  of  the respondent had been carried out  except  for
actually instructing the railway company to move the transformer.
It  was an attempt at a unilateral change in the contract for the
acknowledged sole purpose of giving Westinghouse leverage in  its
dispute with Hydro.


[6]  Had Westinghouse performed the repairs itself, it could have
retained  the  transformer, but it would have had to  suffer  the
consequences of a possible law suit by Hydro for its  return  and
damages for its retention. In our view, it was encumbent  on  the
appellant  and  Hydro to resolve their own differences,  and  not
place the burden of their dispute on the respondent. It should be
noted  that  even when sued for the value of the  work  which  it
authorized the respondent to perform, Westinghouse did  not  join
Hydro in the action.


[7]   It  was  completely  improper for Westinghouse,  after  the
respondent  had, in good faith, performed the repairs  under  the
contract,  to attempt to use the respondent to gain  leverage  in
its dispute with Hydro.


[8]  The appeal is dismissed with costs.
