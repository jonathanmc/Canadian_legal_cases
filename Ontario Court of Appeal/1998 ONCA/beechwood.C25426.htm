O’CONNOR J.A.:
OVERVIEW

[1]  These are the reasons for the first of two related appeals.1
The  only  appellant in this appeal, 942836 Ontario Inc. (“942”),
appeals  the  judgment  of Doyle J. in which  he  set  aside  the
transfer  of  225 shares of the respondent (“Beechwood”)  to  942
because  the  directors  had not consented  to  the  transfer  as
required  by  a by-law of Beechwood first passed  in  1873.   942
raises  three  grounds of appeal:  first, that  the  1873  by-law
requiring the consent of the directors was not authorized by  the
special Act incorporating Beechwood and was therefore ultra vires
the  company; in the alternative, 942 argues that the 1873 by-law
was  repealed  by  a  by-law of Beechwood  passed  in  1941;  and
thirdly,  in the further alternative, it is argued that  pursuant
to  s.  56(3) of the Business Corporations Act, R.S.O.  1990,  c.
B.16,  the  restriction  on  transfer  in  the  1873  by-law   is
ineffective  against 942 because 942 acquired the shares  without
actual  knowledge of the restriction.  For the  reasons  set  out
below, it is my view that the appeal should be dismissed.

FACTS
     The By-laws

[2]   Beechwood  was incorporated by special Act of  the  Ontario
Legislature  (the  “Beechwood Act”) on March  29,  1873  for  the
purposes  of  owning  and operating a cemetery  in  the  City  of
Ottawa.  There are two sections in the Beechwood Act dealing with
the  enactment of by-laws that are relevant to the issues  raised
on this appeal.  Section 2 provides that shares are “transferable
on  the  books  of the company in such manner as the  corporation
shall  by its by-laws direct.”  Section 9 authorized the  company
to  pass  by-laws  “for the management of  the  business  of  the
company.”


[3]   The  original  by-law of the company was  passed  in  1873.
Article XV provided:
          
          XV.   No  shares in the Capital Stock of  the
          Company  shall  be transferable  without  the
          consent of the Board of Directors.
          
          

[4]  In 1941, s. 56(2) of  the Companies Act, R.S.O. 1937, c. 251
which  applied to Beechwood had a specific provision  prohibiting
restrictions on the transfer of shares.2  On April 15, 1941,  the
president  of  Beechwood presented a new by-law to the  directors
advising that the solicitor of the company had been instructed to
examine  the company’s by-laws and to suggest changes that  would
modernize  them. It appears that two by-laws were  enacted:  one,
dated  April 15, 1941; and the other, which is undated,  entitled
By-law  B.   The provisions of the two by-laws are identical  and
for convenience I will refer to them as the 1941 by-law.


[5]   Section  27 of the 1941 by-law which follows  the  heading,
Repeal, reads:
          
          27.  All by-laws of the Company inconsistent with the
               foregoing by-laws are hereby repealed.
[6]   The  1941  by-law does not refer to the transferability  of
shares.


[7]   Before  approving the proposed new by-law at the  directors
meeting  on  April  15,  1941,  the directors  consented  to  two
transfers  of  shares.  During the period following that  meeting
until 1991, there were approximately 262 shares transferred which
were  recorded in the books of Beechwood.  The board of directors
did not consider, give consent or pass resolutions respecting any
of  these  transfers.   Except for the  transfers  that  are  the
subject  to  this  appeal,  none  of  these  transfers  has  been
challenged by Beechwood for failure to obtain the consent of  the
directors.


[8]   Prior  to  the  commencement of this  litigation  in  1992,
Timothy  Graham (no relation to the parties in this  appeal)  who
had  been  the secretary-treasurer of Beechwood since  1975,  was
unaware  of  the  1873 by-law; he believed that the  1941  by-law
which  did  not  contain a requirement for  the  consent  of  the
directors  to the transfer of shares was the operative  governing
by-law of Beechwood.


[9]   The  trial judge found that none of the directors in  1991,
other than John Graham whose knowledge I will discuss below, knew
of  the requirement for consent by the directors contained in the
1873 by-law.

     Transfer of Shares to 942

[10]  John  Graham,  who is an appellant in the  related  appeal,
became a director of Beechwood in 1972 and its president in 1974.
He  continued to hold both positions until the board of directors
removed him in November 1991.


[11]  By  December of 1990, John Graham and his  brokerage  firm,
John  Graham and Company Limited, had acquired 225 shares of  the
authorized  capital  of  387  shares  in  the  capital  stock  of
Beechwood.   The  circumstances under which they acquired  88  of
these  shares are the subject of the related appeal but  are  not
relevant to the disposition of this appeal.


[12] On December 14, 1990, John Graham made an urgent request  to
his  brother,  David  Graham, to borrow 3.2  million  dollars  to
rectify  a capital shortfall in his brokerage company, which  was
licensed under the Quebec Securities Act with the Montreal  Stock
Exchange.   David Graham agreed to make the loan to  his  brother
and  to  take  as  collateral,  a pledge  of  certain  securities
including  the  225  shares of Beechwood  which  were  registered
either in the name of John Graham or in the name of his brokerage
company.


[13]  David  Graham, who is an experienced businessman,  retained
solicitors  to  complete  the  loan  and  security  transactions.
Because  of  the deadline set by the Montreal Stock Exchange  for
rectifying  the  capital shortfall there  was  considerable  time
pressure  in  completing the necessary searches and documentation
for the transactions.


[14]  David  Graham  relied on his advisors, both  financial  and
legal, to advise him of the adequacy and validity of the security
he  was  to  receive.  On December 20, 1990, the loan transaction
was  completed and the moneys were advanced by David Graham.   As
part of the transaction David Graham, through his solicitors, was
provided with a pledge agreement and share certificates  for  the
225  shares  of  Beechwood.  The certificates were  in  the  form
normally  used by Beechwood and contained the words, “subject  to
the  Act incorporating the Company, and to the By-laws, Rules and
Regulations  already passed, or hereafter passed.”  In  addition,
David Graham’s solicitors prepared draft resolutions of the board
of  directors  of  Beechwood consenting to the  transfer  of  the
shares which provided in part:
          
          Whereas John Graham Jr. (“the Pledgor”) is  a
          registered owner of the securities  described
          in  Section 1 hereof (the “Pledged Property”)
          issued  by the Corporation and in respect  of
          which  there  are  certain  restrictions   on
          transfer  as provided in the charter  or  by-
          laws  of  the  Corporation or in a  unanimous
          shareholder agreement and ...
          
          

[15]  They  also prepared a similar draft relating to the  shares
owned  by  John  Graham and Company Limited.   On  closing,  John
Graham  gave  an  undertaking  that  he  would  have  the   draft
resolutions  signed  by the signing officers of  Beechwood  after
approval of the transfers by the directors.


[16]  After  the  loan moneys had been advanced,  David  Graham’s
solicitors  tried  to obtain the executed resolutions  from  John
Graham  on  at least five different occasions over  a  period  of
several months but never succeeded in doing so.  In a memo  dated
May   14,   1991,    David   Graham’s   solicitors   specifically
contemplated  the  requirement  for  obtaining  consent  of   the
directors  to  the transfers.  The memo read in part,   “[u]nless
these  resolutions are obtained, enforcement  of  the  pledge  of
shares will be hampered by the fact that the board may oppose the
transfer into your name ....”


[17]  By April 1991 David Graham was aware that his brother might
default  on  the  loan and that he might have to realize  on  his
security.   On  May  15,  1991, he had 942 incorporated  for  the
purpose  of receiving the 225 shares of Beechwood which had  been
pledged  to  him  in  December of 1990.  Beechwood’s  shareholder
register  records the transfer of 225 shares by John  Graham  and
John  Graham  and Company Limited to 942 on May 30, 1991.   Share
certificates reflecting these transfers were issued in  the  name
of  942  and were signed by John Graham as president, and Timothy
Graham  as secretary-treasurer.  The directors were not requested
either before or after May 30, 1991 to consent to the transfer of
the shares.


[18] In October 1991, there were complaints made to the board  of
directors about John Graham.  An investigation revealed  that  he
had  made  a  number of unauthorized investments  with  Beechwood
funds  and  had  been  paid several improper  expense  claims  by
Beechwood.  At about the same time the directors, other than John
Graham,  learned for the first time of the transfer  of  the  225
shares to 942.  Prior to that time they were not aware that  John
Graham  had  acquired voting control of Beechwood nor  were  they
aware  that  he  had  been  using his position  as  president  to
surreptitiously acquire shares sold to Beechwood by  the  estates
of  deceased shareholders.  The directors were concerned  because
it  was their understanding that Beechwood which was incorporated
to  provide burial services to the community would always operate
without a majority shareholder.


[19]  On  November 4, 1991, the directors removed John Graham  as
president  of  Beechwood and early in 1992  caused  Beechwood  to
bring  this  action seeking among other things to set  aside  the
transfer  of  the 225 shares to 942.3  During the course  of  the
litigation  the  directors learned for  the  first  time  of  the
provision  in the 1873 by-law requiring the consent of the  board
to  the  transfer of shares.  Beechwood then advanced the failure
of  the  directors  to consent to the transfer as  an  additional
ground for setting aside the transfer to 942.


[20] At trial, John Graham testified that he did not know whether
or not he knew of the requirement for the consent of the transfer
of  shares.  However, on July 10, 1991, he wrote a letter to  his
lawyer  which would indicate that he did know of the requirement.
In that letter he said:
          
          I  was  only  able to arrange  Alex  Graham’s
          election at the annual meeting on June  12th.
          I   have   not  yet  secured  the  Directors’
          approval  to  the  ‘transfer/allotment’.    I
          believe  if I attempted to get this  approval
          and  if  the  Directors  were  aware  of  the
          consequences    of   the   transfer,    those
          Directors, independent of myself,: ‘3-i.e.  a
          majority’ would turn it down??
          
          

[21]  David  Graham did not give evidence.  Beechwood  read  into
evidence extracts from his examination for discovery in which  he
said  that he did not know that the consent of the directors  was
required  to the transfer of shares and that he had a  belief  on
information  from his brother that the shares were  transferable.
However, he also said that he knew there were deficiencies in the
security  he  was receiving from his brother, that he  was  aware
that  Beechwood  shares were not traded on a stock  exchange  and
that in some circumstances a resolution of directors was required
for  the  transfer  of  shares.  Further, he  indicated  that  he
proceeded  with the loan despite the fact of the deficiencies  in
the security because he wanted to help his brother.


[22]  In  his discovery evidence David Graham also expressed  the
view  that the draft resolutions were prepared by his solicitors,
“to  be careful” but that it was his understanding that they were
not  necessary.  However, David Graham’s solicitors and financial
advisors did not testify.


[23]  The  trial judge made the following findings of  fact  with
respect to the knowledge of the requirement of the consent of the
board of directors to the transfer of shares:
     
     (a)  John Graham knew of the requirement;
     
     (b)  The other directors and Timothy Graham did not know  of
          the  requirement  until after the commencement  of  the
          litigation;
     
     (c)  The  wording  on  the share certificate  form  used  by
          Beechwood is not conspicuous enough for a purchaser  to
          have  actual  knowledge  of  the  requirement  for  the
          consent of the directors;
     
     (d)  The   blank  resolutions  prepared  by  David  Graham’s
          solicitors  to  be  signed by the signing  officers  of
          Beechwood after approval by the board of directors show
          their  knowledge of the requirement for consent of  the
          directors; and
     
     (e)  The knowledge of David Graham’s solicitors must be imputed
          to him.


[24]  The  trial  judge found that the 1873  by-law  was  validly
enacted,  that  it had not been repealed in 1941 and  that  David
Graham  had  actual  knowledge  of  it.   Because  the  board  of
directors  had not consented,  he set aside the transfer  of  225
shares  from John Graham and John Graham and Company  Limited  to
942 and directed that the shareholder register be rectified.

ANALYSIS
     The Ultra Vires Issue

[25]  The  appellant argues that Article XV of the  1873  by-law,
which  provides  that  no  shares are  transferable  without  the
consent  of  the  board of directors, was not authorized  by  the
Beechwood Act and was therefore ultra vires.  As a result, it  is
argued  that the trial judge erred in setting aside the  transfer
of the 225 shares to 942.


[26]  Beechwood  was created by a special Act of the  Legislature
and  therefore  has  only  those powers which  are  expressly  or
impliedly granted to it by its incorporating statute: Communities
Economic  Development Fund v. Canadian Pickles  Corp.  (1991),  3
S.C.R.  388 at 402.  It was open to the Legislature to confer  on
Beechwood the authority to enact by-laws requiring board approval
for  the transfer of shares:  Canada National Fire Insurance  Co.
v.  Hutchings (1918), 39 D.L.R. 401 (P.C.) at p. 407.  The  issue
therefore is whether the Beechwood Act conferred the authority to
enact Article XV of the 1873 by-law.


[27]  Beechwood  argues  that  there  are  two  sections  in  the
Beechwood Act which give the necessary authority.  The first,  s.
2, provides in part:
          
          ...;  shares to be transferable on the  books
          of  the  corporation in such  manner  as  the
          corporation shall by its by-laws direct;  ...
          [Emphasis added.]
          
          

[28]  I  agree that s. 2 confers the necessary authority.  In  my
view,  the  majority decision of the Supreme Court of  Canada  in
Edmonton Country Club Ltd. v. Case (1974), 44 D.L.R. (3d) 554  is
determinative.    The  Edmonton  Country  Club   was   a   public
corporation  incorporated by articles of  association  under  the
Companies  Act  of Alberta, R.S.A. 1942, c. 240.  The  respondent
challenged art. 20A of the articles which precluded the  transfer
of  shares in the company without the consent of the majority  of
the board whose discretion the articles provided, was unfettered.
At common law the presumption was that corporations created under
a  statute, which included corporations incorporated by  articles
or  memorandum of association, had only those powers  which  were
expressly or impliedly granted to them by statute.  To the extent
that  a  corporation acted beyond its powers,  its  actions  were
ultra   vires   and  therefore  invalid.   Communities   Economic
Development Fund v. Canadian Pickles Corp., supra, at p. 402.4


[29]  In  the  Edmonton Country Club case  Dickson  J.,  for  the
majority,  referred to s. 61 of the Companies Act of Alberta  and
found that the right of a shareholder to transfer shares was  not
absolute.  At p. 567, he said:
          
          The  right  of a shareholder to transfer  his
          shares is undoubtedly one of the incidents of
          share ownership, assured by the Companies Act
          of  Alberta,  s.  61, ‘The  shares  or  other
          interest  of  any  member in  a  company  are
          personal  estate, transferable in the  manner
          provided by the articles of the company  ...’
          ...  but  the  right  is not  absolute.   [My
          emphasis.]
          
          

[30]  Dickson J. concluded that art. 20A was not ultra vires  the
company.   Although Dickson J. did not specifically  analyze  the
meaning of the words “transferable in the manner provided by  the
articles of the company”, it is essential to his conclusion  that
a  provision  in  the  articles  requiring  the  consent  of  the
directors  to the transfer of shares falls within the meaning  of
that  language.  If it were otherwise and the language of  s.  61
did  not  include  restrictions on transfers,  art.  20A  of  the
articles  would have conflicted with s. 61 of the  Companies  Act
and for that reason would have been ultra vires the company.


[31] The language of s. 2 of the Beechwood Act, “transferable  in
such  manner as the corporation shall by its by-laws direct”,  is
not  materially different from the words used in  s.  61  of  the
Alberta Companies Act.  In my view it makes no difference to  the
interpretation  of this language that in one case  Beechwood  was
incorporated  by  special Act and acted by  by-law,  and  in  the
other,  the  Edmonton  Country Club was  incorporated  under  the
general  Companies Act and the provision conferring the authority
on the directors was included in the articles of association.   I
see  no principled or logical basis for interpreting these  words
differently in the two different contexts.


[32] 942 argues that there is a distinction between by-laws which
regulate  the mode of transfer of shares and those which restrict
transferability.  942 relies on two cases in submitting that s. 2
of  the  Beechwood Act permits Beechwood to regulate but  not  to
restrict transfer.  The first is the decision of this court in Re
Good and Jacob Y. Shantz Son & Co. Limited (1911), 23 O.L.R.  544
(C.A.)  in  which Moss C.J.O., for the majority, found  that  the
wording  of  s.  45 (with words similar to s. 2 of the  Beechwood
Act) and s. 80 of the Companies Act, R.S.C. 1906, c. 79, did  not
confer  authority  to  enact a by-law  which  would  require  the
consent  of  the board to the transfer of shares.  His  decision,
however,  turned  on  the language of s.  80  which  specifically
authorized the directors to make by-laws regulating the  transfer
of  shares.   Moss C.J.O. reasoned that regulation was  different
from  restriction, that ss. 45 and 80 must be read  together  and
that  the  by-laws  referred to in s. 45 could  only  mean  those
authorized by s. 80.5  Therefore, he concluded the language in s.
45 was limited to regulating the transfer of shares.


[33] In the second case, Canadian National Fire Insurance Co.  v.
Hutchings,  supra, the Privy Council reached the same  conclusion
on  the  language in ss. 132 and 138 of the Companies Act, R.S.C.
1906,  c.  79.   The wording of these sections was not materially
different from that considered in Good and Shantz.  In  my  view,
these  cases  are distinguishable because in each, the  Companies
Act  contained  a  specific  provision  authorizing  by-laws   to
regulate  the transfer of shares and the court reasoned that  the
reference to by-laws in the more generally worded sections, s. 45
in  the  Good and Shantz case and s. 138 in the Canadian National
Fire  Insurance  case  was limited by the specific  reference  to
regulation in the other section.  There was no similar  provision
referring to the regulation of the transfer of shares in the 1942
Companies  `Act of Alberta which was considered in  the  Edmonton
Country  Club  case,  nor  is there a similar  provision  in  the
Beechwood Act.


[34]  In the case of Beechwood, s. 9 of the Beechwood Act,  which
is  the  only  other  relevant section,  does  not  expressly  or
implicitly  narrow the ambit of s. 2 but rather confers  a  broad
authority to enact by-laws for the management of the business  of
Beechwood.  The relevant part of s. 9 reads as follows:
          
          9.     ... to make and frame by-laws for  the
          government  and control of the said  officers
          and  servants; and also to make and frame all
          other by-laws, rules and regulations for  the
          management of the business of the corporation
          in   all  particulars  and  details,  whether
          herein  specially enumerated or not, and  the
          same  at any time to repeal, alter, amend  or
          modify; ... [Emphasis added.]
          
          

I am therefore of the view that both of the cases relied upon by
942 are distinguishable.


[35]  Beechwood also submits that s. 9 confers the  authority  to
enact  the impugned by-law. Although it is not necessary for  the
conclusion I reach, it seems to me that the phrase “the  business
of the corporation” used in s. 9 should be interpreted broadly to
include  those  matters  normally  found  in  the  organizing  or
original by-laws of companies and which in this case are found in
both  the 1873 and 1941 by-laws of Beechwood.  Section 9  is  the
only section in the Beechwood Act which could possibly confer the
authority  for enacting this type of by-law which often  includes
provisions  relating to the capital stock  of  the  company.   As
there  is  nothing in s. 9 or elsewhere in the Beechwood  Act  to
limit  or  restrict  this authority, s.  9,  in  my  view,  could
independently of s. 2 confer the necessary authority for enacting
the challenged by-law.


[36] Finally, I am satisfied that interpreting the Beechwood  Act
to  require director consent to the transfer of shares  does  not
lead  to an unreasonable fetter on the property interests of  the
shareholders.  The power of the directors to withhold consent  to
transfers of shares is circumscribed by their fiduciary duties to
act  honestly and in good faith with a view to the best interests
of  the corporation, (s. 134(1) of the Business Corporations  Act
of  Ontario) and to act reasonably in the specific context  of  a
particular proposed share transfer.  In the Edmonton Country Club
case, both the majority and the minority (Laskin J.) were of  the
view  that  the power of directors to refuse approval  for  share
transfers  must  be  exercised reasonably and  differed  only  on
whether  that  power should be controlled by  the  context  of  a
particular case (majority view) or whether the provision  in  the
articles  of  association should be struck down  because  it  was
arbitrary on its face (minority view).


[37]  In  this  appeal,  942 has not put  in  issue  whether  the
Beechwood  board  would  be acting unreasonably  if  it  withheld
consent  to the transfer of the 225 shares.  It is therefore  not
necessary to deal with that issue.  I raise it solely to make the
point  that interpreting the Beechwood Act as I do does not  lead
to  an arbitrary or unfettered authority over the transfer of the
personal property of Beechwood’s shareholders


[38] For the above reasons, I am satisfied that the Beechwood Act
empowered  the company to pass Article XV of the 1873 by-law  and
that 942’s argument that it is ultra vires must fail.

     The 1941 by-law Issue

[39] 942 argues that s. 27 of the 1941 by-law repealed Article XV
of the 1873 by-law.  This is strictly an issue of interpreting s.
27 which reads as follows:
          
          27.   Repeal.   All by-laws  of  the  Company
          inconsistent with the foregoing  by-laws  are
          hereby repealed.
          
          

[40]  None  of  the sections preceding s. 27 in the  1941  by-law
referred to the transferability of shares.  The only two sections
set  out  under  the heading “Shares” concern  the  allotment  of
shares and the content of share certificates.  Article XV of  the
1873 by-law which provides, “[n]o shares in the Capital Stock  of
the  Company  shall be transferable without the  consent  of  the
board  of  directors”  is  not  inconsistent  with  any  of   the
provisions of the 1941 by-law.  Therefore, I am of the view  that
the  ordinary meaning of the language in s. 27 would not have the
effect of repealing Article XV.


[41]  942  argues  that an examination of the circumstances  both
before  and  after the 1941 by-law was passed should  lead  to  a
different interpretation.  In 1941, s. 56(2) of the Companies Act
of  Ontario  prohibited restrictions on the transfer  of  shares.
This section had first been enacted in 1912 and was continued  in
the  1937 Revised Statutes of Ontario. In presenting the 1941 by-
law to a meeting of the directors of Beechwood, the president  of
Beechwood  said  that changes were being made  to  modernize  the
corporation’s by-laws.  This, it is argued, showed  an  intention
to  repeal Article XV of the 1873 by-law in order to conform with
what  was by then a basic principle in the companies law  statute
of Ontario.


[42]  The appellant further relies on the fact that from 1941  to
1991  the board of directors of Beechwood did not consider,  give
consent   to  or  pass  a  resolution  respecting  any   of   the
approximately 262 share transfers that were recorded in the books
of the company.  The trial judge found that in 1991 the directors
of  Beechwood,  other  than John Graham,  did  not  know  of  the
requirement  in  the  1873 by-law and that  Timothy  Graham,  the
secretary-treasurer,  considered  the  1941  by-law  to  be   the
operative by-law of the company.  It seems likely that if any one
had  inquired  of the company, other than of John  Graham,  about
restrictions on the transfer of shares he would have been advised
that there were none.  This it is argued shows that the intent of
those who passed the 1941 by-law was to repeal Article XV of  the
1873 by-law.


[43]  The  issue, as I have said, is purely one of interpretation
of  the words used in the 1941 by-law.  The principles that apply
are well settled; a court will not look to extrinsic evidence  to
interpret a by-law unless the wording is unclear or ambiguous and
the  court  may  look to the whole of the document  in  order  to
determine if ambiguity or lack of clarity exists.  See Re  Donner
Canadian  Foundation (1960), 26 D.L.R. (2d) 274 (P.E.I.C.A.)  and
Augdome  Corp.  v. Gray, [1975] 2 S.C.R. 354.  The importance  of
applying these principles to documents such as corporate by-laws,
articles  of  association, letters patent, share  provisions  and
corporate  public  disclosure documents upon  which  arms  length
parties  rely  in  making commercial decisions is  obvious.   The
nature  of this type of document requires that wherever possible,
interpretation  be based solely on the ordinary  meaning  of  the
words  used in the document in order to bring certainty for those
who   rely  upon  the  document.   In  most  situations,  it   is
unrealistic  to expect that arms length parties will have  either
the  means or the capability of ascertaining the types  of  facts
extrinsic  to a document of the nature that 942 argues should  be
considered in interpreting the 1941 by-law.


[44] I am satisfied that the ordinary meaning of the words in  s.
27  of  the  1941  by-law is clear and unambiguous  and  that  an
examination  of the whole of the by-law would lead  a  reader  to
conclude that there may be other by-laws dealing with matters not
referred to in the 1941 by-law which have not been repealed.  One
of   those   other   matters  could  be   restrictions   on   the
transferability of shares.  In my view, this is  not  a  case  to
consider  extrinsic matters when interpreting the by-law.   I  am
therefore satisfied that Article XV was not repealed by the  1941
by-law.


[45]  If  David Graham or his advisors had inquired of  Beechwood
about restrictions on the transfer of shares, they would probably
have  been  advised that there was none.  This fact is irrelevant
to  this  appeal.   They made no such inquiry and  there  was  no
evidence   that  they  relied  upon  statements  or  actions   of
Beechwood,  its  officers  or  directors,  other  than   possibly
statements  of John Graham, in reaching the decision  to  acquire
the  225  shares of Beechwood without first obtaining the consent
of  the board of directors.  To the contrary, as I set out below,
the  trial  judge found as a fact that David Graham and therefore
942  had  actual knowledge that the consent of the  board  was  a
requirement for the transfer of shares.

     The Actual Knowledge Issue

[46]  942 argues that the trial judge erred in finding that David
Graham  and  942 had actual knowledge of the restriction  on  the
transfer  of  shares contained in Article XV of the 1873  by-law.
As  a result, it is argued, he erred in finding that s. 56(3)  of
the  Business  Corporations Act, R.S.O. 1990,  c.  B.16  did  not
operate to render the restriction ineffective against them.


[47]  Beechwood  is  subject to the provisions  of  the  Business
Corporations Act.6  Section 56(3) provides:
          
          56. (3)  Where a share certificate issued  by
          a  corporation or by a body corporate  before
          the   body  corporate  was  continued   under
          section 180 is, or becomes, subject to,
          
          (a)  a restriction on its transfer other than
          a restriction referred to in subsection (8);
          
          ...
          
          the    restriction,   lien,   agreement    or
          endorsement   is   ineffective   against    a
          transferee  of  the share who has  no  actual
          knowledge of it, unless it or a reference  to
          it   is  noted  conspicuously  on  the  share
          certificate.
          
          

[48]  The  trial judge concluded that the words on  the  form  of
share  certificate used by Beechwood were not conspicuous  enough
to  impute actual knowledge to a purchaser pursuant to s.  56(3).
I agree with that conclusion.


[49]  The  other issue to be decided is whether David Graham,  as
pledgee7  of the 225 shares, and 942, the company in  whose  name
the   shares  were  registered,  had  actual  knowledge  of   the
restriction.


[50]  The trial judge approached this issue by first finding that
David Graham’s solicitors had actual knowledge of the restriction
on  the  transfer  of shares.  In my view, there  was  sufficient
evidence  for  him to do so.  David Graham’s solicitors  prepared
draft  resolutions  to  be  signed by  the  signing  officers  of
Beechwood after approval by the board of directors.  John  Graham
gave  an undertaking on the closing to have the draft resolutions
executed.   He  never  did.  David Graham’s solicitors  persisted
over  a  period  of  several months after  December  of  1990  in
attempting to obtain the executed resolutions which could only be
provided  if the board consented to the transfers.   On  May  15,
1991,  the  same solicitors advised David Graham that failure  to
obtain the completed resolutions would hamper enforcement of  the
pledge  because  the  board  might oppose  the  transfer.   David
Graham’s solicitors did not testify at trial.  On these facts  it
was  open  to  the trial judge to draw the inference  that  David
Graham’s solicitors knew of the restriction.


[51]  The  trial  judge also found that John  Graham  had  actual
knowledge of the requirement for the consent of the directors  to
the  transfer of shares.  John Graham gave evidence that  he  did
not  know  if he knew of the requirement; however, his letter  to
his solicitors of July 10, 1991, makes it clear that he did.  The
trial  judge’s  finding  in this respect  was  supported  by  the
evidence.


[52] The trial judge found that David Graham’s solicitors did not
examine  or  ask  to  see  the by-laws or  corporate  records  of
Beechwood.  Although it was not necessary for him to conclude how
David  Graham’s  solicitors became aware of the restriction,  his
reasons  make clear that he drew the inference that they  learned
of  it  from  John  Graham.   I am also  satisfied  that  on  the
evidence, it was open to him to draw that inference.


[53]  The trial judge went on to conclude, correctly in my  view,
that  the knowledge of David Graham’s solicitors who were  acting
on his behalf to carry out the necessary searches and prepare the
documentation  to  complete  the loan and  security  transactions
should  be  imputed to him.  Finally,  it was not  disputed  that
David Graham’s knowledge was also the knowledge of 942.


[54]  For  these  reasons, I would not interfere with  the  trial
judge’s  finding  of fact that 942 had actual  knowledge  of  the
restriction  on transfer and accordingly s. 56(3) of the  Ontario
Business  Corporations Act does not operate so as to  render  the
restriction on transfer ineffective.

DISPOSITION

[55] For all of the above reasons, I would dismiss the appeal  by
942 with costs.
