O’CONNOR J.A.:

[1]   This  is  a  companion appeal to the  appeal  in  the  same
proceeding  (C25426)  brought by a  different  appellant.   These
reasons    should   be   read   with   the   reasons    (released
contemporaneously) in that appeal.

The Allotment Issue

[2]   The  appellants, John Graham and John  Graham  and  Company
Limited,  appeal the judgment of Doyle J. in which he  set  aside
the  issuance of 88 shares in the respondent (“Beechwood”) to the
appellants.  The transactions were set aside because John Graham,
who  was  the  president  and a director  of  Beechwood  had  not
obtained  the  consent  of  the  directors  as  required  by  the
Beechwood by-laws.


[3]  Beechwood was incorporated by special Act of the Legislature
of  Ontario in 1873.  Article XV of the by-laws passed  in  18731
provided, “[n]o shares in the capital stock of the Company  shall
be  transferable  without the consent of the Board  of  Directors
...”.


[4]   In  1941  Beechwood enacted a new by-law in which  art.  20
provided  that “[s]hares in the Company’s capital stock shall  be
allotted  by resolution of the board of directors on  such  terms
and  conditions and to such persons as the directors  shall  deem
advisable.”


[5]   John Graham became a director of Beechwood in 1972 and  its
president  in  December  of  1974.  He  continued  to  hold  both
positions until the board of directors removed him from office in
November  1991.   Prior  to  his  appointment  as  president   of
Beechwood,   John  Graham,  either  personally  or  through   his
brokerage company, John Graham and Company Limited, had purchased
137  shares  of Beechwood from other shareholders.  Although  the
board  of directors was not asked to consent to the transfers  of
these shares, Beechwood has not sought to set them aside.


[6]   In  1975 John Graham hired Timothy Graham (no relation)  as
general manager of Beechwood.  Shortly afterwards, Timothy Graham
was  appointed secretary and treasurer.  Timothy Graham  reported
directly to John Graham, and as directed by John Graham,  to  the
board  of  directors.   Timothy  Graham  had  responsibility  for
Beechwood’s  shareholder register and he  handled  correspondence
with  shareholders  and their agents dealing with  redemption  or
sale of shares held by deceased shareholders.


[7]   Within a week of being hired, Timothy Graham was instructed
by  John  Graham  to  advise him if shares  of  Beechwood  became
available because estates of deceased shareholders wished to have
their  shares redeemed or sold. From 1975 to 1991, Timothy Graham
complied with these instructions.  In each case the estate  would
surrender  its  shares to Beechwood and Beechwood would  in  turn
issue  a cheque, signed by John and Timothy Graham in the  amount
of  $100 per share.  Timothy Graham would then cause Beechwood to
issue  a  new share certificate to either John Graham or  to  his
brokerage  company for a corresponding number  of  shares,  which
certificate  would  also be signed by John  and  Timothy  Graham.
John  Graham  would  pay Beechwood $100 per share  by  issuing  a
cheque  or by arranging for a reduction from his salary or  other
entitlements.


[8]   John  and  Timothy Graham did not tell the directors  about
John  Graham’s  instructions, nor about these share transactions.
The  trial  judge  found  that Timothy Graham  was  placed  in  a
difficult  position and that there was an implied direction  from
John  Graham, his superior, not to tell the other directors about
what was occurring.


[9]   In  this  manner,  John Graham and  his  brokerage  company
acquired  88 shares through 15 separate transactions,  increasing
their  shareholdings from 137 to 225 out of an authorized capital
of 387 shares.  This gave them voting control.  Until the Fall of
1991, none of the other directors examined the share register  or
became  aware  of  the fact that John Graham  and  his  brokerage
company were acquiring shares in this manner.


[10]  The trial judge made a number of findings of fact regarding
the acquisition of the 88 shares.  He found:


·     John  Graham  breached his fiduciary  duty  by  failing to
  disclose that he was acquiring shares in this manner;
·    John Graham acted in a conflict of interest;

·    the procedure created by John Graham did not allow the
directors to consider whether they should consent to the transfer
of shares pursuant to Article XV of the 1873 by-law;

·    the steps taken by John Graham removed the responsibility of
the directors to allot shares pursuant to art. 20 of the 1941 by-
law;

·    the procedure created by John Graham took away the
opportunity of the board of directors to prevent John Graham from
acquiring a majority of the shares of Beechwood unless steps were
taken to annul the transfers;

·    the issuance of the shares required the consent of the board
of directors under Article XV of the 1873 by-law;

·    even if the consent of the board of directors was not
required for these transactions as share transfers, such
transfers and purchases were illegal because only the directors
can allot shares under art. 20 of the 1941 by-law; and

·    John Graham knew of the requirement of the consent of the
board of directors to the transfer of shares.

[11]  Although  the trial judge made a number  of  findings  upon
which  he could have based his judgment to set aside the issuance
of  the  88 shares, he relied primarily upon the failure  of  the
board  of  directors to consent to the transfers as  required  by
Article XV of the 1873 by-law.


[12]  The  appellants challenge this judgment on  three  grounds.
First, they argue that the trial judge erred in finding that John
Graham  breached  his  fiduciary  duty  by  not  disclosing   his
instructions  to  Timothy  Graham to  the  other  directors,  and
second,  that the trial judge erred in relying on s. 248  of  the
Ontario  Business  Corporations Act,  R.S.O.  1990,  c.  B.16  to
support his conclusion.


[13]  The short answer to these arguments is that the trial judge
did  not rely either on the breach of fiduciary duty or on s. 248
of  the Ontario Business Corporations Act in making the order  to
set  aside  the issuance of the shares.  Rather, the trial  judge
relied upon the provision in the by-laws requiring the consent of
the   board   of  directors  to  the  transfer  of   shares   or,
alternatively, the provision requiring a resolution of the  board
of  directors  for the allotment of shares.  In other  words,  he
found  the  transactions were either transfers or  allotments  or
possibly  both.  In either case, consent or approval was required
under  the  Beechwood  by-laws and no  consent  or  approval  was
obtained.


[14]  In any event, I am satisfied that the facts referred to  in
paragraphs  5 to 8 above amply support the trial judge’s  finding
that  John  Graham  acted  in breach of  his  fiduciary  duty  as
president and a director of Beechwood.  He used those offices  to
surreptitiously acquire control of the company.   The  submission
that there was no evidence that John Graham obtained a benefit is
without  merit.   He  obviously  considered  the  acquisition  of
control to be a benefit, perhaps unquantifiable, but nevertheless
a benefit.


[15]  The  appellants’ third argument is that  s.  56(3)  of  the
Ontario  Business Corporations Act rendered the  restrictions  in
the  by-laws ineffective.  There was no dispute that John  Graham
knew  of  the 1941 by-law which required the directors to approve
the  allotment of shares.  Further, the trial judge  found  as  a
fact that John Graham knew of the requirement for consent of  the
board  of directors to the transfer of shares. It is implicit  in
the   trial  judge’s  finding  that  John  Graham  knew  of  this
requirement during the period of time when the shares were issued
to  him.   In view of these findings, there is no basis to  argue
that  the  issuance  of the shares was ineffective  because  John
Graham or his brokerage company did not have actual knowledge  of
the  restrictions contained in the by-laws.  Section 56(3) of the
Ontario Business Corporations Act therefore has no application.


[16] In my view, the trial judge was correct in setting aside the
issuance of the 88 shares.   I reject the appellants’ submissions
on this aspect of the appeal.

Other Grounds

[17]  The  appellants  raise  three  additional  complaints  with
respect  to  the proceeding below.  First it is argued  that  the
trial  judge  erred  in  failing  to  grant  the  appellants   an
adjournment  in  order to obtain the assistance of  a  legal  aid
lawyer.


[18]  This  argument  is without merit.  The appellants  did  not
request  an adjournment.  To the contrary, John Graham  told  the
trial judge prior to trial that he was prepared to go forward.


[19]  Next, the appellant John Graham and Company Limited  argues
that  there  was  no  basis in law for  finding  it  jointly  and
severally  liable  with John Graham for the  losses  suffered  by
Beechwood  for  what  was referred to at trial  as  the  Triple-D
investment.   John Graham and Company Limited was  the  financial
adviser to Beechwood in connection with that investment.   Moneys
owing  to  Beechwood were channeled through the bank  account  of
John  Graham  and Company Limited rather than being deposited  in
the  Beechwood  account and from there invested in  the  Triple-D
project.   John  Graham  was found liable personally  for  losses
incurred   in  connection  with  this  investment.    There   was
substantial  evidence to support the trial judge’s  finding  that
John  Graham and Company Limited was jointly and severally liable
for these losses.


[20] Finally, the appellants argue that the trial judge erred  in
not  giving them credit for an amount in excess of $125,000 being
held in a trust account at Canada Trust.  It was argued that this
amount should be used to reduce the judgments holding them liable
for losses incurred by Beechwood.  The trial judge made it clear,
in  his  additional reasons, that this issue remained open,  that
evidence must still be heard and that it should be treated  as  a
new claim.  I see no merit in this ground of appeal.


[21] For these reasons, I would dismiss the appeal with costs.
