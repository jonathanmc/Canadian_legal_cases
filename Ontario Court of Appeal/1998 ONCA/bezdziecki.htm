                          ENDORSEMENT

[1]  The appellant's claim against Allstate Insurance Company of
Canada ("Allstate") under a residential fire policy was dismissed
at trial on the basis that she was a party to the intentional
setting of the fire that caused damage to her home and its
contents. Judgment was granted against the appellant on
Allstate's counterclaim for recovery of monies paid to the
mortgagee pursuant to the mortgage clause contained in the
insurance policy.


[2]  The appellant argues that the trial judge's finding that she
was involved in the setting of the fire is unreasonable.
Therefore, she seeks an order setting aside both the dismissal of
her action and the judgment on the counterclaim and, instead,
seeks judgment for the amount to which she would otherwise have
been entitled to at trial. In the alternative, the appellant
submits that the trial judge made a number of errors that
necessitate a new trial. Although the latter remedy is sought in
the alternative only, the appellant's contention that the verdict
is unreasonable rests essentially on the alleged errors in the
judgment. We will therefore address these additional grounds
before considering the reasonableness of the verdict.


1.   Standard of proof

[3]  The appellant submits that a finding of complicity in arson
is akin to a finding of fraud in that it must be founded on clear
and convincing proof. She states that the trial judge did not
advert to this distinction and consequently erred in applying a
simple balance of probabilities test.


[4]  In our view, the trial judge's reasons do not reflect any
error on the question of standard of proof. The trial judge
quoted from the decision of this court in Rizzo v. Hanover
Insurance Co. (1993), 14 O.R. (3d) 98 at 105, where the court
states that "[i]n such cases, the proper inquiry should be
whether, on all of the evidence inculpatory of the insured,
including motive and opportunity, the insurer has proven the
defence of arson according  to the standard of proof appropriate
to the establishment of that defence in a civil case."


[5]  There is nothing in the lengthy judgment that would indicate
that the trial judge failed to scrutinize the evidence in a
manner commensurate with the gravity of the allegations.


2.   Evidence of motive

[6]  The appellant submits that the trial judge failed to
consider those items of evidence showing that she did not have a
motive to set her house on fire. In her factum, she notes: this
was a family home and not a business, no item of high cash or
sentimental value had been removed from the home prior to the
fire, her teenage children's belongings were in the house and she
had not given up on her attempts to find financing and regain
possession of the house.


[7]  We see no basis for simply assuming that the trial judge did
not take those facts into consideration.  These matters were
obvious and were fully canvassed at trial. It was not incumbent
upon the trial judge to review every single item of evidence and
to note the absence of other items of evidence in his reasons.
His reasons focused rather on the basis for his finding that the
appellant had a motive. It is not contended that his finding in
that regard is unsupported by the evidence.


[8]  Indeed the trial judge went further and found that no one
else except the appellant would stand to benefit as a result of
the fire. On this point, the appellant contends that the trial
judge disregarded the evidence suggesting motive on the part of
the mortgagee. There is no basis for this latter argument. The
mortgagee was called at trial by Allstate and denied complicity
in the fire. The appellant did not take the position at trial
that the mortgagee had anything to do with the setting of the
fire.


3.   Evidence of opportunity

[9]  The appellant concedes that exclusive opportunity of access
need not be shown before complicity in arson can be found. She
argues nonetheless that the trial judge failed to give sufficient
consideration to the fact that the appellant, who had been
evicted from her house, had no greater opportunity than the
public at large.


[10] We see no merit to this ground of appeal. There is nothing
to indicate that the trial judge gave undue weight to the
evidence of opportunity. He expressly considered the possibility
that the fire may have been set by vandals or others and rejected
it. In fact, this forms the basis of the next ground of appeal.


4.   Possibility of fire being set by vandals or others

[11] The appellant argues that the trial judge erred in accepting
the expert opinion that this fire was not set by vandals. In our
view, it was clearly open to the trial judge to accept this
evidence to support his finding on this point.


[12] The appellant also argues that the trial judge failed to
consider other possibilities such as "a fire masking a theft or a
fire set by someone who enjoyed setting fires... or a fire set by
persons hostile to the [appellant's] family." There is no merit
to this argument. The trial judge adequately and fairly dealt
with the evidence before him. It is not incumbent upon a trial
judge to rebut every speculative argument raised during the
course of the trial.


5.   Misapprehension of evidence

[13] The appellant submits that the trial judge misapprehended
the evidence as to the presence of "timing devices" on the
premises at the time of the fire. We do not accept this argument.
The nature of the devices in question was clear from the
evidence. They were not "timing" devices in the true sense but,
rather, devices intended to delay the start of the fire. There is
no reason to believe that the trial judge misapprehended the
nature of these delaying devices.


[14] The appellant also contends that the trial judge failed to
consider important evidence that showed she could not have been
in Sault Ste-Marie at the time the fire was set.  Although the
trial judge did not review every item of evidence on this point,
we are satisfied, after having reviewed the excerpts referred to
by counsel for the appellant, that the trial judge did not
misapprehend the overall effect of this evidence. There were
serious issues of credibility. It was within the trial judge's
ambit to reject the appellant's evidence with respect to certain
details of the journey to Leamington and to conclude that this
evidence did not "preclude an attendance at the residence by the
appellant or an involved accomplice."


6.   Evidence exculpating the appellant

[15] The appellant submits that the trial judge failed to
consider the evidence which showed her to be innocent of any
complicity in the fire. In particular, counsel notes the evidence
of the appellant's dealings with Mr. and Mrs. Pokusa when she was
attempting to refinance her debts. He submits that this evidence
is inconsistent with the actions of someone who was a guilty
participant in a scheme to burn down her house.


[16] We do not agree with the submission that this evidence was
necessarily inconsistent with the actions of someone involved in
the setting of the fire. The appellant's financial problems
extended far beyond the family home. The probative value of this
evidence was a matter for the trial judge to address.  Hence, we
do not agree with counsel that this evidence was so cogent that
the trial judge was required to deal with it.


7.   Result of a finding that a member of the appellant's family
     was involved in the setting of the fire

[17] Given the trial judge's finding that the appellant herself
was involved in the setting of the fire, directly or indirectly,
we do not find it necessary to deal with this issue.


8.   Reasonableness of the verdict

[18] As set out above, we see no reason to interfere with any of
the trial judge's findings of fact. Consequently, given the
indisputable incendiary origin of the fire, the trial judge's
findings of motive, opportunity and lack of credibility of the
appellant's evidence, it is our view that the finding of
complicity can reasonably be supported by the evidence. It is a
finding that was open to the trial judge on the totality of the
evidence before him.


[19] In light of these conclusions, it is not necessary to deal
with the additional issue raised by the respondent. The appeal is
dismissed with costs.
