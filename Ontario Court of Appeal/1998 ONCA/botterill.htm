                          ENDORSEMENT

[1]  The appellant claims that the trial judge erred in finding
that (1) the respondent was a trust claimant of the bankrupt
corporation and (2) that the appellant, as directing mind of the
corporation, was liable as a constructive trustee for the
corporation's breach of the trust agreement.


Creation of trust

[2]  In our view, there was ample evidence to support the trial
judge's finding that the parties agreed to a trust arrangement
with respect to the respondent's commissions. This finding
accords with the agreed statement of facts, the terms of the
written independent agency agreement between the parties and the
testimony of the respondent. Moreover, the existence of the trust
arrangement was not disputed by the appellant in his testimony.
His testimony, rather, focussed on issues relating to his
personal liability.


Appellant's liability for breach of trust

[3]  In our view, the evidence also supports a finding that the
appellant was personally liable for the corporation's breach of
the trust agreement.


[4]  The appellant was the directing mind of the bankrupt
corporation. He transferred monies from the corporation's trust
account into its general account, from which he issued a cheque
to the respondent in payment of his commission. Of particular
importance is the fact that this transaction occurred at a time
when the appellant was contemplating assigning the corporation in
bankruptcy. The assignment in bankruptcy effectively occurred
before the cheque was honoured by the bank.


[5]  The trial judge disbelieved the appellant's testimony that
he had issued the cheque and then tried to delay the assignment
into bankruptcy in order to assist the respondent in obtaining
his commission. The trial judge, rather, found that the appellant
improperly transferred and deposited trust moneys into the
company's general account with the intention of reducing his
personal liability to the bank. He held that the reduction of the
overdraft in the general account, which was secured by a
collateral mortgage on his home, was of pivotal importance to the
appellant. The trial judge also found that the transfer of monies
in fact resulted in a substantial direct benefit to the
appellant.


[6]  Based on these findings, the trial judge held that the
appellant was personally liable to the respondent. In our view,
the trial judge's findings can reasonably be supported by the
evidence. His conclusion is also supported in law since the
appellant, by his actions, knowingly put the respondent at risk
contrary to the trust agreement. He is therefore liable as
constructive trustee: Re Air Canada and M & L Travel Ltd (1993),
108 D.L.R. (4th) 592 (S.C.C.).


[7]  For these reasons, the appeal is dismissed with costs.
