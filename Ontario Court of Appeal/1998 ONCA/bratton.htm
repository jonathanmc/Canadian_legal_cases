                     ENDORSEMENT

[1]    Counsel disagreed on the basis on which the application
was argued before the motion judge. Justice Day dealt with the
application as if it were a contract case. He found that there
was no binding agreement. In this court counsel for the appellant
concedes that there was no binding agreement, but submits that
the motion judge misconceived the scope of the application.


[2]    Counsel for the appellant submits that the application was
argued as an oppression proceeding, but that the relief was
confined to an order requiring the respondents to purchase the
appellant's shares for $62,000. Even dealing with the application
on this broader basis, we are not persuaded that the conduct of
the individual respondents was oppressive under s.248 of the
Ontario Business Corporations Act. The uncontradicted evidence
shows that the company required additional financing and that the
company's bankers were pressuring the principals to pay down the
bank debt. The respondents Mills and Goodman personally borrowed
money, and gave unlimited guarantees and mortgages on their homes
to satisfy the bank's demands.


[3]    The appellant was unwilling to come up with his share of
the money required for the company's financing. Mills and Goodman
then invoked paragraph 5.04(d) of the shareholders' agreement,
paid the amount required of the appellant ($27,200), and after 45
days purchased the appellant's shares for $1.


[4]    The appellant did not ask for arbitration as he was
entitled to do under the agreement, nor did he commence court
proceedings until over a year later. We also observe that the
figure of $62,000 discussed by the parties in March 1989, in
substance was conditional on Imaje remaining a customer, which
did not happen.


[5]    In these circumstances, the conduct of the individual
respondents was not oppressive or prejudicial and did not
unfairly disregard the appellant's interest. The appeal is
therefore dismissed with costs.
