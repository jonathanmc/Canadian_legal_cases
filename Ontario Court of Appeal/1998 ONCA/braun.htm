                      ENDORSEMENT
                                

[1]  The appellant Zenair Ltd. raises three issues on this
appeal.


[2]  First, it argues that the trial judge erred in that he in
effect placed on onus on the appellant to call parole evidence
demonstrating that the deceased Fred Braun in fact read the
Waiver Form. We agree with this submission. Mr. Braun signed the
Form. The Form clearly states that the signor has read the
document. Mr. Braun had sufficient opportunity to read it. There
is no evidence here to displace the natural conclusion that he
did so.


[3]  Second, it argues that the trial judge erred in finding the
Waiver ineffective because it did not refer to negligence in
terms. Insofar as the trial judge could be said to have done
this, we agree with this submission. In the circumstances of this
case the only possible head of damage for which the appellant
could be liable under the contract lies in negligence. Hence it
is unnecessary that the precise term appear in the Waiver Form.


[4]  Third, the appellant argues that the waiver document is
clear and unambiguous and applies squarely to facts of this case.
It argues that the trial judge erred in finding the document
confusing and ambiguous. We disagree with this submission.


[5]  Mr, Braun was a qualified and experienced ultra light pilot.
On the day of the tragedy he attended at the airport at Reese's
Corners near Sarnia. The deceased, Mr. Aziz acting on behalf of
the appellant, had flown a new ultra light model aircraft to the
airport from the premises occupied by the appellant and Freewinds
Aviation Inc., the flying school owned by Mr. Aziz. His purpose
was not to provide instruction in flying but to give Mr. Braun,
among others, a demonstration flight during which Mr. Braun would
be allowed to fly the aircraft.


[6]  On the other hand, as the trial judge found, the language of
the Waiver Form and the evidence of its origin make clear that it
was at least principally intended to apply to the instructional
activities of Freewinds Aviation School at Midland. The opening
recital of the document reads as follows:
          
          Whereas the undersigned release or wishes to
          use the Freewinds premises to be instructed
          and to ride, fly and/or otherwise operate
          ultra light aircraft … [Emphasis added.]
          
          

[7]  This recital seems clearly to confine the waiver to
instructional activities at the Midland premises. Placed against
the balance of the document this creates at least an ambiguity as
to whether the waiver is so limited or can be extended to
demonstration activity at Reese's Corners.


[8]  Moreover, insofar as the document covers activities in the
air it is unclear whether it extends to negligent mistakes by the
demonstrating pilot, in this case Mr. Aziz. Nowhere in the
document is it clearly stated that such conduct is covered by the
Waiver. Indeed, much of the document addresses the operating of
the aircraft by the student rather than the instructor.


[9]  In these circumstances, a reasonable person in the position
of Mr. Braun would be unclear as to whether the Waiver extended
to the negligence of the demonstrating pilot in operating the
aircraft.


[10] The appellant does not contest the legal principles set out
in Falcon Lumber Ltd. v. Canada Wood Specialty Co. (1978), 23
O.R. (2d) 345 (H.C.J.). A waiver must be strictly construed and
the burden is on the party relying on the exemption to prove that
the particular loss was clearly within the scope of the document.
In addition, any ambiguity will be construed against the maker of
the document.


[11] Here it is far from clear that the waiver applies to these
tragic facts. There is ambiguity as to whether the waiver extends
to the negligent acts in this case. The trial judge was therefore
correct in concluding that the Waiver did not bar the
respondent's recovery.


[12] The appeal is dismissed with costs. It is thus unnecessary
for us to deal with the cross-appeal and it is dismissed but in
the circumstances without costs.
