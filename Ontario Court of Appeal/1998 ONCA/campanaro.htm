OSBORNE J.A.:
OVERVIEW


[1]   Section  258(1)  of the Insurance Act, R.S.O.  1990,  c.  I.8
permits an injured plaintiff, who has recovered judgment against  a
negligent  driver, to sue the driver's automobile insurer.  Section
258(5)  limits the insurer's defence in such an action. It prevents
the  insurer  from  alleging in its defence that an  instrument  it
issued  as  a motor vehicle liability policy is not such a  policy.
The  relevant provisions of s. 258 (particulary s-ss. (4) and  (5))
have  come to be known as the absolute liability provisions of  the
Insurance Act.


[2]   These two appeals, which were argued together, raise the same
issue  --  can  the  insurer defend the action contemplated  by  s.
258(1)  on the basis of its insured's misrepresentation as  to  the
ownership of the insured automobile, notwithstanding the provisions
of  s.  258(5). Put another way, the issue is whether the  defence-
limiting  provisions of s. 258(5) apply when the  misrepresentation
in  issue is a misrepresentation as to the ownership of the insured
automobile.


[3]   The resolution of this issue requires the court to reconsider
its  judgment in Minister of Transport et al. v. London  &  Midland
General Insurance Co., [1971] 3 O.R. 147. In London & Midland, Gale
C.J.O.  held  that, notwithstanding what is now s.  258(5)  of  the
Insurance Act, the insurer may defend the plaintiff's action on the
basis  that  the  policy  that  it issued  is  void  because  of  a
misrepresentation  as to the ownership of the  insured  automobile.
The appellants contend that the "absolute liability" provisions  of
the  Insurance  Act, particularly s. 258(5), preclude  the  insurer
from  defending  a judgment creditor's claim on the  basis  of  any
misrepresentation, including a misrepresentation as to ownership of
the insured vehicle.


[4]   In  the two actions before us, on motions brought under  Rule
22,  the motions judges both concluded that they were bound by this
court's  judgment  in  London & Midland with the  result  that  the
wrongdoers' automobile insurer, State Farm, in both cases, was  not
required  to pay the plaintiffs' claims. It was accepted  that  the
named  insured,  in  each case, was not the  actual  owner  of  the
insured automobiles. Zurich Indemnity Company of Canada ("Zurich"),
in  the Campanaro action, and Royal Insurance Canada ("Royal"),  in
the  Truelove action, provided uninsured motorist coverage  to  the
plaintiffs.  The result of the orders appealed from is that  Zurich
and  Royal,  not State Farm, had to pay the Campanaro and  Truelove
claims.


[5]   Zurich and Royal appeal. Both insurers take the position that
to  the  extent that London & Midland is to be taken to have carved
out  a  fictitious ownership exception from the absolute  liability
provisions of the Insurance Act, it was wrongly decided.


THE FACTS

[6]   The  facts  in  both  cases are not in  dispute  and  can  be
summarized briefly.


     (a)  The Campanaro Action (1995), 24 O.R. (3d) 274

[7]   Ki-Jin Kim bought a Dodge station wagon in 1985 with his  own
money. Shortly after the purchase, he transferred the ownership  of
the Dodge to his brother, Ki-Suk Kim, for no consideration. Only Ki-
Jin Kim drove the Dodge.


[8]  After the ownership transfer, Ki-Suk Kim applied to State Farm
for insurance on the Dodge. In his application, he represented that
he was the Dodge's actual and registered owner. In fact, he was the
registered  owner; his representation that he was the actual  owner
was false. State Farm issued a policy to Ki-Suk Kim.


[9]   On September 8, 1987, the Dodge, negligently driven by Ki-Jin
Kim, its actual, but not registered, owner collided with a GMC  van
driven  by  the  plaintiff, Romualdo Campanaro. Mr.  Campanaro  was
injured.  The  GMC  van  driven by Mr.  Campanaro  was  insured  by
Travelers.  Mr.  Campanaro's policy included  statutorily  required
uninsured motorist coverage.


[10]  On May 30, 1988, State Farm took the position that its policy
on  the  Dodge  station  wagon was void  because  of  Ki-Suk  Kim's
misrepresentation  that he owned it. State Farm returned  the  full
premium that it had received.


[11] On August 4, 1989, Campanaro sued Travelers and Zurich. Zurich
is  a  successor  company  to  Travelers  and  is  responsible  for
Travelers' liabilities. Thus, Zurich is the only appellant involved
in  the Campanaro appeal. Campanaro's claim against Zurich was made
under  the  uninsured motorist coverage in his automobile insurance
policy.  On  December 4, 1990, Zurich added State Farm as  a  third
party.  On  March  5,  1991,  Ki-Jin  Kim  and  Ki-Suk  Kim  sought
contribution  or  indemnity  from  State  Farm  in  a  third  party
proceeding.  On  December  21,  1991,  Zurich  added  Ki-Jin  Kim's
employer, Carpet Clearance Warehouse, as a third party.


[12] When pleadings were completed, State Farm moved under Rule  22
for  a  declaration that it was not required to pay  the  Campanaro
claim  because  of  the named insured's misrepresentation  that  he
owned  the  insured  automobile.  The  motions  judge,  Gotlib  J.,
concluded that she was bound by this court's judgment in  London  &
Midland and dismissed the third party action against State Farm.


     (b)  Truelove Action, [unreported, May 9, 1996]

[13]  Duane  Thompson was the registered owner of two  all  terrain
vehicles ("ATVs"), one being an 1984 Honda. The ATVs were kept at a
hunting  camp  owned by Duane Thompson and were regularly  used  by
members  of  the  Thompson  family, including  the  defendant  Alan
Hamilton,   Duane  Thompson's  son-in-law.  The  defendant,   Scott
Thompson, Duane Thompson's son, was the ATVs' most frequent driver.


[14]  Duane Thompson obtained insurance on the ATVs with Royal from
1984 until some time in 1988, when he asked Alan Hamilton to obtain
insurance  on them. The purpose was to secure coverage at  a  lower
premium.  In November 1988, Alan Hamilton went to the office  of  a
State Farm agent and completed an application for insurance on  the
two  ATVs.  The application stated that Alan Hamilton was both  the
actual and registered owner of the ATVs. State Farm relied upon the
representations  in  the  application,  including   the   ownership
representation, and issued a policy insuring both  ATVs.  In  fact,
Alan  Hamilton was not the registered owner, the actual  owner,  or
the principal driver of the ATVs.


[15]  On  August 20, 1989, Scott Thompson was driving  one  of  the
ATVs.  The  plaintiff,  Carla Truelove was  a  passenger.  She  was
injured in an accident caused by Scott Thompson's negligence.


[16]  After  State  Farm  was informed of the  accident  and  Carla
Truelove's claim, it rescinded the policy and returned the premium.
In  taking  these  steps, State Farm relied  upon  Alan  Hamilton's
misrepresentation as to the ownership of the ATV  involved  in  the
accident.


[17]  In  due  course, Carla Truelove and others  (Family  Law  Act
plaintiffs) sued Scott Thompson, Duane Thompson and Alan  Hamilton.
They  also sued Royal which provided applicable uninsured  motorist
coverage  in  the  circumstances.1 Royal  settled  the  plaintiffs'
claims  and moved under Rule 22 to determine whether it,  or  State
Farm,  was  required  to pay the plaintiffs'  claims.  The  motions
judge,  Rosenberg  J.,  noted  in  his  endorsement  that  it   was
acknowledged that there was no basis upon which to distinguish this
court's judgment in London & Midland. Thus, he held that State Farm
did not have to pay the plaintiff's claims.


THE RELEVANT PROVISIONS OF THE INSURANCE ACT

[18]  The  net effect of s. 233 of the Insurance Act is that  false
representations by an applicant for insurance may render  a  policy
of  automobile  insurance void and cause  the  insured  to  forfeit
his/her right to indemnity under the policy. The provisions  of  s.
233 make it clear that the misrepresentation must be made knowingly
and  that  it  must be contained in the signed written  application
before  the insurer can rely upon the misrepresentation in  defence
of  a  claim  made  under the contract (the  policy).  Section  233
provides:


          233.--(1)  Where,

          (a)  an applicant for a contract,

               (i)  gives   false  particulars   of   the
                    described automobile to be insured to
                    the prejudice of the insurer, or

               (ii) knowingly misrepresents or  fails  to
                    disclose in the application any  fact
                    required to be stated therein;

          (b)  the  insured  contravenes a  term  of  the
               contract or commits a fraud; or

          (c)  the   insured  wilfully  makes   a   false
               statement in respect of a claim under  the
               contract,

          a claim by the insured is invalid and the right
          of   the   insured  to  recover  indemnity   is
          forfeited.

               (3)   No statement of the applicant  shall
          be  used  in  defence  of  a  claim  under  the
          contract  unless it is contained in the  signed
          written  application  therefor  or,  where   no
          signed  written  application is  made,  in  the
          purported application, or part thereof, that is
          embodied in, endorsed upon or attached  to  the
          policy. [Emphasis added.]2

[19]  It  is common ground in both cases before us that  the  named
insured  knowingly  misrepresented that he was  the  owner  of  the
insured motor vehicle. The misrepresentations were contained in the
signed  written application. Thus, were actions against State  Farm
to  be commenced by the named insureds, State Farm could defend  on
the   basis   that   the   policies  are  void   because   of   the
misrepresentations of the named insureds about the ownership of the
insured  vehicles. However, the question to be determined in  these
appeals  is whether, in actions brought against State Farm  not  by
the  named  insureds,  but by the injured third  party  plaintiffs,
State  Farm  is  precluded  by the provisions  of  s.  258(5)  from
defending  the  two  actions on the basis of  the  named  insureds'
misrepresentation about ownership.


[20]  Since  the  1931  amendments to the  Insurance  Act,  injured
plaintiffs  like Campanaro and Truelove have had a statutory  right
of  action  against  the wrongdoer's automobile insurer,  (in  this
case,  State Farm). This right of action is set out in what is  now
s. 258 of the Act. The defence limiting provisions of s. 258 are in
s-s. (4) and (5). The relevant parts of s. 258 are3:


          258.--(1)   Any person who has a claim  against
          an insured for which indemnity is provided by a
          contract evidenced by a motor vehicle liability
          policy,  even if such person is not a party  to
          the  contract, may, upon recovering a  judgment
          therefor in any province or territory of Canada
          against  the insured, have the insurance  money
          payable  under  the  contract  applied  in   or
          towards  satisfaction of the person's  judgment
          and  of  any other judgments or claims  against
          the insured covered by the contract and may, on
          the  person's own behalf and on behalf  of  all
          persons   having  such  judgments  or   claims,
          maintain an action against the insurer to  have
          the insurance money so applied.

                               ...

               (4)  The right of a person who is entitled
          under  subsection (1) to have  insurance  money
          applied upon the person's judgment or claim  is
          not prejudiced by,

               (a)  an   assignment,  waiver,  surrender,
                    cancellation  or  discharge  of   the
                    contract, or of any interest  therein
                    or  of the proceeds thereof, made  by
                    the  insured  after the happening  of
                    the  event  giving rise  to  a  claim
                    under the contract;

               (b)  any  act  or  default of the  insured
                    before   or  after  that   event   in
                    contravention of this Part or of  the
                    terms of the contract; or

               (c)  any  contravention  of  the  Criminal
                    Code  (Canada)  or a statute  of  any
                    province or territory of Canada or of
                    any state or the District of Columbia
                    of  the  United States of America  by
                    the    owner   or   driver   of   the
                    automobile,

          and nothing mentioned in clause (a), (b) or (c)
          is  available to the insurer as a defence in an
          action brought under subsection (1).

               (5)   It  is  not a defence to  an  action
          under this section that an instrument issued as
          a  motor  vehicle liability policy by a  person
          engaged  in  the  business of  an  insurer  and
          alleged by a party to the action to be  such  a
          policy is not a motor vehicle liability policy,
          and   this   section  applies  with   necessary
          modifications to the instrument.4

[21]  The right of action established by s. 258(1) of the Insurance
Act  is  dependent  upon the injured third party being  a  judgment
creditor.  The  injured  parties  in  these  actions  do  not  have
judgments.  There  is a good reason for this. All  plaintiffs  have
been  paid, under arrangements entered into between Royal and State
Farm  in  the  Truelove action, and Zurich and State  Farm  in  the
Campanaro  action. The motions before Gotlib J., in  the  Campanaro
action,  and  Rosenberg J., in the Truelove  action,  were  brought
under  Rule  22  and counsel did not rely upon  the  absence  of  a
judgment  in  favour  of the plaintiffs in either  action.  Indeed,
counsel  wanted  the  question of State  Farm's  liability  to  the
plaintiffs  to be determined. Since counsel have proceeded  on  the
basis that the judgment precondition for the action contemplated by
s. 258(1) has been satisfied, it seems to me we should respect that
understanding.   Indeed,   counsel   involved   in   the   relevant
arrangements should be commended for working things out so that the
injured plaintiffs were paid without undue delay. I thus proceed on
the  basis that it can be taken for purposes of analysis that there
is a judgment against the driver in both actions.


THE 1947 AMENDMENT

[22]  The Insurance Act was amended in 1947 to add what is  now  s.
258(5). The amendment was triggered by the 1946 judgment of the New
Brunswick  Supreme Court, Appeal Division in Bourgeois  et  al.  v.
Prudential  Assurance  Co. Ltd., [1946]  1  D.L.R.  139.  Bourgeois
followed this court's judgment in Comer v. Bussell, [1940] 1 D.L.R.
97,  aff'd., [1940] S.C.R. 506. Before Comer and Bourgeois, it  was
generally   thought   that   a   misrepresentation,   including   a
misrepresentation  as to ownership, would not  defeat  an  innocent
plaintiff's claim against an insurer, if the insurer had issued  an
instrument as a motor vehicle liability policy. The editorial  note
in the D.L.R. report of Bourgeois (at p. 140) reflects this view:


          This is a decision of great importance. It  has
          been  fairly  generally assumed that  where  an
          insurer issues a motor vehicle liability policy
          no  misrepresentation in respect to  its  issue
          would  affect  an injured third persons'  [sic]
          rights under s. 183 of the Insurance Act (N.B.)
          which  is  the  same as s. 205, Insurance  Act,
          R.S.O. 1937, c. 256, and sections in all  other
          provincial  Insurance Acts except the  Province
          of Quebec. [Emphasis added.]

See also McLean, Case and Comment (1946), 24 Can. Bar. Rev. 57.

[23]  Comer was an ownership misrepresentation case where the named
insured  was not the true owner of the insured vehicle.  In  Comer,
the  ownership of a taxi was transferred to Seaman. However, he was
not the true owner of the taxi. Seaman had earlier sold the taxi to
Johnson  whose son ran a taxi business. Johnson's son lived outside
the  town  limits and could not obtain the required municipal  taxi
licence.  To  solve  that problem he had two  taxis  registered  in
Seaman's  name.  The  sole purpose of the  transfer  was  to  cause
municipal  authorities to conclude that residence requirements  for
taxi  licence  purposes had been met. One of the taxis,  driven  by
Kowaluk,  was  involved in an accident. The  plaintiffs  secured  a
judgment  against  the driver Kowaluk, but not  against  the  named
insured,  Seaman.   Kowaluk drove the taxi for  its  actual  owner,
Johnson,  who  was  not, as I have said, the  named  insured.  Long
before  the  accident, the registered owner, Seaman, had  left  the
area.


[24]  In  the plaintiff's action against the insurer under  s.  205
(now.  s.  258)  of the Insurance Act, the trial judge  found  that
there  were  no  material  misrepresentations.  He  therefore  gave
judgment for the plaintiff. However, the Court of Appeal disagreed.
Robertson  C.J.O.  concluded that it was  material  to  an  insurer
"whether  the  person  who  desires to  be  insured  against  risks
incurred in the use of the motor car is a person who has control of
it  and the right to say when and how and by whom it will be used."
He  held that since the named insured was not the true owner of the
taxi,  the policy in question was not "an owner's policy." He  also
raised  the issue whether the named insured, Seaman, had  consented
to  Kowaluk  driving the taxi. He held that the action against  the
insurer   should  be  dismissed.  He  relied  upon  the   ownership
misrepresentation  and, it would appear, the  absence  of  evidence
that  the  named insured consented to the driver, Kowaluk,  driving
the taxi.


[25] On the plaintiff's further appeal ([1940] S.C.R. 506), to  the
Supreme Court of Canada, Duff C.J.C. found that the "precise  point
to  be  decided" was whether the driver, Kowaluk, was  entitled  to
indemnity under the policy. In dealing with this issue he  referred
to the provisions of the Insurance Act that set out the coverage to
be  provided in an "owner's policy." For Kowaluk to be entitled  to
indemnity under Seaman's policy, he had to be driving at  the  time
of  the accident with Seaman's consent. Duff C.J.C. considered  the
form of the purported policy (an owner's policy) in the context  of
his assessment of the consent issue.


[26]  Duff C.J.C. accepted the Court of Appeal's conclusion on  the
consent issue. I note that in the Court of Appeal, Robertson C.J.O.
did  not determine whether the named insured, Seaman, could consent
to  Kowaluk  driving the taxi. He found that there was no  evidence
that Seaman had in fact consented to Kowaluk's use of the taxi.  He
put it this way at p. 101:


               I do not know in what sense it can be said
          that Kowaluk was using the car with the consent
          of  Seaman. These words must surely imply  that
          Seaman's   consent   was  Kowaluk's   effective
          authority  for  using the motor car,  and  that
          Seaman,  by  withholding  consent,  could  have
          prevented the driving of the car by Kowaluk. It
          is  not necessary, however, to discuss this for
          I  do  not  find any evidence of a  consent  by
          Seaman   in  any  sense  whatsoever.  [Emphasis
          added.]

[27]  In Comer, as I have said, the driver Kowaluk was driving  for
the  actual  owner,  Johnson, not the named insured  Seaman.  Thus,
whether  Kowaluk was driving with the consent of the named  insured
was problematic. I will refer to the consent issue as it relates to
these appeals later.


[28]  Bourgeois  was also an ownership misrepresentation  case.  In
Bourgeois, the plaintiffs were injured by a truck operated  by  one
Poirier  and  recovered judgment against him. They  then  sued  his
insurer  to  recover  on  their judgment. In  his  application  for
automobile insurance, Poirier had stated that he had purchased  the
truck  for  $1,400.00 and that it was subject to an encumbrance  of
$500.00  held  by  Nadeau.  The trial judge  found  that,  although
Poirier was the registered owner of the truck, the actual owner was
the  mortgagee,  Nadeau. On these facts, the trial judge  concluded
that Poirier was a "sham" owner and that the real owner was Nadeau.
He  dismissed the plaintiffs' action against the insurer because of
the named insured's ownership misrepresentation. On the plaintiffs'
appeal, Harrison J. accepted the trial judge's findings of fact and
concluded  that  the  policy  was  void.  He  concluded  that   the
plaintiffs'  statutory  action was  an  action  on  "the  insurance
contract."  Thus, if there was no contract because  of  a  material
misrepresentation, the plaintiff could not succeed. He said at  pp.
144-46:


               The principal question here is whether the
          action is based upon the contract contained  in
          the  policy  or  upon  an entirely  independent
          statutory   liability  imposed   on   insurance
          companies. If it is an action upon the  policy,
          under  a  statutory provision  giving  a  third
          party  a  right to sue upon such  policy,  then
          there must be a valid policy in existence, and,
          furthermore,  the  insurance company  would  be
          entitled  to rely upon all defences  under  the
          terms  of the policy which are not specifically
          taken away. In my opinion, s. 183 gives a third
          party   a  right  to  sue  upon  the  insurance
          contract,  if any; and, as the policy  here  is
          void by reason of misrepresentation [as to  the
          ownership],  then there is no "insurance  money
          payable under the policy." In fact, there is no
          policy.  [Emphasis added.]

                               ...

               I  do not consider s-s. (3)(ii) of s.  183
          takes  away  the  defence of misrepresentation.
          That  subsection takes away any defence due  to
          act or default of the insured and is controlled
          by  the  definition of "insured" in s.  165(d):
          "'Insured' means a person insured by a contract
          whether   named   or   not."   The    act    of
          misrepresentation  occurred before  the  policy
          was  issued and the applicant never became "the
          insured".5

[29] Accordingly, Harrison J. agreed with the trial judge that  the
plaintiffs' action against the insurer should be dismissed.


[30]  The  response  of provincial legislatures  to  Bourgeois  was
swift.  In  1947,  the  Insurance Acts of  Ontario,  Prince  Edward
Island,  Manitoba, Saskatchewan and Alberta were amended. In  1948,
the New Brunswick Insurance Act was amended.


[31] In Ontario, s. 205 of the Insurance Act, S.O. 1947, c. 51, was
amended by the addition of s-s. (3a). It provided:


          (3a)  It  shall not be a defence to  an  action
          under this section that an instrument issued as
          a  motor  vehicle liability policy by a  person
          engaged  in  the  business of an  insurer,  and
          alleged by a party to the action to be  such  a
          policy,   is  not  a  motor  vehicle  liability
          policy,  and  the  provisions of  this  section
          shall   apply,   mutatis   mutandis,   to   the
          instrument.

[32]  What was s-s. (3a) S.O. 1947, is now s. 258(5). Although  the
language of the two subsections is not identical, the substance  of
ss. 205(3a) and 258(5) is the same.


[33]  When s-s. (3a) was added to s. 205 of the Insurance Act,  the
explanatory  note accompanying the amendment expressly  linked  the
amendment  to  the Bourgeois decision. The note to  which  I  refer
provided:


          Section  205 of the Act was intended to  create
          an  absolute  liability  on  the  part  of  the
          insurer  toward a person injured  by  a  person
          insured under a motor vehicle liability policy.
          In   the   case  of  Bourgeois  v.   Prudential
          Assurance  Company  the Supreme  Court  of  New
          Brunswick on the same section of their Act held
          that  where  a  material misrepresentation  was
          made  prior  to the issue of the  policy  which
          resulted  in the applicant never being actually
          insured, the provisions of the section were not
          applicable.  This amendment has the  effect  of
          making the section applicable in any case where
          an  instrument  is issued as  a  motor  vehicle
          liability  policy by a person  engaged  in  the
          business  of an insurer which is alleged  by  a
          party  to  the  action to  be  such  a  policy.
          [Emphasis added.]

Cases Decided After the 1947 Amendment and Before London & Midland

[34] The 1947 and 1948 amendments to provincial Insurance Acts were
considered  in a number of cases. I propose to refer  to  three  of
them to illustrate how the courts interpreted what is now s. 258(5)
of  the  Insurance  Act before this court's judgment  in  London  &
Midland.


[35]  In Lewis v. The Northern Assurance Company Limited of London,
England,  [1956] O.R. 404, the insurer alleged that the  policy  it
had   issued   was   void   ab   initio  because   of   significant
misrepresentations  in  the application that  I  need  not  review,
except  to say that none of the alleged misrepresentations  related
to  the ownership of the insured automobile. After referring to the
D.L.R.  editorial  note on Bourgeois, Spence  J.  noted  that  even
before  the  1947  amendment (s. 205(3a)) it was generally  thought
that  the  relevant provisions of the Insurance Act "...provided  a
complete  bar to defences based on misrepresentation and  violation
of  the  conditions." When he dealt with the purpose and effect  of
the 1947 Insurance Act amendment in Ontario, he said at pp. 413-14:


               It  is  proper  to  consider  the  history
          preceding the amendment and the well-recognized
          fact,  as stated in the editorial note to which
          I have referred, that it was generally believed
          that  the then counterpart of s. 214(3) of  The
          Insurance  Act  provided  a  complete  bar   to
          defences   based   on   misrepresentation   and
          violation  of  the conditions: Maxwell  on  the
          Interpretation of Statutes, 9th ed.  1946,  pp.
          24-5; Craies on Statute Law, 5th ed. 1952,  pp.
          118 et seq.

                               ...

          ...It  is  alleged  by  one  party,  i.e.,  the
          plaintiff,  to  be  such  a  policy,  and   the
          defendant in alleging that the policy  is  void
          ab  initio is simply using other words to  deny
          that  the  policy is a motor vehicle  liability
          policy. In my view, the section applies exactly
          to  the present situation, and even had I  come
          to  other conclusions as to the facts and as to
          the effect of the insertion of the word "No" by
          Miss Simpson in the answer to q. 3(D) on ex. 2,
          I  would  have  concluded that the  defendant's
          defence  was  barred  by  s.  214(4)   of   The
          Insurance Act. [Emphasis added.]

[36]  Spence  J. concluded that because of the 1947  Insurance  Act
amendment,  which  he  viewed  as  the  Legislature's  response  to
Bourgeois, the insurer could not rely on misrepresentations of  the
named  insured. His conclusion on this issue was obiter,  since  he
found  that  there  were  no  material  misrepresentations  in  the
application.


[37]  In  Marshall v. Law Union and Rock Insurance Co. Ltd. (1958),
18  D.L.R. (2d) 442, the New Brunswick Court of Appeal had occasion
to  comment on the 1948 amendment brought about by its decision  in
Bourgeois. Marshall was a case of non-disclosure. The applicant for
insurance had neglected to inform the agent that earlier in the day
on  which  the application for insurance was made, the  applicant's
automobile had been involved in an accident. The trial judge  found
that   the   failure   to   disclose   the   accident   constituted
"misrepresentation and fraud". He accepted that the 1948  amendment
of  the  N.B. Insurance Act prevented the insurer from  relying  on
misrepresentations by the named insured or his agent. However,  the
trial  judge  rectified  the contract  of  insurance  so  that  the
coverage  period  began the day after the accident.  The  plaintiff
appealed.  McNair C.J.N.B. also concluded that the  1948  amendment
was  intended  to overcome Bourgeois and prevent the  insurer  from
relying  on  misrepresentations by the named insured.  He  said  at
p. 446:


               It  is  only  reasonable to  conclude  the
          Legislature, in enacting in 1948 the provisions
          of  s.  211(4)  of the present  Insurance  Act,
          intended to overcome the effect of the decision
          in  the Bourgeois case. The language appears to
          me  effectual for that purpose. In consequence,
          in   my   view,  misrepresentation  or   wilful
          concealment  of  the facts by an  insured  when
          applying  for an insurance contract  no  longer
          vitiates the right of a third party to  recover
          from the insurer on the policy.

[38]  In the end, however, McNair C.J.N.B. upheld the dismissal  of
the  plaintiff's  action against the insurer.  He  concluded  that,
although  the plaintiff's claim could not be rejected on the  basis
of fraudulent concealment of the accident that had occurred earlier
in  the  day on which the application for insurance was  made,  the
contract did not provide indemnity against claims that arose before
the policy was in fact issued.


[39]  In Blair et al. v. Royal Exchange Assurance (1968), 67 D.L.R.
(2d)  420 (Man. Q.B.). Hunt J. considered three misrepresentations,
one  of  which was a misrepresentation as to ownership. He referred
to  both Lewis and Marshall and concluded that the defence  of  the
insurer  that the policy in question was void ab initio  was  taken
away  by the Manitoba equivalent of s. 258(5) of the Insurance Act.
He said at p. 426:


          It   was  argued  that  the  portions  of  both
          judgments [Lewis and Marshall] dealing with the
          enactment  in  question were obiter,  but  both
          cases  contain clear explanations of the effect
          of the section which are in my opinion correct.
          In  particular,  a valuable exposition  of  the
          history of the provisions is contained  in  the
          Lewis case.

               In  my view the section [in Ontario now s.
          258(5)] applies to the facts of this case,  and
          the  defence of the insurer that the policy  is
          void   ab   initio  is  barred  by  s.  227(4).
          Accordingly  the  defence on that  ground  must
          fail. [Emphasis added.]

[40]  The  plaintiff succeeded in his action against  the  insurer,
notwithstanding  the named insured's misrepresentation  as  to  the
ownership of the insured automobile.


Minister  of Transport et al. v. London & Midland General Insurance
Co.

[41]  In 1971, without reference to cases such as Lewis, Blair,  or
Marshall (or other cases that similarly interpreted what is now  s.
258(5)), this court in London & Midland limited the application  of
the  absolute  liability provisions of the Insurance  Act  (now  s.
258(4) and (5)) by holding that the insurer could defend an injured
plaintiff's  action (the action contemplated by s. 258(1))  on  the
basis of a material misrepresentation by the named insured, if  the
misrepresentation   was  about  the  ownership   of   the   insured
automobile.  The  appellants  contend  that,  in  carving   out   a
fictitious ownership exception to the absolute liability provisions
of s. 258, London & Midland was wrongly decided.


[42]  In London & Midland, D. wanted to purchase a car but did  not
have a driver's licence. To solve that problem, he entered into  an
arrangement with B. to have the car that he purchased registered in
her  name.  The  arrangement was that when D. obtained  a  driver's
licence the ownership of the car, and the insurance on it, would be
transferred to him. Some time after the car was purchased, B. asked
D.  whether  the  transfers of ownership  and  insurance  had  been
attended  to.  He  told  her that they had. In  fact,  neither  the
ownership nor the insurance had been transferred to D.


[43]  After  the conversation about the transfer of  ownership  and
insurance, D. was involved in an accident while driving the insured
automobile, which was still registered in B.'s name. D. was  killed
in  the  accident. Third parties who were injured in  the  accident
sued  D.'s  estate and B., the registered owner of the  automobile.
London  and  Midland  was added as a third party  pursuant  to  the
provisions  of  the  Insurance  Act.  Eventually,  the  action  was
dismissed  on  consent  against  the  registered  owner,  B.,   and
plaintiffs  recovered judgment against D.'s estate  Their  judgment
was  paid by the Minister who commenced a subrogated action against
London & Midland under what is now s. 258(1) of the Insurance Act.


[44]  London & Midland conceded that the policy in question was  an
instrument  issued  as  a motor vehicle liability  policy.  It  was
accepted that B., the registered owner and named insured,  was  not
the true owner of the automobile at any relevant time.


[45]  In oral reasons delivered on behalf of the court, Gale C.J.O.
concluded that the 1947 Insurance Act amendment overcame Comer  and
Bourgeois  and that the insurer could not rely on misrepresentation
in  the  application for insurance in defence of an action  brought
under what is now s. 258(1). He put it in this way at p. 149:


               We agree with Mr. Holland that if a policy
          is issued to the owner of a motor vehicle as  a
          result of his misrepresentation, then the  fact
          of  the  misrepresentation will not provide  an
          insurance company with a defence against  third
          parties  who  are injured as a  result  of  the
          operation of the vehicle by the owner. In other
          words,  it  has  the effect of  overcoming  the
          results  achieved in the decisions of Comer  v.
          Bussell  et  al.  and in Bourgeois  et  al.  v.
          Prudential Assurance Co. Ltd. [Emphasis added.]
          [Citations omitted.]

[46]  Taken  that  far,  Gale C.J.O. accepted the  defence-limiting
provisions of what is now s. 258(5) of the Insurance Act.  However,
he  went  on to find that the policy in question did not cover  the
risk  because it was not an "owner's policy" and because D did  not
have  B's consent to drive the car at the time of the accident.  He
said at p. 149:


          ...This  policy does not cover  this  risk  not
          only  because it is not an owner's  policy  but
          also  because it cannot be said that  [D.]  had
          [B.'s]  consent to operate the car at the  time
          the  accident occurred. We say that because she
          was  not  in a position to be able to  give  or
          withhold consent ...

[47]  Thus,  Gale  C.J.O. upheld the dismissal of  the  plaintiff's
action  against  London & Midland. He held that  London  &  Midland
could  defend  the  minister's action on the  basis  of  the  named
insured's  misrepresentation about the  ownership  of  the  insured
automobile.  Gale C.J.O., after referring to Comer, also  supported
the  dismissal of the plaintiff's claim against London and  Midland
by  his  analysis of the consent issue. He concluded that,  on  the
facts of the case, it could not be said that the driver, the actual
owner,  had  the  named  insured's consent  to  drive  the  insured
automobile at the time of the accident.6


[48]   London  &  Midland  has  not  been  followed  in   Manitoba,
Newfoundland, New Brunswick and Prince Edward Island. It  has  been
followed in Nova Scotia and Alberta.


[49]  As  I have said, it was on the authority of London &  Midland
that  the motions judges in the two cases before us held that State
Farm  could rely upon the ownership misrepresentations of the named
insureds in both cases.


ANALYSIS

[50]  For reasons that I will set out shortly, I do not think  that
an  insurer  issuing  an instrument that purports  to  be  a  motor
vehicle  liability policy can validly defend a s. 258(1) action  on
the  basis of any misrepresentation by the named insured, including
a  misrepresentation about the ownership of the insured vehicle. In
my opinion, the characterization of an instrument issued as a motor
vehicle liability policy as an owner's policy, or otherwise, is not
relevant to the analysis. Thus, State Farm cannot defend the claims
made  against  it  on  the  basis of  the  misrepresentation  about
ownership by the named insured in either action. Further, I do  not
think  that  consent  is an issue in either of these  two  appeals,
since  in  both  cases the driver at the time of the  accident  was
driving with the consent of the named insured.


[51] I think that it is clear that the 1947 Insurance Act amendment
was  intended to overcome the effect of Bourgeois and  preclude  an
insurer  from defending an innocent third party judgment creditor's
claim  on the basis of any material misrepresentation, including  a
misrepresentation  as  to  ownership  of  the  insured  automobile.
Although  the Legislature's intention seems to me to be clear,  the
question  remains whether the amendment did overcome  Bourgeois  in
those   cases   where  the  misrepresentation   in   issue   is   a
misrepresentation about the ownership of the insured automobile.


[52]  In  State  Farm Mutual Automobile Insurance  Co.  v.  General
Accident  Assurance  Co. of Canada (1995),  127  D.L.R.  (4th)  648
(N.B.C.A.),  Hoyt C.J.N.B. considered the New Brunswick  equivalent
of  s. 258(5) in a case where a mother applied for insurance on  an
automobile  that  her  son  actually  owned.  The  mother  was  the
automobile's registered owner. The son, while driving  the  insured
automobile, caused an accident as a result of his negligence.   The
issue was whether General Accident, the insurer that had issued the
policy  to  the  mother, could defend the action brought  by  State
Farm,  the  injured  third party's insurer, on  the  basis  of  the
mother's   ownership  misrepresentation  in  her  application   for
insurance.7 General Accident contended that since the instrument in
issue was not an owner's policy the named insured could not consent
to  others,  including  her son, driving the  automobile.  It  also
relied  on  this court's judgment in London & Midland. State  Farm,
which  provided uninsured motorist coverage, relied on the absolute
liability provisions of the N.B. Insurance Act, including the  1948
amendment, which brought the equivalent of s. 258(5) into the  N.B.
Act. Hoyt C.J.N.B. disagreed with this court's analysis in London &
Midland.  He did not accept the submission of General Accident  and
an  intervener  insurer that to sustain an action  under  the  N.B.
equivalent  of s. 258(1), there must be a policy of insurance8  and
that  there  are two types of automobile insurance policies  --  an
owner's and a non-owner's policy.9 He said at p. 653:


          The   submissions  of  General   Accident   and
          Canadian    General   [the   insurer    granted
          intervener   status]   ignore   Chief   Justice
          McNair's  conclusion that a statutory right  of
          recovery  is created that cannot be avoided  by
          misrepresentation  or  wilful  concealment   of
          facts.  To say, as General Accident does,  that
          there  can  be  only  two types  of  automobile
          insurance   policies,  an  owner's  policy   as
          described  in  s.  232(1) of the  Act  [Ontario
          s.  239] or a driver's or non-owner's policy as
          described   in   s.  233  ...  disregards   the
          situation  envisaged by s. 250(5)  [Ontario  s.
          258(5)].   "An  instrument"  was  "issued"   by
          General  Accident with respect to  the  vehicle
          that was involved in the accident.

[53] Hoyt C.J.N.B. thus held that General Accident could not defend
the  plaintiff's  action  on  the  basis  of  the  named  insured's
ownership misrepresentation.


[54]  I  agree  with Hoyt C.J.N.B.'s conclusion in respect  of  the
owner's  policy  issue.  See also Murphy  v.  Casualty  Company  of
Canada,  [1987] I.L.R. 1-2229; Dore v. General Insurance  Corp.  of
New  Brunswick  (1991), 5 C.C.L.I. (2d) 266 and Weavers  Estate  v.
Biseau   (1992),  10  C.C.L.I.  (2d)  255  (Ont.Gen.Div.),   appeal
dismissed (1997) 32 O.R. (3d) 480.


[55]  It seems to me that whether or not the instrument in question
purports  to  be  an owner's policy is a non-issue. Section  258(5)
makes  no reference to owner's policies. Instead it refers  to  "an
instrument issued as a motor vehicle liability policy." While there
is  no doubt that in most cases, including the two cases before us,
the  fictitious  "owner"  applied for  coverage  under  an  owner's
policy,  I  do  not  think that that fact is of  any  significance.
Further, s. 258(5) makes no reference to a "contract"; it refers to
an "instrument" issued as a motor vehicle liability policy. Whether
the  instrument  is,  or is not, a contract  or  a  policy  is  not
material  to  the  insurer's liability to a  third  party  judgment
creditor.10 The right of action created by s. 258(1) is a statutory
right  of  action.  It  permits  an injured  third  party  who  has
recovered a judgment to have the insurance money "applied"  to  the
judgment.  The  action is not an action by a  third  party  on  the
policy, owner's or non-owner's.


[56]  In  my opinion, for purposes of the analysis required  by  s.
258,  the  threshold  question  is whether  the  insurer  (assuming
throughout  that  the defendant is engaged in the  business  of  an
insurer)  issued  an  instrument  "as  a  motor  vehicle  liability
policy." How that instrument will be labelled, that is, whether  it
was intended to be an owner's or non-owner's policy, is a matter of
no import, so long as the instrument issued by the insurer purports
to be a motor vehicle liability policy.


[57]  "Motor vehicle liability policy", is defined in the Insurance
Act  to  include  an owner's policy and a non-owner's  policy.  The
relevant definition is:


          "Motor vehicle liability policy" means a policy
          or  part  of  a  policy evidencing  a  contract
          insuring,

          (a)  the owner or driver of an automobile, or

          (b)  a  person  who is not the owner or  driver
               thereof where the automobile is being used
               or  operated by that person's employee  or
               agent or any other person on that person's
               behalf,

          against liability arising out of bodily  injury
          to  or  the death of a person or loss or damage
          to  property caused by an automobile or the use
          or  operation thereof; [R.S.O. 1990, c. I.8, s.
          1]11

[58]  In my opinion, for purposes of the action contemplated by  s.
258(1),  s. 258(5) prevents the insurer from relying upon  material
representations made by, or on behalf of, the named insured, if the
insurer  issued an instrument as a motor vehicle liability  policy.
State  Farm  issued such instruments following the application  for
insurance by both Ki-Suk Kim and Alan Hamilton. For reasons that  I
have  set  out earlier, I do not think that it matters whether  the
instrument  issued as a motor vehicle liability policy  to  Messrs.
Kim and Hamilton was an owner's or a non-owner's policy.


[59]  With respect to the scope of s. 258(5), it makes no sense  to
me  to  interpret  that section in a way that treats  one  type  of
misrepresentation (a misrepresentation about ownership) differently
from  all  other misrepresentations. See Dore v. General Insurance,
supra.  Moreover, the language of s. 258(5) does not  support  that
interpretation.  If  an insurer can defend a third  party  judgment
creditor's  action  brought under s.  258(1)  on  the  basis  of  a
misrepresentation  about ownership, but not on  the  basis  of  any
other  misrepresentation, then s. 258(5)  did  not  have  "...  the
effect  of  overcoming Bourgeois" as Gale C.J.O.  said  it  did  in
London  &  Midland,  at  p.  149. With respect,  I  think  that  in
exempting    one   type   of   misrepresentation,   that    is    a
misrepresentation   about  ownership,  from  the   defence-limiting
provisions  of s. 258(5), London & Midland was wrongly decided.  In
my opinion, if s. 258(5) precludes the insurer from contending that
the instrument it issued as a motor vehicle liability policy is not
a  motor  vehicle liability policy, it must follow that the insurer
cannot  rely  on any misrepresentation which, if given  effect  to,
would  result in the instrument issued by the insurer  as  a  motor
vehicle  liability  policy being taken not to be  a  motor  vehicle
liability policy.


[60]  I  see  no  merit  in the argument that the  introduction  of
uninsured  motorist coverage should give rise to an  interpretation
of s. 258 that would relieve State Farm from the absolute liability
that  I  think it is imposed on it by s. 258 of the Insurance  Act.
There  is  no  doubt that the availability of statutorily  required
uninsured motorist coverage makes the absolute liability provisions
of the Insurance Act less necessary to protect the innocent victims
of  negligent driving. This, however, does not, in my view, suggest
that  s.  258 should be interpreted so as to carve out a fictitious
ownership  exception to the application of the  absolute  liability
provisions of the section. The absolute liability provisions of  s.
258  have  remained unchanged since the introduction  of  uninsured
motorist  coverage. It seems to me that the language of s.  258  is
clear  and  I  see  no  basis to change the interpretation  of  the
section  because there is another available payer. In  the  present
scheme of things the tortfeasor's insurer is the first payer.


[61] I therefore think that State Farm is, by the provisions of  s.
258(5),  precluded from defending the claims made in the  Campanaro
and  Truelove actions on the basis that the named insured  in  each
case falsely represented that he owned the insured automobile.


[62] I do not view consent to be an issue that is properly part  of
the s. 258 analysis. I refer to it only because it was addressed in
London & Midland and Comer. I accept that there will be cases where
an  insurer  may  successfully contend that the driver  causing  an
accident by his or her negligence was not driving with the  consent
of  the named insured. In such a case, the insurer's obligation  to
pay  an injured third party is controlled by the insuring agreement
in  the policy in question. See Walker v. Allstate (1989), 67  O.R.
(2d)  733. Once it is accepted that the insurer cannot defend a  s.
258(1)  action by taking the position that the instrument  that  it
issued  was  not  a  motor vehicle liability policy,  in  my  view,
consent is properly viewed as a coverage issue.


[63] Coverage under an owner's policy is statutorily prescribed  in
s.  239(1)  of  the  Insurance  Act. As  they  must,  the  insuring
agreements  in the two policies in issue here have to  provide  the
coverage  set out in s. 239(1).12 The insuring agreements  provide,
under the heading, "Third Party Liability":


               The   insurer  agrees  to  indemnify   the
          insured and, in the same manner and to the same
          extent as if named herein as the insured, every
          other  person  who with his consent  personally
          drives the automobile ... against the liability
          imposed  by  law upon the insured or  upon  any
          such  other  person for loss or damage  arising
          from  the  ownership, use or operation  of  the
          automobile and resulting from bodily injury  to
          or  death  of any person or damage to property.
          [Emphasis added.]

[64]  It is readily apparent that the "consent" referred to in  the
insuring agreement is the consent of the named insured. The  driver
is  an  unnamed insured under the policy if he is driving with  the
consent  of  the named insured. In such circumstances,  the  policy
provides  the same indemnity to the unnamed insured as it  does  to
the named insured.


[65]  In  those  cases  where there is a  misrepresentation  as  to
ownership,  the  misrepresentation is usually driven  by  economics
(getting coverage at a lower premium),13 or insurability (obtaining
coverage in circumstances where the true owner would not be  viewed
as insurable).14 In most of these cases, the driver will drive with
the  named insured's consent, since that is part of the deal. Here,
there  is no evidence in the Campanaro action that Ki-Jin  Kim  was
driving  the  insured automobile without the consent of  the  named
insured, his brother, Ki-Suk Kim. Similarly in the Truelove  action
there  is  no evidence that Scott Thompson was driving the  insured
ATV without the consent of the named insured, Alan Hamilton.


SUMMARY AND CONCLUSION

[66] In summary, in both cases State Farm issued "an instrument" as
a  motor  vehicle liability policy. It was accepted here and  below
that  the  claimants seeking to have the State Farm policy proceeds
applied   to   the   plaintiffs'  agreed  compensation   meet   the
requirements  of  s. 258(1) of the Insurance Act, even  though  the
claimants  do  not have a judgment as required by  s.  258(1).  The
action  against  an insurer that issues an instrument  as  a  motor
vehicle  liability policy is a statutory action, not an  action  on
the  policy.  Section  258(5)  conspicuously  refers  only  to   an
instrument issued as a motor vehicle liability policy. It  contains
no  reference  to an owner's policy or even a contract.  There  is,
therefore,  nothing in s. 258(5) which requires that  there  be  an
owner's policy. Section 258(5) precludes the insurer from defending
the  statutory action contemplated by s. 258(1) on the  basis  that
the  insurer,  because of misrepresentations rendering  the  policy
void,  did not issue a motor vehicle liability policy. The  defence
limiting  provisions  of s. 258(5) apply to all misrepresentations,
including  a  misrepresentation about the ownership of the  insured
automobile.


[67] Thus, I would allow both appeals with costs. I would set aside
the  orders below with costs. In both actions an order should issue
declaring  that State Farm is absolutely liable to the  plaintiffs,
subject to any defence other than ownership misrepresentation, that
State Farm may have.
