                          ENDORSEMENT


[1]  Canbev Sales & Marketing Inc. ("Canbev") appeals the
judgment of Lederman J. of the Ontario Court (General Division)
dismissing its action against Natco Trading Corporation ("Natco")
and its principal Mohammed Al-Ali. Canbev was the minority
shareholder (33%) in Allen's Food Industries Inc. ("Allen's"),
and Natco was the majority shareholder (67%). Under the terms of
a shareholders agreement between Canbev and Natco, neither party
could sell its shares to a third party without the consent of the
other shareholder or, failing consent, by offering them to the
other shareholder and permitting him to match the offer.


[2]  This action is framed as an oppression remedy by a minority
shareholder against the majority shareholder. The dispute centres
on the sale of Natco's shares by Al-Ali and the manner in which
Canbev's consent to that sale was obtained. The complaint of the
appellant is that in giving its consent, it relied upon the price
to be paid for the Natco shares set out in what is referred to as
a "term sheet". This sheet was shown to him by the purchasers as
being the net price Natco was to receive for its 67% shares of
Allen's, whereas the price was the gross price and subject to
significant set offs. The appellant also states that he relied
upon other representations when he executed a consent/waiver
consenting to the sale to a third party.


[3]  The trial judge canvassed the evidence in detail in his
extended reasons. In substance he found that the appellant,
through its principal officer, John Boese, did not rely upon the
figure set out in the term sheet as being a net figure. In this
court the appellant argues that, had it known the true price, it
would have exercised its right of "first offer" set out in the
shareholders agreement. However, as the trial judge found, the
appellant could have forced the respondent to give it any
information it required in order to clarify the terms of the
third party offer by simply withholding its consent.


[4]   The trial judge concluded that he could not find any
misrepresentation or oppressive conduct on the part of the
respondents and dismissed the action. We can find no error in
this disposition of the case. The appeal is dismissed with costs.
