                     ENDORSEMENT

The Appeal

[1]  The trial judge's findings of fact underlying his conclusion
that  a 12-month notice period was appropriate in this case  find
support  from  the  evidence.   We  cannot  interfere  with   his
conclusion.


[2]   While  we  do not necessarily agree with the trial  judge's
refusal to take the respondent's subsequent employment during the
notice  period into account as mitigation, we are satisfied  that
his  failure  to  do so does not prejudice the appellant  in  the
particular  circumstances of this case.  Had the  appellant  been
given  credit  for  the  respondent's  subsequent  employment  as
mitigation, the respondent would have been entitled to reasonable
expenses  incurred in effecting that mitigation.  In  this  case,
those reasonable expenses would have included the capital loss on
the sale of the respondent's home in Brampton.  That loss was  at
least  equal to the salary earned by the respondent  in  his  new
employment during the notice period.


Cross-appeal

[3]   We  see no error in the trial judge's conclusion  that  the
appellant  was  entitled  to  credit  for  the  payment  of   the
$10,000,00  debt owed by the respondent to a third  party.   That
debt was assumed by the appellant as paid of its efforts to reach
a negotiated settlement with the respondent.


[4]   The  trial judge appears to have factored the  respondent's
claim for various benefits into his assessment of the appropriate
notice  period.   While we do not agree with  that  approach,  we
cannot  say  that the amount eventually awarded did not  properly
reflect  adequate compensation for any benefits  payable  to  the
respondent.


[5]  The appeal and cross-appeal are dismissed with costs.
