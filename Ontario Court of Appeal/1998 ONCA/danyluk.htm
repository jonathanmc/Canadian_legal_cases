ROSENBERG J.A.:

[1]       This appeal concerns the application of the doctrine of
issue  estoppel where an administrative body and, then,  a  court
are  asked to deal with the same matter.  In October of 1993, the
appellant  filed  a  complaint  with  the  Ministry  of   Labour,
Employment  Standards  Branch regarding unpaid  wages,  including
entitlement  to a very large commission. An employment  standards
officer was assigned to investigate the appellant’s complaint.  A
year  later,  the officer determined that the appellant  was  not
entitled  to  the  commission.  Rather than appeal  this  finding
within  the  administrative structure, the appellant  decided  to
pursue  a  claim in the courts for damages for wrongful dismissal
and for unpaid wages and commissions.


[2]        In  the  civil action, the respondent employer  argued
that  the  claim for entitlement to commissions and unpaid  wages
was  barred  by issue estoppel.  The motions judge,  McCombs  J.,
agreed and struck out those paragraphs of the statement of  claim
respecting unpaid wages and commissions.


[3]        The doctrine of issue estoppel precludes a party  from
relitigating  any  issues that have been determined  in  a  prior
proceeding.  There are three requirements of issue estoppel1:
          
          (1)  the same question has been decided;
          
          (2)   the judicial decision which is said  to
          create the estoppel was final; and,
          
          (3)  the parties to the judicial decision  or
          their  privies were the same persons  as  the
          parties  to  the  proceedings  in  which  the
          estoppel is raised or their privies.

[4]        This appeal concerns only the second requirement.  The
appellant  concedes  that the first and third  requirements  have
been  met.   The  appellant  argues  that  the  decision  of   an
employment standards officer was neither judicial nor  final  and
thus issue estoppel cannot operate.  I agree with McCombs J. that
issue  estoppel  does  apply and would  accordingly  dismiss  the
appeal.
          
          THE FACTS

[5]        The  respondents brought the motion before McCombs  J.
under  rule  21.01(3)(d) of the Rules of Civil Procedure  on  the
basis  that the parts of the appellant’s claim relating to unpaid
wages  and commissions were frivolous, vexatious and an abuse  of
the  court’s process.  In addition to the pleadings, the  parties
filed   affidavits.   This  material  discloses   the   following
chronology: in the fall of 1993, the appellant became involved in
a  dispute with her employer,2 Ainsworth Technologies Inc.,  over
unpaid  commissions.  The appellant met with  her  superiors  and
sent  various  letters  outlining her  position.   Her  principal
complaint   concerned  an  alleged  entitlement  to   commissions
exceeding  $200,000 in respect of a project referred  to  in  the
material as the CIBC Lan project.


[6]        The appellant rejected a proposed settlement from  the
employer  and  on  October 4, 1993 filed a  complaint  under  the
Employment Standards Act, R.S.O. 1990, c. E.14.  On October  5th,
the  employer  wrote  to the appellant rejecting  her  claim  for
commissions.  The following week the appellant attended for work.
The  employer  took the position that she had  resigned  and  the
appellant was escorted off the premises.


[7]        An  employment standards officer, Caroline Burke,  was
assigned  to  investigate the appellant’s complaint.   She  spoke
with  the  appellant by telephone and met with her for one  hour.
The  appellant  gave  Ms  Burke various documents  including  her
correspondence  with  the  employer.   On  March  21,  1994,  the
appellant  commenced an action in which she claimed  damages  for
wrongful dismissal and also claimed unpaid wages and commissions.
On  June  1, 1994, solicitors for the employer wrote to Ms  Burke
responding  to  the  appellant’s claim.   The  employer's  letter
included a number of documents to substantiate its position.   Ms
Burke did not provide this material to the appellant nor did  she
ask the appellant to respond to it.


[8]        On  September  23,  1994, Ms Burke  ordered  Ainsworth
Technologies  to  pay  the appellant $2,354.55  representing  two
weeks'  pay  in lieu of notice.  Ms Burke advised Ainsworth  that
she had rejected the appellant’s claim for unpaid commissions  on
the  CIBC  project.   On October 3, 1994, Ms  Burke  advised  the
appellant, in writing, of the order made against the company  for
two  weeks'  termination  pay but which rejected  her  claim  for
commission  on the CIBC project.  The letter explained  that  the
appellant  had  a  right to apply to the Director  of  Employment
Standards for a review of this decision.  Ms Burke repeated  this
advice in a subsequent telephone conversation with the appellant.
The  appellant did not apply to the Director for a review  of  Ms
Burke’s  decision; instead, she pursued her claim  in  the  civil
courts.
          
          THE ISSUES

[9]        This  case  concerns the second requirement  of  issue
estoppel, that the decision which is said to create the  estoppel
be  a  final judicial decision.  The appellant submits  that  the
decision  of an employment standards officer is neither  judicial
nor  final.   She  also submits that, in any event,  the  process
followed  by  Ms  Burke in this particular case  was  unfair  and
therefore   her   decision  should  not   create   an   estoppel.
Specifically, the appellant argues she was not treated fairly  as
she  was not provided with a copy of the submissions made by  the
employer  and thus not given an opportunity to respond  to  those
submissions.
          
          THE LEGISLATIVE SCHEME

[10]  This  appeal engages provisions of the Employment Standards
Act,  R.S.O.  1990,  c.  E.14.   I  will  briefly  summarize  the
legislative  scheme as it existed at the time of the  appellant’s
complaint.   I note that there have been extensive amendments  to
the  Act  since  the  appellant's complaint: see  especially  the
Employment  Standards Improvement Act, 1996, S.O.  1996,  c.  23.
Among other things the amendments affect the jurisdiction of  the
employment  standards officer (see, for example, new s.  65(1.3))
and  the appeal procedure.  These new provisions do not apply  to
this  case  and their impact on the issues before this court  was
not  argued.  My discussion throughout will deal with the Act  as
it stood at the time of the appellant's complaint.


[11]  Section 7(1) requires an employer to pay all wages to which
an  employee is entitled under a contract of employment.  “Wages”
are  defined  in  s.  1  as including any  monetary  remuneration
payable  by  the  employer  under the  terms  of  a  contract  of
employment   and  thus  include  commissions.   Subsection   6(1)
provides that “No civil remedy of an employee against his or  her
employer is suspended or affected by this Act”.


[12] The administrative scheme of the Act is set out in Part  XV.
Under  s. 59(2), the Minister of Labour is required to appoint  a
Director of Employment Standards for the purposes of the  general
administration   of  the  Act  and  regulations   including   the
supervision and direction of employment standards officers.   The
appointment of employment standards officers, such as  Ms  Burke,
is  provided for in s. 59(3).  These officers are appointed under
the  Public Service Act, R.S.O. 1990, c. P.47.  Sections  60  and
60.1 require the Minister to appoint referees and adjudicators.


[13]  Sections  63 and 64 give the employment standards  officers
wide  powers for the purpose of ensuring compliance with the  Act
and  regulations.   Officers may require  production  of  various
records,  enter  business  premises  without  warrant,  and  make
inquiries  of any person.  It is an offence to obstruct  officers
in the exercise of a power or the performance of a duty under the
Act.


[14]  Ms  Burke's decision, which is said to give rise  to  issue
estoppel in this case, was made pursuant to ss. 65 and 67 of  the
Act.   The order to the employer to pay termination pay was  made
under  s. 65.  Subsection 65(6) requires the employer to whom  an
order  is issued to comply with it in accordance with its  terms.
An employer may apply for a review of the order.  However, if the
employer does not apply for a review, s. 65(7) provides that  the
order becomes “final and binding against the employer”.


[15]  Section 67 concerns the refusal of an officer  to  make  an
order  against an employer.  Subsection 67(1) provides  that  if,
following a complaint by an employee, the officer finds that  the
employee  has  no  entitlement to wages and  that  there  are  no
actions  the  employer  must take to comply  with  the  Act,  the
officer  “may  refuse  to issue an order to  an  employer”.   The
employee’s right to have the decision reviewed is also set out in
s. 67 which provides, in part, as follows:
          
          (2)   An  employee who considers  himself  or
          herself aggrieved by the refusal to issue  an
          order to an employer or by the issuance of an
          order  that  in  his  or her  view  does  not
          include   all   of   the   wages   or   other
          entitlements to which he or she  is  entitled
          may  apply to the Director in writing  within
          fifteen days … for a review of the refusal or
          of the amount of the order.
          
          (3)   Upon  receipt  of  an  application  for
          review   the   Director   may   appoint    an
          adjudicator who shall hold a hearing.
          
          …
          
          (5)   The  adjudicator who is conducting  the
          hearing   may  with  necessary  modifications
          exercise   the   powers   conferred   on   an
          employment standards officer under  this  Act
          and  may  make an order with respect  to  the
          refusal  or  an  order to amend,  rescind  or
          affirm  the order of the employment standards
          officer.
          
          …
          
          (7)   The  order  of the adjudicator  is  not
          subject  to review under section  68  and  is
          final  and  binding on the parties. [Emphasis
          added.]

[16] The employer’s right to review an order is set out in s.  68
and  roughly  parallels  the employee’s  right  to  review.   The
employer  may  apply for a review provided that it  has  complied
with any order to pay wages.  The Director is required to appoint
a  referee to review the order “by way of hearing”.  Irrespective
of the other issues that may be involved, s. 68(5.2) provides for
an  expedited hearing into the employee’s entitlement  to  wages.
The  referee is to issue his or her decision within 90 days after
the  first  day  of  hearing unless a referee designated  by  the
Minister   grants  an  extension  for  special  reasons.    Under
s.  68(7), the decision of the referee is “final and binding upon
the parties”.
          
          ANALYSIS

[17]  The  leading  case  on issue estoppel  as  applied  to  the
Employment  Standards  Act  is Rasanen v.  Rosemount  Instruments
Limited (1994), 17 O.R. (3d) 267 (C.A.), leave to appeal  to  the
Supreme  Court of Canada refused (1994), 19 O.R. (3d) xvi (note).
In  Rasanen, the plaintiff’s position in the company  had  become
redundant  as a result of corporate restructuring.  The  employer
offered  the plaintiff two alternative positions.  He refused  to
accept either position and the company terminated his employment.
The  plaintiff  filed a complaint under the Employment  Standards
Act, R.S.O. 1980, c. 137 alleging entitlement to termination pay.
The  Act  requires  an employer to pay to an  employee  specified
amounts  of  termination pay.  An employer is exempt from  paying
this  amount if, among other things, the employee has refused  an
offer of reasonable alternate work.


[18]  The  plaintiff’s complaint was initially considered  by  an
employment  standards  officer.  The officer  found  against  the
plaintiff.  The plaintiff requested a review of that decision  by
a  second  employment  standards  officer  as  permitted  by  the
legislation at the time.  This second officer found in favour  of
the plaintiff.  At this stage, the employer requested a review by
way  of  a hearing before a referee.  The referee considered  the
issue  to  be  whether  the terms of employment  offered  to  the
plaintiff  constituted  reasonable  alternate  employment  or   a
fundamental breach of the contract of employment.  In considering
this question, the referee applied tests similar to those used by
courts  in  determining  claims of constructive  dismissal.   The
referee  found that one of the two offers constituted  reasonable
alternate employment and the plaintiff was therefore not entitled
to termination pay under the Act.


[19]  In  the meantime, the plaintiff had brought an  action  for
wrongful  dismissal. The trial judge held that  in  view  of  the
referee's  findings issue estoppel applied.  In the  alternative,
the  trial  judge  held  there was no breach  of  the  employment
contract and that the plaintiff should have accepted one  of  the
alternate  positions.   The  plaintiff’s  submission   that   the
reassignment  constituted constructive  dismissal  was  rejected.
The plaintiff appealed to this court.


[20] The plaintiff argued that none of the three requirements for
issue estoppel applied. In separate reasons, Morden A.C.J.O.  and
Abella  J.A. held that issue estoppel did apply by reason of  the
earlier  decision of the referee.  Carthy J.A.  held  that  issue
estoppel  did not apply because its third requirement  (that  the
parties  to  the  earlier decision were the same)  was  not  met.
However, he dismissed the appeal because he agreed with the trial
judge’s alternative conclusion with respect to liability.


[21]  The  majority reasons in Rasanen are crucial for  resolving
the  issues in this case.  In Rasanen, the principal argument  of
the  appellant was that the procedure before the referee was  not
sufficiently  judicial. In extensive reasons,  Abella  J.A.  held
that,  in principle, there is no reason not to treat the decision
of  an  administrative  tribunal with the  same  deference  as  a
decision  of  a  court for the purposes of issue  estoppel.   She
pointed  out  that  the  application of  the  doctrine  of  issue
estoppel  involves a significant policy component and that  these
policy    considerations   favour   recognizing   decisions    of
administrative  tribunals.   As  Abella  J.A.  said  at  p.  279,
administrative tribunals were created as “independent bodies  for
the  purpose  of  being an alternative to the  judicial  process,
including  its  procedural  panoplies".   Designed  to  be  “less
cumbersome,  less expensive, less formal and less delayed,  these
impartial  decision-making bodies were  to  resolve  disputes  in
their   area  of  specialization  more  expeditiously  and   more
accessibly, but no less effectively or credibly…”.


[22]  With  respect to whether the proceeding before the  referee
was a judicial one, Abella J.A. concluded at p. 281:
          
          There   is  no  basis  for  restricting   the
          application  of issue estoppel  to  decisions
          made  by  judges  in the ordinary  course  of
          litigation.  By analogy, the hearing  by  the
          referee,  if  not technically “judicial”,  is
          designed   to   be   an  independent,   fair,
          impartial  and binding adjudicative  process,
          and  therefore  satisfies the spirit  of  the
          requirement.   It was a decision  made  in  a
          hearing in which the appellant knew the  case
          he  had to meet, had a chance to meet it, and
          lost.   Had  he won, the decision would  have
          been no less binding.  [Emphasis added.]

[23]  As  to  the requirement that the first decision  be  final,
Abella  J.A.  referred to [now] s. 68(7), which provides  that  a
decision of a referee is final and binding upon the parties.  She
held  that  the  referee’s decision is subject only  to  judicial
review  and,  since judicial review was not sought, the  decision
was final.


[24] Abella J.A. also considered the impact of s. 6(1) of the Act
which,  as  I  have noted, provides that no civil  remedy  of  an
employee is suspended or affected by the Act.  She held that  the
fact  that  an  employee  is  not prevented  from  seeking  civil
remedies does not preclude the application of issue estoppel in a
proper  case.3   She found assistance in the case law  respecting
the   scope   of   judicial  review  of  tribunal  decisions   in
interpreting s. 6(1) and concluded at p. 285:
          
          It  would  be anomalous if the courts,  while
          deferring to the final and binding nature  of
          tribunal   decisions  for   judicial   review
          purposes,  did not treat these same decisions
          as binding in subsequent judicial proceedings
          where  the  same issues arise.  While  it  is
          certainly true, therefore, that the appellant
          was  free to pursue his remedies in either  a
          court  or at the Employment Standards Branch,
          he was not free to presume that he was immune
          from  being  bound  by a  final  decision  of
          either forum on the same issue.

[25]   Abella  J.A.  also  resolved  the  other  issue   estoppel
requirements  against the plaintiff.  She  found  that  the  same
question  had been decided in the proceeding before  the  referee
and that the parties or their privies were the same.






[26]  Morden  A.C.J.O. also held that issue estoppel  applied  in
this  case.   As to s. 6(1) he held, at p. 293, that its  purpose
was  to  make  it  clear  that the “minimum employment  standards
provided  for  in  the Act are not to stand  in  the  way  of  an
employee  seeking  and  obtaining  more  favourable  common   law
relief.”   So interpreted, there is still room for the  operation
of  issue  estoppel if the requirements of the doctrine are  met.
Nevertheless, Morden A.C.J.O. cautioned that issue estoppel  must
be applied with circumspection.  Particularly with respect to the
requirement that the decision be judicial, he advised the  courts
to  proceed  with  care  in applying issue estoppel  to  tribunal
decisions.  He stated at p. 295:
          
          I   do   not  exclude  the  possibility  that
          deficiencies in the procedure relating to the
          first decision could properly be a factor  in
          deciding  whether  or  not  to  apply   issue
          estoppel.   However, in this  case,  whatever
          the procedure was that governed the statutory
          proceeding,  the  appellant frankly  admitted
          that it placed him at no disadvantage in  the
          presentation  of his case and  so  I  do  not
          think  that the procedural aspect is relevant
          in this case.  [Emphasis added.]

[27]  The  majority  decision  in  Rasanen  thus  determines  the
following matters that are of assistance in resolving the  issues
in this case:
          
          1.     The   decision  of  an  administrative
          tribunal  may give rise to issue estoppel  in
          subsequent court proceedings.
          
          2.     Section  6(1)  of  the  Act  does  not
          preclude the operation of issue estoppel.
          
          3.    Although not strictly “judicial” in the
          sense of a formal court hearing, a proceeding
          before   a  referee  results  in  a  judicial
          decision for the purposes of issue estoppel.
          
          4.    Under the Act, a referee’s decision  is
          final and binding on the parties subject only
          to  judicial review and is therefore a  final
          decision for the purposes of issue estoppel.

[28]  In the present case, counsel for the appellant, Mr. Levitt,
sought  to  distinguish  Rasanen by  pointing  out  a  number  of
differences  between the facts of this case and  its  facts.   He
noted  that  this  decision was made by an  employment  standards
officer  rather  than  a  referee as in Rasanen.   Moreover,  the
procedure  employed by the officer was considerably  less  formal
and  bore  even  less  resemblance to a court  hearing  than  the
proceeding before the referee.  In Rasanen, both the employee and
a  representative of the employer testified at a  hearing  before
the referee.  The employee was, in effect, represented by counsel
from the Ministry of Labour.


[29]  In contrast, Ms Burke did not hold a hearing involving both
parties  and she did not receive oral testimony from the parties.
The  appellant  presented her case to Ms Burke through  telephone
calls,  a  meeting and by providing various documents.   Although
the  appellant  was represented by counsel in the  civil  action,
that  counsel  does  not  appear  to  have  participated  in  the
proceedings before Ms Burke.  Lastly, Mr. Levitt argued that  the
appellant  was  not  treated fairly by the  employment  standards
officer; in Rasanen there was no complaint of unfairness  in  the
process  before the referee.  The appellant submits that she  was
not  provided with an opportunity to respond to the June 1,  1994
correspondence  from the employer to Ms Burke.  In  essence,  her
concern is that she did not know the case against her and have an
opportunity to meet it.


[30]   Mr.  Levitt  also  relies  upon  the  different  statutory
provisions  involved  in this case as opposed  to  Rasanen.   For
instance,  under s. 59 of the Act, employment standards  officers
are civil servants appointed under the Public Service Act whereas
referees are appointed by the Minister pursuant to s. 60.   There
has  also  been a change in the legislative scheme since Rasanen.
Under  the former legislation, an employee dissatisfied with  the
decision could only seek a review by another employment standards
officer,  whereas  an employer could apply  for  a  review  by  a
referee.   Under  s. 67 of the present legislation,  however,  an
employee  can  apply  for  a  review  by  an  adjudicator.   Like
referees,  adjudicators are appointed by the Minister.   We  were
informed  that  notwithstanding the  different  terminology,  the
Minister  will  appoint  the  same persons  to  be  referees  and
adjudicators  and the present practice is to appoint  vice-chairs
of  the  Ontario  Labour Relations Board.   Finally,  Mr.  Levitt
points  out  that s. 67 does not expressly state that  employment
standards  officers'  decisions are "final and  binding"  whereas
ss.  67  and 68 state that decisions of adjudicators and referees
are "final and binding".


[31]  In  my  view,  the  distinctions Mr. Levitt  draws  between
Rasanen  and  the  circumstances  of  this  case  have  no  legal
significance for the purposes of applying issue estoppel.   As  a
framework,  I  will  deal  with the  appellant's  submissions  by
grouping them under three headings:
     (1) A final decision;
     (2) A judicial decision;
     (3) The effect of procedural unfairness in this case.
          
          (1)  A Final Decision

[32]  A  decision  is final for the purposes  of  issue  estoppel
notwithstanding  that it may be subject to  appeal.   It  follows
that  the decision of the employment standards officer is a final
decision even though it is subject to review by an adjudicator or
referee.  The  law in this respect is summarized  in  G.  Spencer
Bower  and  A.K.  Turner, The Doctrine of Res Judicata,  2nd  ed.
(London: Butterworths, 1969) at p. 143:
          
          A  judicial decision, otherwise final, is not
          the  less so merely because it is appealable.
          If  it  is incapable of revision by the court
          which  pronounced  it, it is  final  in  that
          court,  which is all that is required  to  be
          shown, and it is wholly immaterial that it is
          capable of being rescinded or varied by  some
          other    court   invested   with    competent
          jurisdiction   in   that  behalf.   [Emphasis
          added.]

[33]  There  is  nothing  in the Employment  Standards  Act  that
indicates that an officer, having made a decision with respect to
an  employee’s  claim  for  wages, is entitled  to  rescind  that
decision.  The fact that the legislation does not expressly state
that the officer's decision is “final” merely recognizes that the
decision of the officer is subject to appeal.


[34]  Not  all decisions of administrative tribunals  or  courts,
however,  are  necessarily  final  for  the  purposes  of   issue
estoppel:   see, for example, Carl-Zeiss-Stiftung v.  Rayner  and
Keeler, Ltd. and Others (No. 2), [1966] 2 All E.R. 536 (H.L.)  at
566 per Lord Guest holding that a judgment on the capacity to sue
at  one  point cannot be final or conclusive at some later  time.
Similarly,  not  all  decisions of employment standards  officers
even  with  respect to a claim for payment of  wages  are  final.
Having  conducted an investigation, the officer may be unable  to
determine  whether the employee is entitled to payment of  wages.
The  officer  will  then be unable to make an order  for  payment
under  s.  65  or to refuse to make an order under  s.  67.   The
officer may, however, make a report to the Director under  s.  69
which provides as follows:
          
          69(1)       Where  an  employment   standards
          officer  reports  to  the  Director  that  an
          employer  may  have failed to pay  the  wages
          owing  to  an employee under this Act  or  to
          comply with this Act and the regulations,  or
          is  of  the  opinion that an act,  agreement,
          arrangement or scheme is intended to have  or
          has  the  effect  directly or  indirectly  of
          defeating the true intent and purpose of this
          Act  and  the  regulations, the Director  may
          appoint  a referee from the panel of referees
          who shall hold a hearing.  [Emphasis added.]

[35]  By  its  terms,  the decision of the  employment  standards
officer under s. 69(1) is tentative and not intended to be  final
or  binding.  The officer is merely acting in an investigatory or
screening  role.  The final decision-maker is the referee:   see,
for example, Re Samuel, Son & Co. (12 May 1995), Dec. No. ESC. 95-
92  (O.E.S.D.) (Referee Wacyk).  Where, however, the officer  has
made  an order under s. 65, or refused to make an order under  s.
67,  that  decision is final for the purposes of  issue  estoppel
and, subject to review either at the instance of the employee  or
the  employer, is binding on the parties.  In this case,  neither
party appealed the decision.  Thus, Ms Burke’s decision was final
for the purposes of issue estoppel.


[36] I would also note that had Ms Burke decided in favour of the
appellant  with  respect to the commission,  and  made  an  order
accordingly,  that order would have been final and  binding  upon
the employer pursuant to s. 65(7) unless the employer applied for
review  of the order in accordance with s. 68.  The Act does  not
suggest  any  policy basis for considering that once an  employee
has  invoked the Act in a dispute with the employer the  employee
should  be able to seek relief outside the Act that would not  be
available to the employer.


[37]  Judges  in  the  General Division  have  reached  the  same
conclusion.   In  Heynen  v. Frito-Lay  Canada  Ltd.  (1997),  32
C.C.E.L. (2d) 183 (Ont. Gen. Div.), MacPherson J. held at p.  191
that  a  final  decision is one that conclusively determines  the
matter  between  the parties.  He found that the decision  of  an
employment  standards  officer denying the employee’s  claim  was
final and he applied issue estoppel. There, as in this case,  the
employee failed to exercise her statutory right of appeal.  To  a
similar  effect  is the decision of Stortini J.  in  Dubreuil  v.
Dubreuil Forest Products Ltd. (1995), 12 C.C.E.L. (2d) 127  (Ont.
Gen. Div.).  I agree with those decisions.4
          
          (2) A Judicial Decision

[38] The final decision of an administrative body cannot form the
basis for issue estoppel if it is not also judicial.  In Rasanen,
a  majority  of  the court was satisfied that the decision  of  a
referee  under the Act was sufficiently “judicial” to  meet  this
requirement.    It  seems  to  me  that  not  all  administrative
decisions,  even those that finally determine the rights  of  the
parties,  will necessarily meet the judicial decision requirement
of  issue estoppel.  In this area, as with other aspects of issue
estoppel,  there is a significant policy component.  The  private
and  public  interests in putting an end to  litigation  must  be
balanced with the importance of ensuring fairness to the parties.
Lord  Upjohn summarized some of these concerns at p. 573  of  his
speech in Carl-Zeiss-Stiftung:
          
          [T]here may be many reasons why a litigant in
          the earlier litigation has not pressed or may
          even  for  good  reasons  have  abandoned   a
          particular issue.  It may be most  unjust  to
          hold him precluded from raising that issue in
          subsequent  litigation …  All  estoppels  are
          not  odious but must be applied so as to work
          justice  and not injustice, and I think  that
          the  principle  of  issue  estoppel  must  be
          applied   to   the   circumstances   of   the
          subsequent    case   with   this   overriding
          consideration in mind.  [Emphasis added.]

[39]  Trial  courts  have  expressed concerns  similar  to  those
expressed by Lord Upjohn.  For example, in Randhawa v. Everest  &
Jennings  Canadian Ltd. (1996), 22 C.C.E.L. (2d)  19  (Ont.  Gen.
Div.)  affirmed  November 21, 1997 (Ont. Gen.  Div.)  at  p.  25,
Sharpe J. suggested that it may be unfair to apply issue estoppel
where  the  party had only a limited opportunity to  present  the
case   because  the  immediate  practical  consequences  of   the
administrative decision were relatively trivial:
          
          I   note  finally  that  there  may  well  be
          situations where one would hesitate to  apply
          the  doctrine of issue estoppel where a party
          participated  in  an  administrative  hearing
          having  insignificant  consequences  and  the
          result of that hearing was then raised  later
          in  a  suit  which had enormous consequences.
          It  may be that in such a situation one would
          have  to consider carefully whether the party
          had  an  adequate opportunity to present  its
          case.

[40]  The most important consideration in determining whether  to
recognize a prior administrative decision as "judicial"  must  be
the  process  through which the decision was  reached.   Judicial
process  is  not  simply an end in itself – the process  is  also
intended to maximize the accuracy of the result.  Fundamental  to
fair judicial process is the opportunity to state one's case  and
respond  to  the  opponent's argument.  When  the  decision-maker
entertains only one side of the argument, mistakes are  bound  to
be made.  The caution to apply issue estoppel with circumspection
issued by Morden A.C.J.O. in Rasanen demonstrates sensitivity  to
this concern.  It is worth repeating the statement from p. 295 of
his reasons:
          
          I   do   not  exclude  the  possibility  that
          deficiencies in the procedure relating to the
          first decision could properly be a factor  in
          deciding  whether  or  not  to  apply   issue
          estoppel.

[41] I do not intend to attempt to set out a list of factors that
put  the  earlier decision on the judicial end of  administrative
decision  making.  Obviously, the realistic opportunity to  state
one’s  case is crucial.  Aside from that, one can obtain guidance
from the principles of administrative law and the requirements of
natural justice.


[42]   In   decisions  such  as  Nicholson  v.  Haldimand-Norfolk
(Regional)  Police  Commissioners,  [1979]  1  S.C.R.   311   and
Martineau  v.  Matsqui Institution Disciplinary Board,  [1980]  1
S.C.R. 602, the Supreme Court has attempted to reduce the need to
categorize  decisions as judicial, quasi-judicial, or ministerial
by  holding that tribunal decisions affecting rights are at least
required to act fairly.  Nevertheless, the categorization may  be
necessary  in  some contexts either because of the wording  of  a
statute5  or, as in this case, because of the requirements  of  a
common  law doctrine.  The common law doctrine of issue  estoppel
only  applies  where  the decision that is  said  to  create  the
estoppel  is judicial.  Thus, in this context, the categorization
is important.


[43] Whether an administrative decision is one required by law to
be  made  on a judicial basis “will depend in large measure  upon
the  legislative intention”.  Even where the enabling statute  is
silent, the courts will supply the legislative omission “in order
to  give  such procedural protection as will achieve justice  and
equity without frustrating all parliamentary will as reflected in
the  legislation”:  Canada  (Minister  of  National  Revenue)  v.
Coopers and Lybrand, [1979] 1 S.C.R. 495 at 503.


[44]  It  remains  to  consider whether the employment  standards
officer's  decision  is  a judicial decision.   In  my  view,  Re
Downing  and  Graydon et al. (1978), 21 O.R. (2d) 292  (C.A.)  is
determinative of this issue.


[45]  In  Downing, a majority of this court held that  employment
standards officers are required to act judicially.  The Act then,
as  now,6 expressly provided that Part I of the Statutory  Powers
Procedure  Act, 1971 (Ont.) c. 47 does not apply to the  exercise
of  any  power conferred on the employment standards officers  by
sections  65  and 67(1) or (2). Unlike referees and adjudicators,
officers are not expressly required to hold hearings. Blair J.A.,
with  whom  Wilson J.A. substantially agreed, held at  pp.  305-6
that the officers nevertheless exercise a judicial power:
          
          In the present case, the employment standards
          officers have the power to adjudicate as well
          as  to  investigate.  Their investigation  is
          made  for the purpose of providing them  with
          information  on  which to base  the  decision
          they must make.  The duties of the employment
          standards  officers embrace all the important
          indicia  of the exercise of a judicial  power
          including  the  ascertainment of  facts,  the
          application of the law to those facts and the
          making  of  a decision which is binding  upon
          the   parties.    Whatever  the   forms   and
          procedures  followed, there is no doubt  that
          this  process  is  adjudicative  because   it
          interprets the law and declares the rights of
          parties  in the manner described by  S.A.  de
          Smith  in  Judicial Review of  Administrative
          Action, 3rd ed. (1973), at pp. 72-3:
               
               A  typical  lis inter parties culminates
               in  a  decision by a tribunal  resolving
               any  disputed questions of law or  fact;
               the  legal  issues  are  determined   by
               reference   to  principles   and   rules
               already  in being.  A tribunal or  other
               deciding body is therefore likely to  be
               held to be acting in a judicial capacity
               when,     after    investigation     and
               deliberation,  it  determines  an  issue
               conclusively by the application of a pre-
               existing legal rule or another objective
               legal standard to the facts found by it.
               That    interpreting,   declaring    and
               applying   the  law  are  characteristic
               hallmarks  of the judicial  function  is
               too elementary a proposition to call for
               authoritative support.
          
          In  this  case,  it  is the establishment  of
          rights  and  liabilities as  between  parties
          which  is  crucial to the determination  that
          the   power   exercised  by  the   employment
          standards  officers  is judicial.   [Emphasis
          added.]

[46]  Blair J.A. first noted the developing doctrine of  fairness
applicable  to  the exercise of administrative powers  generally,
and then went on to describe the specific requirements of natural
justice  that  governed the exercise of this  judicial  power  at
pp. 307-8:
          
          The  authorities establish that the appellant
          was  not  only  entitled to a full  and  fair
          opportunity to make out her own case but also
          to  know the case against her and to meet and
          refute  that case.  In order to  do  so,  she
          should have been informed of the information,
          on  which  the  adverse decision  was  to  be
          based, and have been given a fair opportunity
          to   correct  or  contradict  any  which  was
          unfavourable to her position…

[47] Blair J.A. held that these requirements could be met without
the  formalities of a full-scale hearing or other  time-consuming
procedures that could defeat the statute’s purpose of giving  the
employee a speedy and effective remedy.  As Justice Blair pointed
out,  officers are given exceptional powers to assist in reaching
an   expeditious  decision,  including  powers  to  inspect   and
otherwise  gather evidence in the absence of the  employee.   The
officer  must,  however, “advise the complainant  of  information
discovered   in   the  inquiry  which  is  prejudicial   to   the
complainant’s  interest …  In simple terms, the  complainant  has
the right not only to complain but also to reply.”: p. 312.


[48]  Wilson  J.A. reached a similar conclusion  and  would  have
imposed additional duties on the officer.  Concurring with  Blair
J.A., Wilson J.A. stated at p. 314:
          
          These    things   [the   results    of    the
          investigation and the factual findings]  they
          … certainly should have disclosed to her, and
          disclosed  them  to her in advance  of  their
          adjudication on the merits of her  claim,  in
          order  to  give  her  a real  opportunity  to
          controvert,  if  she  could,  these   factual
          findings.

[49]  In the present case, counsel for the appellant argued  that
Downing  no  longer properly describes the role of the employment
standards  officers because of statutory amendments.7  Under  the
Act  as  it  stood when Downing was decided, while  the  employer
could appeal to a referee who was required to hold a hearing, the
employee  had  no  similar right of appeal.  The employee’s  only
right  of review was to have another employment standards officer
review the case.  The present legislation permits the employee to
apply  for  a review by an adjudicator who, like the referee,  is
required  to hold a hearing.  In my view, the amendments  to  the
legislation which give employees and employers access to  similar
appeal  procedures  do not indicate an intention  to  remove  the
requirement  that  the  officer  act  judicially.   Rather,   the
amendments simply remove an element of apparent inequality in the
appeal process.


[50]  Accordingly,  I am satisfied that the employment  standards
officer’s decision under s. 67 is a judicial decision.
          
          (3)  Effect of procedural unfairness

[51]  Counsel  for  the appellant argues that Ms  Burke  did  not
comply  with the requirements laid down by this court in  Downing
and  therefore  failed to act judicially. Relying  on  procedural
defects  and upon the excerpt from the reasons of Morden A.C.J.O.
in Rasanen quoted in paragraph 25 above, counsel argues that this
court  should not recognize the officer's decision as  “judicial”
for  the  purposes of issue estoppel.  On the other hand, counsel
for  the  respondent  argued that there  was  no  defect  in  the
process.  He submitted that even though Ms Burke did not  provide
the  appellant with the employer’s submissions contained  in  the
June 1, 1994 letter, the appellant was aware of the substance  of
the  materials  and  the employer's position  from  her  previous
meetings and correspondence with the employer.  Counsel  for  the
appellant denied that this was factually correct and argued  that
the appellant was not aware of several matters referred to in the
June 1, 1994 letter.


[52]  I  need  not  resolve this factual dispute.   The  majority
judgment in Downing makes it clear that it is not sufficient that
the  employee may be aware of the evidence if she is nevertheless
denied  the  opportunity  to respond  to  it.   The  officer  was
required  to give the appellant access to, and an opportunity  to
refute, any information gathered by the officer in the course  of
her  investigation that was prejudicial to the appellant’s claim.
At a minimum, the appellant was entitled to a copy of the June 1,
1994  letter  and a summary of any other information gathered  in
the  course  of  the  investigation that was prejudicial  to  her
claim.   She was also entitled to a fair opportunity to  consider
and  reply  to  that information.  The appellant was  denied  the
opportunity  to know the case against her and have an opportunity
to  meet  it:  Ms  Burke  failed  to  act  judicially.   In  this
particular  case,  this  failure does not,  however,  affect  the
operation of issue estoppel.


[53]   Employment   standards  officers  are  required   to   act
judicially.  The fact that in a particular case the officer fails
to  do so should not preclude the operation of issue estoppel, at
least   where  there  is  an  avenue  of  appeal.    The   policy
considerations  underlying two rules relating  to  administrative
law  persuade me to reach this conclusion.  These two rules  are:
(1)  that the discretionary remedies of judicial review  will  be
refused where an adequate alternative remedy exists; and (2)  the
rule  against collateral attack.  These rules, in effect, require
that the parties pursue their remedies through the administrative
process established by the legislature.  Where an appeal route is
available  the  parties will not be permitted  to  ignore  it  in
favour of the court process.


[54] The adequate alternative remedy rule was first enunciated by
the Supreme Court in Re Harelkin and University of Regina (1979),
96  D.L.R. (3d) 14 (S.C.C.) and subsequently followed:  see,  for
example, Canadian Pacific Ltd. v. Matsqui Indian Band (1995), 122
D.L.R. (4th) 129 (S.C.C.).


[55]  Harelkin is particularly relevant to this case since there,
as here, the applicant had not been afforded natural justice.  In
Harelkin,   the   applicant  was  ordered   by   the   university
administration to discontinue his studies.  The applicant  sought
a  hearing before a committee to review that decision.  Under the
University  of  Regina Act, 1974, 1973-74 (Sask.),  c.  119  that
committee  was  obligated to “hear and decide”  the  issue.   The
courts held that this power was quasi-judicial in nature and  the
committee  had  a  statutory  duty  to  hear  the  student.   The
committee  admittedly failed to do so.  It met without notice  to
the student and heard only the submissions of the administration.
It  confirmed  the decision requiring the student to  discontinue
his  studies.  The legislation provided for a right of appeal  to
the university senate, which was also required to hear and decide
the issue.  The student, however, applied for judicial review  of
the  committee’s decision by way of an application for certiorari
and mandamus to the superior court.


[56]  Beetz J., speaking for the majority of the court, held that
judicial  review  should be refused even though the  student  had
been  denied  natural justice.  He found that  the  discretionary
remedies  of  certiorari and mandamus will be  refused  where  an
adequate alternative remedy exists.  Beetz J. was satisfied  that
the  appeal  to the senate provided the student with an  adequate
alternative remedy.  In applying the doctrine, Beetz J. noted, at
p.  54,  that the appeal was “a more convenient remedy  for  [the
applicant]  as well as for the university in terms of  costs  and
expeditiousness.”  He also expressed the concern, at p. 55,  that
the  courts “should not use their discretion to promote delay and
expenditure  unless there is no other way to  protect  a  right”.
These  policy considerations are also applicable to  the  present
appeal.


[57]  In Susan Shoe Industries Ltd. v. Ricciardi (1994), 18  O.R.
(3d) 660 (C.A.), this court applied this doctrine with respect to
this  very legislation.  In Susan Shoe Industries Ltd., the court
held  that an employer was precluded from seeking judicial review
of  the  decision of an employment standards officer rather  than
applying for a review by a referee under the Act.  At p. 662 this
court held:
          
                 The  intention  of  the  Act  is  that
          questions of entitlement under the Act are to
          be  determined  by  specialized  officers  or
          referees  established  under  the  Act.   The
          courts  ought  not  to interfere  before  the
          procedures under the Act have been exhausted,
          except in exceptional circumstances, which do
          not exist in this case ….
          
                A  party in proceedings under the  Act,
          absent  exceptional circumstances, ought  not
          be  permitted to come directly to the courts.
          Put  in positive terms, the review procedures
          established  in the Employment Standards  Act
          should generally be followed to a conclusion.
          In  this way the intended standards of review
          of  decisions of Employment Standards officer
          and,    where   applicable,   of   Employment
          Standards   referee   will   be   maintained.
          [Emphasis added.]

[58]  The  same  considerations suggest that the failure  of  the
employment standards officer in this case to carry out  her  duty
to  act  judicially is not a basis for refusing  to  apply  issue
estoppel.    The  appellant  had  access  to  a  convenient   and
expeditious  remedy by way of an appeal to an  adjudicator.8   To
allow  the  appellant to relitigate the same issue in the  courts
would  not only promote delay and expenditure but would undermine
the  integrity of the legislative scheme established to deal with
these very issues.


[59] The most recent consideration of the rule against collateral
attack  is  in R. v. Consolidated Maybrun Mines Ltd. (1998),  123
C.C.C.  (3d)  449 (S.C.C.).  The accused in Consolidated  Maybrun
were  charged with violating a director’s order made under s.  17
of  the  Environmental Protection Act, R.S.O. 1980, c. 141.   The
accused  sought  to  defend the charges on  the  basis  that  the
Director  did  not  have  the requisite reasonable  and  probable
grounds  to make the order.  The accused had not taken  advantage
of  the procedure under the Act that provides for an appeal of  a
Director’s order to an expert tribunal, the Environmental  Appeal
Board.   Speaking for the court, L’Heureux-DubJ J. held  that  it
was  not open to the accused to defend the quasi-criminal  charge
by a collateral attack on the Director’s order.


[60]  While there are considerations that underlie the collateral
attack  doctrine  that  are not necessarily  the  same  as  those
underlying issue estoppel, at p. 463 L’Heureux-DubJ J. made  this
observation, in relation to the rule of law:
          
          The rule of law viewed, in particular, as the
          submission  of  the executive branch  to  the
          authority of the law, is clearly an essential
          component  of  our constitutional  structure.
          This  principle requires that it be  open  to
          concerned  citizens to bring the excesses  of
          government  to the attention of  the  courts,
          especially   where   penal   sanctions    are
          involved… However, the rule of law  does  not
          imply  that  the procedures for achieving  it
          can  be  disregarded, nor does it necessarily
          empower  an  individual to apply to  whatever
          forum  he  or she wishes in order to  enforce
          compliance with it.  [Emphasis added.]

[61]   L’Heureux-DubJ  J.  noted  the  role  and  importance   of
administrative structures in the organization of various  sectors
of contemporary society.  She then held, at pp. 463-64 that:
          
          to    ensure   the   integrity    of    these
          administrative structures, while at the  same
          time   seeking  to  protect  the  rights   of
          individuals  affected by government  actions,
          the  legislature is free to set  up  internal
          mechanisms  and establish appropriate  forums
          to  enable  such individuals to assert  their
          rights.

[62]  Similar  considerations apply here.  Under  the  Employment
Standards  Act, the legislature has established an administrative
body  that is required to act judicially and whose decisions  are
subject  to  a  convenient,  effective  and  expeditious   review
including a hearing before an expert tribunal.  The courts should
be loath to undermine the utility and integrity of that system by
allowing   the   individual  to  abandon  the  statutory   review
mechanism.   That, however, would be the effect of allowing  this
appeal: an employee having elected to file a complaint under  the
Act  but  being  dissatisfied with the result of that  procedure,
rather than pursuing an appeal, could simply ignore that decision
and start all over again in the courts.


[63]  I  also  note that in his dissenting reasons  in  Harelkin,
Dickson J., at p. 29, makes the following distinction between two
different types of remedial capacity:
          
          The  capacity of the remedial body may be  of
          importance.  Where the body which  may  grant
          the  remedy  exercises original  jurisdiction
          (King v. University of Saskatchewan (1969), 6
          D.L.R. (3d) 120, [1969] S.C.R. 678, 68 W.W.R.
          745; Re Chromex Nickel Mines Ltd. (1970),  16
          D.L.R. (3d) 273, [1971] 1 W.W.R. 163 sub nom.
          Re  Hretchka  et  al and Chromex  Investments
          Ltd. [appeal quashed 19 D.L.R. (3d) 1, [1972]
          S.C.R.  119,  [1972] 1 W.W.R. 561,  sub  nom.
          Hretchka  et  al.  v. A.-G.  B.C.  et  al.]),
          perhaps even hearing the matter de novo,  the
          remedy  will  be  more  often  perceived   as
          adequate, even conceivably in cases of denial
          of  natural justice.  On the other hand,  the
          normal sort of purely appellate function will
          rarely  be seen as capable of curing a breach
          of natural justice.

[64] In the analysis which Dickson J. then develops, he states at
p.  31  that, in the circumstances of the case before the  court,
"an  appeal is simply not a sufficient remedy for the failure  to
do  justice  in  the first place".  He contrasts  this  with  the
situation  in the King case where the appeal, as he  puts  it  at
p. 30, "amounted to a rehearing from the beginning".


[65]  In  the  present case, under s. 67(3)  the  adjudicator  is
required to "hold a hearing" and the section provides as follows:
          
          The adjudicator who is conducting the hearing
          may with necessary modifications exercise the
          powers  conferred on an employment  standards
          officer under this Act and may make an  order
          with  respect to the refusal or an  order  to
          amend,  rescind or affirm the  order  of  the
          employment standards officer.

[66]  Thus, in such a review the employee would be entitled to  a
full  hearing of the kind apparently contemplated by  Dickson  J.
This  consideration provides further support for  the  conclusion
that  the  review  process provided in the  Act  is  an  adequate
alternative  remedy and thus issue estoppel should be  considered
to apply.


[67]  I  wish  to  make one final observation.  Counsel  for  the
appellant  seemed  to  place some weight on  the  fact  that  the
employment  standards  officer is a  civil  servant  whereas  the
referees  and  adjudicators are appointed by the Minister.   With
vague allusions to the hiring and promotion procedures under  the
Public  Service Act, counsel argued that the officers’  decisions
were  therefore entitled to less deference by the courts.  In  my
view,  there  is no merit to this submission.  This province  has
the  advantage  of a professional and politically  neutral  civil
service.  Some of these public servants have been given important
decision-making  powers.  Where, as in  this  case,  the  officer
exercises  a  function  that  is primarily  adjudicative  she  is
expected  to comply with the standard of impartiality  applicable
to  courts.   Her  conduct must be such that there  could  be  no
reasonable  apprehension  of bias with regard  to  her  decision:
Newfoundland   Telephone   Co.   v.   Newfoundland   (Board    of
Commissioners  of Public Utilities) (1992), 89 D.L.R.  (4th)  289
(S.C.C.) at 299.


[68]  Employment standards officers may not have the security  of
tenure of judges and their method of appointment may be different
from  those of referees or adjudicators, but I do not  see  those
circumstances  as  a basis for refusing to give  full  effect  to
their  decisions.   The  principles of  natural  justice  do  not
require   every  administrative  tribunal  with  an  adjudicative
function to have the degree of independence required for  courts:
Committee  for  Justice  and Liberty v. Canada  (National  Energy
Board)  (1976), 68 D.L.R. (3d) 716 (S.C.C.) at 736; R. v. Valente
(1985),  24 D.L.R. (4th) 161 (S.C.C.) at 175 and Canadian Pacific
Ltd. v. Matsqui Indian Band (1995), 122 D.L.R. (4th) 129 (S.C.C.)
at 155-59.


[69]  In  summary, Ms Burke did not accord this appellant natural
justice.   The  appellant's  recourse  was  to  seek  review   of
Ms  Burke's  decision.  She failed to do so.   That  decision  is
binding upon her and her employer.
          
          DISPOSITION

[70] Accordingly, I would dismiss the appeal with costs.
