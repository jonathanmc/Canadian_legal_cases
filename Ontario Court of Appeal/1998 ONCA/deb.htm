McKINLAY J.A.:



[1]   This is an appeal by Bivash Deb of the dismissal, following
trial,  of  his  claims against insurers American Life  Insurance
Company  and  American Life Home Assurance  Company  for  payment
under  a  disability  policy issued  by  them  and  for  punitive
damages.


SUMMARY OF FACTS

[2]   In July of 1990, the appellant purchased a Lincoln Town Car
for use as a limousine, primarily for chauffeuring passengers  to
and from Pearson International Airport. The vehicle was purchased
under a conditional sales contract from Bay Lincoln Mercury Sales
Limited  ("Bay Lincoln"), with financing arranged by Bay  Lincoln
with  Ford  Credit  Canada Limited ("Ford Credit"),  title  being
retained  by  Bay  Lincoln pending full payment of  the  purchase
price. The conditional sales contract was assigned by Bay Lincoln
to Ford Credit.


[3]   The appellant took possession of the vehicle, obtained  the
usual  automobile  insurance from Continental  Insurance  Company
("Continental"), and obtained motor vehicle registration  in  the
name of a limousine fleet operator. He operated the vehicle as  a
licensed  carrier until a motor vehicle accident on February  14,
1991 resulted in the virtual destruction of the vehicle.


[4]  The conditional sales contract between the plaintiff and the
dealer,  assigned  by the dealer to Ford Credit  Canada  Limited,
provided  for  a  balance  to  be  financed  in  the  amount   of
$28,000.00,  plus  a  premium for life and  disability  insurance
purchased  by  the  appellant in the amount of $1,852.02,  and  a
registration  fee  of  $20,  for  a  total  amount  financed   of
$29,872.02.  The  total  balance  due,  including  the  cost   of
borrowing,  was  payable in thirty-six instalments  of  $1,076.20
each.


[5]   In  return for the premium paid in the amount of $1,852.02,
the  respondent American Life Insurance Company ("ALICO")  issued
to the appellant Certificate D2182407, which provided for payment
in  the  event of the appellant's total disability in the monthly
amount  of  $999.00. Under an earlier certificate issued  to  the
appellant  by ALICO, the monthly amount was $1,076.00, the  exact
amount  of the appellant's monthly instalment payments under  the
conditional   sales  contract;  this  earlier   certificate   was
cancelled  and  replaced  by  Certificate  D2182407  because  the
appellant  disclosed  in his application that  he  suffered  from
diabetes,  and  therefore could not satisfy ALICO's  requirements
for policies providing monthly benefits of $1,000.00 or more.


[6]   The  trial judge found that the accident resulted in  total
disability  of the appellant, as defined in the ALICO certificate
of insurance, for a period of at least 24 months.


[7]   Four  days after the accident, the appellant  notified  Bay
Lincoln,  which  acted as agent for ALICO, of the  event.  Within
another   four  days  he  notified  it  of  his  disability.   He
subsequently completed claim forms at its request.


[8]   ALICO denied the appellant's claim by letter dated  May  7,
1991,  72 days after its Cleveland office received written notice
of the claim.


[9]   The  appellant submitted a claim to his automobile insurer,
Continental  Insurance Company, in respect of the total  loss  of
his  vehicle, and signed a proof of loss in connection  with  the
claim.  The appellant's evidence was that he believed himself  to
be the owner of the vehicle for this purpose, and does not recall
reading the form before signing. The proof of loss was eventually
completed in three different colours of ink, and another person B
the  president of the limousine fleet operator in whose name  the
vehicle  was  registered and through which the vehicle  insurance
arranged B was also a signatory.


[10] The cheque in payment of the Continental claim was made  out
in  favour of three individuals - the insured named in the policy
(which  was the limousine service company), the registered  owner
of the automobile (who owned the license to operate the limousine
in and out of the airport), and the appellant.


[11]  The  trial judge noted in his reasons that in his proof  of
loss  to Continental, the appellant stated that he was the  owner
of  the vehicle and that there were no liens against it. There is
no  doubt  that  the appellant did sign the proof  of  loss  form
stating  that  there were no liens and also no conditional  sales
agreements  on the vehicle. Strangely, that proof  of  loss  form
does  not  state that the appellant is the owner of the  vehicle.
The  printed  form  does say that the vehicle  is  owned  by  the
insured, but the line on the form for the name of the insured  is
blank.  It  is fairly clear that at least three different  people
were  involved in filling out the proof of loss form. In my view,
nothing  much can be drawn from the appellant's oral evidence  as
to  his opinion of ownership, but certainly nothing at all can be
drawn  from the proof of loss form other than the denial  of  any
liens or conditional sales agreements on the vehicle.


[12]  Because  the appellant had purchased the vehicle  from  Bay
Lincoln  under a conditional sales contract which reserved  title
to the vendor, and because the automobile registration was in the
name  of  the limousine fleet operator, the trial judge  did  not
accept  the evidence of the appellant that he thought himself  to
be the owner of the automobile.


[13] The appellant's understanding and expectations were that  he
was  to use the proceeds of the Continental policy to replace the
vehicle  destroyed  in  the accident and that  ALICO  would  make
payments to carry the Ford Credit Canada Limited financing  while
he remained disabled.


[14] Continental paid the sum of $27,024.00 to the appellant, who
took  the funds to the defendant dealer and attempted to purchase
another  Lincoln  Town car to replace the one  destroyed  in  the
accident.  For  reasons unknown to the appellant,  the  defendant
dealer refused to sell the appellant a replacement vehicle.


[15] In dismissing the action against ALICO, the trial judge held
that  the  Continental  proceeds should have  been  used  by  the
appellant  to satisfy the debt owing under the conditional  sales
contract.


ISSUES ON THE APPEAL

[16] Only two issues were dealt with on appeal:


     1.   Was  the  appellant  entitled to have  payment  of  the
          disability  benefits provided under  the  ALICO  policy
          made   to   Ford   Credit  and  applied   against   his
          indebtedness?

     2.   Should  punitive damages have been awarded against  the
          respondent?

1.   Entitlement to Disability Payments

     (a)  The characteristics of the ALICO and of the Continental
insurance

[17] The term of the ALICO insurance was concurrent with the term
of  the  conditional sales contract between Bay Lincoln  and  the
appellant - both being for three years commencing from  the  date
the  vehicle  was  purchased. ALICO's  certificate  of  insurance
provided for payment of the monthly indemnity benefit not to  the
appellant,  but to the "Creditor Policyholder", i.e. Ford  Credit
as assignee of Bay Lincoln. The purpose of the payment was stated
in  the  certificate  to be "to reduce or extinguish  the  unpaid
indebtedness  of  the Insured". On its face  the  certificate  is
entitled   "Creditor's   Group   Life"   and   "Accident-Sickness
Coverage",  and  includes the apparent advertisement,  "Peace  of
Mind with Payment Guard".


[18]  The  certificate defines "total disability" and, as  stated
earlier  in  these  reasons,  the  trial  judge  found  that  the
appellant   was  totally  disabled  within  the  terms   of   the
certificate for a period of at least 24 months.


[19] There can be no doubt that this insurance was not to be paid
to  the  appellant,  but was to give him, and also  Ford  Credit,
"peace  of mind" that the debt attached to the vehicle  would  be
paid  by  ALICO  during  any period of total  disability  of  the
appellant. It would have been paid whether or not the vehicle was
destroyed,  and even if the appellant's disability  had  resulted
from a cause completely unrelated to the vehicle.


[20]  The  Continental  insurance was standard  vehicle  accident
insurance,  providing  liability  and  collision  coverage.   Its
purpose was to provide funds to repair or replace the vehicle  in
the  event  of  damage or destruction. The insured value  of  the
automobile,  less  the $3,000 deductible,  was  received  by  the
appellant from Continental on a timely basis.


     (b)  Purpose of Policies

[21] The position of the respondent on this issue is, simply put,
that  the  appellant was obliged to apply the  payments  made  by
Continental  to  a  reduction  of  the  amount  owing  under  the
conditional  sales  contract and that, had that  been  done,  the
indebtedness  to Ford Credit would have been fully  extinguished,
and  any  liability of ALICO to pay disability  benefits  to  the
appellant terminated.


[22] If ALICO is correct that the appellant was required to remit
the  Continental payment to Ford Credit, then there is  no  doubt
that the liability of ALICO under its certificate would have been
extinguished,  because the proceeds were approximately  equal  to
the balance owing to Ford Credit and the termination provision on
the certificate reads:


          Insurance     hereunder    shall    terminate
          automatically  upon  the  earliest   of   the
          following dates: 1) the date the indebtedness
          is fully discharged ....

[23]  The  appellant takes the position that he paid premiums  on
two  different types of insurance - one for repair or replacement
of  his vehicle in the event of damage or destruction, and one to
protect  against  the necessity of paying under  the  conditional
sales  contract in the event of his death or disability.  In  his
view, the one has nothing to do with the other, and he should  be
entitled  to benefits under both for the purpose for  which  each
type of insurance was purchased.


[24]  The  amount paid on the Continental insurance was equal  to
the  value of the automobile at the time of the accident less the
$3,000 deductible. According to the factum of the respondent, the
amount  of  the  debt  owed to Ford Credit on  the  date  of  the
Continental payment was only a few hundred dollars less than  the
amount of that cheque. Therefore, for the sake of discussion,  we
can  assume  that  the amounts were equal.  Thus,  prior  to  the
accident, the appellant had the vehicle which he used to earn his
livelihood, he owed a debt for the purchase of that vehicle in an
amount equal to the value of the vehicle, and he used the vehicle
to  generate  income.  If the respondent is  correct,  after  the
accident the appellant would have no vehicle with which to earn a
living, the insurance for the value of the vehicle would be  paid
to  Ford  Credit to reduce the amount of the debt  to  zero,  and
because the purchase money debt would be satisfied, he would have
no disability insurance available from ALICO. If the appellant is
correct,  after the accident he would have money to  replace  the
vehicle  from the Continental payment, he would have a  debt  for
the  unpaid purchase price of the vehicle, and he would have  the
disability  insurance  from ALICO to satisfy  that  debt  to  the
extent of $999 for a minimum of 24 months.


[25] The trial judge held that the position of the respondent was
the correct one. Before reviewing the reasons of the trial judge,
I  wish  to  point out a strange result of his decision.  If  the
accident   had   resulted   in  damage  which   was   repairable,
Continental, in all likelihood, would have paid for the repair of
the vehicle, and the appellant, assuming a disability, would have
been  entitled  to  have the ALICO benefits  applied  during  his
disability  to  satisfy his payments under the conditional  sales
contract. The appellant would have been left in the same position
as  he  was in before the accident. It is only the fact that  the
vehicle  was  considered not repairable that  a  cheque  for  its
insured value was received by the appellant, thereby opening  the
way  for  ALICO's argument that the funds should have  been  paid
over  to  Ford Credit. It is strange that the appellant's  rights
under the ALICO insurance should depend on the extent rather than
the fact of accidental damage. Indeed, no accident is required to
trigger  the  disability payments under the ALICO insurance.  Had
the  appellant died or fallen ill and been unable to work,  ALICO
would have been liable to make payments under the policy to  Ford
Credit,  and the appellant or his estate would have had a vehicle
free of debt.


[26]  A  1994  decision  of  the  Ontario  Divisional  Court  was
concerned  with  a  somewhat similar issue -- Maidana  Estate  v.
Maplex General Insurance Co. (1994), 19 O.R. (3d) 85 (Div.  Ct.).
In that case, a deceased, whose estate was one of the plaintiffs,
died  in  a  motor vehicle accident in which his  automobile  was
demolished.  Under  a  standard liability policy,  the  defendant
insurer agreed to indemnify the deceased against accidental  loss
of  or  damage to the vehicle. The deceased had also purchased  a
life   insurance   policy  through  General   Motors   Acceptance
Corporation ("GMAC"), which financed the purchase of the vehicle.
The liability insurer prepared a cheque, payable to GMAC, for the
value  of  the  vehicle, but did not forward the  cheque  pending
receipt  of  a proof of loss. However, it later denied  liability
under the policy on the ground that the full value of the car had
been paid to GMAC under the life insurance policy and that to pay
under  the  liability  policy would result  in  double  recovery.
Speaking for the court on this issue, Southey J. stated at p.90:


          The  plaintiff is not disentitled to  recover
          from the defendant insurer because the policy
          was  for indemnification and GMAC had already
          been paid the value of the vehicle under  the
          policy  of  insurance that the  deceased  was
          required  to  obtain when  he  purchased  the
          vehicle.   That  policy  was  one   of   life
          insurance,  which would have been payable  on
          the  death of Mr. Maidana, regardless of  the
          cause  of death. If he had died in his sleep,
          with  the  vehicle sitting in  the  driveway,
          G.M.A.C.  would  have  been  paid  off.   The
          vehicle  would  then have been  part  of  his
          estate, and available for sale or use by  the
          persons entitled thereto. Those persons  have
          suffered  the  loss of the  vehicle  and  are
          entitled  to be indemnified under the  policy
          of insurance on the vehicle.



[27]  The trial judge seemed to be of the view that the appellant
was  attempting  in this case to effect double  recovery  -  once
under the policy with Continental and once under the policy  with
ALICO.  He  did not seriously consider the proposition  that  the
appellant  was  insured for two separate and distinct  hazards  -
loss  or damage of the vehicle, and his inability to service  the
Ford Credit debt because of disability.


     (c)  Additional factors considered by the trial judge




[28]  In addition to the basic facts recited above, other factors
--  some contractual and some factual -- were considered  by  the
trial judge in reaching his conclusion that the appellant was not
entitled to the disability coverage provided by ALICO.


[29] On the reverse side of a "Retail Instalment Contract" signed
by  the appellant and Bay Lincoln, the appellant assigned to  Bay
Lincoln  "the benefits of any agreements relating to the property
and  contract". Bay Lincoln assigned its rights under the  Retail
Instalment Contract to Ford Credit.


[30]  When the plaintiff arranged insurance with Continental,  he
apparently  did  not  disclose that  the  vehicle  was  financed.
Following  the  accident, he made his claim to  Continental.  The
adjuster's  log  book  contains the following  entry:  "Insd.  is
saying vehicle not leased. Outright owner". On his proof of  loss
claim  to  Continental, the appellant made  a  declaration  that,
"During the term of the policy there has been no other insurance,
valid  or  invalid,  on  the vehicle, and  no  person,  firm,  or
corporation,  other  than  the  Insured,  has  had  any  interest
therein,  and there is no lien, chattel mortgage, or  conditional
sales agreement thereon."


[31] On payment by Continental, the appellant did not immediately
replace  the  vehicle, although he did ask Bay  Lincoln  to  sell
another  vehicle  to him and they refused. He actually  used  the
monies for paying off other personal debts.


          (i)  The "Retail Instalment Contract"

[32]  The  provision  on the back side of the "Retail  Instalment
Contract", assigning "the benefits of any agreements relating  to
the  property and contract", which the appellant did  not  recall
seeing  or  reading,  is clearly binding  on  him.  However,  the
question  is  whether "the benefit of any agreements relating  to
the  property  and contract", which benefit was assigned  by  the
appellant  to  Bay  Lincoln and by Bay Lincoln  to  Ford  Credit,
included  the benefit of the Continental insurance,  and  if  so,
whether ALICO can claim the benefit of that assignment.


[33] There was no specific assignment of automobile insurance  in
either  of  the Bay Lincoln agreements. The trial judge expressed
surprise  at that fact, but considered that the general provision
assigning the benefit of agreements was sufficient to effect  the
same result.


[34] He stated at pp. 30 and 31 of his reasons:


          ...  it  is provided in the Retail Instalment
          Contract  that the buyer (Deb) was  to  "keep
          insurance for all risks on the property"  and
          that  the buyer (Deb) "assigns to the  seller
          the  benefits of any agreements  relating  to
          the property and the contract". In my opinion
          the  required property insurance would be  an
          "agreement relating to the property".

[35]  I  have some difficulty with that position - first, because
the assignment of all "benefits of any agreements relating to the
property and the contract" is found in paragraph 3 of "Terms  and
Conditions",  in  very small print on the  reverse  side  of  the
Retail  Instalment Contract. It is a vague and general  provision
inserted  by the drafter of a standard form contract, and  should
be  read  contra  proferentum. Paragraph  7  of  the  "Terms  and
Conditions" requires that the buyer keep insurance for all  risks
on  the  property,  and  also states  that  the  seller  may  buy
insurance and charge the buyer. It does not contain any  specific
words  of  assignment of insurance. The absence of  any  specific
assignment in the insurance provision itself, combined  with  the
fact  that  that provision provides for the seller to obtain  its
own  insurance,  certainly tends to obscure its  intent.  I  have
difficulty agreeing with the trial judge that the combination  of
paragraphs 3 and 7, neither of which refers to the other, amounts
to  an assignment in favour of Bay Lincoln of the benefits of the
buyer's insurance. Indemnity insurance contracts are personal  to
the  insured,  and without clear indication of an  assignment  of
interest  in  favour of another, the insured is entitled  to  the
proceeds for his own use.


[36]  In  any  event,  even if it could be  considered  that  the
appellant's coverage with Continental was assigned to Bay Lincoln
as a result of the provisions discussed above, it is my view that
given  the  purpose  of the insurance with Continental,  and  the
relationship  of  Bay  Lincoln with ALICO,  it  would  have  been
incumbent on Bay Lincoln, or its assignee Ford Credit,  to  apply
the  insurance proceeds to repair or replacement of the  vehicle,
rather than to satisfaction of the appellant's indebtedness.


[37]  Cases involving application of insurance proceeds to either
repair  or reduction of mortgage debt in general are not helpful,
since  results vary depending on the specific facts of each case,
or  the possible application of statute - the latter not being an
issue  in this case. However, it must be remembered that in  this
case  Bay  Lincoln acted as a de facto sales agent for  ALICO  in
obtaining the disability insurance. If it were available  to  Bay
Lincoln to apply the Continental proceeds to pay off the purchase
money  debt of the appellant, it would in fact be nullifying,  by
its  own act, the effect of disability insurance which it, as  de
facto agent, sold to the appellant.




[38] ALICO, on the other hand, had no interest in or right to the
Continental  proceeds. It had a duty, in return for  the  premium
paid  by  the appellant, to service the appellant's debt to  Ford
Credit in the event of and during his disability, whether through
illness or accident. Any right of ALICO to claim a termination of
its  insurance, depends on the payment in full of the debt of the
appellant  to Ford Credit; but, as stated above, Ford Credit  had
no  right  to apply the proceeds of the Continental insurance  in
this case in reduction of the appellant's debt to it and, in  any
case, it did not have the opportunity to do so.


[39]   ALICO is attempting to take advantage of rights which Ford
Credit,  as assignee of Bay Lincoln, might have had to apply  the
proceeds  of  the insurance to the debt owed by the appellant  to
it.  It  has  no  independent  rights  to  the  proceeds  of  the
Continental  insurance.  Given  the  trial  judge's  finding   of
disability  of  the appellant, and the fact that the  Continental
proceeds  were not actually paid over to Ford Credit and  applied
in  payment of the appellant's indebtedness, it is my  view  that
ALICO must satisfy the claim under the policy it provided.


          (ii) Misstatements of fact by the appellant

[40]  In  his application for insurance, the appellant apparently
did not inform Continental that the vehicle was financed. He did,
however, inform the insurer that another person owned the license
which  permitted the vehicle to be used as a limousine  servicing
Pearson International Airport, and also of the name of the  fleet
operator   with  whom  he  contracted.  The  cheque   issued   by
Continental following the accident was made out to all  three.  I
see no particular relevance to this action of those facts.


[41]  When  the appellant told the adjuster that the vehicle  was
not  leased, he was, of course, telling the truth. If he said  he
was  the  outright owner (and the evidence is not clear  that  he
did),  and  when  he signed the proof of claim  saying  that  the
vehicle was not subject to a conditional sales agreement, he  was
not  being truthful. However, those statements do not in any  way
affect  the results of this appeal. They resulted in no  loss  to
anyone. The issues involved are legal ones alone.


[42]  I  see  no  reason,  on the facts of  this  case,  why  the
appellant  should  be  deprived  of  the  benefit  of  the  ALICO
insurance.


2.   Punitive Damages

[43]  The trial judge stated that had he found that the appellant
was  entitled  to the disability insurance he would have  awarded
punitive damages as a result of the behaviour of the respondents,
basically  because  they provided insurance in this  jurisdiction
and  failed  to  deal  with  the claim  of  the  appellant  in  a
reasonable  manner,  given the nature of the insurance.  He  also
pointed out that they denied his disability, and alleged that  he
acted  fraudulently in making his claim. While I agree  with  the
comments  of the trial judge, I am of the view that the behaviour
of the respondent in this matter is more properly addressed by an
award  of  solicitor and client costs of the trial  and  of  this
appeal.


RESULT

[44]  I  would  allow the appeal, set aside the judgment  of  the
trial judge, and replace it with a judgment directing payment  by
the  defendant, ALICO, of disability benefits in  the  amount  of
$999.00  for a period of 24 months plus pre-judgment interest.  I
would order costs in favour of the appellant at trial and on  the
appeal on a solicitor and client basis.
