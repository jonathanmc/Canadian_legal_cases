McKINLAY J.A.:

[1]  This is an appeal by the father, David Stephen Wilson, of
the judgment of the Honourable Mr. Justice Walsh dated June 15,
1995, varying the divorce judgment of the Honourable Judge
Gotlib, dated October 4, 1988 by increasing the amount of child
support for the one child of the marriage, Sara Wilson, born July
22, 1980.


[2]  The parties were married on November 25, 1979, and separated
in November of 1984. The separation agreement between the parties
was effective as of January 1, 1985, but was not executed until
June of 1988. It was incorporated into the divorce judgment. It
provided that the parties have joint custody of the infant child,
with her residence to be alternated between the mother and father
upon an agreed basis. Subsequent to the commencement of the
application brought by the mother to vary the amount of support,
and shortly prior to the final arguments in the variation
proceedings, the arrangement was changed, on the recommendation
of the daughter's therapist, to provide for the infant child to
reside primarily with the mother.


[3]  At the time of the separation in 1984, the mother was
working as a freelance journalist, having left a teaching career
in 1982. When she commenced the application for variation, the
mother had voluntarily given up a reasonably lucrative career as
a journalist and author to undertake a new career in television
broadcasting, for which she had no previous experience or
training.


[4]  The separation agreement provided that the father pay the
sum of $850 per month for the maintenance of the infant child,
subject to an annual cost of living increase. It required that
the mother pay for specific expenses for the child -- the major
items being clothing, day care, dental and medical care, extra
curricular courses and recreational equipment. The mother was
further required to maintain a trust fund for the child and to
deposit therein the sum of $75 per month plus the monthly "baby
bonus" allowance. The agreement further required the father to
pay two-thirds and the mother one-third of fees for private
school, religious school, summer camp, orthodontia, and post
secondary education, subject to the proviso that the maximum
payment by the mother for camp not exceed $500 in any one summer
period. By the time the application was heard, the monthly amount
being paid by the father, as a result of the cost of living
increase, was $1,042.


[5]  There was no indication, or comment by the trial judge, that
he considered the original agreement, entered into with legal
advice, was other than fair at the time it was executed. However,
the result of his decision was to require the father to pay to
the mother $3,900 per month in before tax child support -- three
and three-quarters times the amount he was paying under the
original agreement.


[6]  There were a number of changes in the circumstances of both
the father and the mother between the execution of the original
agreement and the hearing of the application. The most important
of those changes were:


     i)   The mother


[7]  The mother, at the time of the hearing had sold her house
and purchased a condominium with a male friend with whom she
resided. He was also divorced, and had two children. He had an
income in excess of $62,000 per annum, and paid no child support.
He contributed $1,000 monthly to the respondent's household
expenses.


[8]  The appellant agreed that the costs of maintaining his
daughter had increased with her age.


[9]  There were two other important changes in circumstances
affecting the mother. First was the child's residence change to
live solely with her mother, except for every other weekend, and
arranged vacations. Second was the mother's decrease in personal
income as a result of her intentional choice of a new career.


     ii)  The father


[10] The father's income had not increased appreciably from the
time the agreement was executed until the time of the hearing.
Any increase had varied from year to year, but was approximately
5%. He had remarried, to a former Air Canada flight attendant,
and adopted an infant child. His new wife no longer worked, and
he provided the sole support for her and the child.


[11] Expenses of providing housing and maintenance for his
daughter during the time she spent with her father following the
new residence arrangements decreased only minimally. The decrease
would involve food consumed by her at her father's home, as well
as transportation and incidentals.


          
EFFECT OF THESE CHANGES ON QUANTUM OF CHILD SUPPORT


     i)   Cost to the mother of the residence change


[12] In arriving at his decision as to quantum of child support
to be paid by the father, the trial judge made no finding as to
the reasonableness of the mother's estimate of the amount
required, other than to say that the amount requested by her
"stretches even the limits of these new standards" -- referring
to the tendency of courts to increase substantially the amount of
child support awards which had, over time, drifted to the "low
end of any acceptable scale".


[13] The only reason given for the trial judge's decision that
the amount paid under the agreement was not sufficient appears to
have been the affidavit of the father to the effect that the
total child care expenses for the child on a monthly basis would
approximate $35,856 annually. Accepting that figure, he struck an
amount of $36,000 per annum (exclusive of special expenses such
as orthodontia, camp, counselling etc.), and required that the
husband pay two thirds of that amount, grossed up for tax
purposes to $3,900 per month.


[14] In making his decision based on the husband's figures, he
apparently did not consider the situation of the child and the
parents at the time that affidavit was sworn. At that time, the
child was spending equal amounts of time with each parent. The
expenses estimated by the father referred to expenses incurred at
each of the two homes in which the child resided for equal
periods of time. He actually estimated that the mother's
reasonable expenses would approximate $1,279.47 per month, and
his would approximate $1,708.80. Each set of expenses included
cost of housing as the major item of expense, and other items
such as a portion of the cost of automobiles, food, clothing and
other usual incidentals.


[15] The changed residence of the child was, by agreement, to be
one of the changed circumstances which the trial judge could look
to in making his decision. It is obvious that in making such a
change, the cost of the fathers' home and automobile did not
become an expense of the mother. Her home expenses would remain
the same. However, the trial judge does not appear to have
considered that the increase in expenses to the mother as a
result of having the child ten days more in each four week period
would not be substantial, as she was already providing, under the
original agreement, a home, an automobile. and other basic
expenses. The incremental expenses would involve food for the
additional ten days in each four week period, and other
incidental expenses for the same period.


     ii)  Savings to the father as a result of the residence
     change


[16] As with the mother, the most significant cost under the
original agreement was the cost of providing a home and an
automobile used for the child's transportation. Those expenses
would not change, although the father could no longer attribute
them to maintenance of the child of this marriage. As stated
above, the true savings would be only in food and incidental
expenses.


[17] In my view, the trial judge erred in accepting, as the basis
of his award, the estimate of the husband of expenses based on
the upkeep of two households, when the mother provides only one.


     iii) Mother's chosen change in career


[18] The appellant takes the position that the mother's decrease
in personal income results from her own choice in deciding on a
change of career from one which earned her a reasonable income to
one for which she was neither trained nor experienced, and that
his share of child care expenses should be based on her ability
to earn, and not on her preference in occupations. The law varies
substantially on this point. It is clear that the court will not
countenance an enhancement or maintaining of the life style of
the applicant in the guise of increased child maintenance.
However, if the change of occupation is reasonable in the
circumstances, a decreased income need not be fatal to a claim to
increased child support. The considerations are always fact
specific in these cases.


[19] In this case, the trial judge made no finding as to the
reasonableness or otherwise of the change. However, he was
clearly aware of all the facts, including the father=s new
marriage and child, and his apportionment of the child support
burden as two-thirds to the husband and one-third to the wife
appears to me to be entirely reasonable in the circumstances.


FEDERAL CHILD SUPPORT GUIDELINES


[20] While the new Federal Child Support Guidelines were not in
force at the time of this application, they did come into force
on May 1, 1997, just prior to the hearing of this appeal. Given
my view that the trial judge erred in his assessment of the
correct total cost of maintaining the child, I feel that the
guidelines are instructive.


[21] It is difficult to ascertain the income of the mother, given
the very recent nature of her change in occupation. However, a
very low estimate of her probable income would be $40,000. She
also obtains a monthly contribution of $1,000, tax free, from her
male companion. Grossing that amount up for tax purposes would
result in approximately an additional $19,000 per year, resulting
in an income of approximately $59,000. The husband's income is
approximately $165,000.


[22] If one were to apply the guidelines to the original
situation of full joint residential custody, s. 9 of the
guidelines would appear to apply. Applying the tables to the
incomes of each partner, the amount attributable to the mother
would be $500. The amount attributable to the husband would be
$1,208. The amount payable by the husband to the wife would be
$708 after tax. This would be approximately the same as an amount
of $1,350 to the wife, grossed up for tax purposes. This does
not, of course, take into account all of the factors required by
s. 9 to be considered in such a case, and it would not be
productive here to attempt the exercise. It would appear,
however, that the amount being paid under the original agreement
was on the low side.


[23] Applying the guidelines to the new residential arrangement
in this case, the payment required of the husband would be $1208
after tax per month. Grossed up for tax purposes this would
amount to approximately $2,400 per month.


[24] In my view, $2,400 per month, with income tax to be paid by
the wife, would be reasonable on the facts of this case. I would
set aside the judgment below, and replace it with a judgment with
monthly child support payments as stated above, but with all
other terms to remain unchanged.
