                     ENDORSEMENT


Downey's Conviction Appeal


[1]  Put at its best for the appellant Downey, but viewed
realistically, the evidence offered no air of reality for the
submission that Downey was acting in self-defence as defined in
s. 37 or s. 34(1) of the Criminal Code when he chased and tackled
the victim during the melee in which Downey, but not the victim,
was a participant. We note that Downey did not testify.


[2]  As we read the transcript, counsel for Downey did not ask
for a ruling as to the availability of any of the statutory self-
defence provisions in advance of addressing the jury. It follows
that it cannot be said that the judge's failure to provide that
ruling resulted in a miscarriage of justice. We note that counsel
for a co-accused did specifically ask for a ruling as to the
applicability of certain self-defence provisions before he
addressed the jury and the trial judge gave it to him.


[3]  The trial judge properly instructed the jury as to the law
applicable to liability under ss. 21(1)(b) and (c) of the
Criminal Code. He made the mens rea clear. Although it would have
been preferable had the trial judge gone on to specifically
relate those legal principles to the factual issues in this case,
we are satisfied that the charge and the review of the evidence,
considered in their entirety, made it clear to the jury that they
could only convict Downey if they were satisfied that his acts of
assistance to Watson (eg. procuring the knife or tackling the
victim) not only had the effect of assisting Watson in the
assault, but were performed for that purpose.


Watson's Conviction Appeal


[4]  The trial judge did not tell the jury that after-the-fact
conduct could not assist in determining whether Watson had the
intention to commit murder. It would have been preferable had he
given that specific instruction. He did, however, tell the jury
that the after-the-fact evidence could only be used to determine
whether the accused was attempting to "conceal his part" in the
crime. That instruction would direct the jury to the conduct
alleged against Watson and not to his mental state. Nowhere did
the trial judge suggest to the jury that the after-the-fact
conduct could be used as evidence of intent. Considered as a
whole, we find no error in the trial judge's instruction with
respect to after-the-fact conduct.


Downey's Sentence Appeal


[5]  The trial judge imposed a sentence of 8 years in addition to
21 months pre-trial custody. In doing so, we think he committed
three errors in principle. First, he erred in concluding that
Downey knew that Watson was a "user of knives" when Downey
assisted Watson in the assault of the victim. There was simply no
evidence from which that inference could be reasonably drawn.


[6]  Second, the trial judge erred in principle in totally
discounting Downey's age. Downey was very young (18) and, despite
a bad record, his age should have been given more consideration
than it was by the trial judge.


[7]  Finally, we are satisfied that the trial judge erred in
principle in failing to give effect to the objective basis of
Downey's culpability. Downey was convicted as an aider and
abettor and on the basis that he should have known Watson would
cause bodily harm during the assault. He was not found to have
intended any harm toward the victim. His culpability rested
primarily on his participation in the street fight.


[8]  Even though Downey has a bad criminal record and this is a
most serious offence, we are satisfied that the sentence is too
long. We think an appropriate sentence would be time served,
which is four and a half years. Taking into consideration the
time spent in custody prior to conviction, this amounts to a
sentence of about 7 years.


[9]  Downey is granted leave to appeal sentence, the appeal is
allowed and the sentence is varied in accordance with these
reasons.


Watson's Sentence Appeal


[10] The trial judge, notwithstanding the unanimous
recommendation of the jury that there be no increase in Watson's
parole ineligibility, imposed a period of parole ineligibility of
17 years. In doing so, he erred in principle in the following
respects. Assuming the evidence of the other untried outstanding
charges was admissible at all, we are satisfied that he should
have given no weight to the evidence concerning the "7-11
incident". That evidence was simply too unreliable to warrant
using it for anything. We are also satisfied that the trial judge
was wrong in using the evidence of the other two incidents to
draw the extreme conclusions he did as to Watson's character.


[11] We are also satisfied that the trial judge failed to give
adequate consideration to the jury's recommendation. The jury had
heard the evidence and although they had not heard about Watson's
criminal record (which was minor), we think they were in a good
position to assess Watson's involvement in this offence. Their
unanimous recommendation reflects that assessment.


[12] Finally, in our view, the trial judge mischaracterized the
nature of the offence that was proved by the Crown against
Watson. The trial judge, in the course of his reasons for
sentence, said the following:

          In my view, the evidence shows behaviour on
          the part of Watson that was cruel,
          calculating and deliberate. He stepped up and
          while one or more of his friends held Paul
          Semple down, he buried five inches of an
          eight inch knife in Paul Semple's back. What
          could be more deliberate.
          [Emphasis added.]
          

[13] The trial judge later described Watson's conduct as "out and
out gratuitous, vicious violence." He referred to Watson as
showing no more empathy than one would if he were "squishing
bugs". While the seriousness of this crime cannot be denied, and
that is why Watson is serving a life sentence, the above
references from the trial judge's reasons are not justified on
the evidence. This was a street fight, albeit a terrible one,
that got very much out of hand and led to the murder of a totally
innocent victim. Up to the point where Watson aimed the single
knife thrust at the victim, his conduct was no different than
that of the several other participants in the brawl.


[14] In our view, a proper sentence in this case is one of life
imprisonment without eligibility for parole for ten years. The
sentence appeal of Watson is allowed and his sentence is varied
accordingly.
