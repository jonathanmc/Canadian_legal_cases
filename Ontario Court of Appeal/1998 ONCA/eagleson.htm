OSBORNE J.A.:

[1]            The appellant raised five issues on his appeal from the dismissal of his action for defamation consequent upon the respondents’ successful application for a non-suit.  We think that the first three issues raised by the appellant can properly be collapsed into one issue.  The other two issues concern the procedure that the trial judge followed when the respondents applied for a non-suit and whether the trial judge erred in exercising her discretion to fix costs as she did.

ISSUE 1 – Assuming, for purposes of analysis, that the television broadcast in issue was about the plaintiff were the words used capable of defamatory meaning?

[2]            The trial judge concluded that the words used were not capable of defamatory meaning.  In her extensive review of the evidence and the law, she wrote:

            In looking at the ordinary and natural meaning of the words complained of (i.e. the Statements), I have concluded that no reasonable right thinking person would come to the conclusion that the Statements are capable of any defamatory meaning to the plaintiff.  The Statements contain no suggestion that the plaintiff committed any wrongdoing nor is there any derogatory statement contained therein about him.  To put it another way, the Statements do not amount to disparagement of the plaintiff’s reputation in the eyes of right thinking people.

[3]            The trial judge then addressed the false innuendo pleaded in paragraph 7(a) to (d) of the appellant’s statement of claim.  On that issue she wrote:

The plaintiff’s evidence does not support a conclusion that the words complained of bear defamatory meaning as alleged in paragraph 7. ... The innuendoes pleaded are not capable of bearing defamatory meaning.

[4]            Although the trial judge held that the false innuendo alleged in paragraph 7 of the statement of claim should not be considered in context because the appellant’s pleadings do not assert that position, she nonetheless went on to find that “even if context had been pleaded, the plaintiff could not succeed.”

[5]            Apart from the implications of the context issue, the appellant takes no issue with the principles applied by the trial judge in determining whether the broadcast in issue was capable of defamatory meaning in respect of the plaintiff.

[6]            In our view, the trial judge carefully and fairly considered all of the relevant evidence and correctly applied the law when she concluded that the ordinary meaning of the words in issue, including pleaded false innuendo, could not be taken by a reasonable right thinking person to be capable of defaming the appellant. We agree with the trial judge’s conclusion and with the path that she followed in reaching it. We would, therefore, not give effect to this ground of appeal.

ISSUE 2 – Should the respondents have been put to their election to call, or not call, evidence when they applied for a non-suit.

[7]            The appellant submits that the respondent should have been required to elect whether to call evidence as part of the non-suit application process.  The respondent’s position is that in a defamation action, it is well established that the defence will not be put to an election when applying for a non-suit.

[8]            The trial judge held that since this was an action for defamation, she should not put the respondents to their election to call evidence as part of the non-suit motion process.  In reaching that conclusion she relied upon Sopinka, Lederman and Bryant, The Law of Evidence in Canada, 1992, Butterworths, at p. 135 where the authors, citing M.V.“Polar Star” v. Louis Denker Inc. (1965), 53 D.L.R. (2d) 181 (P.E.I. C.A.) stated:

In actions of libel and slander, on a motion for a non-suit, the defendant is not put to his or her election as to whether he or she intends to call evidence.  Rather, the judge decides the question at the moment that the motion is brought.

[9]            In Polar Star, the Prince Edward Island Court of Appeal set out the procedure to be followed on non-suit motions.  The prescribed procedure involved putting the defence to an election whether to call evidence.  However, the P.E.I. Court of Appeal recognized that in defamation actions the election requirement did not apply.  See Polar Star at p.187.[1]

[10]        In the circumstances of this case, any failure of the trial judge in respect of the defence election issue is of no consequence. Indeed, the appellant conceded that if we upheld the trial judge’s finding on the issue whether the words used were capable of defamatory meaning, the failure of the trial judge to put the respondents to their election was of no practical or legal consequence in the circumstances of this case.  We think this concession was appropriate in light of the basis upon which the non-suit was granted.  We thus do not think that it is necessary to exhaustively analyze this issue in this case.  Suffice it to say, we are not persuaded that the trial judge was wrong in not requiring the respondents to elect particularly in light of the basis upon which the respondents sought a non-suit – that the words in issue were not capable of defamatory meaning, even if taken in context.  In these circumstances, there is no prospect that defence evidence could fill the gaps, that is, cure the problems faced by the appellant at the close of his case.  We do not wish to be taken to have concluded that in all defamation cases, where a plaintiff moves for a non-suit the trial judge should not require the defence to elect.  That issue can be considered in another case when the court is required to consider it.

ISSUE 3 – Fixing Costs

[11]        Although the appellant did not formally apply for leave to appeal costs we have taken it that the appellant seeks leave to appeal costs and if leave is granted to appeal costs.

[12]        The appellant submitted that the central issue giving rise to the non-suit (i.e. was the broadcast capable of defamatory meaning in respect of the plaintiff) could have been determined by an application under Rule 21.01.  The trial judge considered this submission and rejected it.  We cannot say that she was wrong in reaching the conclusion she did in respect of this submission.

[13]        The trial judge received extensive submissions on the issue of costs and she gave careful consideration to the question whether she should fix costs.  We do not think she erred in deciding to fix costs or in fixing them at $32,382.42 plus GST.  The process followed by the trial judge in fixing costs was entirely appropriate. It was not an arbitrary process as suggested by the appellant. In our view, the trial judge in fixing costs, in the amount that she did, met the requirements set out by Morden A.C.J.O. in Murano et al. V. Bank of Montreal, [1998] O.J. 2897.

[14]        We see no merit in this ground of appeal.  Leave is granted, but the appeal in respect of costs is dismissed.

CONCLUSION

[15]        The appeal is dismissed with costs.

Released:  October 1, 1998


[1]               It would appear that in defamation actions trial judges in Ontario have with some consistency not put the defence to an election as part of the non-suit process. For example, in Eagleson v. The Globe and Mail et al. Ferrier J., following the trial judge’s finding on this issue in this case, did not put the defence to an election.
