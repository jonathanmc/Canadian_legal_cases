                          ENDORSEMENT
[1]  We are not persuaded that the trial judge erred in the
exercise of his discretion under rule 26.01 in concluding that
the defendants had not shown prejudice and, accordingly, in
granting the amendment to the prayer for relief in the statement
of claim to substitute $105,000 for $25,000. The defendants
submitted to the trial judge that they would have conducted the
case differently in that they would have had the medical experts
attend personally at the trial rather than have had their reports
read in. This does not alter the fact that the jury would have
been required to deal with the same medical evidence, and it is
not apparent that the calling of the evidence orally would have
redounded more to the benefit of the defendants than to the
plaintiff.


[2]  We agree with Carruthers J. in Hill v. Church of Scientology
of Toronto (1992), 7 O.R. (3d) 489 (Gen. Div.) at 503 that the
submitted unreasonableness of the verdict should not be a factor
in refusing the amendment. It is, however, a matter that may be
reviewed by the Court of Appeal and we turn to this issue now.


[3]  We have considered all of the awards in previous cases which
have been brought to our attention on both sides and are mindful
of not only the deference which we must pay to the amount awarded
but also to the findings of fact which the jury could have made
as a basis of the award.


[4]  We are satisfied that the amount of the award, $105,000, is
so inordinately high that it must be a wholly erroneous estimate
of the damages. After giving the evidence the interpretation most
favourable to the plaintiff of which it is reasonably capable,
and paying due regard to the opinion expressed by the trial
judge, we exercise our power under s. 119 of the Courts of
Justice Act to substitute our assessment of the plaintiff's
damages in the amount of $35,000 in place of $105,000.


[5]  The appeal is allowed, with costs, and the judgment is
varied to give effect to our assessment. The trial costs order
will stand.
