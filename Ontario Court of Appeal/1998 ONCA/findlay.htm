DUNNET J. (ad hoc):

[1]   The appellant, Russell Findlay, is 51 years old and suffers
from  chronic progressive multiple sclerosis. He and his  parents
commenced  an  action against his family doctor, the  respondent,
Dr.  Frank R. Holmes, for medical malpractice as a result of  the
development of steroid-induced osteoporosis. The trial judge held
that the action was statute-barred by virtue of section 17 of the
Health Disciplines Act, R.S.O. 1990, c. H.4 (the "Act").


[2]   The appellants allege errors on the part of the trial judge
with respect the appropriate limitation period, standard of care,
informed consent and damages.


Overview


[3]   The  appellant was 25 years of age when he began to develop
certain  neurological symptoms. His wife at the time was a  nurse
and  recommended that he see the respondent, who referred him for
neurological  consultation. Demyelinating disease  was  suspected
but  his symptoms subsided. Six years later the symptoms recurred
and  the  respondent referred him to another neurologist.  Again,
demyelinating disease was suspected. He was subsequently referred
to  Dr. H.J.M. Barnett in the Department of Clinical Neurological
Sciences  at University Hospital, London, Ontario ("MS  Clinic").
In  his  letter of June 27, 1978 to the respondent,  Dr.  Barnett
wrote: "I think the diagnosis here is multiple sclerosis and that
there  is very little equivocation about it. I think that  he  is
young  and has an illness that is going to cause him serious  and
eventually  possibly  disabling  difficulty  in  the  foreseeable
future."


[4]   In  October  1978, the appellant saw  the  respondent  with
renewed   symptoms   of  multiple  sclerosis  ("MS"),   including
increased  numbness and lack of control in his legs and  slurring
of  speech.  It was at this point that the respondent  prescribed
prednisone, a dehydrogenated analogue of cortisone.


[5]   The  respondent  testified that when  he  first  prescribed
prednisone, he told the appellant that it was a powerful drug and
could  not be used indiscriminately. It was a safe drug  if  used
under  his  direct supervision. He warned that it could  cause  a
number   of  side  effects,  including  salt  retention,  changes
affecting his blood electrolytes and softening of the bones  with
a  tendency  of the bones to break. He explained that  prednisone
must  be  discontinued gradually after a short period of  use  or
there  could be serious and dangerous effects. He testified  that
during the years that followed, whenever he prescribed the  drug,
he  would  not  give the same warning in great detail  but  would
remind  the appellant of the fact that he had given the  previous
warnings.


[6]   The  appellant testified that he was told by the respondent
that  prednisone  was an anti-inflammatory  drug  that  would  be
beneficial in treating flare-ups of his condition. He was  warned
not to use added salt because the drug caused water retention. It
could also cause euphoria. He was advised to taper the dosage and
not   to  stop  the  medication  abruptly.  He  denied  that  the
respondent  told him anything about softening of  the  bones.  He
also   denied  that  he  received  information  or  advice  about
prednisone  from the various doctors who monitored his  condition
at the MS Clinic.


[7]   In  October 1978, the respondent prescribed the drug  in  a
tapered  dosage regime over a three week period with good results
and  a  diminution  of symptoms. The appellant  did  not  require
prednisone again until August 1979 when he experienced  a  marked
increase  in  numbness and weakness in his legs.  Again,  it  was
prescribed in tapering doses. In November 1979, the appellant was
examined  at the MS Clinic by a neurologist, Dr. D.W.  Paty,  who
documented his use of prednisone and noted: "I see no  reason  to
change his management at this point."


[8]   He  did not require the drug again until May 1981  when  he
went to the respondent, complaining of increased weakness in  his
legs,  fatigue  and  a  drop  foot on the  left.  Prednisone  was
prescribed  in  a tapered regime. The respondent noted  that  his
symptoms  responded  well to the drug. The  following  month  the
appellant  was  seen  by Dr. John H. Noseworthy,  a  resident  in
neurology at the MS Clinic, who recorded the dosage of prednisone
without  comment, other than the fact that the appellant felt  it
had helped him.


[9]   Between  October 1981 and June 1982, the appellant  visited
the  respondent  monthly. His clinical  notes  reflect  that  the
dosage  was reduced as symptoms subsided. In June, a neurologist,
Dr.  T.  E.  Feasby at the MS Clinic, recorded that the appellant
remained on a small dose of prednisone which he suggested that he
stop.   In  September,  however,  the  appellant  went   to   the
respondent's office, complaining that he felt worse than he  ever
had.  There was numbness everywhere except for his head;  he  had
difficulty  walking and was suffering from extreme  fatigue.  The
respondent prescribed prednisone. The appellant continued to  see
the  respondent monthly until June 1983 and continued to  take  a
variable low dosage of prednisone for symptoms of numbness in his
hands and feet, spasticity and fatigue.


[10]   In June, when he attended the MS Clinic, he had not  taken
prednisone  for  three weeks. Dr. Feasby wrote to the  respondent
that  he would prefer to have the appellant off the drug for  the
immediate future. He recommended baclofen as an alternative.  One
month later, however, the baclofen had produced unacceptable side
effects.  The  appellant had also sustained a fall.  Having  been
unable   to   reach  Dr.  Feasby  by  telephone,  the  respondent
prescribed  prednisone and then wrote to the doctor,  documenting
the severity of the appellant's symptoms and adding:

          At this point I felt I was unable to withhold
          the  prednisone to which he has responded  so
          well  in the past, and started him on 10  mgm
          q.i.d.  with a prompt and dramatic  response.
          On   29   July   he   reported   an   amazing
          improvement.  better  balance.   better   leg
          control,   and  was  able  to  climb   stairs
          normally. The prednisone was reduced to 5 mgm
          q.i.d. and I will continue to reduce the dose
          as  quickly  as possible. I am aware  of  the
          problems concerning steroid therapy,  but  as
          this   patient  was  in  acute  and   rapidly
          progressing  distress there seems  to  be  no
          alternative at the moment.
          I  would  be  pleased to  hear  your  further
          suggestions.

Dr.  Feasby replied that he agreed with the use of prednisone  to
treat  the  appellant's acute relapse. He wrote that he  was  not
comfortable  with  its long-term treatment in multiple  sclerosis
and  agreed  with  a reduction in the dosage. He  concluded:  "It
might  be possible to get him off it again, but I realize he  was
on  alternate day therapy before with some success. I  think  you
will have to judge what is best for him."


[11]  The  respondent continued to reduce the  prednisone  regime
until June 1984, when an exacerbation of symptoms necessitated  a
higher  dosage. The appellant was seen three months later at  the
MS Clinic by a neurologist, Dr. George Rice, who noted:

          Over   the   last   year   there   has   been
          considerable progression of his disease in an
          insidious  fashion. He has had  deterioration
          in   his  strength,  walking  abilities   and
          balance.  He  has been prone to  falling  and
          indeed has taken some bad spills. He recently
          fractured  his left arm and broke some  ribs.
          Over  the last year he has been treated  with
          several courses of prednisone. Indeed he  has
          been  receiving the medication for the better
          part  of  10 months in the last year.  He  is
          currently taking 10 mg. and is attempting  to
          taper his dosage.

No  suggestion  was made for a change or discontinuation  of  the

drug.

[12]  The  appellant remained on a low dose of  prednisone  until
January  1985, when he was admitted to the MS Clinic for a  trial
of  cyclophosphamide. Two weeks following his discharge, however,
he  developed an acute exacerbation of symptoms and on the advice
of Dr. W.J. Oczkowski at the MS Clinic, the respondent prescribed
prednisone  daily  for 10 days with gradual reduction.  The  drug
appeared   to  produce  effective  results  and  the  appellant's
condition improved to the point where he was able to ski. As  the
drug  was tapered, however, he developed exacerbations.  In  May,
the  appellant  increased the dosage  on  his  own  with  a  good
response and reduced it again on his own. In June 1985, Dr.  Rice
examined  the appellant and observed that he could easily  become
steroid dependent. He told him to try to taper the drug.


[13]  In  September, the respondent was becoming frustrated  that
the  appellant's  MS symptoms were worsening  and  consulted  Dr.
Weinshenker at the MS Clinic, who suggested that he increase  the
dosage  for seven days with gradual tapering. Following a further
exacerbation, Dr. G.C. Ebers at the MS Clinic recommended to  the
respondent  that he increase the dosage, admit the  appellant  to
hospital and thereafter, taper the dosage. The respondent carried
out those recommendations.


[14] Between October and December 1985, the respondent prescribed
a  reduction in the prednisone in accordance with the  advice  of
the  doctors  at  the  MS  Clinic. His  notes  reflect  that  the
appellant  did  not  reduce his intake  in  accordance  with  the
instructions he received from the respondent. In December, his MS
symptoms   recurred.  He  also  began  to  experience   vertebral
fractures, initially when lifting a fifty pound box,  and  a  few
weeks  later  when  moving a microwave oven. The  fractures  were
determined  to  be  caused  by osteoporosis.  The  appellant  was
hospitalized  by the respondent and came under the  care  of  Dr.
J.M.J.  Leon,  an  internist. While  in  hospital,  he  developed
additional compression fractures. Dr. Leon's notes state that the
appellant  was started on higher doses of prednisone  which  were
slowly reduced. He was subsequently admitted to the MS Clinic  in
February 1986, where it was decided to introduce the drug  imuran
into  the appellant's treatment regime. This drug enabled him  to
be weaned off prednisone by May 1986.


[15]  Thus  as  a  result of the therapeutic  use  of  prednisone
between 1978 and 1986, the appellant suffered vertebral collapse,
which  caused a significant reduction in his body height as  well
as  pain  and reduced mobility. The appellant testified  that  in
1988,  because  of  his  physical condition,  he  was  unable  to
maintain  employment  as  District  Officer  with  the  Teacher's
Federation  in  Welland, Ontario, a position he had  assumed  ten
years  earlier  when  he left his occupation  as  a  high  school
teacher.


Standard of Care


[16]  The  trial judge accepted the evidence of Dr. Boyd Clifford
Hodinott, a family practitioner, and Dr. William McIlroy, a noted
neurologist  in  the  field of MS and  Chairman  of  the  Medical
Advisory Board of the MS Society of Canada in the late 1970's and
early  1980's,  that  prednisone  was  the  most  commonly   used
medication   for   treatment  of  MS,  particularly   for   acute
exacerbations of the disease. The trial judge also  accepted  the
evidence  of Dr. Robert Henderson, a family physician,  that  the
respondent's  use  of referrals and follow up  by  telephone  and
letter  with  the  consultants at the MS Clinic went  beyond  the
standard  one would have expected from a general practitioner  at
the  time.  Further,  his  notes reflect  that  he  prescribed  a
decreasing  dosage  of  prednisone and  carefully  monitored  his
patient.


[17]  The  standard  of care expected of the respondent  is  that
which   could  be  reasonably  expected  of  a  normal,   prudent
practitioner  of  the  same experience  and  standing  (Crits  v.
Sylvester (1956), 1 D.L.R. (2d) 502 (Ont. C.A.)). There was ample
evidence  upon  which  the trial judge could  conclude  that  the
management  of  the appellant's symptoms with prednisone  met  an
acceptable standard of care in these circumstances.


Informed Consent


[18] The findings of the trial judge were that the appellant  was
a  very  alert  and  bright patient. He studied  and  brought  in
articles  and  had  long discussions with the respondent  on  his
visits.  The  respondent  was  quite  methodical,  referring  the
appellant to other doctors on many occasions and noting carefully
his   symptoms  and  responses.  The  trial  judge  accepted  the
respondent's  evidence  that  he warned  his  patient  about  the
dangers  of prednisone and specifically, the dangers of softening
of  his  bones  and  fractures, which  necessarily  included  the
possibility of vertebral collapse. He accepted that the  warnings
were  appropriate and sufficient. In contrast, he found that  the
appellant was experiencing serious symptoms. He was euphoric  and
may  not  have  remembered what he was told. He  noted  that  the
manner  in  which  the respondent prescribed  the  prednisone  in
carefully decreasing dosages would, of itself, logically lead  to
discussions between doctor and patient of the side effects of the
drug.


[19] Further, the trial judge described the appellant as a man of
inquiring intellect. As such it was difficult to accept that  the
appellant had no discussions with doctors at the MS Clinic  about
the advantages and disadvantages of his regimes of prednisone. It
is  clear from the evidence that the doctors were concerned  with
the  possibility of side effects from the drug and their  medical
records  contain  many  references  to  the  appellant's  use  of
prednisone.  The trial judge concluded, therefore,  that  it  was
inconceivable that the side effects were not discussed  with  the
appellant.  Moreover, despite the appellant's  testimony  to  the
contrary,  he  found that a reasonable person in the  appellant's
position, knowing the risks, would have consented to the regime.


[20]  In my view, there was evidence to support the finding  that
the  respondent did not fail in his duty of disclosure and in any
event,  a  reasonable person in the appellant's  position,  on  a
balance  of probabilities, would have consented to prednisone  as
prescribed for pain. It is significant to note that the appellant
consented  to the use of experimental drugs cyclophosphamide  and
imuran to address his MS symptoms, knowing that each drug carried
a significant risk of malignancy and death. Given the appellant's
stated eagerness to continue working, his desire to minimize  his
MS  symptoms,  the apparent positive effect of the prednisone  on
those  symptoms and his demonstrated willingness  to  take  other
risks  to  alleviate those symptoms, it can fairly  be  concluded
that  a reasonable patient in the appellant's circumstances would
have  taken  the prednisone after a full warning of the  possible
side effects.


Damages


[21]  The  trial judge determined that the appellant's disability
was  the  result of MS complicated by steroid-induced  fractures.
There  was  evidence  that  the  pain  from  the  fractures   had
substantially  subsided  by  1986  and  1987.  Nevertheless,  the
appellant  was  left with residual problems, including  pain  and
loss  of  height  and mobility. The trial judge  found  that  the
appellant  would not have terminated his employment as  early  as
1988; however, his MS would have prevented him from continuing to
work  after March 1994. He assessed general damages in the amount
of  $100,000; past lost income of $143,289 and future care  costs
(including gross up at 54.86%) of $408, 593. He attributed 25% of
the  future  care  costs  to the effect of  osteoporosis  on  the
appellant's condition and awarded no damages for the  Family  Law
Act claims because of a paucity of evidence.


[22]  The  medical records of the MS Clinic demonstrate  that  in
1984  and  1985,  there  was a considerable  progression  of  the
appellant's  MS  disease.  Dr.  McIlroy  testified  that   common
symptoms of the disease are spasticity, lack of balance, fatigue,
weakness and severely restricted physical mobility, all of  which
were   the  appellant's  continuing  physical  difficulties.   In
addition,  MS patients commonly suffer from musculoskeletal  pain
along  the  spine.  Dr.  McIlroy stated that  the  appellant  had
entered the chronic progressive stage of the disease and  it  was
most likely that he would continue to deteriorate.


[23] With respect to his fractures, in March 1986, Dr. Leon noted
that  the  appellant no longer had any back pain. In April  1987,
Dr.  Noseworthy recorded that the appellant told him that he  was
pain  free  and  could stand straight. Also,  there  was  medical
evidence  that  pain  from vertebral fractures  usually  resolves
after  a  few  months  and the appellant's  complaints  were  not
consistent  with ongoing fractures. Dr. Keith Hayes, a specialist
in  rehabilitation and future care costs, testified that  of  the
future  care  costs, 75% would be attributable to MS  progression
rather than to osteoporosis.


[24]  Thus  there was evidence upon which the trial  judge  could
have  reached his conclusion concerning the causative role of  MS
in  the  chronic  progressive state of the  appellant's  disease.
Accordingly, there is no reason to interfere with his  assessment
of damages.


The Limitation Period


[25]  The  respondent submits that the appellant's action,  which
was  commenced on February 22, 1989, is statute-barred.  Where  a
defendant  pleads  a  limitation period, the  onus  is  upon  the
plaintiff  to  prove that the cause of action  arose  within  the
statutory  limitation period (Clemens v. Brown (1958), 13  D.L.R.
(2d) 488 (Ont. C.A.) at p. 491).


[26] Section 17 of the Act states:

          17.    No duly registered member of a College
          is  liable  to  any  action  arising  out  of
          negligence  or  malpractice  in  respect   of
          professional services requested  or  rendered
          unless  such action is commenced  within  one
          year from the date when the person commencing
          the  action knew or ought to have  known  the
          fact  or  facts upon which the person alleges
          negligence or malpractice.

There is an evidentiary burden on the appellant to prove that the
material  facts  giving rise to the action were  not  within  his
knowledge  within  one  year from the date  when  he  issued  the
statement of claim.


[27]  The  trial judge determined that on February 9,  1987,  the
appellant completed a medical information form stating  that  one
of  the abnormal symptoms or medical problems he was experiencing
was  "steroid induced osteoporosis". He found that the  form  was
evidence  of the appellant's knowledge of the fact or facts  upon
which he alleged negligence. He concluded that the appellant  had
been examined prior to that by a large number of specialists  and
was  undoubtedly aware, even earlier, of the relationship between
the  prednisone  he  was  prescribed and the  fractures  that  he
suffered. He concluded that the limitation period had expired  in
March 1988 at the latest.


[28]  Since  the  decision  of the Supreme  Court  of  Canada  in
Peixeiro  v. Haberman (1997), 151 D.L.R. (4th) 429, it  is  clear
that the discovery principle postpones the running of a statutory
limitation  period until the plaintiff knows,  or  by  reasonable
diligence  could  have known, the material facts  upon  which  to
bring  an action. In Peixeiro, the court considered the issue  in
relation  to  the Highway Traffic Act, R.S.O. 1990,  c.  H.8,  s.
206(1),  and found that the discoverability principle applied  to
avoid  the  injustice of precluding an action before a person  is
able to sue. Major J., speaking for the court, held at p. 439:

               Short  limitation periods indicate  that
          the   legislature  put  a  premium  on  their
          function as a statute of repose. This is  one
          of  the  three rationales which serve society
          and   the   courts'  continued  interest   in
          maintaining  the  respect of these  statutes.
          Whatever interest a defendant may have in the
          universal application of a limitation  period
          must  be  balanced against  the  concerns  of
          fairness  to  the plaintiff who  was  unaware
          that   his   injuries  met   the   conditions
          precedent to commencing an action: Murphy  v.
          Welsh,  supra; M. (K.) v. M. (H.),  [1992]  3
          S.C.R.  6,  96  D.L.R.  (4th)  289.  All  the
          rationales  were set out in  M.  (K.)  v.  M.
          (H.),   where   this  Court  considered   the
          Limitations  Act, R.S.O. 1980,  c.  240  (now
          R.S.O.  1990,  c.L.15) in order to  determine
          the time of accrual of the cause of action in
          a manner consistent with its purposes (at pp.
          29-30):
               There   are  three,  and  they  may   be
               described as the certainty, evidentiary,
               and diligence rationales....
                    Statutes  of limitations have  long
               been  said  to be statutes of repose....
               The reasoning is straightforward enough.
               There  comes a time, it is said, when  a
               potential defendant should be secure  in
               his  reasonable expectation that he will
               not  be  held  to  account  for  ancient
               obligations....
                    The second rationale is evidentiary
               and  concerns  the desire  to  foreclose
               claims based on stale evidence. Once the
               limitation   period  has   lapsed,   the
               potential defendant should no longer  be
               concerned  about  the  preservation   of
               evidence relevant to the claim....
                    Finally, plaintiffs are expected to
               act  diligently and not "sleep on  their
               rights"; statutes of limitation  are  an
               incentive  for plaintiffs to bring  suit
               in a timely fashion.
               M.  (K.)  v. M. (H.) applied  the  three
          rationales  to the fact situation  there  and
          found  that neither the guarantee of  repose,
          the  evidentiary concerns nor the expectation
          of  diligence  on the part of  the  plaintiff
          precluded    the    application    of     the
          discoverability principle.
               Since this Court's decisions in Kamloops
          (City  of) v. Nielsen, [1984] 2 S.C.R. 2,  10
          D.L.R.  (4th) 641, and Central Trust  Co.  v.
          Rafuse,  [1986] 2 S.C.R. 147, at p.  224,  31
          D.L.R.  (4th)  481,  discoverability   is   a
          general  rule applied to avoid the  injustice
          of  precluding an action before the person is
          able  to raise it. See Sparham-Souter v. Town
          & Country Developments (Essex) Ltd., [1976] 1
          Q.B. 858 (C.A.), at p. 868, per Lord Denning,
          M.R.,  citing Carledge v. E. Jopling  &  Sons
          Ltd., supra:
                    It appears to me to be unreasonable
               and  unjustifiable in principle  that  a
               cause of action should be held to accrue
               before  it  is possible to discover  any
               injury,  and  therefore  before  it   is
               possible to raise any action.

The  discoverability principle provides that a limitation  period
will  not begin to run until the plaintiff has knowledge  of  the
material  facts upon which the cause of action is based; however,
the plaintiff must act with reasonable diligence.


[29]  In  Gaudet et al. v. Levy et al. (1984), 47 O.R.  (2d)  577
(H.C.J.), White J. dealt with the interpretation of s. 17 of  the
Act.  In  dismissing an application for an order to set down  for
hearing as a preliminary point of law, the applicability  of  the
limitation period, he stated at pp. 582-3:

               I  am of the view that s. 17 imports, in
          the alternative, two concepts: (1) knowledge,
          that   is,   actual   knowledge,   which   is
          subjective  and (2) negligence in failing  to
          know, which is objective. The words "ought to
          have  known",  is an expression  familiar  to
          practitioners  at  the negligence  bar.  They
          mean that the plaintiff is to be assessed  in
          terms  of the standard of the reasonable  man
          in   failing   to  acquire  the   appropriate
          knowledge.
               So  the  issue, as I see it, is  one  of
          fact.  Did the plaintiff know, or in all  the
          circumstances, ought he to have known of  the
          fact   or   facts  upon  which   he   alleges
          negligence or malpractice one year before the
          commencement of the action? It would seem  to
          me,  that among the facts that he would  have
          to  know would be the fact of the appropriate
          standard   of   care   which   the    medical
          practitioner was required to adhere to.  That
          fact   could  only  normally  reside   in   a
          qualified professional opinion. A lay  person
          even  with legal advice would not necessarily
          be  deemed  to  have been  in  possession  of
          knowledge  of the standard, nor would  he  be
          deemed to have been necessarily negligent  in
          not  acquiring knowledge of the  standard  at
          any  given  point, even after the glimmer  of
          hope  had  been  raised by  a  discussion  as
          between   his  solicitor  and  a   consultant
          doctor, that he might have a case.
               It  is a question of fact as to when the
          information developed by his solicitor or  by
          himself   has  reached  the  stage   that   a
          reasonably  prudent person, with  appropriate
          access  to medical knowledge (appropriate  in
          the  sense  of that which could be discovered
          by   a   reasonably  prudent  solicitor,   or
          plaintiff  following  a  reasonably  diligent
          investigation) would have determined that  he
          had  prima  facie grounds for inferring  that
          his  doctor had been negligent or had engaged
          in  malpractice upon him. Section 17  of  the
          statute  imports  that all  of  the  material
          facts  were available, or ought to have  been
          available  to  the plaintiff,  including  the
          meaning of the facts in terms of duty of care
          appropriate   to  the  medical  practitioner.
          Without  medical advice interpreting it,  the
          raw  medical data cannot be presumed to  mean
          anything  to  a  layman.  Now,  if  after   a
          reasonably  prudent person would have  formed
          the  judgement that he has been a  victim  of
          negligence  or  malpractice,  he   fails   to
          commence  his  action within the  appropriate
          statutory period, it would appear to me  that
          his  cause  of action is barred. Essentially,
          whether   or   not   the   plaintiff    knew,
          subjectively, or whether or not the plaintiff
          ought  to have known or was negligent in  not
          knowing,  is  a  question of fact  which  can
          only,  in  my  opinion, be  determined  by  a
          tribunal  of  fact  capable  of  hearing  the
          witnesses   as   to   the  facts,   assessing
          credibility,   looking   at   all   of    the
          circumstances including the complete  history
          of  the  matter  and hearing  expert  opinion
          evidence, and ruling, as a question of  fact,
          whether  applying  the subjective  test,  the
          plaintiff  knew,  or  whether  applying   the
          objective test he ought to have known of "the
          fact or facts", contemplated in s. 17 of  the
          Act. [Emphasis in original.]

Although the trial judge concluded, relying on the clear  wording
of  the  statute, that the action was statute-barred, he declined
to  follow Gaudet, supra, as well as the case of Law v.  Kingston
General  Hospital  et al. (1983), 42 O.R. (2d) 476  (H.C.J.).  In
Law,  Griffiths  J. dismissed an appeal from  the  order  of  the
Master dismissing an application to set aside an order adding two
doctors as party defendants. He stated that the time under s.  17
of  the Act will frequently run from the moment the solicitor for
the  plaintiff  carrying  out a diligent investigation  is  first
apprised of all the facts.


[30]  The  trial judge held that those cases were distinguishable
in  that they involved pre-trial decisions. In my view, the  fact
that those decisions were not rendered following a trial is of no
import  and the trial judge erred in distinguishing them on  that
basis. The elements required in order to give rise to a cause  of
action are the same, whether the finding is made before trial  or
at trial.


[31] I adopt the reasoning of White J. in Gaudet, supra, when  he
states  that s. 17 of the Act requires the plaintiff to act  with
reasonable  diligence in discovering the material fact  or  facts
upon which to found an action in negligence or malpractice. It is
a question of fact, depending on the circumstances of the case as
a  whole, as to when knowledge of the material fact or facts  was
acquired  by the plaintiff. While in many cases, as suggested  by
White  J.,  the facts will only become known upon  receipt  of  a
medical opinion with respect to the appropriate standard of care,
there will also be cases where the plaintiff will have actual  or
deemed  knowledge  of the material facts: immediately  after  the
surgery  or  treatment,  some  time  later  if  the  results  are
unexpected but the plaintiff is advised to wait until the problem
resolves, or upon receipt of the clinical history.


[32] The appellant submits that he did not know the cause of  his
symptoms  until March of 1988 when he was referred  to  the  bone
clinic at St Michael's Hospital in Toronto where he was told  for
the  first  time  that  he  had prednisone-induced  osteoporosis.
Alternatively, even if he was aware of the cause of his  symptoms
before  March of 1988, he was not aware of the legal standard  to
which the respondent was bound to adhere, nor of the relationship
between prednisone, osteoporosis and vertebral collapse.


[33] The respondent testified, and his office notes confirm, that
in  December  1985,  he told the appellant  he  had  sustained  a
vertebral  fracture due to osteoporosis caused by the prednisone.
In  February  1986,  the hospital notes record  that  Dr.  Parvez
Ansari,  an  orthopaedic surgeon, Dr. Leon  and  the  respondent,
discussed  with the appellant the further process of  compression
in  the region of L1 and L4. Dr. Ansari noted that "basically  it
is  a  steroid type. He is having compression with regard to  the
fragile  bones and this was explained clearly to him....  We  had
quite  an  extensive  discussion with the patient  and  this  was
explained to him clearly, what the problems he has".


[34]  In March of 1986, the appellant was examined by Dr. Anthony
B.  Hodsman for assessment of his bone loss. In his letter to Dr.
Feasby,  the doctor wrote: "One wonders whether he did  not  have
some  pre-existing osteopenia, which has been accelerated by  the
steroids,  or whether the steroid therapy dramatically  increased
bone  turnover."  Dr. Hodsman recommended further  investigation,
which the appellant declined. In April 1986, Dr. Leon and Dr.  M.
Collins  told  the  appellant that with  increased  activity  and
discontinuation of the prednisone, his bones should remineralize.
The  appellant  was removed from the drug by doctors  at  the  MS
Clinic  in  early 1986. The hospital records include  notes  with
respect to prednisone's causal relationship to osteoporosis.


[35]  The  findings of the trial judge are that the appellant  is
very  alert and bright man with a probing intellect. He had  long
discussions with the respondent and he discussed the  regimes  of
prednisone  treatment with medical consultants at the MS  Clinic.
The  evidence  as  a  whole, therefore, leads to  the  inevitable
conclusion that the appellant must have known of the relationship
between  his osteoporosis, vertebral fractures and the prednisone
treatment.  There was, therefore, ample evidence to  support  the
trial  judge's  finding that the appellant had actual  or  deemed
knowledge by March of 1987 at the latest, of the material fact or
facts  upon  which he alleged negligence against the  respondent.
Thereafter he failed to commence his action within the applicable
statutory period under the Act. Accordingly, there is no error in
the trial judge's finding that the action is statute-barred.


[36]   For these reasons, I would dismiss the appeal with costs.
