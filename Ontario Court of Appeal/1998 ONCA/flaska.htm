                     ENDORSEMENT

[1]   The  Divisional Court correctly held that the  audit  judge
erred  in  principle in determining that "... it  would  be  more
appropriate  in  my  view to fix compensation of  the  individual
executors according to the time they each spent."


[2]  The assessment of an executor's compensation exclusively  as
a  multiplier  of time spent and documented is inconsistent  with
the language of s. 61(1) of the Trustee Act, R.S.O. 1990, c. T.23
and  the approach set down in Re Jeffery Estate (1990), 39 E.T.R.
173    at   179.   In   Re   Laing   Estate   (reasons   released
contemporaneously with this endorsement), this court specifically
approved the approach taken in Re Jeffery Estate.


[3]   The  audit  judge's error in principle  led  her  to  award
compensation  equal to about 15% of the amount which  would  have
been  awarded  under the tariff guidelines. Counsel advised  that
their research indicated that this was the most radical departure
from the guidelines to be found in the reported cases. The amount
awarded  by  the  audit judge can only be described  as  "grossly
insufficient."


[4]   Having  correctly  found that  the  audit  judge  erred  in
principle,  the  Divisional  Court  proceeded  to  determine  the
appropriate  level  of  compensation. It  fixed  compensation  at
$381,550.00, about 60% of the amount that would have been awarded
under  the  guidelines.  We see no error  in  principle  in  that
assessment  and  would  not characterize the  amount  awarded  as
"grossly  excessive."  We also see no basis  upon  which  we  can
interfere  with the allotment of the compensation as between  the
capital and revenue accounts.


[5]  The order of the Divisional Court is affirmed and the appeal
is dismissed with costs.
