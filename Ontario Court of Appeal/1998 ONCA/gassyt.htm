CHARRON J.A.:
[1]  Peter Gassyt and Arnold Markowitz were jointly tried before
a judge and jury on three counts of conspiracy to commit murder
contrary to section 465 of the Criminal Code R.S.C. 1985, c. C-
46. After the close of the Crown's case, the trial judge directed
a verdict of acquittal on two of the counts. The trial continued
on the remaining count. The accused were convicted and were each
sentenced to a term of imprisonment of five years. The Crown
appeals against the verdicts of acquittal and against the
sentence. At the hearing, Crown counsel limited the appeal
against sentence to the failure to impose a prohibition order
pursuant to s.100 of the Criminal Code. Each accused appeals
against conviction and abandons his appeal against sentence.
 
[2]  In my view, all appeals must succeed. I would order a new
trial on the three counts. In view of this conclusion, I will
summarize the facts only to the extent necessary to explain the
issues raised on the appeals.
 
A.   The Facts
[3]  Gassyt and Markowitz were jointly charged with three counts
of conspiracy to commit murder. In the first count, it is alleged
that Gassyt and Markowitz conspired with each other in June and
July 1992 to cause a business associate to whom they owed money,
Stephen Dulmage, to be murdered. In the second count, it is
alleged that, during the same time period, they conspired with
each other to cause a man whom they believed had facilitated the
contract killing, Irwin Krakowski, to be murdered. In the third
count, again in the same time period, it is alleged that they
conspired with each other and with Dr. Stanley Bernstein to cause
a third unknown person to be murdered.
 
[4]  It was the theory of the Crown at trial that Gassyt and
Markowitz contacted Irwin Krakowski, whom they understood to have
links to organized crime, to hire a "hit man" to murder their
business partner, Steven Dulmage. Krakowski went to the police
and told them about the situation in the hope of gaining favour
with respect to several outstanding fraud charges against him.
Krakowski did not testify at trial.
 
[5]  As a result of the information provided by Krakowski, the
police arranged for an undercover officer, Detective Garry
Carter, to pose as a hitman during a meeting to be set by
Krakowski. The police also arranged for the intended victim,
Dulmage, to pose for a photograph in which he appeared to have a
bullet wound on the side of the head with blood flowing from the
wound onto the floor. The photograph was taken with Carter's
hands holding Dulmage's head. The photograph was to be used to
prove to Gassyt and Markowitz that the contract murder had been
carried out. Dulmage also gave the police his wallet and other
personal items.
 
[6]  Carter, using a code phrase provided to him by Krakowski,
contacted Gassyt and arranged for a meeting in a parking lot on
July 14, 1992. Two meetings took place between Carter, Gassyt and
Markowitz on the afternoon of July 14. Carter testified that he
discussed killing Dulmage, Krakowski and an unknown man with
Gassyt and Markowitz. He also testified that he was given money
in partial payment for killing Dulmage. Carter testified that,
during the second meeting Gassyt and Markowitz referred to the
fact that they had "a rich partner", that they had the "backing
of a rich doctor", and that it was this doctor who wanted the
person, whose identify was not yet known, eliminated. Gassyt and
Markowitz were to meet with the doctor later that day and
finalize the plan. They would also get the money for the doctor's
job.
 
[7]  Immediately after the first meeting, telephone records show
that Markowitz placed a call to Bernstein. At trial, Gassyt
confirmed that Markowitz had placed that telephone call on
Markowitz's cellular phone. After the second meeting, Markowitz
again made a telephone call on his cellular phone to Dr.
Bernstein.
 
[8]  After the second meeting, Gassyt and Markowitz proceeded to
a donut shop and met Bernstein, at which time they were all
arrested. At the time of his arrest, Markowitz had on his person
close to $700 U.S. and over $1,500 Canadian with $1,000 of the
Canadian money being wrapped in the same manner as the money he
had given to Carter earlier that day (50 twenty dollar bills
bundled separately in an elastic band).
 
[9]  The Crown sought to adduce evidence through the booking
officer that Bernstein was found to be in possession of over
$18,000 at the time of his arrest. The trial judge would not
admit it unless the Crown called Bernstein and gave the defence a
chance to cross-examine him. The Crown did not call Bernstein and
the evidence was not admitted at trial.
 
[10] The Crown called surveillance officers who stood at a
distance and took notes and photographs during the two meetings.
Some photos taken during the second meeting showed that the
window to the Lincoln Continental in which Markowitz was sitting
was closed and Carter sitting in his truck with Gassyt. Carter
conceded that, if the window was indeed closed as shown, the
three-way conversation he had described could not have occurred.
Upon being confronted with the photographs, he explained that it
started to rain during the meeting and that Gassyt raised the
window in the truck. He could not say whether Markowitz had done
the same with the window of the Lincoln Continental. Carter
stated that this would have happened at the end of the meeting.
There were further inconsistencies in the evidence between the
surveillance officers and Carter on the timing of the meetings.
 
[11] At the close of the Crown's case, counsel for each accused
moved for a directed verdict on each count. The trial judge
granted the motions on the first and third counts. The trial
continued on the charge of conspiracy to murder Krakowski.
 
[12] Gassyt testified that although the meetings had taken place,
the subject of those meetings was never about contract killings
but, rather, to procure stock certificates owed to them by
Krakowski. He testified that they assumed Carter was Krakowski's
partner. Markowitz did not testify.
 
[13] It was the theory of the defence that Carter's evidence was
either completely fabricated or greatly embellished.  It was the
position of the defence that Carter intimidated and threatened
Gassyt and Markowitz in order to get money from them. The defence
relied on the surveillance photographs mentioned earlier in
support of its contention that Markowitz could never have
participated in the conversation during the second meeting as
alleged by Carter.
 
[14] Gassyt and Markowitz seek to adduce fresh evidence on appeal
to show that, subsequent to the trial, Carter has been charged
with criminal offences. On September 26, 1997, he was charged
with theft over $5,000, fraud over, and breach of trust.  It is
alleged that he diverted for his own benefit funds that were
intended to finance a major undercover project, unrelated to the
present case.  In February 1998, he was charged with attempting
to obstruct justice on the basis that he failed to disclose
information to the Crown on another case, leading to the
withdrawal of charges against a prime target of an undercover
operation.  He has not yet been tried.  The facts alleged in
support of the allegations range from 1992 to 1995. The trial in
the proceeding before us took place in May and June 1993. The
investigation into Carter's alleged misconduct did not commence
until after the trial.
 
B.   Crown Appeal on Count One: Conspiracy to Murder Dulmage
[15] The defence sought a directed verdict on count one on the
basis that there was no evidence to support the allegation that
there was a conspiracy in the past between Gassyt and Markowitz
to do something in the future. At the time of the meeting with
Carter, the deed, in so far as the accused  were concerned, had
already been done.
 
[16] The trial judge agreed with defence counsel. In a brief
ruling, he stated as follows:
 
          With respect to Count No. 1, I have extreme
          difficulty because a conspiracy, in my view,
          envisages an agreement to do something. Here
          we have the evidence starting, as I have
          indicated, in the middle of it. It sort of
          floats from the air where the deed, in the
          eyes of the accused, is already done. There
          is no question on the evidence that they were
          delighted to be rid of the problem of Dulmage
          because he was causing them, and still is
          apparently causing them, some problem on
          finances. But to me, that does not create
          evidence from which an inference could be
          drawn that they had previously agreed to do
          away with Dulmage.
          I am not satisfied there is any evidence that
          a jury should consider in this instance. I am
          aware of cases where it does not matter if
          the object of the conspiracy can or cannot
          occur, but in this case it's already occurred
          and I do not see how one can, from that fact
          in the mind of the accused, transport it back
          to the front end and say: Yes, they had
          planned this from the beginning.
[17] First, it is my view that there was no merit to counsel's
argument at trial. The fact that the evidence was gathered after
the deed was (seemingly) done is of no moment. Indeed, before the
introduction of wiretap evidence in the investigative process, I
would think it would have been the rare case where direct
evidence would be available that the conspirators met, discussed
and actually agreed to carry out a common unlawful purpose. A
conspiracy is more likely to be proven by evidence of overt acts
and statements by the conspirators from which the prior agreement
can be logically inferred. The following words of Rinfret J. in
Paradis v. The King (1934), 61 C.C.C. 184 at 186 (S.C.C.)  are
apposite to this issue:
 
          Conspiracy like all other crimes, may be
          established by inference from the conduct of
          the parties. No doubt the agreement between
          them is the gist of the offence, but only in
          very rare cases will it be possible to prove
          it by direct evidence. Ordinarily the
          evidence must proceed by steps. The actual
          agreement must be gathered from "several
          isolated doings" (Kenny, Outlines of Criminal
          Law, 13 ed., p.294) having possibly little or
          no value taken by themselves, but the bearing
          of which one upon the other must be
          interpreted; and their cumulative effect,
          properly estimated in the light of all
          surrounding circumstances, may raise a
          presumption of concerted purpose entitling
          the jury to find the existence of the
          unlawful agreement.
[18] Second, it is my view that there was ample evidence that
would allow the jury to infer both the agreement between the
accused and their membership in the conspiracy on count one. I
say this with respect for the experienced trial judge's
assessment of the evidence, but I cannot agree with his
conclusion. If the only evidence had been that Gassyt and
Markowitz were "delighted to be rid" of Dulmage without more, the
trial judge would have been correct. However, there was much
more. I note the following from Carter's testimony:
*    there was evidence from which the jury could infer that
     Carter's call to arrange a meeting came as no surprise
 
*    there was discussion about arrangements previously made with
     Krakowski with respect to payment for the job
 
*    monies were paid to Carter for the killing of Dulmage
 
*    there was discussion about the disposal of Dulmage's body
     and of his belongings
 
*    there was evidence that the accused wanted to get rid of
     Krakowski because his killing would eliminate another person who
     knew about Dulmage's killing.
 
[19] Consequently, it is my view that the trial judge erred in
directing a verdict of acquittal on count one. I would allow the
Crown appeal, set aside the verdict of acquittal, and order a new
trial on count one.
 
C.   Crown Appeal on Count Three: Conspiracy to murder an unknown
     person
[20] It was the defence position at trial that a verdict of
acquittal should be directed on counts two and three on the basis
that there was no concluded agreement. The trial judge was of the
view that there was "ample evidence to be considered by the jury
on count No. 2". However, on the third count, he concluded:
 
          On Count No. 3 I have no problem with it
          because in my view there is no evidence that
          a jury should consider. It is not a matter of
          whether they are properly instructed. In my
          view there just isn't any evidence admissible
          that the Court has heard.
It would appear from comments made during the course of the
submissions that the trial judge was of the view that there was
no evidence because the evidence of the money on Bernstein was
not before the jury. The Crown also challenges the trial judge's
ruling on the admissibility of this evidence.
 
[21] As indicated earlier, the Crown sought to introduce evidence
from the booking officer that Bernstein had a large sum of money
on him at the time of his arrest. The defence objected to the
introduction of this evidence. During the course of counsel's
submissions, the trial judge commented as follows:
 
          All he [Bernstein] is at the moment is an
          unindicted co-conspirator. How can anything
          involving him be admissible in a trial
          against these two men?
The trial judge ruled that, without calling Bernstein as a
witness, the evidence was inadmissible.
 
[22] With respect, if the trial judge was indeed of the view, as
his comments and ruling would suggest, that no evidence involving
an unindicted co-conspirator could be admissible, he was clearly
wrong. It is settled law that acts and declarations by one
conspirator in furtherance of the common design are admissible
against a co-conspirator. It is equally settled that the fact
that the actor or declarant, in this case Bernstein, is
unindicted is no obstacle to the admission of the evidence:
Cloutier v. R. (1939), 73 C.C.C. 1 at 6-7 (S.C.C.).
 
[23] The evidence involving Bernstein was highly relevant and it
was provisionally admissible against Gassyt and Markowitz,
subject to the principles set out in R. v. Carter (1982), 67
C.C.C. (2d) 568 (S.C.C.). The jury would have to be instructed
that the ultimate use which could be made of this evidence would
be subject to the three stages set out in Carter.
 
[24] Stage one calls for the jury to determine whether on all the
evidence they are satisfied beyond a reasonable doubt that the
conspiracy charged in the indictment existed.  If the jury answer
this question affirmatively, they must then decide whether, on
the basis of the evidence directly receivable against the
accused, they are satisfied that he probably was a member of this
conspiracy.  If they so conclude, the jury can then consider the
whole of the evidence, including the acts and declarations of co-
conspirators performed and made in furtherance of the conspiracy,
to determine whether they are satisfied beyond a reasonable doubt
that the accused was a member of the conspiracy and, accordingly,
guilty.
 
[25] Bernstein's "act" in carrying the money concealed on him
would be evidence against the accused which the jury could
consider at stage three of the process if they were satisfied, on
the basis of evidence directly receivable against Bernstein, that
Bernstein was probably a member of the conspiracy and that the
act was done in furtherance of the conspiracy. The presence of
$18,000 on his person, $10,000 of which was in his sock, and his
association with Gassyt and Markowitz on the day in question,
would certainly constitute two items of evidence directly
admissible against him. In assessing the evidence directly
admissible against Bernstein, the jury would have to be reminded
that they need not view this evidence in isolation. It is not
only permissible but sometimes necessary to put the acts and
declarations in context in order to interpret their meaning. See
R. v. Baron and Wertman (1976), 31 C.C.C. (2d) 525 (Ont. C.A.);
R. v. Filiault and Kane (1981), 63 C.C.C. (2d) 321 at 326 (Ont.
C.A.); R. v. White and Sennet (1997), 114 C.C.C. (3d) 225 at 268
(Ont. C.A.).
 
[26] For these reasons, it is my view that the trial judge erred
in not allowing the Crown to admit this evidence without calling
Bernstein. Although it was hearsay, it was subject to the co-
conspirator's exception to the hearsay rule as set out above.
 
[27] Had this evidence been admitted, there would have been
sufficient evidence to go to the jury on count three. Therefore,
the trial judge's error on this issue necessitates a new trial. I
would  allow the Crown appeal on count three, set aside the
verdict of acquittal, and order a new trial on count three.
 
D.   Appeal Against Conviction: Conspiracy to Murder Krakowski
[28] Gassyt and Markowitz each contend that the verdict of guilt
on count two should be set aside as unreasonable and that a
verdict of acquittal should be entered. In the alternative, they
seek a new trial on the following grounds:
*    the proposed fresh evidence
 
*    the trial judge's failure to provide a limiting instruction
     on the use to be made of the evidence on counts one and three
 
*    the failure to instruct the jury fully on the proof required
     to establish conspiracy in accordance with the three stages in
     Carter
 
The last ground is raised only by Gassyt.
 
     1. Unreasonable Verdict
[29] It is beyond dispute that Carter's evidence, if believed, is
capable of supporting the verdict of guilt on count two. However,
Gassyt and Markowitz contend that it was unreasonable for the
jury to rely on Carter's evidence on the key issue of Markowitz's
participation in the conversation during the second meeting. In
support of this contention, they essentially rely on the
surveillance photos that showed that the window on the Lincoln
Continental was closed with Markowitz sitting alone in the car.
It is equally beyond dispute that without evidence of Markowitz's
participation, there cannot be a conspiracy as alleged.
 
[30] There is no doubt that the inconsistency in the evidence on
this issue was a serious one. However, Carter's testimony and the
surveillance evidence were not entirely inconsistent with one
another. The surveillance photos did not cover the entire period
of time and there was some confusion in the evidence as to the
timing of the photos and of the meeting. It was open to the jury
to accept the surveillance evidence and also believe Carter on
the essence of his testimony. Carter was cross-examined at length
on this issue and at no time retreated from his firm testimony
that Markowitz had fully participated in the conversation. Carter
also offered an explanation that the window to the car may have
been rolled up towards the end of the meeting when it started to
rain. He also testified that he was not certain of the chronology
of the conversation set out in his notes since he had prepared
the notes some time after the meeting. It was open to the jury to
accept this explanation. On the totality of the evidence, they
could find that a three-way conversation as alleged could have
taken place and, if they believed Carter, they could find that it
had indeed taken place at some point in time during the course of
the second meeting in the parking lot.
 
[31] Consequently, I would not give effect to this ground of
appeal.
 
     2. The Proposed Fresh Evidence
[32] The authority to admit fresh evidence on appeal is found in
s.683(1) of the Criminal Code, where the court determines that it
is "in the interests of justice" to do so. The oft-cited criteria
for the admission of fresh evidence are set out in Palmer and
Palmer v. R. (1979), 50 C.C.C. (2d) 193 at 205 (S.C.C.) as
follows:
 
          (1)  the evidence should generally not be
               admitted if, by due diligence, it could
               have been adduced at trial provided that
               this general principle will not be
               applied as strictly in a criminal case
               as in civil cases:  see McMartin v. The
               Queen, [1965] 1 C.C.C. 142, 46 D.L.R.
               (2d) 372, [1964] S.C.R. 484;
          (2)  the evidence must be relevant in the
               sense that it bears upon a decisive or
               potentially decisive issue in the trial;
          (3)  the evidence must be credible in the
               sense that it is reasonably capable of
               belief, and
          (4)  it must be such that if believed it
               could reasonably, when taken with the
               other evidence adduced at trial, be
               expected to have affected the result.
[33] The proposed fresh evidence meets the first three criteria.
It would appear that the investigation into Carter's alleged
wrongdoing started after the trial was over. Therefore, it could
not have been adduced at trial.  The evidence is relevant in the
sense described in Palmer since it bears on the credibility of a
key Crown witness on a potentially decisive issue at trial. As
stated above, if Carter's testimony about what transpired at the
second meeting was not accepted because it conflicted with the
surveillance photos, there would have been no evidence of
conspiracy on the second count. Finally, it is conceded by the
Crown that the evidence, which results from an official police
investigation,  is reasonably capable of belief.
 
[34] The real question on this fresh evidence application is
whether the evidence, if believed, could reasonably be expected
to affect the result. Counsel for Gassyt seeks a new trial on the
basis of this fresh evidence so as to allow the defence to cross-
examine Carter on the fact that he is facing outstanding criminal
charges and on the underlying facts in support of the charges.
Counsel for Markowitz takes the position that the underlying
facts are irrelevant and that cross-examination of the witness on
the fact that he faces outstanding criminal charges alone could
reasonably be expected to affect the result. Both counsel rely on
Titus v. R. (1983), 2 C.C.C. (3d) 321 (S.C.C.).
 
[35] In my view, the principles set out in Titus do not assist
the defence in this case. In Titus, the court held that the trial
judge was in error in precluding a Crown witness from being cross-
examined on an outstanding indictment preferred against him (at
p.323-24):
 
          It is not by any means impossible that the
          jury's verdict might have been affected if
          they had known the facts of Miehm's
          background and the motive which it afforded
          him to favour the prosecution's case
          [Emphasis added.].
          ...
          [C]ross-examination of a Crown witness
          concerning an outstanding indictment against
          that witness is proper and admissible for the
          purpose of showing a possible motivation to
          seek favour with the prosecution  [Emphasis
          in original.].
          I think it essential to stress the purpose
          for which the cross-examination is permitted,
          namely, in order that the defence may explore
          to the full all factors which might expose
          the frailty of the evidence called by the
          prosecution.  That the accused as he stands
          in the prisoner's box on trial for murder is
          deemed to be innocent until proven guilty
          beyond a reasonable doubt is one of the
          fundamental presumptions inherent in the
          common law and as such the accused is
          entitled to employ every legitimate means of
          testing the evidence called by the Crown to
          negative that presumption and in my opinion
          this includes the right to explore all
          circumstances capable of indicating that any
          of the prosecution witnesses had a motive for
          favouring the Crown.
[36] In my view, the decision in Titus should not be interpreted
so as to abrogate the general principle that all persons who
stand charged with a criminal offence are presumed innocent of
that offence. In keeping with the presumption of innocence, the
fact that a witness is charged with an offence will generally
have no bearing on his or her credibility and cross-examination
on that fact will not be permitted. An exception is recognized in
circumstances where, as was the case in Titus, the witness may
have a motive to favour the prosecution by reason of the
outstanding charge.  In these circumstances, the outstanding
charge is obviously relevant to credibility and cross-examination
on that fact will be permitted.
 
[37] Cross-examination on any facts underlying an outstanding
charge is a different matter. In any case, an ordinary witness,
unlike an accused, may be cross-examined on the underlying facts
to the extent that these facts may be relevant to the witness's
credibility or to another issue in the trial. This distinction
between cross-examination with respect to the existence of
charges which have not resulted in a conviction, and cross-
examination on the underlying facts of the outstanding charges is
made in  R. v. Gonzague (1983), 4 C.C.C. (3d) 505 at 510-11 (Ont.
C.A.):
 
          Clearly, the fact that a person is charged
          with an offence cannot degrade his character
          or impair his credibility, but an ordinary
          witness, unlike an accused, may be cross-
          examined with respect to misconduct on
          unrelated matters which has not resulted in a
          conviction:  see R. v. Davison, DeRosie and
          McArthur (1974), 20 C.C.C. (2d) 424 at 443-4,
          6 O.R. (2d) 103 [C.A.].  Consequently,
          counsel was entitled to cross-examine the
          witness, Charbonneau, on the facts underlying
          the 15 charges of fraud in order to impeach
          his credibility.
          There was an additional basis also upon which
          defence counsel would have been entitled to
          cross-examine Charbonneau with respect to the
          pending charges if a proper foundation had
          been laid [Emphasis added.].
 
 
The court goes on to quote the above-noted passages from Titus.
See also R. v. Hoilett (1991), 4 C.R. (4th) 372 at 376 (Ont.
C.A.).
 
[38] It is therefore clear that cross-examination of a witness on
the fact that the witness is facing an outstanding charge will
not be permitted without a proper foundation being laid to
establish its relevance. It will be relevant, for example, where
the outstanding charge can be shown to provide the witness with a
motive to favour the prosecution. In this case, no basis has been
advanced to show relevance.  It is certainly clear that no motive
to favour the prosecution could be shown. Carter testified long
before he was charged with any offence. Indeed, the investigation
into his alleged wrongdoings had not even started at the time of
trial.  Therefore, the defence should not be permitted to cross-
examine Carter on the fact that he is facing outstanding charges.
 
[39] This would leave the defence with the opportunity to cross-
examine on the underlying facts. The facts relied upon by the
Crown in support of the charges of theft, fraud and breach of
trust are complex and involve forensic accounting reports. The
scope of any cross-examination on those facts would necessarily
have to be curtailed in order to keep it within reasonable
limits. More importantly, while these facts are of some relevance
to Carter's credibility, they are irrelevant on any other issue
in the trial. They are therefore clearly collateral. It is well
settled that, on collateral matters, the defence would be bound
by Carter's answers and could not call other witnesses to
contradict him: Attorney General v. Hitchcock (1847), 1 Ex. 91,
R. v. Cassibo (1982), 70 C.C.C. (2d) 498 at 506 (Ont. C.A.).  If
his answers should consist of general denials, a situation which
is not unreasonable to expect given his plea of not guilty to the
outstanding charges, there would be no evidence at the end of the
day to contradict him and, depending on the course of the cross-
examination, it may well become incumbent upon the trial judge to
instruct the jury accordingly.
 
[40] In these circumstances, I am of the view that the proposed
fresh evidence, even if it had been admitted at trial, could not
reasonably be expected to have affected the result. Consequently,
I would dismiss the motion to admit the fresh evidence and would
not give effect to this ground of appeal.
 
     3. The Failure to Provide a Limiting Instruction
[41] In their respective factums, Gassyt and Markowitz take the
position that the evidence in relation to counts one and three,
in respect of which there was a directed verdict of acquittal,
was inadmissible on count two and that it was therefore incumbent
upon the trial judge to instruct the jury to disregard this
evidence. They submit that his failure to do so constitutes
reversible error. At the hearing of the appeals, counsel
somewhat shifted their position and conceded that much, if not
all, of the evidence adduced on counts one and three was relevant
to and admissible on count two. Nonetheless, it is argued that
the trial judge should have given a limiting instruction on the
use which could be made of this evidence.
 
[42] The Crown takes the position that all of the evidence heard
by the jury was properly admissible on the remaining count and
that no limiting instruction was required. In any event, Crown
counsel argues that the trial judge did give a limiting
instruction after the close of the Crown's case and before the
commencement of the defence evidence when he stated as follows:
 
               Members of the jury, on Friday we
          resolved two of the three counts, Count No. 1
          and count No.3. That's the one involving Mr.
          Dulmage and the one involving the unindicted
          co-conspirator, Dr. Bernstein. I have decided
          in law that there is no evidence upon which
          those counts could be substantiated, so I am
          directing you to find a verdict of not guilty
          on Count 1 and Count 3. You can do so by
          simply a nod because it's a matter of law.
               That leaves us with the one count which
          really pertains to what's been referred to as
          the second meeting. So that is the only count
          that you will be dealing with in your
          deliberations.
[43] Crown counsel also notes that, in his charge, the trial
judge focussed his review of the evidence on the second meeting
which was most directly relevant to count two. In reply, the
defence points to three occasions when the trial judge also
instructed the jury to decide the case on the basis of all of the
evidence they had heard.
 
[44] I do not think it can seriously be maintained that any of
the evidence which directly pertained to counts one and three was
inadmissible on count two. Had the trial proceeded from the
beginning on count two alone, I would expect that much the same
evidence would have been called.  Most of it was essential to
provide the context for the evidence of the second meeting which,
everyone agrees, forms the core of the evidence on count two.
 
[45] Further, the evidence with respect to counts one and three
was highly relevant to the issue of motive on count two. It was
Carter's evidence that Gassyt and Markowitz wanted Krakowski
killed because he had been "fucking them around for years."
Krakowski's failure to pay a debt, which was to be applied in
partial payment for the murder of Dulmage, was but one example.
Further, killing Krakowski would eliminate another person who
knew what was going on. Finally, they also needed Krakowski
eliminated before they could move on to something else. An
inference could be drawn from the evidence that the killing of
the unknown person as alleged in count three was part of what
they wanted to move on to after Krakowski's elimination.
 
[46] Therefore, the trial judge was correct in instructing the
jury that they could consider the whole of the evidence they had
heard. He also effectively focussed his review of the evidence on
the second meeting which was more directly probative of count
two. However, I am of the view that, having regard to the fact
that the trial judge had directed a verdict of acquittal on
counts one and three, it was incumbent on him to instruct the
jury on the limited use they could make of the other evidence,
particularly with respect to the first meeting.
 
[47] The evidence relating to count one, if accepted by the jury,
was rather cogent. Notwithstanding the fact that the jury brought
in a verdict of not guilty on that count in accordance with the
judge's direction, they may have been persuaded by this evidence
that the accused had conspired to kill Dulmage. In the absence of
a limiting instruction, there is, in my view, a real danger that
the jury may have concluded that the accused conspired to kill
Krakowski largely on the basis that they had conspired to kill
Dulmage. The evidence relating to count three may have had a
similar effect. They were not entitled to use the evidence in
this way and should have been instructed that they could not
embark on this forbidden line of reasoning. There is a danger
that the jury may have arrived at the verdict of guilt without
scrutinizing the evidence on count two to the extent that they
should have. The importance of this limiting instruction is
highlighted when one considers the serious inconsistencies in the
evidence on count two.
 
[48] Of course, the failure to give this kind of limiting
instruction would not necessarily be fatal in every case. The
fact that defence counsel did not object to the charge on this
point is also of relevance. Nonetheless, in the peculiar
circumstances of this case, I am of the view that the failure to
give a limiting instruction resulted in an unsafe verdict on
count two.
 
[49] I would therefore give effect to this ground of appeal and
order a new trial on count two.
 
     4. The Failure to Instruct the Jury in Accordance With
Carter
[50] As stated earlier in this judgment, the Supreme Court of
Canada has set out the correct three-stage instruction to the
jury in conspiracy cases in R. v. Carter. In this case, the trial
judge did not instruct the jury in accordance with the second
step. He did not instruct them that they had to decide whether,
on the basis of the evidence directly receivable against each
accused, they were satisfied that each was probably a member of
the conspiracy. Counsel for the Crown objected to the  charge on
this point but the trial judge refused to recharge the jury,
stating that he could not see how this instruction would have any
application to this case.
 
[51] Counsel for Gassyt submits that this alleged failure was
fatal. Crown counsel argues that, on the unique facts of this
case, where the evidence directly receivable against each accused
was essentially the same, the trial judge did not err in omitting
the second stage instruction. Counsel submits that, in fact, this
instruction would only have served to confuse the jury. Counsel
for Markowitz agrees with the Crown's position.
 
[52] In my view, the trial judge's instruction was correct on
this point. On the evidence before the jury, the second stage
Carter instruction would not have served any useful purpose and
could potentially have confused them. The same reasoning may not
apply at the conclusion of the new trial. As set out earlier, the
admission of the evidence concerning Bernstein would require a
full Carter instruction.
 
E.   Disposition
[53] For these reasons, I would allow the Crown appeal against
the verdicts of acquittal, set aside the verdicts, and order a
new trial on counts one and three. I would dismiss the Crown
appeal against sentence as moot. I would also allow the defence
appeals against conviction, set aside the convictions, and order
a new trial on count two. I would dismiss the defence appeals
against sentence as abandoned.
