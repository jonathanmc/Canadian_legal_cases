RE:            HER MAJESTY THE QUEEN (Respondent) v. FARID GENDEY

               Brian McNeely, for the respondent
 
HEARD:         August 19, 1998
 
                          ENDORSEMENT
 
[1]  The appellant was charged with three counts of sexual
assault, one count of sexual assault with a weapon and one count
of sexual interference in relation to three complainants.
Following his trial by judge alone, he was acquitted on the count
of sexual assault with a weapon and convicted on the three counts
of sexual assault. The charge of sexual interference was stayed
on the basis of the principle in R. v. Kienapple, [1975] 1 S.C.R.
729. He was sentenced to a total of four years' imprisonment. The
appellant appeals against his conviction on the three counts of
sexual assault and seeks leave to appeal his sentence.
[2]  The appellant seeks leave to introduce fresh evidence with
respect to the credibility of one of the complainants and raises
several grounds of appeal. One of the grounds of appeal relates
to the use made by the trial judge of the evidence on one count
in the indictment to support findings of guilt on the other
counts. We are of the view that the trial judge erred in this
respect and that this ground alone necessitates a new trial.
[3]  The trial judge held as follows:
               The evidence of the three complainants
          is reciprocally confirmatory on the issue of
          the system adopted by the accused. In this
          context the evidence of the other
          complainants is properly admissible to show
          the system adopted by the accused.  (See
          Regina v. Green (1988), 40 C.C.C. (3d) 333
          (S.C.C.), and Regina v. C. (M.H.) (1991), 63
          C.C.C. (3d) 385 (S.C.C.)).
               In my assessment, the probative value of
          the evidence on that issue outweighs its
          prejudicial effect: among other factors, the
          acts involving the playing of video games
          with young boys in his apartment is such a
          strikingly unusual feature that the
          similarities cannot be attributed to
          coincidence.  I have at the same time
          remained aware of the need not to use the
          evidence of the three complainants viewed
          cumulatively as evidence of propensity.  (See
          Regina v. B. (F.F.) (1993), 79 C.C.C. (3d)
          112 (S.C.C.) and cases referred to therein,
          Regina v. R. (G.) (1993) 80 C.C.C. (3d) 130
          (O.C.A.)).
               I note that the testimony of all three
          complainants was consistent with respect to
          the details of the approach taken by the
          accused.  As well, all three gave evidence
          that the accused did have Atari games in his
          apartment and that he played these games with
          them.  I accept the evidence of each of them
          on this point and, as noted before, I reject
          the evidence of the accused.
[4]  While the trial judge was correct in her view that the
evidence on any one count could be confirmatory of the evidence
on the other counts if it showed Aa system adopted by the
accused@, we are of the view that the evidence does not support
this finding. There was no evidence that the accused had played
Atari games with any of the complainants. While the testimony of
the complainants established that each had played games in the
appellant=s apartment, their evidence did not go further to
establish a system adopted by the appellant in relation to the
sexual assaults.
[5]  It is clear from the totality of the reasons that the trial
judge placed much emphasis on the confirmatory nature of the
evidence of the three complainants in rejecting the appellant=s
testimony and in ultimately finding him guilty. Therefore the
misapprehension of evidence necessitates a new trial.
[6]  In light of our conclusion on this ground of appeal, we do
not find it necessary to deal with the other grounds raised in
support of the request for a new trial. We see no merit in the
two grounds raised in support of the request for an acquittal.
Further, in light of this result, it is not necessary to make a
ruling on the admissibility of the fresh evidence. Nonetheless,
we find it imperative to express our concern with respect to the
manner in which the recantation was obtained from the young
complainant.
[7]  The appeal against conviction is allowed, the three
convictions are quashed, the stay on the count of sexual
interference is lifted and a new trial is ordered on the four
counts.
