                      ENDORSEMENT
                               
                              
[1]  The defendants / appellants appeal on two issues only –
 
first, whether the respondent was obliged to make a claim under
 
her employer’s long term disability plan and, second, the order
 
for payment of solicitor and client costs.
 
[2]  We find no reversible error in the trial judge’s finding
 
that the plaintiff / respondent, Angeline Gibson, was not
 
obligated to make a claim on her long term disability policy and
 
in not reducing her award for future wage loss to account for
 
same. The trial judge properly pointed out that the plaintiff was
 
not yet entitled to make any such claim and that such a claim was
 
subject to contingencies. Further, the trial judge followed the
 
recommended procedure by Morden J. in Cox v. Carter (1976), 13
 
O.R. (2d) 717 in dealing with future contingencies, as reflected
 
in section 5 of the judgment entered.
 
[3]  With respect to costs, the trial judge found that a written
 
offer to settle contained in a pre-trial memorandum could
 
constitute an offer to settle within the meaning of Rule 49 of
 
the Rules of Civil Procedure. This case is quite different on its
 
facts from the decision of this court in Bell v. Olympia & York
 
Developments Limited et al., 17 O.R. (3d) 135. In that case, the
 
respondent, in an appeal as to costs only, attempted to put
 
before this court offers to settle made following the pre-trial
 
judge’s recommendation. In this case, the trial judge was
 
informed, subsequent to his decision on the merits, of an offer
 
to settle included in the pre-trial memorandum. This offer
 
remained open for acceptance until the presentation of a second
 
enhanced offer. Both were more beneficial to the defendants /
 
appellants than the result at trial. The trial judge found that
 
the offer to settle included in the pre-trial memorandum
 
constituted an offer within the meaning of Rule 49 of the Rules
 
of Civil Procedure. In our view, the trial judge did not proceed
 
on an erroneous principle of law and exercised his discretion as
 
authorized by the Rules in awarding costs.

 

[4]  The plaintiff cross-appeals the trial judge’s award for lost
 
opportunity in respect of obtaining a vice-principalship. The
 
trial judge applied a contingency factor of ninety per cent to
 
the award in this regard. The plaintiff seeks on the cross-appeal
 
to raise the award from $10,000.00 to $63,000.00. The trial judge
 
gave full and proper consideration to this issue in his lengthy
 
reasons for judgment. We do not see any reason for interfering
 
with his award.
 
[5]  We were made aware of two arithmetic errors which, off-set
 
one against the other, result in an increase in the judgment of
 
$6,251.16. The amount of the judgment is thus amended to reflect
 
that increase. The appeal and the cross-appeal are otherwise
 
dismissed. Because of the mixed success, there will be no order
 
as to costs.
