                     ENDORSEMENT

[1]    The appellants made three submissions:
    (1) the respondent was not entitled to terminate the
        agreement in June 1989;
    (2) if the respondent was entitled to terminate the
        agreement, its failure to accept the appellants'
        valid tender on September 15, 1989 was a breach of
        its duty to take reasonable steps to avoid the loss;
    (3) the appellants are entitled to a return of their deposit.


[2]    We did not call on the respondent on the first issue. We
agree with the trial judge that the respondent was entitled to
terminate the agreement . The appellants concede that, having
lost on this issue, they are not entitled to the return of their
deposit.


[3]    The main issue on this appeal, therefore, is whether the
respondent acted reasonably in refusing to accept the appellants'
tender.


[4]    When one party to a contract breaches the contract, the
law requires the innocent party to take reasonable steps to avoid
the loss. These reasonable steps may include an obligation to
accept a subsequent offer from the defaulting party. The trial
judge found that the appellants were ready, willing and able to
complete the transaction on the closing date of September 15,
1989 and made a valid tender on that date. Regrettably, however,
the trial judge did not address the question whether the
respondent acted reasonably in refusing to accept this tender.


[5]    Although this is a question of fact, we are of the view
that we can resolve this question on the material before us.
Looking at the question from the respondent's perspective, we are
not persuaded that the respondent did act reasonably in refusing
to accept the tender.


[6]    The respondent submits that it was justified in its
refusal because it was dealing with difficult purchasers. The
respondent points to the aspenite issue and to the position taken
by the appellants on the third deposit. We do not agree that
these considerations justified the respondent's refusal to close.


[7]    The appellants had no obligation to sign the aspenite
amending agreement proffered by the respondent. And even though
the appellants had no such obligation, they did eventually sign
this agreement and they tendered it on closing.


[8]    The appellants did not wilfully withhold payment of the
third deposit. They simply did not have the money to make the
deposit until the end of June 1989. Although they may have
overstated their position in their June 27, 1989 letter, the
appellants had reason to think that the respondent would extend
the time for payment until the end of June. Moreover, by
September 15 the house was 60% completed and once finished, the
parties would not have had a continuing relationship with each
other. Finally, the trial judge found that the residential real
estate market was declining in the fall of 1989 and the
respondent must therefore have known that refusing the
appellants' tender would inevitably result in a sale of the
property at a substantially lower price. For all of these reasons
we do not think that the respondent acted reasonably in refusing
to close with the appellants on September 15, 1989.


[9]    Therefore the appeal is allowed and paragraph 2 of the
judgment of Dilks J. is set aside. In its place there shall be an
order that the balance of the plaintiff's claim is dismissed.


[10]   The court heard the submissions of counsel on costs. There
shall be no costs of the trial. The appellants shall have their
costs of the appeal.
