McKINLAY J.A.:

[1]   This  is  an  appeal of a judgment of  the  Honourable  Mr.
Justice Ground, dated April 24, 1997, and reported as Re Goodyear
Canada  Inc. and Burnhamthorpe Square Inc.; Canada Life Assurance
Company,  Intervener,  32  O.R. (3d)  657.  By  order  of  Morden
A.C.J.O.  the cross-appeal of Burnhamthorpe and Canada  Life  was
argued as an appeal, and the appeal of Goodyear was argued  as  a
cross-appeal.


[2]  The matter commenced by application before Ground J. brought
by  Goodyear,  a  tenant of premises owned by Burnhamthorpe.  The
facts  are set out below as outlined by Ground J. in his reasons,
but  with some minor alterations and additions interspersed which
may be relevant to the appeal.


[3]   In  1979, Saracini Investments Ltd. ("Saracini")  and  Four
Twenty-Seven    Investments   Limited    ("Four    Twenty-Seven")
established  a  commercial office development in  Etobicoke  that
included two buildings municipally known as 21 Four Seasons Place
and 10 Four Seasons Place.


[4]   The  development  was constructed with  the  assistance  of
financing   provided   by   Excelsior  Life   Insurance   Company
("Excelsior"),  which  later  changed  its  name  to  Aetna  Life
Insurance  Company  of  Canada ("Aetna"). Four  Twenty-Seven  and
Saracini  granted  a  first mortgage against the  development  to
Aetna  in  October of 1979. This mortgage contained  a  provision
which   purported  to  assign  all  present  and  future   leases
pertaining to any part of the development to the mortgagee.


[5]   Saracini  and  Four  Twenty-Seven each  also  entered  into
separate  assignments of rents agreements  in  favour  of  Aetna,
which  were  registered  on title in November,  1979  (the  "1979
Assignments").


[6]   Goodyear had been a tenant at 21 Four Seasons  Place  since
December, 1980 pursuant to a lease made as of December 1, 1980. A
Notice  of Agreement to Lease was registered on title by Goodyear
on  September  18,  1980. On or about May 25, 1981  Goodyear  and
Aetna  entered  into an Attornment and Non-Disturbance  Agreement
with  respect  to the 1980 lease. On the same day,  Four  Twenty-
Seven  and Saracini gave Goodyear a Notice of Assignment of Rents
and Leases, referring to the 1979 assignments to Aetna.


[7]   On or about December 20, 1982, Aetna took a second mortgage
against the development which was registered on title on December
21,  1982. This mortgage was given as collateral security for the
indebtedness  secured by the first mortgage and, along  with  the
first  mortgage, covered all the property of the development.  It
also   contained   a  clause  wherein  the  mortgagor   expressly
covenanted to perform and observe all of the covenants set out in
the  first  mortgage. On or about the same day, Four Twenty-Seven
and  Aetna  executed and registered on title a further Assignment
of  Rents  Agreement (the "1982 assignment"). The 1982 assignment
was  in  the  same form as the 1979 assignments, and assigned  in
favour  of Aetna all rents and leases pertaining to any  part  of
the development.


[8]   On  or about May 22, 1984 Canada Life took a third mortgage
against the development which was registered on title on May  28,
1984.  Angeles Investment Properties Inc. ("Angeles"),  the  then
owner  of  the  development, also provided  Canada  Life  with  a
General  Assignment  of  Rents  made  May  22,  1984  which   was
registered on title on May 25, 1984 (the "1984 assignment").


[9]   In  the  summer of 1987, Goodyear negotiated to reduce  its
space  in  21  Four Seasons Place and to take over a  substantial
part  of  the office tower at 10 Four Seasons Place. As  part  of
this  arrangement,  Goodyear negotiated a letter  agreement  with
Angeles  dated  July 30, 1987. As part of this letter  agreement,
the parties agreed, inter alia, that consents and non-disturbance
agreements  would  be  obtained from all existing  mortgagees  to
either  a surrender of the 1980 lease and the substitution  of  a
new lease for a reduced portion of 21 Four Seasons Place or to an
amendment  of  the 1980 lease. Ultimately, two  new  leases  (the
"1987 leases") were executed, but the consents of mortgagees  and
non-disturbance agreements were not. The rental income from these
leases  represents  over 30 per cent of the total  rental  income
from  the  development. The total rent due over the remainder  of
the original term of the 1987 leases is over $11,000,000.


[10]  In  the  summer of 1992, V&A Properties  Inc.  ("V&A")  was
appointed  by  Angeles as property manager  of  the  development.
Shortly  thereafter, V&A and Goodyear began to discuss Goodyear's
occupancy  costs in both buildings. Commercial leasing rates  had
declined  considerably from 1987 and Goodyear  was  seeking  rent
relief  in  return  for an extension in the  terms  of  the  1987
leases.


[11]  Goodyear and Angeles were unable to negotiate an acceptable
basis for a reduction in the rental rate and an extension of  the
terms of the 1987 leases and ultimately V&A advised Goodyear that
Angeles was not willing to consider a buy-out of the 1987 leases.


[12]  In  February of 1993, Aetna assigned the first  and  second
mortgages  to  Canada  Life  and  entered  into  Assignments   of
Assignments  of Rents with Canada Life which were  registered  on
title.


[13]  In June of 1993, Angeles went into default under the first,
second  and third mortgages. Canada Life appointed Ernst &  Young
Inc. to be receiver and manager of the development. Ernst & Young
Inc.  administered the development as agent of  Angeles  and  V&A
remained as property manager.


[14]  Ernst  &  Young was discharged as receiver and  manager  in
December,  1994.  On December 12, 1994 Ernst  &  Young  wrote  to
Goodyear  stating  that Canada Life had assumed  control  of  the
property as of that date and that Goodyear should continue to pay
rent  to  V&A. On December 22, 1994 Canada Life wrote to Goodyear
saying that it was in possession of the property, and instructing
Goodyear to pay rent to V&A.


[15]  In  January of 1995 Goodyear made its rent payment for  the
premises  for  that month by submitting a cheque payable  to  V&A
dated  January  3,  1995 in the amount of  $150,807.38.  V&A,  on
behalf  of Canada Life, accepted the cheque and processed it  for
payment on January 5, 1995.


[16] On August 31, 1995 Canada Life entered into an Agreement  of
Purchase and Sale with V&A, which agreement was later assigned to
Burnhamthorpe,  for  the  acquisition  of  the  development.  The
transaction   was  to  take  place  through  the  assignment   to
Burnhamthorpe  of the first, second and third mortgages  and  the
foreclosure of Angeles by Burnhamthorpe under those mortgages. As
part  of  the purchase price, Canada Life agreed to take  back  a
mortgage, along with an assignment of rents.


[17] On October 26, 1995 Canada Life assigned the three mortgages
and the assignment of rents to Burnhamthorpe. On October 31, 1995
Canada  Life,  acting  in the name of Burnhamthorpe,  obtained  a
judgment for immediate foreclosure against Angeles.


[18]   At   closing  on  December  18,  1995,  Canada  Life   and
Burnhamthorpe  entered  into  an  Assignment  and  Assumption  of
Assumed Contracts and Leases.


[19]  Just prior to the closing of the transaction between Canada
Life  and Burnhamthorpe, Goodyear delivered Estoppel Certificates
to  Burnhamthorpe  in  relation to the 1987  leases,  as  it  was
required  to  do  within  10  days of notice  from  Canada  Life,
pursuant  to  section  21.09  of the 1987  leases.  The  Estoppel
Certificates delivered had been amended from the drafts  prepared
by counsel for Canada Life.


ANALYSIS

[20]  We  start  with the fact that Goodyear  executed  the  1987
leases,  and did intend, at that time, to take advantage of  them
and to be bound by them. Goodyear now wishes to be freed from its
obligations.  It  takes  the  position  that  the   leases   were
terminated  on  December  12, 1994,  when  Ernst  and  Young  was
discharged  as  receiver  and  manager  and  Canada   Life   took
possession and control of the property. Burnhamthorpe  takes  the
position that Goodyear continues to be bound by the terms of  the
leases because:


          1.   It   is  in  privity  of  contract  with
               Burnhamthorpe, as ultimate  assignee  of
               the mortgagees;

          2.   It   is   in  privity  of  estate   with
               Burnhamthorpe, as ultimate  assignee  of
               the mortgagees;

          3.   There  was  an implied contract  between
               Canada Life and Goodyear that both would
               be  bound  by  the  terms  of  the  1987
               leases;

          4.   After    the    mortgagee   went    into
               possession,  both lessee  and  mortgagee
               were  bound  by the terms  of  the  1987
               leases; and

          5.   The  foreclosure  resulted  in  Goodyear
               being  bound  by the 1987 leases  either
               because  they  were enforceable  in  law
               after   foreclosure,  or  Goodyear   was
               estopped     from     denying      their
               enforceability.

1.   PRIVITY OF CONTRACT

[21] Broadly speaking, the doctrine of privity of contract stands
for  the  proposition that a contract cannot, as a general  rule,
confer  rights  or impose obligations arising  under  it  on  any
person  except the parties to it: see London Drugs Ltd. v. Kuehne
&  Nagel International Ltd. et al (1992), 97 D.L.R. (4th) 261  at
343.  However,  as  a  doctrine, it is subject  to  a  number  of
exceptions, some of which are applicable to this case.


     (a)  Privity of Contract - 1980 Lease to Goodyear

[22]  As  stated  above,  Burnhamthorpe agreed  to  purchase  the
property  from Canada Life in the fall of 1995. The purchase  was
carried  out  by Burnhamthorpe taking assignments  of  the  three
mortgages  from Canada Life, and then Canada Life  continuing  to
judgment  in  the name of Burnhamthorpe an action for foreclosure
commenced  by  Canada  Life. On October  31,  1995  Burnhamthorpe
obtained a judgment for immediate foreclosure against a number of
companies registered on title; however, Goodyear was not named in
that  action. Consequently, any rights which Goodyear had in  the
property at the time of the foreclosure were not foreclosed,  but
remained  intact. However, that does not answer the  question  of
whether there were contractual rights between the original lessor
and  lessee which have survived the various assignments  and  the
foreclosure.


[23] Burnhamthorpe, in its argument, refers to the first mortgage
and  Assignment of Rents Agreement entered into between  Saracini
as  mortgagor and Excelsior as mortgagee, both dated October  18,
1979. It relies on a number of factors involving those documents:
first, a provision in the mortgage to the effect that it was Athe
intention  of  the parties to establish an absolute transfer  and
assignment of all such leases and agreements, and all the  avails
thereunder, unto the Mortgagee@; second, that the liabilities and
obligations of the mortgagor were, by the terms of the  mortgage,
binding  on  the  assigns and successors of  the  mortgagor;  and
third,  that  the  Assignment  of Rents  Agreement  entered  into
between Saracini as mortgagor and Excelsior as mortgagee  was  to
secure payment of the first mortgage and to assure performance of
the  agreements contained in the assignments, which included  all
present  and  future  leases of any parts of  the  premises.  The
assignment also included a provision that it would be binding  on
all persons obtaining any interest in the land.


[24]  In  my  view,  none of these factors is  of  assistance  to
Burnhamthorpe. First, the short statement of intent  included  in
the  mortgage  - that the assignment of leases contained  therein
was intended to be absolute - does not avail to overcome numerous
other provisions in the same document which indicate clearly that
the assignment was intended as security only. The landlord was to
continue  to  collect rents and act as landlord in  all  respects
unless  and until any default by the mortgagor in payment of  the
amounts  due  under  the  mortgage. In  any  event,  as  will  be
discussed later in these reasons, it is my view that the question
of  whether the assignment of rents was absolute does not resolve
any issue in this case.


[25]  Second,  the relevant provision in the mortgage  respecting
its  binding  effect  on  successors  and  assigns  reads:  AYall
covenants  , liabilities and obligations entered into or  imposed
hereunder   upon  the  Mortgagor  or  Mortgagors,  Mortgagee   or
Mortgagees shall be equally binding upon his, her or their heirs,
executors,  administrators and assigns or successors and  assigns
as  the case may beY@. That portion of the mortgage binds assigns
to  carry out only covenants, liabilities and obligations of  the
mortgagor  and mortgagee under the mortgage. There  are  no  such
obligations  imposed  on the mortgagee in the  mortgage  document
with  respect to the lessor=s obligations to the lessee. In other
words, there was no indication that the mortgagee intended or was
obliged to carry out any of the obligations of a landlord.


[26]  Third,  the  purpose of the Assignment of Rents  Agreement,
which  was executed at the same time as the mortgage, was  stated
to  be,  ATo  secure the payment of the Mortgage  and  to  assure
performance  of  the  agreements  contained  herein  and  in  the
Mortgage...@. The mortgagor assigned to the mortgagee all present
and future leases of the premises. Present and future lessees are
authorized  and directed by clause 4.7 of the assignment  to  pay
rents  to the mortgagee, without inquiry, on written demand  from
the mortgagee stating that a default has occurred. Similar to the
mortgage, there are no provisions in the assignment which require
the  mortgagee to perform obligations of the landlord  under  the
leases.  Indeed, clause 4.5 of the assignment does precisely  the
reverse.  It  states that the AMortgagee shall not by  reason  of
this  Assignment of Leases and Rents or the exercise of any right
granted  herein  be  obligated to perform any obligation  of  the
lessor  under  any  of  the  LeasesY.@.  This  agreement  clearly
constitutes  an assignment to the mortgagee of rights  under  the
leases but, equally clearly, does not constitute an acceptance by
the  mortgagee  of  the  landlord=s  responsibilities  under  the
leases.  Although the assignment is specifically stated to  enure
to  the  benefit of and be binding on successors and  assigns  of
both the mortgagor and the mortgagee, that provision adds nothing
to  the  rights  and  obligations of the immediate  parties,  and
cannot  expand  in  any way the rights and obligations  of  their
successors  and  assigns. The assignment, without more,  operates
only  as  an  assignment of a chose in action (i.e.  a  debt  for
rental  payments), for which the mortgagee may be able to sue  in
its own name, depending on future events.


[27]  Goodyear's  first lease of a portion of  the  premises  was
obtained  on  December  1,  1980 -  almost  one  year  after  the
execution  of  the  first mortgage and the  Assignment  of  Rents
Agreement  on  October  18,  1979. Thus,  at  the  time  Goodyear
executed that first lease, it was subsequent in time to the first
mortgage, and had no priority in law over it. There is nothing in
the  first mortgage or in the Assignment of Rents Agreement which
gives  future  lessees  any  rights  over  the  first  mortgagee,
although the mortgagee clearly has the right to demand payment of
rent from future lessees on default by the mortgagor.


[28]  Section 53(1) of the Conveyancing and Law of Property  Act,
R.S.O. 1990, c.C-34 permits the assignee of a chose in action  to
sue in his own name on that chose in action. Thus, the mortgagee,
by  virtue of the assignment of rents could sue in its  own  name
for  recovery of those rents. This right is not denied by any  of
the  parties to this litigation. The relevant question is whether
a  party  to a contract (here the mortgagor/lessor Saracini)  can
assign  its obligations under a contract (the leases) to a  third
party  (the  mortgagee), since without the  existence  of  mutual
rights  and  obligations  there can be  no  privity  of  contract
between  the  lessee  and  the  mortgagee.  The  common  law  has
consistently  held  that  obligations  cannot  be  assigned,  and
Burnhamthorpe  acknowledges in its factum that only  by  novation
can obligations under a contract be transferred to a third party.
It fails to explain in what way novation occurred in this case.


[29]  There  are  vital gaps in the argument of Burnhamthorpe  on
this point. It states in paragraphs 38(c) and (d) of its factum:


          (c)  ...   Where   there   is   a   purported
               assignment  of the burden or obligations
               of a contract, what occurs is that there
               may    be   vicarious   or   substituted
               performance  of  the  contract  by   the
               assignee/third   party.   However,   the
               original party (the "assignor")  remains
               liable  in  the contract, and the  other
               party  can sue that "assignor", but  not
               the third party "assignee".

          (d)  As a result, the fact that the mortgagee
               in  the present case did not take on any
               obligations  of  the  lessor  under  the
               leases  is  irrelevant  to  whether  the
               assignments  of the leases are  absolute
               and thus create privity of contract.  ..
               ..  Since  an assignment is absolute  if
               the  entire interest of the assignor  in
               the  chose in action (i.e. in the rights
               under  the  contract) is  for  the  time
               being transferred unconditionally to the
               assignee,  and such occurred  here,  the
               assignments   of  leases  are   absolute
               assignments    under    contract    law.
               [Emphasis added.]

[30]  In  my  view,  the whole basis of this argument  is  flawed
because,  as  I have pointed out earlier in these reasons,  there
was no purported assignment to and acceptance by the mortgagee of
the  burden or obligations of the lease contracts, but  precisely
the  reverse. The argued irrelevance of assignment of obligations
(see  clause  38(d) quoted above) to the question of whether  the
assignment  is  absolute  or by way of  security  only  and  thus
creates  privity of contract, is itself irrelevant  without  some
foundation for the conclusory phrase Aand thus creates privity of
contract@.  Burnhamthorpe gives no authority for the  proposition
that  the  assignment of a major benefit of a contract alone  (in
this  case  rents) creates privity of contract between the  party
primarily liable on the contract and the assignee. In my view, on
the  facts  of this case, privity of contract between the  tenant
and  the mortgagee does not result from the assignment of  rents,
given  the substantial remaining rights and obligations  on  both
sides  of  the lease agreement, none of which was either assigned
by the landlord or accepted by the mortgagee.


[31]  Burnhamthorpe argued that the trial judge erred  in  basing
his  decision that there was no privity of contract  between  the
tenant  and  the mortgagee on his conclusion that the assignments
in question were not absolute assignments, because they were made
by  way  of security only. Its view is that the assignments  were
absolute, since they were specifically stated to be so, and  were
also  stated to be binding as against successors and  assigns  of
both parties.


[32] The question of whether or not an assignment is absolute  or
by  way of security is a vexing one. It is clear that parties  to
an  assignment  cannot, merely by a statement in  the  assignment
that  it is intended to be absolute, make it so as against  third
parties.  If  the  assignment is one of a right only  to  receive
moneys,  such  as an assignment of book debts, the  issue  is  at
least  simple  to comprehend. In Alberta (Treasury  Branches)  v.
M.N.R.,  [1996] l S.C.R. 963 Cory J. dealt with this question  in
the context of a conflict between an assignment of book debts  in
favour  of Alberta Treasury Branches ("A.T.B."), which had loaned
moneys  to a hotel operator, and the Minister of National Revenue
to  which the hotel operator owed moneys on account of income and
excise tax. The relevant provisions in the Income Tax Act and  in
the Excise Tax Act provided for the collection of taxes by way of
garnishment.  It  was  argued  on  behalf  of  A.T.B.  that   its
assignment of book debts was an absolute assignment of  the  book
debts  of  the hotel operator and that, consequently, A.T.B.  was
the  owner of the book debts and, therefore, the garnishee notice
of  the M.N.R. had nothing to which it could attach. Speaking for
the majority, at p. 980, Cory J. said:


          ....  the  same instrument cannot be  both  a
          "security    interest"   and   an   "absolute
          assignment". If an instrument is an  absolute
          assignment,  then since it  is  complete  and
          perfect in itself, there cannot be a residual
          right  remaining with the debtor  to  recover
          the  assets.  By definition, a  complete  and
          perfect   assignment  cannot  recognize   the
          concept  of  an  equity  of  redemption.   An
          absolute  assignment  cannot  function  as  a
          means  of  "securing" the payment of  a  debt
          since  there would be no basis for the debtor
          to  recover  that  which has been  absolutely
          assigned.    An   absolute   assignment    is
          irrevocable. To say that the same  instrument
          can  operate  both as an absolute  assignment
          and   as   a   security   interest   is    to
          simultaneously  put forward two  incompatible
          positions.   The  two  conflicting   concepts
          cannot live together in the same document.

[33]  Although that is a very broad statement, it is  binding  on
this  court,  and clearly resolves any issue as  to  whether  the
assignments in this case are absolute assignments. The assignment
of  existing and future leases in both the mortgages and  in  the
assignment of lease agreements were for purposes of securing  the
mortgage  debt. Numerous provisions in those documents make  this
clear.  However, I agree with counsel for Burnhamthorpe that  the
answer  to that question as between mortgagor and mortgagee  does
not  answer  the question of privity of contract as  between  the
lessee  and the mortgagee. That question is resolved by reference
to the facts as analyzed above.


[34]  An  additional  argument on  the  question  of  privity  of
contract which should be commented on is the execution on May 25,
1981   of  a  non-disturbance  agreement  between  Goodyear   and
Excelsior. That non-disturbance agreement incorporated many,  but
not  all,  of the covenants in the 1980 lease. There is no  doubt
that it created privity of contract as between the lessee and the
mortgagee, but it did so not by virtue of any assignment  by  the
mortgagor  but, rather by virtue of the non-disturbance agreement
itself,  which  was  limited  to its  own  terms.  Although  that
agreement  has  a provision that it is binding on successors  and
assigns of both parties, it refers to a specific lease which  was
no longer in existence after execution of the 1987 leases.


[35] The original mortgagees under the second and third mortgages
also  obtained from the mortgagors assignments of rents. As  with
the assignment of rents to the first mortgagee, these assignments
did  not result in privity of contract between the lessee and the
mortgagees. In addition, neither of those mortgagees  obtained  a
non-disturbance agreement with Goodyear, so there was no  privity
as a result of a direct contract between them.


[36]  For  purposes of completeness I wish to comment shortly  on
one additional matter under the heading of privity of contract. I
agree  with  the  applications judge that  notice  provisions  of
neither  the Registry Act, R.S.O. 1990, c. R.20 nor of  the  Land
Titles  Act, R.S.O. 1990, c. L.5 affect the issue of  privity  of
contract or of estate. In so stating, however, I do not  wish  to
imply  agreement with or dissent from his view that an assignment
of lease could be an "encumbrance" within the meaning of s.lll of
the Registry Act.


[37]  For  the  above reasons, it is my view that, prior  to  the
execution  of the 1987 leases, there was no privity  of  contract
between the mortgagees and the lessee.


     (b)  Privity of Contract B 1987 Leases to Goodyear

[38] In 1987 Goodyear entered into new leases of premises in  the
development  with the then owner, Angeles Investment  Properties,
Inc.  Goodyear vacated some of the space leased in 1980 and  took
possession  of new space. The initial term of the leases  was  to
end  on July 31, 2002. There can be no doubt on the evidence that
these were completely new leases, intended by the parties to them
to  replace the leases entered into in 1980. They were registered
on  title  subsequent to the registration of all three  mortgages
referred to above.


[39]  It  was argued on behalf of Burnhamthorpe that the original
assignments  of  rents and leases made in the early  1980's,  and
referred  to  earlier  in  these  reasons,  were  assignments  of
existing  and  future  rents and leases and,  therefore,  created
privity of contract between the mortgagees and Goodyear as lessee
under  the  1987  leases. This argument  suffers  from  the  same
defects as the argument with respect to privity of contract under
the 1980 leases discussed above.


2.   PRIVITY OF ESTATE

[40]  The background of the concept of privity of estate  in  the
landlord  and tenant context is clearly summarized by Prof.  R.E.
Scane  in  the  1965 Law Society of Upper Canada Special  Lecture
series in his lecture entitled "The Relationship of Landlord  and
Tenant" at pages l and 2, as follows:


          In considering commercial leases....it is too
          easy  to  think of the lease as  a  contract,
          pure  and  simple, and, in turn, to  consider
          the  rights  and obligations of  your  client
          purely in contractual terms. Probably in  the
          greatest majority of cases this suffices. But
          you should never forget that the relationship
          between  landlord and tenant is more  than  a
          contract  -  it also involves  an  estate  in
          land. On that estate, contractual obligations
          may be, and usually are, engrafted....

          You  will  recall that, in English  law,  the
          estate  of  a  tenant or lessee  is  a  "non-
          freehold  estate", or "an  estate  less  than
          freehold". In the eyes of the law, it  is  in
          theory inferior in quality to even the  least
          of  the  freehold estates, the  life  estate,
          even  though  the lease be for  one  thousand
          years.

[41]  Through the ingenuity of the English courts, the rights  of
tenants  evolved over the years from mere rights in  personam  to
rights in rem. Prof. Scane continues at pp. 2 and 3:


          This  right  in  rem gave the leaseholder  an
          estate in land in the true sense, and brought
          the  leasehold within the system  of  tenure,
          i.e., the tenant held an estate in land  from
          his  lord. The result is that an interest  in
          land,  which was originally not part  of  the
          feudal system of tenure at all, is now almost
          the   only  part  of  that  system  with  any
          remaining practical importance.

          Because the relationship is that of a  tenant
          of  an estate to his lord, the parties to the
          original  tenancy are not only....in  privity
          of  contract,  but  are also  in  privity  of
          estate. It is the relationship of privity  of
          estate  which continues between the assignees
          of  the  reversion and the assignees  of  the
          [tenant's  interest in the] lease,  who,  not
          being  the original contracting parties,  are
          no longer in privity of contract.

[42] It appears to me that much of the confusion in the arguments
with respect to privity of estate between the mortgagees and  the
lessee  in  this  case arises because of the  use  of  the  words
"assignment of the lease" when what is meant is assignment of the
landlord's rights under the lease. An assignment of a  lease,  as
referred to in most, though not all, of the authorities, means an
assignment of the term of the lease to a new lessee.


[43]  Canada  Life takes the position that there was  privity  of
estate between it and Goodyear first, by reason of assignment  of
the  leases by Angeles to Aetna, Canada Life's predecessor,  and,
second  by reason of Canada Life having given implied consent  to
the 1987 leases. Privity of estate, it argues, binds both parties
having as interest in the same estate to the terms of the lease.


     (a)   Can  there  be  privity of estate between  lessee  and
mortgagee
          by assignment of lease from mortgagor to mortgagee?

[44]  In  paragraph  54  of its factum  Canada  Life  states  the
following:


          It   is   respectfully  submitted  that   the
          landlord's  reversion  related  to  the  1987
          Leases, was assigned to Aetna pursuant to the
          assignment of future leases contained in  the
          Aetna mortgage and pursuant to the assignment
          of   replacement  leases  and  future  leases
          contained  in  the 1979 Aetna Assignment  and
          the  1982  Aetna Assignment. Aetna's interest
          was  then assigned to Canada Life in February
          1993  pursuant  to  the transfer  of  Aetna's
          Mortgage to Canada Life and pursuant  to  the
          Assignment of Assignment of Rents from  Aetna
          to   Canada  Life  whereby  the  1979   Aetna
          Assignment  and  the 1982  Aetna  Assignments
          were  specifically assigned to  Canada  Life.
          Canada  Life  is  in privity of  estate  with
          Goodyear  in  respect of the 1987  Leases  by
          reason of the assignments. The effect is that
          Canada  Life  may enforce those covenants  of
          the  lease which touch and concern the  land,
          such as the obligation to pay rent. This will
          be  so independent of whether or not there is
          privity of contract between them.

[45]   The  problem with that paragraph is that  the  assignments
referred  to are assignments "of the lease" from the landlord  in
favour  of  mortgagees. Those assignments do  not,  nor  do  they
purport to assign the landlord's reversionary interest. The  most
that  they  can assign is the landlord's interest in  the  lease.
Since they do not assign the reversion, they must assign only the
contractual  rights  of  the  landlord.  The  authorities   cited
establish  simply that privity of estate results  when  a  tenant
assigns  the  term  of  his lease, or the  landlord  assigns  his
reversionary interest.


[46]  Canada  Life takes some comfort from the  decision  of  the
Alberta  Court of Appeal in Re Manufacturer's Life Insurance  Co.
and  J.K.P. Holding Co. Ltd. (1986), 26 D.L.R. (4th) 461. In that
case, a lease was deemed in law to be subsequent to a mortgage by
reason  of the operation of a registration statute. The  landlord
assigned  to the mortgagee "rentals", "lease and rents"  and  the
"lessor's interest in the lease". The lessee acknowledged  notice
of  the  assignment  of lease and rents. The mortgagor  defaulted
under  its mortgage, and the mortgagee obtained a final order  of
foreclosure,  which  was specifically made subject  to  a  caveat
filed by the lessee against the title to the lands.


[47]   Harradence  J.A.,  speaking  for  the  majority,  referred
frequently  in his reasons to "privity", but did not always  make
it  clear  whether  he  was speaking of privity  of  contract  or
privity of estate. He expressed his view that for a lease  to  be
enforceable  against a lessee after foreclosure, the relationship
of  landlord and tenant "must be established through  privity  of
contract or estate with the lessee prior to the foreclosure." His
final conclusion at p. 467 was that:


          both the documentation and the conduct of the
          parties support the conclusion that there was
          an   absolute  assignment  of  the  lease  in
          question  and that privity of estate  between
          the  appellant  and  respondent  was  thereby
          established.

[48] Without reviewing the reasons in that case in detail, it  is
evident that the majority considered an "assignment of lease"  to
be  the  same as an assignment of the reversion. However,  it  is
clear  from  the  facts  that there  was  no  assignment  of  the
landlord's  reversionary interest in the property.  None  of  the
cases  cited by the majority stand for the proposition  that  the
"assignment  of a lease" by a lessor amounts to an assignment  of
the reversion. The facts in the cited cases involve an assignment
of the lessor's reversionary interest in the land. I cannot agree
with the reasoning of the majority. In my view, privity of estate
could not have existed between the mortgagee and lessor prior  to
foreclosure on the facts of that case.


[49]  A  large portion of Canada Life's argument with respect  to
privity  of  estate (as Burnhamthorpe's argument with respect  to
privity  of  contract)  was based on the  issue  of  whether  the
assignments  were  absolute  or by way  of  security.  As  stated
earlier  in these reasons, the decision of the Supreme  Court  of
Canada  in Alberta (Treasury Branches) v. M.N.R., [1996] l S.C.R.
963  and  the nature of the assignments makes it clear  that  the
assignments  in  this case were not absolute. However,  even  had
they been absolute, the subject matter of the assignments was the
leases,  and  not the reversionary interest of the  lessor.  That
being the case, absolute assignments of the leases would not have
resulted  in  privity  of estate between  mortgagee  and  lessee,
because  there  would have been no commonality of estate  between
them.


[50] I agree with the application judge that no privity of estate
between lessee and mortgagee exists in this case (at least  prior
to  foreclosure,  about  which  I will  comment  later  in  these
reasons).  To  the  extent  that he may  have  inferred  that  an
absolute  assignment of the leases would have created privity  of
estate  between  the  lessor and mortgagee, I  would  not  agree,
unless that absolute assignment amounted to an assignment of  the
lessor's reversionary interest in the land.


     (b)   Can  there  be  privity of estate between  lessee  and
mortgagee
          by  reason  of  implied consent to the  leases  by  the
mortgagee?

[51]  It was argued by Canada Life that on the facts of this case
the  court  should find that there was implied consent by  Canada
Life to the 1987 leases. On p. 666 the application judge states:


          In  any event, in my view, even if there  had
          been a written consent to the 1987 leases  by
          Canada  Life  or  its predecessor  mortgagee,
          this  would not create a landlord and  tenant
          relationship based on privity of contract  or
          privity  of estate between the mortgagee  and
          the    tenant.   One   cannot   create   such
          relationship by a unilateral consent  to  the
          lease on the part of the mortgagee.

[52]  I  agree  with that statement as it relates to  privity  of
estate.  Consent  by  the mortgagee could  never  result  in  the
mortgagee and the lessee being in a tenurial relationship,  which
is the basis of privity of estate.


[53]  A  substantial portion of Canada Life's factum relating  to
privity  of estate is devoted to arguments that there was implied
consent  to the leases on the part of Canada Life. This  argument
does not assist in resolving the issue of privity of estate,  but
it  could  assist in resolving the issues of privity of  contract
and estoppel, and will be referred to in that context.




3.   IMPLIED CONTRACT BETWEEN CANADA LIFE AND GOODYEAR

[54]  There  is  substantial evidence as to the  actions  of  the
mortgagor,  mortgagees  and lessee prior  to  and  following  the
execution  of the 1987 leases. The question that arises  from  an
analysis  of  those facts is whether they result in a contractual
relationship  between Canada Life as mortgagee  and  Goodyear  as
tenant, making the terms of the 1987 leases binding on both.


[55]  It is argued that both Goodyear and Canada Life agreed with
Angeles,  and with each other, to be bound by the 1987  leases  -
Goodyear  by signing the non-disturbance agreement,  and  by  its
conduct,  and Canada Life by letters exchanged between solicitors
and by its conduct.


[56]  The  application judge correctly found that  there  was  no
consent  in  writing by Canada Life itself to  be  bound  by  the
leases, and stated in addition, at p. 666:


          The   only   documents   which   could   have
          constituted  consent by Canada  Life  to  the
          1987 leases were the proposed Non-Disturbance
          Agreements  which  were  never  finalized  or
          executed  by all of the parties. The evidence
          appears  to be that Aetna would not sign  the
          NDAs  and accordingly they were never entered
          into  or  assigned  to Canada  Life.  In  any
          event,  in my view, even if there had been  a
          written consent to the 1987 leases by  Canada
          Life or its predecessor mortgagee, this would
          not create a landlord and tenant relationship
          based  on  privity of contract or privity  of
          estate  between the mortgagee and the tenant.
          One  cannot  create  such relationship  by  a
          unilateral consent to the lease on  the  part
          of the mortgagee.

[57]  Because  tenant and mortgagee have interests  in  different
estates,  it  is  clear there cannot be privity of  estate  by  a
unilateral  act of the mortgagee. However, the law  seems  to  be
that if a lease is executed by a mortgagor after the mortgage has
been  granted, the mortgagee will be bound by the  terms  of  the
lease  if  it  is made with his express or implied  consent:  see
Davidson  v.  McKay  (1867), 26 U.C.Q.B.  306;  Canada  Permanent
Building  and Savings Society v. Rowell (1860), 19 U.C.Q.B.  124;
and  District  Bank, Ltd. v. Webb, [1958] l All E.R.  126  (Ch.).
Strangely, although the law seems clear that the mortgagee  would
be  bound in such a situation, whether the tenant would be  bound
without  attorning to the mortgagee is unclear. Thus, the  normal
practice  is  for  the  parties  to  enter  into  non-disturbance
agreements, as were contemplated in this case. Such an  agreement
does  not  depend upon the nature of their respective estates  in
the land, but rather on the general law of contract.


[58]  The  documents  relied on by Canada Life  to  indicate  its
implied  consent  to  the 1987 leases are  primarily  the  leases
themselves  and the non-disturbance agreement which was  executed
by  Goodyear  but  was  never  executed  by  Canada  Life  or  by
Excelsior. The leases include a provision that the landlord  will
have  obtained consent to the new leases from all mortgagees  and
also  non-disturbance agreements from all mortgagees. The  leases
contain no execution date, but are made as of August 1, 1987.  By
letter dated July 30, 1987, Goodyear agreed to execute the leases
on  the undertaking of Angeles that it would obtain the necessary
documentation from the mortgagees. Following substantial exchange
of  draft  documents between the parties, in  September  of  1989
Goodyear executed a non-disturbance agreement. By the end of 1990
the  mortgagees  had still not executed either  consents  to  the
lease or the non-disturbance agreements, although there had  been
additional correspondence between them with respect to both.  The
last correspondence to which we were directed was dated March 19,
1992, and at that time the documents had still not been executed.


[59] The form of non-disturbance agreement which was executed  by
Goodyear  provided  that, in default by the mortgagor  under  any
mortgage,  the  mortgagee  would permit  Goodyear  to  remain  in
possession  of  the  premises on the terms of  the  l987  leases.
Burnhamthorpe and Canada Life take the position that by executing
the lease and the non-disturbance agreement Goodyear agreed to be
bound  by  the  terms of the lease and that, by the  exchange  of
letters between all parties or their counsel, including Goodyear,
Angeles,  Excelsior  and  Canada Life, it  is  evident  that  all
parties intended the lease to be binding on them, and that  there
was, therefore, a binding contract to that effect.


[60] Having reviewed the correspondence between the parties, I do
not  glean  from it any intention by the mortgagees to be  bound.
There  is  no doubt that Goodyear intended to be bound,  but  the
mortgagees  - both sophisticated mortgage lenders represented  by
counsel  --  gave  no unequivocal indication of  such  intention.
Consequently, I see no implied agreement between the  lessee  and
the mortgagees that they be bound by the terms of the lease.


4.   MORTGAGEE GOING INTO POSSESSION

[61]  As I said, it is my view that at the time of the making  of
the 1987 leases and up until at least March of 1992 there was  no
consent  to  the  leases  by the mortgagees,  either  express  or
implied.  However,  toward the end of 1992 matters  were  altered
between the parties by the fact that the mortgages of both  Aetna
and  Canada  Life had matured and were not paid. On February  16,
1993  Canada  Life  purchased the indebtedness  and  security  of
Aetna,  and  thus  became  the sole mortgagee  of  the  property.
Angeles'  default  continued, and on June 29,  1993  Canada  Life
appointed  Ernst & Young as receiver and manager  to  manage  the
property  as  agents of Angeles. On December 12, 1994  Ernst  and
Young  was  discharged as receiver and manager and, on that  day,
wrote to tenants of the development as follows:


          We   confirm   that  we  have  obtained   our
          discharge  as  Receiver and Manager  of  [the
          premises] as of December 12, 1994. The Canada
          Life Assurance Company has assumed control of
          the  development effective from the  date  of
          our discharge.

          We  have  assigned  our  property  management
          agreement with V&A Properties to Canada  Life
          and  we  understand that V&A Properties  will
          continue  to  manage  the  development  under
          Canada  Life's  direction.  Accordingly,  all
          rent  cheques should be made payable  to  V&A
          Properties after December 12, 1994.

[62]  On  December  22, 1994 Canada Life wrote to  Goodyear  with
respect to the leased premises as follows:


          We  are  in  possession  of  the  above-noted
          property and we have appointed V&A Properties
          Inc. as manager of the property. Accordingly,
          we require you to pay all rent and additional
          rent due under the terms of your lease to V&A
          Properties Inc.

[63]  That  letter was an unequivocal statement by the  mortgagee
that  it had taken possession of the property. There are no facts
that  indicate  that it did not take possession in  law  on  that
date. The issue then becomes the effect of that possession on the
enforceability by it of the 1987 leases.


[64] As I understand their positions, there is no dispute between
the  parties as to the applicable law on this issue.  It  is  its
application  to the facts of this case on which they differ.  The
decision  of  the English Court of Appeal in Corbett v.  Plowden,
[1884]  25  Ch.D.  678  (C.A.) sets out the  basic  law  on  this
question at pages 681 and 682:


          If  a  mortgagor left in possession grants  a
          lease   without   the  concurrence   of   the
          mortgagees...the  lessee  has  a   precarious
          title,  inasmuch as, although  the  lease  is
          good as between himself and the mortgagor who
          granted  it,  the  paramount  title  of   the
          mortgagees  may be asserted against  both  of
          them.

          .....it  is  well  settled law  that  when  a
          mortgagee   intervenes  by  virtue   of   his
          paramount  title, and claims rent,  which  he
          has  right to do without setting up any lease
          whatever,  the  rent  if  paid  will  be  the
          previously existing rent, under a new tenancy
          from year to year under the mortgagee.

[65]  Another question considered by the court in that  case  was
whether  the  tenant had become a tenant under the mortgagee,  or
continued as a tenant under the original lease. At page 684  Fry,
L.J. states:


          That  raises  this further question,  whether
          Mr.  Plowden  [the lessee] occupied  at  that
          time the position of tenant from year to year
          to  the  mortgagees, or of tenant  under  the
          agreement  [the original lease].  That  turns
          upon  this  further  question,  whether   the
          mortgagees  by their notice .... and  by  the
          correspondence,  asserted  their  independent
          superior title as mortgagees, or whether they
          claimed to receive the rents as agents of the
          mortgagor.

[66]  In  this case there can be little doubt that the  mortgagee
was  asserting  its superior title. There had previously  been  a
receiver and manager collecting rents as agent for the mortgagor,
and  that  receiver and manager was discharged and the  mortgagee
immediately informed the tenants that it was in possession of the
property.  That  being the case, and the tenant not  rejecting  a
tenancy with the mortgagee, there was a deemed tenancy from  year
to  year established between Goodyear and the mortgagee, given no
facts which would compel a different result.


[67]  From  the time Canada Life took possession in  December  of
1994, Goodyear continued to abide by the terms of the 1987 leases
and  paid  rent  to the property manager without any  comment  or
suggestion  that the terms of the lease had changed. In  June  of
1995  Goodyear  initiated discussions about  the  possibility  of
subletting one floor of its premises for the balance of the  term
of the leases, and requested Canada Life to agree to an extension
of  the  lease  term  of that portion of the property  for  three
years,  because  the proposed sublessee wished a  ten-year  term.
These  facts,  in my view, clearly indicate an intention  on  the
part of Goodyear, at least until June of 1995, to be bound by the
terms  of  its  lease  for the full term. However,  there  is  no
evidence that the mortgagee intended to be so bound. Thus,  I  am
satisfied  that the tenancy, at that time, was one from  year  to
year.


5.   EFFECT OF FORECLOSURE

[68] In August of 1995 Canada Life entered into an agreement with
V&A  for  purchase  by  V&A  of Canada  Life's  security  in  the
property. That agreement was later assigned to Burnhamthorpe, and
the  securities and all interests of Canada Life  in  leases  and
other   contracts  involving  the  property  were   assigned   to
Burnhamthorpe on October 31, 1995. On that same date an order  of
foreclosure was obtained in favour of Burnhamthorpe. Goodyear was
not added as a party to the foreclosure proceedings, although  it
knew  of them, and could have moved to be added as a party if  it
had wished to redeem.


[69]  On December 5, 1995 V&A, on behalf of Burnhamthorpe,  wrote
to  Goodyear  requesting, inter alia, that  Goodyear  execute  an
estoppel  certificate acknowledging its 1987 leases with Angeles,
and stating that there were no existing defaults under the leases
by  the  landlord.  Goodyear declined  to  execute  the  estoppel
certificate  in the form presented by V&A. On December  15,  1995
Goodyear wrote to V&A stating:


          In  response  to your letter of  December  5,

          an   Estoppel   Certificate  with   schedules
          attached  for...our lease of [the  premises].
          This    Estoppel   Certificate    has    been
          substantially  amended  from   the   Estoppel
          Certificate  which  you forwarded  with  your
          letter  of December 5, 1995. You should  read
          it carefully.

[70]  It  is clear from the wording of this letter that  Goodyear
had considered its position very carefully, and had no doubt that
the estoppel certficate which it executed was in a form which was
acceptable to it.

[72]  Provisions  added by Goodyear to the  estoppel  certificate
which are of particular importance are paragraph (b); an addition
to  paragraph (d) stating, "unless terminated sooner by reason of
the  intended  foreclosure referred to in paragraph  (b)  above";
and, paragraph (j).


[73]  This  estoppel  certificate makes it  clear  that  Goodyear
wished  to  preserve  any rights it might have  as  a  result  of
foreclosure. It takes the position now that it has the  right  to
terminate, on appropriate notice, the year-to-year tenancy  which
the  law  deemed it to have with Canada Life. That right,  if  it
exists, does not result from the foreclosure, but from the nature
of  the  tenancy  as one from year to year. A significant  right,
which  Goodyear  might have had as a result  of  the  foreclosure
action, is a right to redeem the property. Paragraph (j)  of  the
certificate  is  a  specific provision stating that  Goodyear  is
retaining  whatever  rights it may have to redeem  the  mortgages
affecting  the  property.  Goodyear did  not,  in  its  argument,
disclose  any  other  rights it might  have  resulting  from  the
foreclosure.


[74] Burnhamthorpe argues that the lease which existed between it
and  Goodyear following the foreclosure, and up to  the  time  of
commencement  of  this action, was a lease in the  terms  of  the
original  leases  between Goodyear and Angeles for  two  reasons:
first,  the  lease  between  Goodyear and  Angeles  continued  as
between them despite the taking of possession of Canada Life, and
when  Burnhamthorpe took over the position of Angeles as a result
of the foreclosure, it was entitled to enforce the original lease
against  Goodyear; second, Goodyear is estopped  by  its  conduct
from denying that it is bound by the terms of the original lease.


     (a)  Continuance of Original Lease

[75]  The  application  judge expressed  his  view  that  when  a
mortgagee  goes into possession of premises subject  to  a  lease
subsequent to the mortgage, unless there is an agreement  between
the  lessee and the mortgagee to the contrary, the original lease
is terminated. He cites no authority for that proposition, and  I
have  some  difficulty with it. The only case to  which  we  were
directed  where  that conclusion is stated is  Page  v.  Welford,
[1941]  4 D.L.R. 448, a decision of a single judge of the Ontario
Court of Appeal on facts very different to those involved in this
case.  The  judge  in Page v. Welford relied on the  decision  in
Corbett  v.  Plowden, supra, for authority  that  a  lease  given
subsequent  to  a  mortgage  is terminated  by  notice  from  the
mortgagee to the tenant to pay rent to him. A careful reading  of
Corbett  v. Plowden indicates that it does not in fact stand  for
such  a  proposition.  Corbett v.  Plowden  does  stand  for  the
proposition that such notice results in a new tenancy  from  year
to  year  under the mortgagee. It does not deal with the question
of  termination of the original lease. In any event, the facts in
Page  v.  Welford are substantially different from the  facts  in
this  case, since neither the tenant, nor his sub-tenant in  that
case, was continuously in possession of the mortgaged premises. I
do  not  consider it to be authority for the general  proposition
that  a  lease  is always terminated by notice from a  subsequent
mortgagee to pay rent to it.


[76]  Burnhamthorpe  takes  the position  that,  although  a  new
tenancy may be created after possession between the mortgagee and
the  tenant,  the  original lessee's interest in  the  equity  of
redemption  does not come to an end, and the original lease  does
not  terminate  merely  as  a  result  of  the  mortgagee  taking
possession.  Burnhamthorpe concedes  that  the  mortgagor,  as  a
result  of his default under the mortgage, has placed the  lessee
in  the  precarious  position  of possibly  being  evicted  by  a
mortgagee  with paramount title. The mortgagor, by  his  default,
breached his covenant for quiet enjoyment, and the lessee, if  he
does  not  remain in possession, cannot be sued by the mortgagor.
However,  if the lessee remains in possession under a lease  with
the  mortgagee, that lease can only be a lease of the mortgagee's
interest  in the property, since the mortgagor still retains  the
equity  of  redemption. The mortgagee's right to possession  will
come to an end on payment by the mortgagor of the mortgage debt.


[77] There can be no doubt that this analysis of the position  of
the  parties is correct as far as it goes. The next step  in  the
analysis, and one for which we were given no clear authority,  is
Burnhamthorpe's proposition that after redemption by a mortgagor,
the  lessee  (who is still in possession under a lease  with  the
mortgagee) can enforce the original lease against the mortgagor's
interest in the land. It seems to me that he must be able  to  do
so,  since  otherwise a mortgagor, by virtue of his own  default,
could  later redeem the mortgaged premises free and clear of  the
lease agreement which he himself entered into.


[78]  If the lessee then stays in possession after redemption  by
the  mortgagor,  the lease, being enforceable  by  him,  must  be
enforceable against him. If the lessee does not wish to  stay  in
possession,  then  it should follow, that the  mortgagor,  having
breached his covenant for quiet enjoyment, should not be able  to
force the tenant to remain in possession and abide by the lease.


[79]  But  this argument is taken a step further by Burnhamthorpe
in this case. The core of its theory is that the original leases,
which are carved out of the equity of redemption of the landlord,
Angeles,  continued as against Angeles as a contract, and  as  an
interest  in  the  Angeles' equity of redemption,  and  when  the
equity  of  redemption  of  Angeles was foreclosed,  Angeles  was
replaced  by  the  foreclosing mortgagee (or its assignee)  which
then  took  subject to the lease, and was entitled to enforce  it
against  Goodyear. We were directed to no authority which  stands
specifically for this proposition, and there is, as I see  it,  a
basic  flaw  in  this portion of Burnhamthorpe's  argument.  When
Angeles'  equity  of redemption was foreclosed,  the  foreclosing
mortgagee did not replace Angeles by becoming owner of its equity
of   redemption.  That  equity  was  gone  as  a  result  of  the
foreclosure,  and  the  legal estate of the  mortgagee  became  a
vested estate free from the mortgagor's equitable claim.


[80]  Burnhamthorpe  argues further that  the  lease  granted  to
Goodyear was a partial transfer of Angeles' equity of redemption.
It  is  for this reason that a lessee in the position of  Angeles
has  a  right  to  redeem in a foreclosure action.  As  indicated
above,  Goodyear  was  careful in its  estoppel  certificate,  to
retain  that  right,  although it  did  not  exercise  it.  Since
Goodyear's  interest  in  the  equity  of  redemption   was   not
foreclosed,  Burnhamthorpe  argues  that  Goodyear  remained   in
possession as a result of that interest, and must, therefore,  be
subject to the lease which attached to that equity.


[81]  I  see  no  reason in law, and none was cited  to  us,  why
Goodyear  should  be  required to remain  in  possession  of  the
property  under a lease made with Angeles merely because  it  was
not  joined  in  a foreclosure action by Canada Life.  Goodyear's
relationship  with Canada Life was one of a tenant from  year  to
year,  and  I  see  no  reason why the  foreclosure  of  Angeles'
interest should change that relationship.


     (b)  Estoppel

[82]  Burnhamthorpe's last argument is that Goodyear is  estopped
as  against it from taking the position that the 1987 leases  had
terminated because of its estoppel certificate dated December 15,
1995,  quoted  earlier  in  these  reasons,  and  Burnhamthorpe's
reliance  on  that  certificate. I  agree  with  the  application
judge's  conclusion with respect to this argument.  Burnhamthorpe
in no way changed its position in reliance on the certificate. As
of  the  date the certificate was signed, Burnhamthorpe, even  if
the   transaction  of  purchase  had  not  been  completed,   was
irrevocably  bound  to  its contract. Its argument  that  it  had
various  options  which were not taken, such as not  closing  and
suffering  the attendant damages, is completely unpersuasive.  In
addition,  the  provisions  in  the  agreement  with  respect  to
confirmation of leases on which it relies did not refer to leases
for  any  particular term, but merely required a confirmation  of
leases such as they happened to be.


GOODYEAR'S APPEAL

[83] Goodyear's appeal, argued as a cross-appeal, dealt with  the
application  judge's decision that its notice  to  quit  was  not
effective  to  terminate  its  year-to-year  tenancy.  Goodyear's
notice  to  quit was delivered to Burnhamthorpe on May 31,  1996,
seven  months before the year-end of the tenancy, which was found
by  the  trial  judge  to be December 31,  1996.  He  based  that
conclusion  on the fact that in response to Canada Life's  taking
possession  and demanding that Goodyear pay rent to it,  Goodyear
paid  the January, 1995 rent on January 3, 1995, and thus a year-
to-year tenancy was agreed to as of January 1, 1995.


[84]  The  creation of a year-to-year tenancy between lessee  and
mortgagee  depends  on  a demand for rent by  the  mortgagee  and
acceptance of that demand by the tenant. Actual payment  of  rent
constitutes  acceptance of that demand, and the  commencement  of
the  tenancy: see Guscon Enterprises Ltd. v. Andsam  Masonry  Co.
(1995),  48  R.P.R.  (2d) 255 (Gen. Div.); Towerson  v.  Jackson,
[1891]  2  Q.B. 484 (C.A.); and Evans v. Elliot (1838), 112  E.R.
1242.


[85]  The  application judge having found, correctly in my  view,
that  the tenancy commenced on January l, 1995, went on  to  hold
that  to  terminate the year-to-year tenancy effectively  it  was
necessary  for  Goodyear to give at least six months'  notice  to
quit,  the  notice period to end on December 31 of  the  year  of
termination  -  in  this case 1996. The actual  notice  given  by
Goodyear on May 31, 1996 stated:


          Take  notice that Goodyear Canada Inc.  will,
          at   the   expiration  of  its   year-to-year
          tenancies  for the Premises on  December  21,
          1996,  quit and deliver up possession of  the
          Premises to Burnhamthorpe Square Inc.

[86] Since he found that the term of the year-to-year tenancy ran
from  January l to December 31, the applications judge held  that
Goodyear's  notice to quit the premises on December  21  was  not
adequate  to terminate the tenancy and that, therefore,  a  fresh
six  months'  notice to quit, would have to be given  to  end  on
December  31,  1997  if Goodyear still wished  to  terminate  the
tenancy.


[87]  That decision was no doubt based on the law that  at  least
six  months' notice to quit must be given to end on the last  day
of  the  tenancy, that the notice must be unambiguous  and  state
that  the tenant will deliver up the premises on the proper date:
see  Cohen  v. Taylor Service Stores, [1948] 4 D.L.R.  658  (Ont.
C.A.)., and the cases cited therein.


[88]  The  law, which had for many years been applied in  a  very
technical fashion, evolved to the state where persons wishing  to
terminate  a  tenancy  would insert in  the  notice  to  quit  an
alternative to the precise date of termination stating  something
akin  to  "at  the end of the year of the tenancy".  In  Cohen  v
Taylor,  supra, the term of the tenancy was clear, and there  was
no element of unfairness in applying the law strictly. However, a
recent   decision of the House of Lords - Mannai  Investment  Co.
Ltd. v. Eagle Star Life Assurance Co. Ltd., [1997] 2 W.L.R. 945 -
dealing  with  notice  to terminate a commercial  tenancy,  where
there was some element of doubt as to the termination date of the
tenancy, applied a flexible test. At p. 962, Lord Steyn stated:


          ... the inquiry is objective: the question is
          what reasonable persons, circumstanced as the
          actual parties were, would have had in  mind.
          It  follows  that one cannot  ignore  that  a
          reasonable  recipient of  the  notices  would
          have  had  in the forefront of his  mind  the
          terms of the leases.

[89]  The facts in that case were simpler than those in this case
but,  in  my  view,  the  test  as articulated  is  a  fair  one,
preferring  as it does fairness over technicality. In  our  case,
the  confusion  was  caused  by a number  of  possible  dates  of
commencement  of  the  year-to-year tenancy.  Goodyear  obviously
thought  that it commenced on December 22, 1994, the date  Canada
Life  gave notice to pay rent to V&A as its agent. It could  have
taken  the  position that it commenced on December 12, 1994,  the
date  Ernst & Young gave notice to it to pay rent to V&A as agent
of  Canada  Life. It could also have taken the position  that  it
commenced  on January 3, 1995, the date of Goodyear's  cheque  in
response to notice to pay to Canada Life's agent. In my view, the
application  judge was correct in his choice of January  1,  1995
for  the  reasons  he  gave. However, that does  not  answer  the
question  posed by the Mannai test. The question is,  what  would
Canada  Life  have reasonably considered to be  intended  by  the
notice.  The  clear answer to that question is that  Canada  Life
would  have known that Goodyear wished to vacate the premises  at
the  end of the current year-to-year term. Canada Life also  knew
that  there could be varying views as to when the term ended.  In
my view, it was incumbent on it to either resolve that issue with
Goodyear, or ask the court to resolve it. In effect, it  did  the
latter.  That  being the case, I consider the appropriate  remedy
for  Canada  Life to have been the requirement that Goodyear  pay
rent  up to the end of December 1996, the last day of the current
term, not that Goodyear be required to stay in possession, or pay
rent, until the end of 1997.


[90] There were cases cited by Burnhamthorpe from other provinces
which  appear to have come to a different conclusion.  They  are,
however,  substantially  different  on  their  facts.  I  do  not
consider  it necessary to review them in detail because,  to  the
extent that they reach a different conclusion, I would not follow
them.  The decision of the Ontario Divisional Court in Re Bianchi
et al. v. Aguanno et al. (1983), 42 O.R.(2d) 76 (Div. Ct.), cited
by  Burnhamthorpe, is not helpful, as it deals with a statutorily
mandated notice in a residential tenancy.


[91]  I consider that the notice given by Goodyear was sufficient
to terminate the tenancy as of December 31, 1996.


RESULT

[92]  I  would  dismiss  the cross-appeals of  Burnhamthorpe  and
Canada Life with costs, but allow the appeal of Goodyear in  part
with costs and amend the judgment of Ground J. on the application
by striking the word "not" from paragraph 2 and adding at the end
of  that  paragraph  the  words "as of December  31,  1996",  and
striking paragraphs 3, 4.
