                     E N D O R S E M E N T

[1]  The appellant was injured in a car accident. Liability was
admitted. At the conclusion of the evidence, the respondents
brought a motion to dismiss the action under s. 266(3) of the
Insurance Act, R.S.O. 1990, c.I.8 (the Act). The motion succeeded
and the action was dismissed. The appellant appeals from that
dismissal and, if successful on that issue, also challenges
certain aspects of the damage assessment made by the trial judge.
There is no appeal from the judgment as it relates to Pilot
Insurance Company.


[2]  Section 266(1)(b) of the Act provides in part, that the
owner of an automobile is not liable for loss or damage for
bodily injury arising from the operation of that automobile
unless the injured person has sustained:

          permanent, serious impairment of an important
          bodily function caused by continuing injury
          which is physical in nature.
[3]  The trial judge accurately identified Meyer v. Bright
(1993), 15 O.R. (3d) 129 (C.A.), leave to appeal refused 1994 1
S.C.R. v.i. as the controlling authority and correctly
articulated the principles set down in that authority for the
determination of whether an injury met the criteria established
in s. 266(1)(b).


[4]  The trial judge found that the appellant had sustained
permanent impairment of a bodily function caused by a continuing
injury which was physical in nature. She further found that the
bodily function which was permanently impaired was an important
one. These findings are accepted on appeal. The trial judge also
found that the permanent impairment of the important bodily
function was not a "serious" impairment within the meaning of s.
266(1)(b) of the Act. This finding of fact precipitated the
dismissal of the action and is the focal point of the appeal.


[5]  Appellate deference to findings of fact is a firmly
established and essential component of appellate review. Counsel
for the appellant acknowledges the limited scope of review
available. However, he submits that the trial judge's reasons
reveal a failure to give appropriate weight to certain medical
reports, a misapprehension of evidence and inconsistent
conclusions all of which, when taken together, render her finding
that the injuries were not "serious" unreasonable and properly
subject to reversal on appeal.


[6]  It is appropriate to begin the review of the trial judge's
reasons with the caution that they must be read as a whole.
Anomalies or ambiguities which appear in a specific sentence or
paragraph may disappear when placed in the context of the
entirety of the reasons.


[7]  In deciding whether the appellant's injuries brought her
within s. 266(1)(b), the trial judge examined at length the
various medical diagnoses advanced for the appellant's injuries.
Those diagnoses were found in medical reports filed by the
appellant, medical evidence called by the appellant, and medical
evidence called by the respondent. The trial judge ultimately
concluded that the appellant had suffered a thoracic outlet
compression as a result of the injury sustained in the car
accident. That condition left the appellant with intermittent
pain in her arm, numbness in two fingers on her left hand and
neck pain. It affected her ability to lift and led to fatigue.
One of the physicians, whose evidence was accepted by the trial
judge, described the appellant's injury as resulting in a "mild
permanent disability."


[8]  The trial judge rejected the diagnosis of chronic pain
syndrome (also referred to as fibromyalgia) because the requisite
permanence of the symptoms associated with that diagnosis had not
been established. She also rejected the diagnosis of ulnar nerve
entrapment as inconsistent with the timing of the appearance of
some of the appellant's symptoms.


[9]  Counsel for the appellant contends that in reaching her
conclusion as to the proper diagnosis of the appellant's
injuries, the trial judge improperly discounted the probative
value of the diagnoses contained in the medical reports filed by
the appellant. Counsel relies on s. 52 of the Ontario Evidence
Act, R.S.O. 1990 c.E.23 in support of the proposition that
medical reports are entitled to as much weight as viva voce
medical testimony.


[10] We cannot accept that submission. Clearly, s. 52 makes the
reports admissible. It also implies that resort to reports as
opposed to viva voce evidence does not in and of itself diminish
the probative value of the reports. In every case, however, the
trial judge is charged with the responsibility of weighing the
evidence, including the evidence offered by way of medical
reports. The weight to be given to a medical report will depend
on the circumstances. In this case, the medical reports on which
the appellant relied to establish chronic pain syndrome were
prepared years before the trial. The trial judge found that some
of these reports contained significant inconsistencies and
ambiguities. Those findings are fully supported by a review of
the relevant reports. The trial judge was entitled to take those
factors into account in deciding what weight to give to the
diagnoses found in the reports.


[11] The appellant also submitted that the trial judge wrongly
rejected the viva voce evidence of Dr. Wright, the only physician
retained by the appellant who had seen the appellant near the
time of trial. The trial judge's reasons for rejecting Dr.
Wright's evidence are sound and supported by the evidence.


[12] After determining the nature of the injuries suffered by the
appellant and concluding that it met the criteria in s. 266(1)(b)
of the Act, save the "seriousness" requirement, the trial judge
turned to that requirement. Following Meyer v. Bright, she said:


          I turn then to consider whether the permanent
          impairment of an important bodily function is
          serious. It is clear from Meyer v. Bright
          that not all permanent impairments of
          important bodily functions are to be
          considered serious. I must be satisfied that
          the impairment, which is not the injury
          itself, causes substantial interference with
          Mrs. Daues' [the appellant's married name]
          ability to perform her regular daily
          activities or to continue with her regular
          employment. In either event, it can be
          considered to be serious. [Emphasis added.]


[13] The trial judge held that the appellant's injury did not
substantially interfere with her ability to perform her regular
daily activities. This finding is not challenged. In considering
whether the injuries substantially interfered with the
appellant's ability to "continue her regular employment", the
trial judge had first to decide the nature of the appellant's
"regular employment."


[14] The appellant was a registered nurse. It was her position
that her "regular employment" should be taken as the employment
she enjoyed when the accident occurred. At that time she was
working as a registered nurse in a coronary care unit. That job
involved physical demands beyond those placed on registered
nurses working in other parts of a hospital. It was the
respondents' position that the appellant had worked as a coronary
care unit nurse for a relatively brief time before the accident
and that her "regular employment" should be taken as that of a
registered nurse working in a hospital setting.


[15] The trial judge examined the evidence on this issue at some
length. The appellant contends that she misapprehended the
evidence as to how the appellant came to begin working on the
coronary care unit and wrongly concluded that the appellant's
employment on that unit had begun as a result of hospital
cutbacks and not by any choice of the appellant. We have reviewed
the evidence relied on by the trial judge and it does appear that
she overstated its effect. That evidence does not refer
specifically to the reason the appellant (as opposed to her
friend) started work in the coronary care unit. There is no
direct evidence as to what led her to commence work in the
coronary care unit when she did.


[16] While acknowledging this error in the appreciation of the
evidence, we are satisfied that the rest of the evidence relied
on by the trial judge amply supported her conclusion. The
appellant's employment should be viewed as that of a registered
nurse working in a hospital setting and not necessarily a
registered nurse working in the coronary care unit. The trial
judge concluded:

          In this case, there is some interference in
          the ability of Mrs. Daues to perform the job
          of a coronary care unit nurse. However, I do
          not think that this amounts to an
          interference in her ability to perform her
          regular employment.
          Her regular employment was as a registered
          nurse, specifically as a hospital nurse
          providing patient care. While I am satisfied
          that Mrs. Daues enjoyed her work and was a
          competent coronary care unit nurse, the
          evidence is that she also enjoyed and was a
          competent and caring nurse on the medical
          floor at Centenary Hospital where she had
          worked for two years before moving to the
          coronary care unit.
[17] Having determined the nature of the appellant's employment
for the purpose of assessing whether her injuries would
substantially interfere with her ability to continue that
employment, the trial judge turned next to the effect of those
injuries on the appellant's ability to work as a registered nurse
in a hospital setting. That issue turned largely on whether the
appellant was able to work the 12-hour shifts customary in
hospitals. There was opinion evidence from two defence experts
that she was able to perform 12-hour shifts. The medical reports
relied on by the appellant were of little assistance although one
strongly suggested the likelihood that the appellant would be
able to work 12-hour shifts. Dr. Wright's evidence that the
appellant could not perform those shifts was not accepted by the
trial judge. On this record, the trial judge was entitled to
find, as she did, that the appellant would be able to work 12-
hour shifts. More to the point, she was certainly entitled to
find that the appellant had not shown that she was unable to work
12-hour shifts. As the onus was on the appellant, it was
incumbent upon her to establish the requisite "seriousness" of
her injuries.


[18] It remains to consider the main argument advanced by the
appellant in oral submissions. Counsel contends that the trial
judge accepted the appellant as a credible witness who was not
malingering or deliberately overstating her injuries. He further
submits that the appellant's evidence and her description of her
injuries is consistent only with a diagnosis of chronic pain
syndrome. It is his contention that the trial judge could not,
acting reasonably, reject that diagnosis and yet accept the
appellant's evidence. Counsel further argues that the appellant's
evidence makes it clear that she was unable to work a 12-hour
shift as a nurse because of her fatigue, pain and inability to
lift the necessary weight. Counsel contends that the trial judge
could not accept that evidence and yet make the finding that the
appellant was able to work a 12-hour shift.


[19] Clearly, the trial judge found the appellant to be a
credible witness. She was speaking the truth as she believed it
to be. We cannot, however, read her reasons as accepting the
appellant's claim that she could not work 12-hour shifts as of
the time of trial. The passage in the reasons relied on by the
appellant is a reference to the appellant's evidence of her
condition in June 1993, some seven months after the accident and
some three and half years before trial. The trial judge clearly
accepted that, as of that time, the appellant was unable to work
in a hospital as a registered nurse because of the lifting and 12-
hour shifts. Her rejection of chronic pain syndrome as the proper
diagnosis was an implicit rejection of the appellant's contention
that her fatigue and pain continued to have the same debilitating
effect at the time of trial as they had in 1993. It was also a
rejection of the contention that those problems would render her
unable to work 12-hour shifts in the hospital in the future.


[20] Nor is there any inconsistency between the trial judge's
finding that the appellant acted reasonably to mitigate her
damages by accepting a job involving less hours in a non-hospital
setting and the trial judge's finding that the appellant was
capable of working 12 hour shifts in a hospital. The question of
the reasonableness of mitigation is entirely distinct from the
question of whether the appellant's injuries rendered her
incapable of working as a nurse in a hospital.


[21] In summary, we see no basis upon which we can interfere with
the factual finding of the trial judge. Her conclusion that the
appellant had not met the criteria in s. 266(1)(b) must stand. It
is unnecessary to consider the issues relating to the trial
judge's assessment of damages.


[22] The appeal is dismissed with costs.
