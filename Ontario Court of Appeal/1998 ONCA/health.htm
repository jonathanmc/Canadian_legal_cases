CARTHY J.A.:
[1]  This appeal arises from a determination of a question of law
by MacFarland J. pursuant to a motion under Rule 21 of the Rules
of Civil Procedure. The defendants sought an order declaring that
the plaintiff had no legal basis for its cause of action for
breach of contract, as the action was based on restrictive
covenants which were unenforceable. MacFarland J. held that the
covenants were unenforceable because the parties were not
carrying on the same businesses C the plaintiff is a corporation
providing space and associated services and the defendants are
practising doctors.


[2]  The motion judge found herself bound by the judgment of
McLennan J. in Carruthers Clinic Limited v. Herdman, [1956] O.R.
770 (H.C.), which limits the enforcement of restrictive clauses
to parties in the same business. In her endorsement, MacFarland
J. wrote that "[t]he Carruthers Case is still the law in the
province and that being so the non-competitive clauses are
unenforceable..."


[3]  The issue before this court is whether the Carruthers case
stands for so broad a prohibition against the enforcement of
restrictive covenants, or alternatively, whether Carruthers was
wrongly decided and should be overruled.


[4]  The appellant operates a health centre and entered into
agreements with the two respondent doctors to grant them a
licence to practice within the centre on terms that the doctors
would receive a particular percentage of their gross receipts and
the balance would be retained by the appellant as a contribution
to its overhead and profit. Both agreements contain the following
provision which gives rise to the issue on this appeal:

          8.02 For a period of one year following the
          termination of the Agreement, in accordance
          with its terms, the Physician covenants and
          agrees not to conduct his practice or
          business or compete with the Manager either
          directly or indirectly in any manner or form
          by himself or in combination with others
          within an area of two and one-half mile
          radius from the Centre without having first
          obtained the written consent of the Manager.
[5]  The respondent doctors terminated the agreements and the
statement of claim alleges that each was, within the one year
following the termination, practicing medicine within the
proscribed radius.


[6]  The Carruthers decision is similar to the instant case, both
involving an agreement between a clinic and a practicing
physician containing a restrictive covenant which the physician
was allegedly violating at the time of the institution of the
proceedings. There are distinguishing facts from those presented
on this appeal, but at the heart of the reasons McLennan J.
states his conclusion so bluntly that it stands squarely in the
path of these proceedings.


[7]  McLennan J. concludes at p.778 that:

          A covenantee is not entitled to restrain a
          covenantor from carrying on business other
          than that which the covenantee carries on:
          Rogers v. Maddocks, [1892] 3 Ch. 346; Bromley
          v. Smith, [1909] 2 K.B. 235; 32 Halsbury, 2nd
          ed. 1939, p.407, s.681; p.417, s.697.
[8]  Prior to making the above statement, McLennan J. states the
general principle quite accurately, in effect stating that a
restrictive covenant is an unlawful restraint of trade unless the
plaintiff can show a legitimate business interest in restricting
the defendant's right to practice, and that the restraint is
reasonable. McLennan J.'s reasons at p.777 read as follows:

               The main question in connection with the
          defence that the covenant is an unlawful
          restraint of trade is whether the plaintiff
          had any interest in restricting the
          plaintiff's[sic] right to practise after
          termination of the agreement. The other
          question is, if the plaintiff had such an
          interest, were the restrictions as to time
          and space greater than was reasonably
          necessary for the protection of that
          interest?
               In discussing the first of these
          questions there is no dispute about the
          proposition that an essential element in the
          justification of a covenant such as the one
          in this case, is that the person in whose
          favour it is imposed should have some
          business or proprietary interest which the
          law recognizes as requiring protection and
          that a mere restraint in gross is contrary to
          public policy and void: Nordenfelt v. The
          Maxim-Nordenfeldt Guns and Ammunition
          Company, Limited, [1894] A.C. 535; Mason v.
          Provident Clothing and Supply Company,
          Limited, [1913] A.C. 724; Herbert Morris,
          Limited v. Saxelby, [1916], 1 A.C. 688.
               In determining whether there is any
          interest that requires protection the inquiry
          must in each case be, what the interests of
          the covenantee are that are to be protected
          and against what is he entitled to have them
          protected: Herbert Morris, Limited v.
          Saxelby, supra, at p.701.
[9]  I do not question any of the above as a statement of the law
then and now. My concern is the leap from these general
propositions to the narrow conclusion quoted above that the
covenantor must be carrying on the same business as the
covenantee in order for the covenants to be enforceable. As
indicated above, McLennan J. finds support for that conclusion in
Rogers v. Maddocks and Bromley v. Smith. I do not agree that
either of those cases justifies the narrowing of the focus to
what is really a very arbitrary rule that as a covenantor you do
not have a business interest to protect unless you are in the
same business as the covenantee.


[10] In Rogers v. Maddocks, [1892] 3 Ch. 346 (C.A.), the
plaintiff was a brewer and was held entitled to restrain a former
salesman from selling beer in a prescribed area. However, it was
also held that it would not be reasonable for the brewer to
restrain the salesman from selling aerated water, given that he
had not been involved with that product while employed by the
plaintiff. In making its determination of the business interests
of the plaintiff and the reasonable restraints that could be
imposed upon the defendant without being oppressive and without
affecting the public interest, the Court of Appeal simply
analyzed the factual circumstances of the case. I find no
statement, or even inference, in the reasons for judgment that
the parties must be in the same business. As in most such cases,
the two parties were, generally, functioning in the same
business. That, however, is not sufficient to create a rule of
exclusion.


[11] In Bromley v. Smith, [1909] 2 K.B. 235, the plaintiff was a
baker who employed the defendant to sell baked goods and
purported to restrain him, after his employ, from selling similar
goods and from being a restaurant keeper. The trial judge held
that the agreement was unenforceable in so far as it sought to
restrain the defendant from engaging in the business of a
restaurant keeper because the provision went beyond what was
necessary for the protection of the business carried on by the
plaintiff.


[12] Both these cases deal with whether the covenant was
reasonable in all the circumstances. In the Bromley case it was
considered to be unreasonable to seek to restrain employment in a
business other than that of the covenantee. In the Maddocks case
it was considered unreasonable to restrain a former employee in
an area of business that he was not involved in while in the
covenantee's employ. However, these cases do not lay down a rule
that in all cases it would be unreasonable to impose a restraint
simply because the two parties are in different businesses.


[13] It is my conclusion that McLennan J. erred in the statement
quoted above from p.778 of his reasons and, to that extent, his
judgment should be considered overruled. We cannot know how the
evidence will develop through discoveries and trial in the
present case, but the pleadings leave it open to develop evidence
supporting a legitimate business reason for the plaintiff as a
provider of space and facilities to protect itself from the
activities of the doctors after they leave the health clinic. It
should be for a trial judge to determine whether there is a
legitimate business interest to protect and, if so, whether the
covenant is reasonable in scope and time.


[14] I would therefore set aside the order of MacFarland J. with
costs here and below in the cause in the action. This form of
order of costs is made in recognition of the fact that the
Carruthers case ostensibly stood in the way of the proceedings
and justified the motion and this appeal as legitimate steps to
decide the need for a trial. The questions posed on the motion as
to whether the plaintiff has a cause of action can only be
answered after the evidence is considered at a trial.
