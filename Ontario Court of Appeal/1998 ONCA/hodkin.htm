1. Facts
[1]  The appellant, Anne Hodkin, and respondent, Martin Bigley,
own adjoining properties that have a 12 foot strip of land
running between them. Access to the appellant's garage was for
many years gained by way of the 12 foot strip of land, of which
eight feet are owned by the appellant and four feet by the
respondent. The issue in this appeal essentially concerns a fence
placed by the respondent along his property line which the
appellant alleges interferes with motor vehicle access to her
garage.


[2]  The appellant's property was previously owned by the Sargent
family. The Sargents' son-in-law, Hugh Judges, testified that the
Sargents had enjoyed the use of the entire strip of property
between both houses as far back as 1954. He also testified that
the Sargents assumed responsibility for the maintenance of the
entire strip to the extent that they maintained it as a driveway.


[3]  The respondent erected a fence along the property line in
August, 1995, which left eight feet for the appellant's driveway.
The previous owner of the appellant's property subsequently
commenced an action claiming ownership of the entire twelve feet
by adverse possession. The appellant purchased her property on
September 29, 1995 and obtained an order to continue the action
commenced by the previous owner.


[4]  At trial, the appellant claimed the previous owner's use
entitled her to title by adverse possession or, alternatively, a
right-of-way by prescriptive easement over the 12 feet in issue.
The relief sought by the appellant was necessary since the fence
makes it difficult for her to park her car on the driveway,
access her garage and alight from her car when it is parked
between the houses.


2. Issues

[5]   (a) Does the use of the driveway made by the previous owner
entitle the appellant to title by adverse possession.
     (b)  Is the appellant entitled to a right-of-way by
prescriptive easement.
     (c)  Does the court have jurisdiction to at least order the
removal of the fence in the circumstances.


3. Analysis

a) Adverse Possession

[6]  It is a well settled principle of property law that title to
realty may be conferred through adverse possession, thereby
extinguishing the interest of the party holding paper title to
the property. One of the main reasons for this rule is to protect
the settled expectations of parties who have acted on the
assumption that their occupation will not be disturbed (B. Ziff,
Principles of Property Law, 2nd ed., (Toronto: Carswell, 1996) at
120).


[7]  A claimant for adverse possession must prove:
     a)   actual exclusive possession of the property by the
          adverse possessor;
     b)   possession with the intention of excluding the true
          owner; and
     c)   a use of the land which is inconsistent with the rights
          of the true owner (Keefer v. Arilotta (1976), 13 O.R.
          (2d) 680 (C.A.)).


[8]  At trial, the respondent and other witnesses who were called
on behalf of the respondent testified that the respondent and
others often used the four feet of the respondent's property to
wash windows, maintain eavestroughs and access the back lawn.
There was also some evidence of a drain pipe extending onto that
strip of land from time to time. While it is true that the
respondent may have used the disputed strip of land less
frequently than the appellant, there was no evidence that the use
made by the appellant was exclusive and with the intention of
excluding the respondent, nor that the use made by the appellant
was inconsistent with the property rights of the respondent. The
trial judge was therefore correct in dismissing the appellant's
claim to title through adverse possession.


b) Right-of-way by Prescriptive Easement

[9]  In order to establish a right-of-way by prescriptive
easement, the claimant must demonstrate a continuous,
uninterrupted, open and peaceful use of the land for a period of
20 years. In addition, if the use of the property is by the
permission of the owner, the use cannot create an easement
(Henderson v. Volk (1982), 35 O.R. (2d) 379 at 383 (C.A.)).


[10] A right-of-way by prescription must satisfy the four
elements required to establish an easement:


     a)   there must be a dominant and serviant tenement;
     b)   the easement must accommodate the dominant tenement;
     c)   the dominant and serviant owners must be different
          persons; and
     d)   the lands in question must be capable of forming the
          subject matter of a grant (Re Ellenborough Park, [1955]
          2 All E.R. 667 (C.A.)).


[11] In the view of the trial judge, the second and fourth
conditions had not been met and I see no error in the trial
judge's finding that "[t]he four or four and a half feet that the
defendant owns...is not critical or even significant to the
enjoyment of the house by the owners of Number 11." The appellant
still had substantial use of her driveway even after the
respondent's land was fenced off.


[12] The benefit that accrued to the appellant before the
erection of the fence facilitated the appellant's parking but I
agree with the trial judge that it cannot be said to accommodate
the dominant tenement in such a way as to justify the creation of
an easement. I see no merit in this ground of appeal.


c) Removal of the Fence

[13] The real difficulty in this case, as identified by the trial
judge, is the erection of the fence. While the eight feet owned
by the appellant is sufficient for a driveway, she would
undoubtedly enjoy the greater convenience in parking and using
her garage without the fence. However, the defendant has erected
the fence entirely within his property line and the fence does
not encroach upon the property of the appellant; nor does it
substantially interfere with the appellant's legitimate use of
the driveway.


[14] The trial judge had the benefit of hearing the testimony of
the witnesses and reviewing the relevant evidence. He came to the
following conclusion:

          If I were satisfied that the fence seriously
          impeded the plaintiff's access to a
          garage...I would be inclined to require the
          defendant to remove the fence. There do not
          seem to be any fences in the immediate
          environs and in my view its only purpose is
          the establishment of some sort of property
          division....I shall not make such an order,
          only because the photographs and
          contradictory material...indicate that it is
          not a major problem. I do hope, however, that
          the defendant would see fit to remove it
          voluntarily in due course.

[15] The trial judge therefore found that the fence did not
seriously impede the appellant's access to the garage, and I am
not satisfied that the trial judge made any palpable or
unreasonable error in coming to this conclusion.


Conclusion

[16] In my view the absence of the fence in question would in no
way derogate from the respondent's ownership of the four foot
strip of land "protected" by the fence. In the circumstances,
while I am not ordering the removal of the fence, it is obvious
that its removal would enhance neighbourly relations.


[17] There will be no costs of this appeal.
