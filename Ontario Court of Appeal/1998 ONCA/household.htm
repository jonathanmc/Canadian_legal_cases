                      ENDORSEMENT
                                

[1]  The court has delayed its decision in this matter to
consider the reasons for judgment in Dawson et al. v. Rexcraft
Storage and Warehouse Inc. et al. and Pacific & Western Trust
Company v. Carroll released on August 13, 1998 and the additional
submissions provided by the parties. In our view, this appeal
must be dismissed, except with respect to the enforceability of
the third mortgages.


[2]  While there are superficial similarities between these
appeals and the appeal in Rexcraft, we are satisfied that Cusson
J. was correct in granting summary judgment allowing the claims
by the respondents, except with respect to the third mortgages,
and dismissing the counter-claims by the appellants.


[3]  Fundamental to the success of the appellants’ case is their
allegation that there is a genuine issue for trial as to whether
the respondents were aware of the assumed breach of the
Securities Act. They rely upon the acknowledged fact that Mr.
Chick, a lawyer with Nelligan/Power, received a copy of the OSC
bulletin about a month before these transactions. Nelligan/Power
did not act for the Royal Bank and so his knowledge cannot affect
the claim against it. The Royal Bank denied knowledge of any
violation and there is no circumstantial evidence from which it
could be inferred that the Royal Bank had actual knowledge of any
breach of the Securities Act as required by this court’s decision
in Armstrong v. R.J. Nichol Homes Ltd., [1997] O.J. No. 1545. The
appellants argue that as a sophisticated financial institution,
the Royal Bank ought to have known of the breach of the Act. The
cases in this court indicate that only actual knowledge (probably
including wilful blindness) will suffice. The alleged violation
of the Securities Act had no impact on the enforceability of the
loans granted by the Royal Bank.


[4]  We are also of the view that there is no evidence that the
respondent Household Realty had actual knowledge of the breach of
the Act. The uncontradicted evidence from Household is that it
did not have such knowledge (Affidavit of Paul Lavoie). Mr. Chick
was extensively cross-examined by counsel for the respondents. He
provided no evidence that he was aware that the transactions may
have violated the Act or that he passed such information on to
Household. Even if he ought to have known that the Act applied,
that was not sufficient to render the second mortgage
unenforceable. The appellants heavily rely on the reasons of
Borins J.A. respecting the appeal in Pacific & Western Trust
Company v. Carroll. They argue that there are striking
similarities in the facts of Carroll and this appeal. We do not
agree. In particular, we note that in Carroll no evidence was
provided by Standard Trust or Nelligan/Power in respect of their
knowledge of whether the promoters were in compliance with the
Act. See para. 117 of the reasons of Borins J.A. The alleged
violation of the Securities Act had no impact on the second
mortgages provided by Household.


[5]  We take a different view of the third (vendor take back)
mortgages assigned by Reicor to Household Realty. There is
evidence sufficient to raise a genuine issue for trial as to
whether Reicor was aware of the violation of the Act. If so, the
transaction may be voidable. That issue can only be resolved
after a trial. See the discussion of this issue in Rexcraft by
Borins J.A. at paragraph 97 and by Rosenberg J.A. at paragraphs
140 to 145. Accordingly, we would set aside that part of the
judgment granting summary judgment to Household Realty with
respect to the third mortgages.


[6]  We also agree that the trial judge properly dismissed the
counter-claims by the appellants. The claims made in this case
are factually different from Rexcraft. There is no evidence that
respondents were parties to any misrepresentations by the Reid
group. There is no person in these cases in a position similar to
that of Mr. Turk of the Bank of Nova Scotia in the Rexcraft case.
The appellants also failed to produce any evidence from which it
could be said that the respondents were aware of any
misrepresentation. In particular, the material shows no
misrepresentation of the present value of the property. The “soft
costs” are clearly set out in the material. If the promoters made
any misrepresentations of some other kind the respondents were
unaware of them.


[7]  We are also of the view that there is no evidence that the
respondents breached any duty of care to the appellants or that
they did anything to induce the appellants to enter into the
transactions. The evidence is all the other way and shows that
the appellants had decided to enter into the transactions before
becoming aware of the involvement of any of the respondents in
the financing. There is no evidence that the respondents favoured
the promoters at the expense of the appellants.


[8]  In light of that conclusion, the question of equitable set-
off does not arise and accordingly we express no opinion on the
correctness of Cusson J.’s conclusion on that issue.


[9]  Accordingly, the appeal is dismissed except with respect to
the Household Realty third mortgages. The appellants were
unsuccessful in respect of the appeal except in relation to the
enforceability of the Household Realty third mortgages. This
represents a very small part of the case and in our view the
respondents are entitled to their costs of the appeal.
