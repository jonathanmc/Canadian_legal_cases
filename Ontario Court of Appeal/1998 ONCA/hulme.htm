                      ENDORSEMENT
 
[1]  Based on the unique facts of this case, we agree with
Montgomery J. that there was no constructive dismissal when the
appellant was removed from his responsibilities in the
Corporation’s Southern Region. The appellant concedes that the
Corporation was entitled to remove him from the Houston Center
project. As found by the trial judge, it was an implied term of
the contract that the appellant was required to accept a new
project after he was taken off the Houston Center project. The
evidence fully supports the trial judge’s finding that it would
have taken some time to find a new project of sufficient
importance commensurate with the appellant’s status in the
Corporation. During that time, the appellant would have retained
his title as senior vice-president and his salary. His management
responsibilities were not terminated—they were merely in a hiatus
until a new project was found.
 
[2]  The suggestion that is now made that the Corporation should
have left the appellant in Houston to carry on with the other
projects was not a viable one on the evidence. Those other
projects constituted an insignificant part of the appellant’s
responsibilities and as counsel for the respondent put it such a
course of action might itself have constituted constructive
dismissal.
 
[3]  The appellant now argues that even if there was no
constructive dismissal, in its letter of February 25, 1981 the
Corporation wrongfully terminated his employment. However, this
letter came only after the appellant had commenced an action
against the Corporation on January 27, 1981. In the letter, the
Corporation took the position that by instigating the action the
appellant obviously intended to terminate his employment with the
Corporation. The appellant did not reply or indicate in any other
way that this was not his intention. In our view, it is clear
from the evidence that the appellant had already decided to
terminate his relationship with the company and to repudiate the
employment contract. He had taken other steps, in addition to
launching the action, to terminate the relationship. As found by
the trial judge, the appellant had repudiated the contract and it
was open to the Corporation to terminate the relationship, as it
did in the letter. This does not seem to have been a serious
issue in dispute at the trial.
 
[4]  In view of our conclusion concerning constructive dismissal,
it is unnecessary to deal with any of the other issues raised by
the appellant except the question of the appellant’s alleged
entitlement to an equity interest in one of the Corporation’s
other projects. The trial judge found that there were too many
important undefined terms to create a collateral contract and
that some of those terms had to be the subject of agreement. It
was open to the trial judge to make that finding, especially in
view of the testimony of Mr. Prentiss, which the trial judge
expressly accepted. The alternative finding by the trial judge,
based on the evidence of Mr. Bullock, that in any event the
appellant would have received nothing even if an equity interest
could be determined was also a finding open to the trial judge.
 
[5]  Accordingly, the appeal is dismissed with costs.
