                     ENDORSEMENT

[1]  Unfortunately, the "O'Connor" application brought at trial
was procedurally flawed and counsel's supporting material was not
as well developed as it might have been. That material, however,
made it clear that the complainant had been taken to the hospital
the day after the second alleged assault and while at the
hospital had "given an account of what happened to her." In the
circumstances of the case, we think this material constituted a
sufficient evidentiary foundation to clear the first hurdle of
O'Connor and to require production of those records for
examination by the trial judge. The trial judge committed
reversible error in failing to order production of those records.


[2]  The Crown's closing was improper in that it invited the
jury, without any evidentiary basis, to find that the complainant
had made prior consistent statements and, secondly, in that it
improperly suggested to the jury that they could use those
alleged statements to bolster the complainant's credibility. The
trial judge did not correct these improper submissions despite a
specific request that he do so. The complainant's credibility was
crucial in this case as credibility was clearly a "close call."
The jury acquitted on one of the two counts. In our view, absent
any correction of the improper submissions made by the Crown, the
Crown's improper comments resulted in a miscarriage of justice
and standing alone would warrant a new trial.


[3]  The appeal is allowed, the conviction is quashed and a new
trial is ordered. In so ordering, we observe that the appellant
has served four months in relation to the conviction we have now
quashed. We think a four-month sentence would have been entirely
adequate had in fact the appellant been properly convicted.
