                      E N D O R S E M E N T
                                


[1]  Counsel for the appellant submit that the trial judge erred
in respect of the three factors that he used to find that the
appellant was guilty of dangerous driving causing death, namely
speed, point of impact and impairment. With respect to the
evidence of speed and point of impact, counsel submit that the
trial judge either misapprehended the expert evidence or erred in
giving it any weight. However, the trial judge’s findings
respecting speed and point of impact were primarily based upon
the evidence of Mr. MacDonald. The trial judge accepted this
evidence which, as he pointed out, was uncontradicted. In our
view, the evidence of MacDonald is, together with the undisputed
physical evidence of the scene, sufficient to sustain the
verdict.


[2]  MacDonald's evidence was that the appellant never came out
of the curve, that he came "straight across the road" at the
Valade car, that the accident happened well over on the side of
the road for southbound traffic, and that he was “driving higher
than the speed limit”. Each aspect of this is supported to one
degree or another by the physical evidence. The point of impact
is established by the marks made by the Valade car and the
dragging marks made by the appellant’s motor vehicle after the
impact. The extent and nature of the damage to the truck are
consistent, even to a layman, with speed. It was conceded by the
appellant's counsel on appeal that it was a high-speed collision.


[3]  Mr. Addario, who was not counsel at trial, also argues that
the trial judge erred in failing to find that there was a
violation of the appellant’s rights under s. 9 of the Charter of
Rights and Freedoms and in failing to exclude the police
observations of the appellant’s conduct at the hospital. Although
the trial judge excluded the evidence of the results of the blood
test, he relied upon the appellant’s conduct at the hospital as
evidence that he was impaired.


[4]  Counsel for the appellant at trial made no application to
exclude the evidence of the appellant’s conduct at the hospital.
The written notice of application does not refer to this
evidence. In his oral submissions, trial counsel limited his
application to the results of the testing of the blood samples.
Counsel for the respondent argues that in those circumstances the
appellant is precluded from raising this issue for the first time
on appeal. She submits that the exclusion of this evidence raises
issues of fact that were not explored at trial because they were
not relevant to a determination of the only issue raised at
trial, the admissibility of the results of the testing of the
blood samples. We agree.


[5]  The exclusion of the hospital conduct depended primarily
upon a finding that the appellant had been arbitrarily detained.
While Crown counsel conceded at trial that the appellant had been
detained, there was no concession that any detention was
arbitrary. The burden was on the appellant to prove an arbitrary
detention. The trial judge found that the appellant’s rights
under s. 8 of the Charter were infringed by the unlawful seizure
of the blood samples. However, he made no finding that the
appellant was arbitrarily detained or that his rights under s.
10(b) were infringed.


[6]  The trial judge also made a finding, which is supported by
the evidence, that there was a “factual basis for the police
taking [the appellant] to the hospital for medical treatment and
observation”. In our view, in light of that finding, the
possibility that the appellant was arbitrarily detained would
depend on the appellant at least establishing that he did not
agree to go to the hospital for treatment. In this respect, we
see a distinction between mere acquiescence in the face of a
police request for assistance, information or evidence from the
motorist as, for example, in R. v. Wills (1992), 70 C.C.C. (3d)
529 (Ont. C.A.) at 541 and R. v. Mellenthin (1992), 76 C.C.C.
(3d) 481 (S.C.C.) and failure to raise any objection in a case
where, as found by the trial judge, the officer is offering
assistance to the motorist. In the latter circumstance, there is
no question of waiver of constitutional rights and the burden is
on the accused to establish the detention was arbitrary. The
appellant did not testify and what evidence there is on the
record shows that the appellant raised no objection to being
taken to the hospital. Moreover, had the issue been properly
raised at trial, it is possible that the Crown could have called
other evidence. In particular, the Crown may have chosen to lead
the evidence of Sergeant Vanderwoude, who spoke to the appellant
shortly before he left with Constable Amirault for the hospital.


[7]  Even if the appellant had been able to establish a violation
of s. 9 of the Charter, it would have been open to the Crown to
resist the exclusion of the hospital conduct evidence by showing
that, inter alia, its admission would not affect the fairness of
the trial because its discovery was inevitable. The Crown was
denied that opportunity because the issue was not raised at
trial.


[8]  For all these reasons, it is our view that this is not a
proper case to allow the appellant to raise this issue for the
first time on appeal. It follows that the trial judge did not err
in using this evidence. In any event, as indicated earlier, it is
our view that the finding of dangerous driving is supported by
the other evidence, especially the evidence of Mr. MacDonald: see
R. v. Reed (1998), 124 C.C.C. (3d) 257 (S.C.C.).


[9]  Accordingly, the appeal is dismissed.
