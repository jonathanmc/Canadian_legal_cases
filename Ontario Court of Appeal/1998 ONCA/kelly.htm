AUSTIN J.A.:

[1]               This appeal revisits the question in what circumstances the Crown's decision to prosecute a hybrid offence by indictment will constitute an abuse of process where it made an earlier election to proceed summarily with respect to the same allegations.

[2]               On September 11, 1994, an information was sworn charging Paul Warner Kelly ("Kelly") with assaulting his girlfriend on that date. On October 5, 1994, the Crown elected to proceed summarily on the charge.

[3]               On December 4, 1994, Kelly was charged with assaulting the same complainant on August 21, 1994. On December 7, 1994, the Crown elected to proceed summarily on this charge.

[4]               On January 18, 1995, the informations were marked for trial, the first on July 19, and the second on July 26, 1995.

[5]               On February 6, 1995, the Crown wrote to defence counsel advising that it intended to join the two charges together in a new information with a view to going to trial on either July 19 or July 26, whichever the defence preferred. The defence responded on June 12, 1995, that it preferred July 26.

[6]               On June 29, 1995, a third information was sworn containing the two assault charges. On July 19, 1995, the Crown withdrew the first information.

[7]               On July 26, the Crown elected to proceed summarily on the third information, the accused pleaded not guilty and the complainant testified. At the end of her examination-in-chief, the trial judge called a brief recess. Immediately following the recess, counsel for the defence advised the court that it had just come to his attention that the third information was sworn outside the six-month limitation period for summary conviction proceedings: s. 786(2) of the Criminal Code. The Crown could therefore not proceed summarily on either of the charges in the third information.

[8]               Defence counsel expressed the view that the Crown's election to proceed summarily was a nullity, although the information itself was not. Crown counsel agreed with this view and took the position that, because both offences charged in the third information were "hybrid offences," the Crown could proceed on the third information by way of indictment. It would then be up to the defence to decide whether it wanted to proceed with a trial or to turn the hearing into a preliminary inquiry.

[9]               A hybrid offence is one which may be prosecuted either summarily or by way of indictment. The charge is indictable until the Crown elects to proceed summarily. R. v. Ellerbeck (1981), 61 C.C.C. (2d) 573 (Ont. C.A.) at p. 574.

[10]           Defence counsel objected that the Crown had elected, with respect to all three informations, to proceed summarily and it did not seem appropriate that, because of a mistake on the part of the Crown, his client should now be faced with a proceeding by way of indictment.

[11]           In the result, the matter was adjourned to enable the defence to bring an application for a stay for abuse of process if so advised.

[12]           Such an application was brought and was argued on January 10, 1996. The defence's position was that, while it had consented to the setting of the trial date for July 26, it had not agreed to the swearing of the third information outside the six-month limitation period, nor had it agreed to have Kelly placed in greater jeopardy through the institution of indictable proceedings.

[13]           The Crown's position was that the swearing of the third information was, by inadvertence, outside the time limited, as a result of which the Crown could now proceed only by way of indictment. There would, however, be no prejudice to Kelly as the Crown would not be asking for "a higher penalty than they otherwise would have." It was now up to Kelly to elect whether to proceed to trial before the provincial court judge or to turn that hearing into a preliminary hearing.

[14]           On February 12, 1996, Her Honour Judge Inger Hansen, stayed the third information as an abuse of process.

[15]           On May 26, 1996, the Crown applied to G. Morin J., of the Ontario Court (General Division), for an order in the nature of mandamus with certiorari in aid. The Crown sought an order quashing the stay and directing a Provincial Division judge to arraign Kelly and put him to his election under s. 536(2) of the Code, that is, to be tried by a provincial court judge without a jury and without having had a preliminary inquiry, or to have a preliminary inquiry and to be tried by a judge without a jury, or to have a preliminary inquiry and to be tried by a judge and a jury.

[16]           The application was brought on two grounds:

(1)            The trial judge exceeded her jurisdiction in ordering the stay of the proceedings; and

(2)            She erred in refusing to exercise her jurisdiction as required by s. 536(2) of the Code.

[17]           Counsel for Kelly consenting, G. Morin J. made an order which may be paraphrased as follows:

(1)            the order of February 12, 1996, staying the proceedings, was quashed; and

(2)            Kelly was ordered to appear before the Provincial Court judge on June 10, 1996, at which time the information would be read to him and he would be put to his election.

[18]           On June 6, 1996, counsel appeared before Her Honour Judge Hansen. Defence counsel advised the court that Kelly would be electing to be tried by "this court so the court could have the authority to make [the] decision staying the proceedings."

[19]           Her Honour Judge Hansen said, amongst other matters:

I will re-state that my decision on this charge will be the same as it was on the earlier one and for the same reasons as given before [i.e., on February 12, 1996].

[20]           The Crown appeals from that stay.

[21]           It does not appear from the record that the second information was ever disposed of. It was not withdrawn by the Crown and was before the court on July 26, 1995. It appears to have been adjourned with the third information and would have been before the court on January 10, 1996.

PROCEDURE

[22]           Before dealing with the merits of the appeal, it may be helpful to comment on the procedure followed. In that regard, R. v. Currie (Ont. C.A., January 13, 1983, unreported) provides some guidance.

[23]           Currie was charged with assault causing bodily harm. When he appeared for trial, the Crown indicated that it was proceeding summarily. Currie pleaded not guilty and his counsel then pointed out that the information was a nullity, having been sworn more than six months after the commission of the offence alleged.

[24]           The trial judge chose to treat the matter of the date of swearing as a defence to which there was no answer and dismissed the charge.

[25]           The Crown appealed from the dismissal to the Summary Conviction Appeal Court. The court allowed the appeal on the basis that, in the circumstances, the Crown had no right to proceed summarily and there was therefore no jurisdiction on the part of the trial judge to accept a plea. The dismissal was set aside and the matter returned to the Provincial Court to proceed upon indictment.

[26]           Currie then appealed to this court which held that the Crown should not have appealed to the Summary Conviction Appeal Court. As there was no valid summary proceeding before the Provincial Court, the Crown should have taken the appropriate proceedings to challenge the dismissal of an indictable offence.

[27]           The Crown acted on that advice and appealed to this court from "the judgment of acquittal in proceedings by indictment."

[28]           The endorsement of this court reads as follows:

MacKinnon A.C.J.O., Martin and Weatherston JJ.A.

Court of Appeal for Ontario

13 January 1983

"The election of the Crown to proceed summarily was, in the circumstances of this case, a nullity, as were all the proceedings before Judge Boyd thereafter (R. v. Karpinski (1957), 117 C.C.C. 241 (S.C.C.); 1957 S.C.R. 343). Accordingly, Judge Boyd had no jurisdiction to dismiss the charges in the information as he purported to do. The proceedings before Judge Greco did not end in a determination which would support the plea of autrefois acquit. The appeal is allowed and the orders of the learned provincial court judge (Judge Boyd) are set aside as made without jurisdiction. We would suggest to the Crown, in view of the lengthy proceedings that have taken place through no fault of the respondents, they consider whether the interests of justice would be served by proceeding further."

"MacKinnon A.C.J.O."

[29]           The conclusion I draw from Currie is that the Crown could have proceeded with this matter by appealing directly to this court from the order of February 12, 1996.

PROVISIONS OF THE CODE AND JURISPRUDENCE

[30]           Part XXVII of the Code deals with summary convictions. Section 786 falls within that Part and s. 786(2) provides that:

No proceedings shall be instituted more than six months after the time when the subject-matter of the proceedings arose [unless the prosecutor and the defendant so agree].

(The words in square brackets were added after the events in issue, by S.C. 1997, c. 18, s. 110.)

[31]           It is common ground that the information in issue in the instant case, namely, the third information, was sworn more than six months after the time when the subject matters of the proceedings arose. It is also common ground that a purported election by the Crown to proceed by way of summary conviction upon such an information, and any proceedings that follow, such as a plea or an election by the accused, are null and of no effect.

[32]           It is likewise common ground that the information itself is not void. If it was otherwise a valid information charging an indictable offence, it remains so. R. v. Karpinski (1957), 117 C.C.C. 241 (S.C.C.).

[33]           Karpinski was charged with leaving the scene of an accident, contrary to s. 221(2) of the Criminal Code. That is a "hybrid offence." The information on its face showed that more than six months had expired between the event and the swearing of the information. Notwithstanding this, the Crown elected to proceed summarily. Karpinski then pleaded not guilty and his counsel moved for dismissal on the basis of the late swearing.

[34]           The motion was refused. The magistrate permitted the Crown to withdraw the information. A new information was sworn, the Crown proceeded by way of indictment, and Karpinski was convicted.

[35]           On appeal to this court, a verdict of acquittal was directed upon the ground that once the accused had pleaded, he was on trial, and to try him again by way of indictment with respect to the same offence would constitute double jeopardy.

[36]           A divided Supreme Court of Canada restored the conviction. The majority held that there was no acquittal and that the plea of autrefois acquit was not available. Kerwin C.J.C. held, at p. 242, that "[t]he Crown had the right to change its election before the Magistrate and also the right to withdraw the information ... what occurred at that time did not amount to an acquittal." Fauteux and Abbott JJ. held, at p. 249, that non-compliance with the limitation period "was fatal to the validity of the [Crown's] election [to proceed summarily] and [the accused's] plea, both of which were void."

[37]           Taschereau J. found, expressly, and Abbot J., by implication, that the accused was never in jeopardy.

[38]           Cartwright J. dissented. He concluded that once the accused pleaded, the magistrate had jurisdiction over him and over the offence with which he was charged and that the withdrawal of the information by the Crown was tantamount to an acquittal. Accordingly, the accused had been in jeopardy. Cartwright J. would therefore have dismissed the Crown's appeal.

[39]           As noted above, under the heading "Procedure," Karpinski was followed by this court in Currie.

[40]           In asking this court to lift the stay, the Crown relies on the decisions in Karpinski, Currie and R. v. Belair (1988) 41 C.C.C. (3d) 329 (Ont. C.A.).

[41]           In Belair, the accused was charged with having committed a sexual assault during the period between June 30, 1984, and March 31, 1985. The information was sworn on November 1, 1985. On November 18, 1985, the Crown elected to proceed by way of summary conviction, notwithstanding the fact that the whole period covered by the information was beyond the limitation prescribed by what was then s. 721(2). The trial was set for June 30, 1986, and on that date Crown counsel apparently realized the problem. The information was then quashed prior to any plea. A new information was sworn on June 30, 1986, alleging a sexual assault between June 1, 1983, and August 31, 1984. On September 10, 1986, the Crown elected to proceed by way of indictment. On April 23, 1987, the accused appeared and applied to stay the proceedings on the second information on two alternative grounds, autrefois acquit and abuse of process. The stay was granted on both grounds. With respect to abuse of process, the court said [at p. 332]:

When one looks at the reason for which the second information, the information that we are dealing with today, has arrived before this court, one must take into account the fact that the Crown, as mentioned earlier, elected to proceed summarily on the first information. They must have had some good reasons for electing to proceed by way of summary conviction. There are different penalties imposed in respect to the Crown's election by way of summary conviction and to have the matter dealt with at the trial date, it would seem to me that now to come back before the court on the second information alleging the same offence and to elect to proceed by indictment, the only purpose that the Crown has elected to proceed by indictment is to get around the prescription period. It has nothing to do with their discretion in respect to electing to proceed by summary conviction or indictment vis-à-vis the penalties involved upon a conviction. In my view, the defendant has met the challenge of the allegation of the Crown on the first information in which the Crown elected to proceed summarily, that the matter was disposed of at that stage and to allow the Crown now to bring forth another information and to elect to proceed by indictment for the sole purposes indicated earlier, to avoid the prescription period, would place the defendant in an unfair position in the eyes of the law.

On appeal, Martin J.A., speaking for this court with respect to autrefois acquit, said at pp. 332-333:

It has never been doubted that where an offence is triable on summary conviction or on indictment, proceedings by indictment may be taken notwithstanding that proceedings by way of summary conviction are prescribed. If the learned Provincial Court judge held that the plea of autrefois acquit applied, I am, with the greatest deference, of the view that he erred.

The respondent had never entered a plea to the first information and was never in jeopardy on that information.

[42]           As to abuse of process, Martin J.A. said at p. 339:

I confess that I was initially attracted by the submission of respondent's counsel that Crown counsel had assessed the offence as not being sufficiently grave to warrant proceeding by indictment, and that his failure to notice, prior to electing to proceed by way of summary conviction, that proceedings by way of summary conviction were precluded by s. 721(2), resulted in the laying of a second information, which had the potential effect of subjecting the respondent to an increased penalty. On more considered reflection, however, I have concluded that this argument is unsound. The offence charged was at all times triable by indictment, and indeed the information charged an indictable offence until the Crown elected to treat the offence as one punishable on summary conviction: see Re Abarca and The Queen (1980), 57 C.C.C. (2d) 410 at pp. 413-4 (Ont. C.A.). The respondent in this case was in no way prejudiced by the error of Crown counsel. If Crown counsel had noticed, prior to electing to proceed by way of summary conviction, that summary conviction proceedings were precluded by s. 721(2), he would, no doubt, have proceeded by indictment, as he ultimately did. The respondent in this case suffered no prejudice as a result of Crown counsel failing to notice prior to election that summary conviction proceedings were barred by s. 721(2). Furthermore, in the view of Mr. Justice Fauteux (with whom Mr. Justice Abbott concurred) in R. v. Karpinski, supra, the Crown's purported election, in the circumstances, to proceed by way of summary conviction proceedings was void and of no effect.

Conclusion

The public has an interest in charges being properly tried. In the circumstances, the trial of this charge would not offend the community's sense of fair play and decency. On the contrary, staying the proceedings would, in my view, offend the community's sense of justice. This is not a case where the doctrine of abuse of process is properly invoked.

[43]           The language of Martin J.A. applies equally to the instant case.

[44]           The respondent relies on the decisions in Re Parkin and The Queen (1986), 28 C.C.C. (3d) 252 (Ont. C.A.) and Re Boutilier (1995), 104 C.C.C. (3d) 327 (N.S.C.A.).

[45]           Parkin was charged with a sexual assault of a young girl "during the months of January to August in the year 1983." The only part of the information not barred by the six-month limitation period in what was then s. 721(2) of the Code was the period from August 4 to August 31, 1983. The Crown elected to proceed summarily. Parkin pleaded not guilty.

[46]           The complainant testified that the abuse ended when she and her mother left the accused's home. During a recess, after this evidence was given, Crown counsel, in conversation with the complainant's mother, learned that the mother and complainant had left the accused's home in July 1983. If that evidence was given at the trial and accepted, the offence would have been established as having occurred at a time beyond the reach of the information as a summary proceeding.

[47]           When court resumed, the Crown sought leave to withdraw the information and to present a new one on which he intended to proceed by way of indictment so as to avoid the limitation imposed by s. 721(2). Although opposed by defence counsel, leave was granted to present a new information. Defence counsel applied to quash the new information. This application was refused. The appeal to this court was allowed and further proceedings stayed.

[48]           In Karpinski, Currie and Belair and the instant case, the informations in issue were nullities in so far as summary proceedings were concerned. Accordingly, the accused were never in jeopardy.

[49]           In Parkin, on the other hand, the information had some validity, albeit limited, and, as a consequence, the accused was in jeopardy. Had the mother testified, she would have been in a position to provide a complete defence by reason of the limited reach of the information. This court considered that, having regard to the particular circumstances of the case, it would have been unfair to the accused to permit the Crown to shift its ground, so to speak, from proceeding summarily to indictment, in order to close off that defence. There is no parallel reason for shifting ground in the instant case. Parkin is therefore distinguishable from this case.

[50]           Turning to Boutilier, he was charged, in error, with failing to stop his motor vehicle after striking another motor vehicle contrary to s. 252(1) of the Code. The Crown elected to proceed summarily. Boutilier pleaded not guilty and a trial date was set. On that date, before any evidence was called, the Crown indicated that the charge should have been one of failing to remain after hitting a pedestrian rather than a motor vehicle. Another information was sworn but it was too late to proceed summarily. The Crown advised that it would proceed by way of indictment and that it intended to stay the original information. The accused applied for, and was granted a stay. The Crown appealed. In dismissing the appeal, Freeman J.A., speaking for the court, said at pp. 331-336:

Nowhere in the Crown's brief to the trial judge, nor in this appeal, is it alleged that the involvement of a pedestrian rather than a vehicle altered the Crown's understanding of the seriousness of the circumstances so as to justify proceeding by way of indictment rather than by summary conviction. No explanation for proceeding by indictment rather than by summary proceedings is offered, save for the expiration of the limitation period. The Crown's brief addressed this point before the trial judge:

In the present case, the accused alleges that there is "no rational basis" for the Crown's decision to proceed by way of indictment save for the fact that the prescription period contained in Section 786(2) of the Criminal Code has elapsed.

The Crown, however, respectfully submits that there is a rational basis to continue with the prosecution of the accused on the basis that the public has an interest in charges being properly tried. And, in the present circumstances, the trial of this charge would not offend the community's sense of fair play and decency. On the contrary, staying the proceedings would offend the community's sense of justice and thus bring the administration of justice into disrepute.

There is nothing in the submission to suggest that the Crown would not have proceeded by way of summary conviction with respect to the second information if the limitation period had not expired.

                  ....

Therefore, the only issue in this appeal is whether it was an abuse of process warranting a stay of proceedings for the Crown to re-elect to proceed by indictment, having previously elected to treat the matter as a summary conviction offence. In my view, that is a question of law to be determined from the acknowledged facts.

The creation by Parliament of summary conviction offences, indictable offences, and hybrid offences merely reflects the commonplace observation that some offences are more serious than others. The Crown is entrusted with classifying offences and, in the case of hybrid offences, with making the necessary determination whether to proceed summarily or by indictment. The choice is governed by the seriousness of the circumstances of each alleged offence; the object is to do justice expeditiously and fairly. The Crown's discretion in electing between summary proceedings and proceedings by indictment is virtually unfettered, and it is entitled to great respect.

                  ....

In the present appeal, the election to proceed by way of summary conviction, in September, 1994, was a proper and valid one. It must be presumed to have been a reasoned decision, regularly made, based on due consideration of all relevant circumstances, and tested by objective criteria. The offence the respondent was alleged to have committed was, therefore, in the eyes of the legal system and of the world, a summary conviction offence, that is, a relatively minor one, not serious enough to justify proceedings by indictment.

                  ....

In hybrid offences, the same essential elements must be proven whether the Crown elects to proceed by way of indictment or by summary conviction. Once an offence under s. 252 has been determined by the Crown to be a summary conviction offence, the provisions of the Criminal Code relating to summary conviction offences apply, not procedures relating to indictable offences. The Crown's option has been exercised: the case has been determined to be less serious than an indictable offence and the procedure is less formal. The Crown cannot arbitrarily reverse its decision and arrive at a different result; there must be sound reasons to justify such a change. The Crown's election is protected by a strong presumption of regularity and the offence is deemed to have been correctly characterized. The exercise of the Crown's discretion is subject to judicial review only for flagrant impropriety" (R. v. Balderstone (1983), 8 C.C.C. (3d) 532, 4 D.L.R. (4th) 162, 6 C.R.R. 356 (Man. C.A.)) or "improper or arbitrary motives" (R. v. Beare (1988), 45 C.C.C. (3d) 57, 55 D.L.R. (4th) 481, [1988] 2 S.C.R. 387).

The offence with which the respondent was charged was therefore a summary conviction offence because the legal system, by the device of the Crown discretion, had determined it to be a summary conviction offence. It was wrongly described in the information, but the Crown acknowledges that it was not fooled by its own mistake — it was aware throughout the whole matter that an injured pedestrian was involved, not another vehicle. The circumstances to which the Crown initially applied its criteria for exercising its discretion did not change. It would strike at the integrity of the system if the Crown is permitted to disavow its original election, in the absence of supporting circumstances related to the offence itself, and make a second election which necessarily disregards its own criteria, simply to patch up a drafting mistake in one information. (The same criteria, applied to the same circumstances, could not yield a conficting result.) It is very clear that the Crown's motives were arbitrary. In my view, this system is too important, and works too well, to expose it to public disrepute in this way.

To permit proceedings by indictment in the circumstances of this case would, in my view, damage the integrity of the system: integrity cannot be slightly violated any more than an eggshell can be slightly broken. It is an absolute concept. Either it is intact or it is not.

Fairness to the accused is a consideration in every case. There can be no doubt that proceedings by way of indictment are more prejudicial to an accused person than summary proceedings, if for no other reason than that the respondent in the present case would be liable to imprisonment for five years on indictment instead of a maximum sentence of six months in jail and a fine of $2,000 for a summary conviction offence. The additional procedural safeguards for indictable offences, the preliminary inquiry, and the right to a jury trial, attest to their seriousness and confront the accused with greater expense and delay. Indictable offences carry a greater stigma, of potential relevance to foreign travel, future employment, security clearance, and in the event of a subsequent offence. A pardon can be sought three years after conviction for a summary offence, but only after five years for an indictable offence. It would be callous to suggest the respondent's position has not worsened substantially if proceedings are not to be summary but by indictment. If the stay imposed by the trial judge is set aside, the respondent would be required to bear serious consequences flowing not from his own alleged misdeeds but merely from the Crown's, that is, the judicial system's, own inadvertence.

[51]           Freeman J.A. then went on to discuss Parkin and Belair and then the standard set by the Supreme Court of Canada before a stay should be granted for abuse of process. Reference was made to the reasons of L'Heureux-Dubé J. in R. v. Power (1994), 89 C.C.C. (3d) 1 at 10 as follows:

I, therefore, conclude that, in criminal cases, courts have a residual discretion to remedy an abuse of the court's process but only in the "clearest of cases", which, in my view, amounts to conduct which shocks the conscience of the community and is so detrimental to the proper administration of justice that it warrants judicial intervention.

[52]           Freeman J.A. dismissed the Crown's appeal, concluding his reasons as follows, at pp. 340-42:

While the standard in Power is undoubtedly a difficult one to meet, I am satisfied that the present appeal is sufficiently clear to justify a finding of abuse of process. The facts are acknowledged. There is no question as to what the Crown did, or any need to second-guess its intentions. More importantly, the detrimental effect of the proposed proceedings by indictment on the integrity of the system is equally clear. It is not reprehensible nor shocking nor likely to give rise to community outrage, but it is clear. The Crown discretion was to be used for the wrong purpose, a purpose precluded by its previous valid exercise, and one that could not stand up to objective scrutiny. Its use for an inappropriate purpose would be unfair and oppressive.

                   ...

In the present appeal, the striking of the pedestrian in the crosswalk was a wrongful act subjecting the accused to prosecution for a summary conviction offence, not, as determined by the Crown in the valid exercise of its discretion, to proceedings by indictment. Assuming a prosecution would succeed on the evidence, the accused has done nothing to entitle him to an acquittal on a summary conviction offence, but the state has done something which disentitles it to a conviction on indictment. What the Crown did, and what motivated its actions, could not be more clear.

                   ...

The trial judge considered a stay to be appropriate, and I have not been satisfied that he was in error. Accordingly, I would dismiss the appeal.

[53]           Boutilier can be distinguished from the instant case in that, in Boutilier, the accused had pleaded to the first information which was valid since it was sworn within the six months limited by the Code. At least technically he was in jeopardy. In the present case, no plea had been entered on the first or the second information.

[54]           But I do not accept the reasoning in Boutilier in a number of respects.

[55]           At p. 333, it is said that the accused was alleged to have committed a relatively minor offence "not serious enough to justify proceedings by indictment." With respect, this is to misinterpret or misunderstand the Crown's election. That election, to proceed summarily, signified a choice between two means of prosecuting, not a choice between prosecuting by way of indictment and not prosecuting at all.

[56]           At pp. 334 and 335, it is said that:

... the exercise of the Crown's discretion is subject to judicial review only for "flagrant impropriety" or "improper or arbitrary motives." [Citations omitted.]

At p. 335, it is concluded that the Crown's motives here were arbitrary. With respect, I disagree. Someone in the prosecuting process mistakenly stated that the accused hit a car when, in fact, he had hit a pedestrian. There was nothing arbitrary in seeking to correct that error.

[57]           As to penalty, that can, and in my respectful opinion should be, accommodated in the sentencing process if, after trial, that is appropriate.

[58]           The "additional procedural safeguards for indictable offences," with their attendant expense and delay, need not be borne by the accused who can waive them.

[59]           The "greater stigma, of potential relevance to foreign travel, further employment, security, clearance" and subsequent offences, as well as the additional time to wait in applying for a pardon are, I agree, all matters for consideration. They do not, however, have overwhelming weight. They must be put in the balance as against staying the charge in considering whether what is proposed "shocks the conscience of the community and is so detrimental to the proper administration of justice that it warrants judicial intervention."

[60]           Freeman J.A. himself concluded, at p. 341, that what was proposed "is not reprehensible nor shocking nor likely to give rise to community outrage." I agree.

[61]           In Power, the Supreme Court of Canada set a very high standard for the granting of a stay for abuse of process:

To conclude that the situation "is tainted to such a degree" and that it amounts to one of the "clearest of cases", as the abuse of process has been characterized by the jurisprudence, requires overwhelming evidence that the proceedings under scrutiny are unfair to the point that they are contrary to the interest of justice.

L'Heureux-Dubé J. at p. 10.

I am not persuaded that such evidence existed in Boutilier. I am persuaded that it is not present in the instant case.

[62]           On the contrary, I share the view of Martin J.A. in Belair at p. 339 and of Tremblay J.A. in R. v. Jans (1990), 59 C.C.C. (3d) 398 at 402-03 (Alta. C.A.), that the risk of community outrage would be greater if a stay were granted than if it were not granted.

[63]           In addition, regard must be had to the position of the Crown as prosecutor in these proceedings. In that regard, L'Heureux-Dubé J. said in Power, at p. 10:

... As will be developed in more detail further in these reasons, the Attorney-General is a member of the executive and as such reflects, through his or her prosecutorial function, the interest of the community to see that justice is properly done. The Attorney-General's role in this regard is not only to protect the public, but also to honour and express the community's sense of justice. Accordingly, courts should be careful before they attempt to "second-guess" the prosecutor's motives when he or she makes a decision. Where there is conspicuous evidence of improper motives or of bad faith or of an act so wrong that it violates the conscience of the community, such that it would genuinely be unfair and indecent to proceed, then, and only then, should courts intervene to prevent an abuse of process which could bring the administration of justice into disrepute. Cases of this nature will be extremely rare.

[64]           This is not one of such cases. There is no suggestion of improper motive or bad faith. There was only an error or oversight on the part of the prosecution. That error or oversight may have some negative consequences for the respondent. The significance of that risk, however, is a relatively minor matter when compared with the interest of the public in having the charge prosecuted.

[65]           I would therefore allow the appeal, set aside the order below staying the third information, and remit the matter to the Provincial Division for trial.
