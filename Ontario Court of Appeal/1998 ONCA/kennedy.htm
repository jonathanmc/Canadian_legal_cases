                          ENDORSEMENT

[1]  In our view, the verdict cannot stand for two reasons.
First, the instruction on the fact-finding process that the jury
should follow, in the context of the instruction on the burden
and standard of proof, was, at best, confusing. The trial judge
instructed the jury that they "must" make their findings of fact
on the whole of the evidence which tended to favour the Crown.
She then said "conversely" that the jury "may" make findings from
the whole of the evidence which favoured the accused or at least
left the jury in a state of reasonable doubt. Assuming that she
had earlier given a correct R. v. W.D. instruction,
notwithstanding her additions to it, this final instruction was
to the contrary of the W.D. approach and it was the last general
instruction which was given to the jury on this point.


[2]  Second, the focus of the jury's question during their
deliberations was the manner in which the appellant left the
scene of the alleged transaction, although in asking the
question, and in its clarification by the foreperson, it seemed
the jury wanted a review of the evidence of the appellant and the
undercover officer only. There was evidence from two other
witnesses that was relevant. The evidence of one of them was
capable of supporting the appellant's defence. The trial judge
rejected the request of the appellant's counsel that the evidence
of these two witnesses also be reviewed. In our view, she erred
in doing so. The appellant was entitled to a fair, comprehensive
and accurate answer to the true purpose of the question, which
was a review of all the relevant evidence in respect to how the
appellant left the scene.


[3]  Accordingly, the appeal is allowed, the conviction is set
aside, and a new trial is ordered.
