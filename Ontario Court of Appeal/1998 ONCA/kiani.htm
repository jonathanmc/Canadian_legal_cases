ENDORSEMENT

[1]                           We did not call on the respondents on the two issues of liability argued by the appellant. In our opinion the trial judge's finding that the appellant's failure to erect a guardrail caused the accident and his finding that the respondent Kiani was 30% responsible for the accident were reasonably supported by the evidence.

[2]                           The appellant made four submissions on damages. We did not call on the respondents on the first two issues, that is whether the trial judge erred in finding that Kiani could only work at a minimum wage job and whether he erred in finding that Kiani could no longer work as a taxi driver. Those findings were also reasonably supported by the evidence and we see no basis to interfere with them.

[3]                           We called on the respondent on the remaining two damages issues argued by the appellant.

(1)      Did the trial judge err in finding that Kiani would never find work as a convenience store operator or manager?


[4]                           The trial judge held that Kiani was capable of working as a convenience store manager. But he also held that "the vagaries of the economy" were too uncertain to permit him to conclude that Kiani could realistically find work as a convenience store manager. We think that the trial judge was entitled to reach this conclusion. Although his reasons could have been more detailed he was in the best position to assess Kiani's job prospects. He heard considerable evidence about Kiani's background, age, experience, education and work history. All these considerations factored into his conclusion that Kiani would not find work as a convenience store manager.

(ii)      Did the trial judge err in finding that Kiani's claim for past and future loss of income was barred by the rule in Hall v. Hebert preventing recovery for illegal activity?

[5]                            The trial judge awarded Kiani past and future income loss based on driving a taxi. The appellant's highest position is that Kiani was driving his taxi in Ontario with a Quebec licence obtained by misrepresentation at a time when he did not have an Ontario driver's licence. Hall v. Hebert reflects the underlying principle that a plaintiff will not be allowed to profit from his or her wrongdoing. That principle cannot be invoked here because the evidence does not establish that Kiani could not lawfully work as a taxi driver from the date of the accident. Therefore, we are not prepared to interfere with the trial judge's award of past and future loss of income.

[6]                           We find no merit in the other points raised in the appellant's factum, but not argued orally.

[7]                           Accordingly, the appeal is dismissed with costs.
