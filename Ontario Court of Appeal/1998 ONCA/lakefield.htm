GOUDGE J.A.:

[1]   On  October  13,  1987 the Ontario  Ministry  of  Municipal
Affairs  approved  a draft plan of subdivision  for  a  piece  of
property  in the Village of Lakefield.  It did so after receiving
a   representation  from  Lakefield,  the  appellant   in   these
proceedings, that municipal water services would be available  to
the  property  when  needed and that  it  was  not  premature  to
subdivide  the  land.  Armed with this draft plan  approval,  the
respondent  Black met with his lawyer and determined to  purchase
the property for development.


[2]   This  representation was made negligently  and  was  false.
When this became apparent and thus the property could not then be
subdivided,  the respondent disposed of it, suffering significant
losses as a result.


[3]   The central issue in this case is whether Black can recover
for  Lakefield's negligent misrepresentation.  In particular, was
there a sufficient proximity between the two that Lakefield  owed
a duty of care to Black, despite the fact that the representation
was  not addressed to him but rather to the Ministry of Municipal
Affairs.  At trial Black succeeded.  For the reasons that  follow
I  have  concluded  that the trial judge was correct  in  finding
Lakefield liable to Black for negligent misrepresentation.


THE RELEVANT FACTS

[4]   The original owners of the property were George Miller  and
his  wife.   On  July 14, 1986 they applied to  the  Ministry  of
Municipal Affairs for approval of a draft plan of subdivision  of
the  property.  The Ministry then elicited responses to the draft
plan from various agencies that might be affected by the plan.


[5]   The  Village of Lakefield was one of these  agencies.   Its
response  to  the Ministry's questionnaire, sent to the  Ministry
with a covering letter acknowledging that it was with respect  to
the proposed draft plan of subdivision, contained the following:


          Question  1:  Which of the following services
          will be available to this subdivision?

          Municipal Water Supply?

          Answer: Yes.

          Question 16: Is it too soon to subdivide this
          land?

          Answer: No.

[6]   The  trial  judge found that the meaning conveyed  by  this
response  was  that municipal water services would  be  available
when  needed  by  the  subdivider and that  the  subdivision  was
therefore not premature.


[7]  On October 13, 1987 the Ministry approved the draft plan  of
subdivision.   The only conditions attached to the approval  were
standard conditions unrelated to water supply.  Nothing in  those
conditions  indicated  that there was a  problem  with  municipal
water  service  to the property.  The trial judge concluded  that
the  Ministry  would  not  have  approved  this  draft  plan   of
subdivision  if Lakefield had indicated that the subdivision  was
premature  or that it would not be served by the municipal  water
supply.   On  the evidence before him this inference was  clearly
available.


[8]   When  it made its representation to the Ministry, Lakefield
was,  however,  well  aware that there was  a  significant  water
service problem.  Indeed, because of long-standing problems  with
low  water pressure and the water distribution system,  both  its
own  professional staff and its planning consultant  had  advised
that   Lakefield  should  consider  the  subdivision  application
premature  until further studies had provided a solution  to  the
provision of water services to the property.  Nonetheless,  after
meeting  with the owners' solicitor, the Village Council  decided
to  proceed  without  any  studies and  returned  the  Ministry's
questionnaire with the representations quoted above.


[9]   In  December 1988 the respondent met with his solicitor  to
discuss acquiring the property, given the existence of the  draft
plan  approval.  His solicitor was a very experienced real estate
practitioner  whose  evidence  was  that  where  there  had  been
approval  by  the  Ministry of a draft plan of subdivision,  that
meant that the municipality concerned had been consulted and  had
returned its questionnaire to the Ministry indicating no  problem
in  providing  the  necessary municipal  services.   He  and  the
respondent reviewed the draft plan approval for the property  and
the  solicitor advised that the conditions attached  were  simply
the standard conditions.  It was the solicitor's opinion that the
draft plan approval indicated that municipal water services would
be  available  when  needed  and that  the  subdivision  was  not
premature.


[10]  On  the basis of the advice he received from his solicitor,
the  respondent  purchased the property from  the  Millers.   The
importance  of Lakefield's representation to Black's decision  to
purchase was put this way by the trial judge:


               I  find  that Mr. Black would  not  have
          entered  into  the assignment  of  the  Howe-
          Kingdon  agreement of purchase of the  Miller
          lands  but  for the draft-approved status  of
          this  land and lack of any references in  the
          conditions    to    the    water    servicing
          deficiencies and requirements.  I  find  that
          Mr.  Black entered into the assignment of the
          Howe-Kingdon  agreement of  purchase  of  the
          Miller lands as a result of the misstatements
          in  the questionnaire that were reflected  in
          the draft plan approval conditions.  He would
          not  have completed the purchase but for  the
          Village's negligent statements.

[11] Subsequent to his purchase, it became apparent to Black that
Lakefield  had  a  serious problem in supplying  municipal  water
services  to  the property C a problem the Village had  known  of
well before responding to the Ministry questionnaire.  By June of
1990,  Lakefield was advising the respondent that  it  could  not
provide   water  service  without  significant  new  water   main
construction  which it was unable to finance.  Without  municipal
water  servicing the property could not be subdivided.  In  these
circumstances  the  respondent was  obliged  to  dispose  of  the
property incurring significant losses as a result.


THE REASONS OF THE TRIAL JUDGE

[12]  The trial judge determined that the representation made  by
Lakefield  to  the  Ministry concerning the supply  of  municipal
water  service was false and was made negligently.  He then found
that Black was in a position of proximity to Lakefield sufficient
to  trigger a duty of care on the appellant and that there was no
policy  reason for concluding otherwise.  Lakefield's  breach  of
this  duty  caused the respondent to purchase the property  which
ultimately  resulted in loss to him because of the unavailability
of   municipal  water  supply.   He  summarized  his  finding  on
liability as follows:


               In summary, I find that there was a duty
          of  care  owed by the municipality to persons
          in  the position of this Plaintiff, that  the
          Village   breached  this  duty  of  care   by
          misrepresenting  negligently  the   servicing
          issue   confronting  this   draft   plan   of
          subdivision,  that  this was  an  operational
          function  and  not  one of policy,  that  the
          Plaintiff  relied  on the resulting  approved
          status and conditions in agreeing to purchase
          the land, that the Plaintiff suffered loss as
          a  result,  and  that there  are  no  further
          limiting   considerations   save   that   the
          Plaintiff should be entitled to no more  than
          to  be put in the position financially as  if
          he had not entered the contract of purchase.

[13]  He then went on to award damages to the respondent for  the
loss  sustained in disposing of the property and for the carrying
cost  he  had incurred since its purchase.  He declined to  award
the respondent compensation for the profit he would have made  in
subdividing the property.  In making these calculations the trial
judge  accepted  the  respondent's evidence  that  while  he  had
originally purchased the property in partnership with his brother-
in-law,   when  difficulties  arose  with  the  development   the
respondent took over that interest to ensure that his brother-in-
law   suffered  no  loss.   As  the  full  beneficial  owner  the
respondent received judgment for the full damage award.


ISSUES

[14] The appellant raises two issues on this appeal.  Firstly, it
argues  that on these facts the respondent cannot recover on  the
basis  of  negligent  misrepresentation  primarily  because   the
representation in question was directed not to the respondent but
to the Ministry of Municipal Affairs.


[15]  Secondly, it argues that the trial judge erred  in  several
respects in his approach to damages.


[16]  The respondent cross-appeals from the refusal of the  trial
judge  to  award damages for the profits lost when  the  property
could not be subdivided.


ANALYSIS

[17]  I  turn  first to the issue of negligent misrepresentation.
The elements or general requirements of this tort were set out by
Iacobucci J. in Queen v. Cognos Inc. (1993), 99 D.L.R. (4th)  626
at 643:


               The  required elements for a  successful
          Hedley  Byrne claim have been stated in  many
          authorities, sometimes in varying forms.  The
          decisions  of this court cited above  suggest
          five general requirements:

          (1)  there must be a duty of care based on  a
               "special   relationship"   between   the
               representor and the representee;

          (2)  the  representation in question must  be
               untrue, inaccurate, or misleading;

          (3)  the    representor   must   have   acted
               negligently      in     making      said
               misrepresentation;

          (4)  the  representee must have relied, in  a
               reasonable  manner,  on  said  negligent
               misrepresentation; and

          (5)  the  reliance must have been detrimental
               to  the  representee in the  sense  that
               damages resulted.

[18]  The first of these requirements was further dealt  with  in
Hercules Managements Ltd. v. Ernst & Young, [1997] 2 S.C.R.  165,
a  decision  released  after the trial  judgment  in  this  case.
There, La Forest J., speaking for the Court, set out the analysis
to  be  undertaken to determine whether a duty of care exists  in
any particular case where negligent misrepresentation is alleged.
He made clear that, as in any tort case,  the existence of a duty
of  care in the tort of negligent misrepresentation required  the
application  of  the two-part test first enunciated  in  Anns  v.
Merton  London Borough Council, [1978] A.C. 728 (H.L.) and  later
restated in Kamloops (City of) v. Nielsen, [1984] 2 S.C.R.  2  at
10-11:


          (1)  is    there    a   sufficiently    close
               relationship  between the  parties  (the
               [defendant]  and  the  person  who   has
               suffered  the  damage) so that,  in  the
               reasonable    contemplation    of    the
               [defendant], carelessness  on  its  part
               might  cause damage to that person?   If
               so,

          (2)  are there any considerations which ought
               to  negative or limit (a) the  scope  of
               the duty and (b) the class of persons to
               whom  it  is owed or (c) the damages  to
               which a breach of it may give rise?

[19]  La  Forest  J.  elaborated  on  the  first  branch  of  the
Anns/Kamloops  test,  namely the prima  facie  duty  of  care  in
Hercules Managements Ltd., supra at 200:


          A  prima facie duty of care will arise on the
          part   of   a   defendant  in   a   negligent
          misrepresentation action when it can be  said
          (a)  that  the defendant ought reasonably  to
          have  foreseen that the plaintiff would  rely
          on  his  representation and (b) that reliance
          by the plaintiff, in the circumstances, would
          be reasonable.

[20]  Finally,  he addressed the second or policy  considerations
branch  of the test.  He explained the rationale for this  branch
as follows at 192:


               As  Cardozo C.J. explained in Ultramares
          Corp.  v.  Touche,  174  N.E.  441  (N.Y.C.A.
          1931),  at  p.  444,  the fundamental  policy
          consideration  that  must  be  addressed   in
          negligent  misrepresentation actions  centres
          around  the  possibility that  the  defendant
          might   be  exposed  to  "liability   in   an
          indeterminate  amount  for  an  indeterminate
          time to an indeterminate class".

He  then  went  on  to explain how in a given case  these  policy
concerns might indeed be satisfied.  At 197-198 he said this:


               As discussed earlier, looking to factors
          such  as "knowledge of the plaintiff  (or  an
          identifiable class of plaintiffs) on the part
          of  the defendant" and "use of the statements
          at   issue   for  the  precise   purpose   or
          transaction  for  which they  were  prepared"
          really  amounts  to an attempt  to  limit  or
          constrain the scope of the duty of care  owed
          by  the  defendants.  If the purpose  of  the
          Anns/Kamloops   test  is  to  determine   (a)
          whether  or  not a prima facie duty  of  care
          exists and then (b) whether or not that  duty
          ought  to be negated or limited, then factors
          such as these ought properly to be considered
          in  the  second branch of the test  once  the
          first branch concerning "proximity" has  been
          found  to  be  satisfied.  To  my  mind,  the
          presence of such factors in a given situation
          will   mean   that  worries   stemming   from
          indeterminacy  should not  arise,  since  the
          scope  of potential liability is sufficiently
          delimited.   In other words, in  cases  where
          the  defendant  knows  the  identity  of  the
          plaintiff  (or of a class of plaintiffs)  and
          where the defendant's statements are used for
          the specific purpose or transaction for which
          they   were   made,   policy   considerations
          surrounding indeterminate liability will  not
          be   of  any  concern  since  the  scope   of
          liability   can   readily  be  circumscribed.
          Consequently,  such considerations  will  not
          override  a  positive finding  on  the  first
          branch  of the Anns/Kamloops test and a  duty
          of care may quite properly be found to exist.

[21]  What  remains, then, is to apply these  principles  to  the
facts of this case to determine whether the trial judge's finding
of liability against Lakefield can stand.


[22]  Setting  aside for the moment the duty of care requirement,
there  is  no  question that the representation here was  untrue.
When  Lakefield represented in its response to the Ministry  that
municipal  water services would be available as needed  and  that
the  planned  subdivision  was not premature,  that  was  clearly
false.   Significant water main construction was  required  first
which Lakefield had no ability to finance.


[23]  There is also no doubt that Lakefield acted negligently  in
making this representation.  It did so having known for some time
of  the  water  servicing problem and against the advice  of  its
professional staff and consultant.


[24]   The  evidence  also  sustains  the  conclusion  that   the
respondent    relied   in   a   reasonable    manner    on    the
misrepresentation.  He relied on his lawyer who knew, because  of
his experience, that draft plan approval meant that Lakefield had
made  the  representation that municipal water services would  be
available  when  needed.   As the trial judge  said,  Lakefield's
representation about water supply was reflected in the draft plan
approval.   Since  the lawyer knew from that  approval  that  the
representation  had  indeed  been  made  by  Lakefield,   it   is
irrelevant  that the representation was directed to the  Ministry
rather than the respondent.


[25]  The facts supporting the reasonableness of the respondent's
reliance  can  be  summarized as follows.  The representation  by
Lakefield  concerning municipal water supply had been made.   The
respondent's  solicitor  knew  it  had  been  made,  advised  the
respondent  to  buy  on that basis, and the  respondent  did  so.
Without  an  assurance  that municipal water  services  would  be
available  the respondent would not have purchased the  property.
He was entitled to act on the basis that Lakefield would do as it
said.  In my view, given these circumstances, the respondent  can
clearly be said to have relied on the negligent misrepresentation
of the appellant in a reasonable manner.


[26]  Finally,  there  is no doubt that the  respondent  suffered
damage  as a result of this reliance.  The property could not  be
subdivided  because  municipal water  services  were  unavailable
contrary  to  the appellant's representation.  As  a  result  the
respondent  had to dispose of the property, which  he  did  at  a
loss.


[27]  This, then, leaves for analysis the requirement that  there
be  a duty of care owed by Lakefield to Black.  As set out above,
this requires the application of the two-part Anns/Kamloops test.


[28]  As  to the first branch of the test, in my opinion a  prima
facie  duty of care arises on the facts of this case.   Lakefield
ought  reasonably to have foreseen that Black would rely  on  its
representation.  Lakefield made the representation based  on  its
special  knowledge of the circumstances and as a formal  response
to  permit  evaluation of a request for approval  of  a  plan  to
subdivide  this  property.  It ought to have  foreseen  that  its
representation  would be relied on by someone seeking  to  effect
that very subdivision, particularly where its representation  was
reflected  in  the  draft plan approval which resulted  from  the
representation.   Moreover,  for the  reasons  I  have  indicated
earlier,  the  respondent's reliance on  the  representation  was
entirely reasonable.


[29]  As to the second branch of the test, in my view it  too  is
met.  The class of those who might rely on the representation  as
the  respondent did is strictly limited.  It is those  who  could
seek to proceed with the subdivision of the property.  Given that
Lakefield was fully aware that its representation was being  made
in  the  context of a proposed plan of subdivision,  it  must  be
taken  to  know of the class of those who could properly complain
about  its  misrepresentation, namely those  who  would  seek  to
subdivide  the property.  Moreover, the representation  was  made
for  the  very  purpose of facilitating the  subdivision  of  the
property.   It was in just this context that the respondent  used
the representation.  He relied on it to purchase the property for
development.  Hence, there is no risk here of indeterminacy.  The
scope  of Lakefield's liability is readily circumscribed.   There
is  no  policy basis, therefore, for overriding the  prima  facie
duty of care resting on the appellant.


[30]  I  therefore conclude that the trial judge was  correct  in
finding  the  appellant  liable to the respondent  for  negligent
misrepresentation in the circumstances of this case.


[31]  The second issue raised by the appellant is that the  trial
judge  erred in several respects in his award of damages.  First,
it  is argued that the trial judge should have determined damages
on  the  basis of the difference between the price paid  for  the
property  and the market value of the property without  municipal
water.  Second, the appellant says that the trial judge erred  in
finding the respondent entitled to the entire loss despite  being
shown on title as merely a co-owner.  Third, it is said that  the
trial  judge  erred in including in the damage award compensation
for a sum which the respondent had not yet paid to the mortgagee.


[32]  In  my  view,  none of these arguments  can  succeed.   The
measure  of damages for negligent misrepresentation is  that  the
plaintiff is to be put in the position he would have been in  had
the   misrepresentation  not  been  made.   See   B.   G.   Checo
International Ltd. v. British Columbia Hydro and Power Authority,
[1993]  1  S.C.R. 12 at 37.  The appellant's argument erroneously
assumes  that  if the misrepresentation had not  been  made,  the
respondent would have purchased the property but at a lower price
to  reflect the absence of municipal water.  In this case, if the
misrepresentation had not been made the respondent would not have
purchased the property at all because it could not be subdivided.
The damage award was correctly made on this basis.


[33] Nor did the trial judge err in allowing full recovery to the
respondent.  The evidence clearly demonstrated that although  his
brother-in-law  remained on title, the respondent  was  the  full
beneficial owner of the property.


[34]  Finally, although the respondent had not yet paid a sum  to
the mortgagee which the trial judge included in the damage award,
as  the  respondent  admitted,  he  was  clearly  liable  to  the
mortgagee  for  this sum and was entitled to recover  damages  to
compensate for it.


[35] I would therefore dismiss the appeal as to damages.


[36]  Equally I would dismiss the cross-appeal.  The trial  judge
found  that there was not satisfactory proof that Black  had  the
financial  ability to complete the project and as  a  consequence
declined  to  award damages for lost profits in  subdividing  the
property.   There was ample evidence on which this finding  could
be made.  In addition, as I have said, the respondent is entitled
to  be  put  in  the  position he would  have  been  in  had  the
representation not been made, not in the position he  would  have
been in if the representation were true.


[37]  In  the result, I would dismiss the appeal with  costs.   I
would  dismiss the cross-appeal but since it constituted  only  a
very small part of the proceedings I would do so without costs.
