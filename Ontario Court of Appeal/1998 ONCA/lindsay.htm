                      ENDORSEMENT
[1]  The respondent’s duties as the Assistant Chief Revenue
Collector were primarily supervisory and included maintaining and
controlling staff, resolving problems, promoting morale and
handling and securing large amounts of money and fares. The trial
judge found that an essential element of the employment
relationship was mutual trust; this would obviously include trust
by the appellant in the respondent’s ability and integrity to
carry out his duties.


[2]  The trial judge found that the conduct of the appellant in
January and February 1990 undermined this essential element of
the relationship.


[3]  Prior to the alleged theft, on November 29, 1989, the
respondent was well respected as a reliable and conscientious
employee. After being interviewed on December 17th during the
course of the ensuing investigation, the respondent was told that
Sthis seems to be the end of it.”  In actual fact, he remained a
prime suspect and rumours continued in the workplace about his
involvement in the alleged theft. A division developed within
crew members between those who believed the respondent’s denial
of involvement and those who did not.


[4]  On December 28th, the respondent’s solicitor wrote the
appellant complaining of the tarnishing of the respondent’s
reputation, requesting a vindication and warning of the
possibility of a constructive dismissal action. Although the
investigation continued through January and early February, the
appellant did not respond to this letter, did not do anything to
quell the rumours and kept the respondent in the dark about
whether there was an ongoing investigation and if so when it
might end.


[5]  Prior to these events, the appellant’s practice was to
consult the respondent about changes in his work schedule. The
January 1990 schedule, which had been set in early December, was
changed at the end of December without any discussion with the
respondent because of its security concerns relating to the
alleged theft. The appellant reduced the number of supervisory
shifts for the respondent in January.


[6]  In answer to his inquiry about the reason for the change,
the respondent was advised that the changes were made for
employee training purposes. There was no mention of the
continuing investigation.


[7]  On February 2, 1990, the respondent’s schedule for the
period of February 4 to March 3, 1990 was changed by the
appellant; again this was done without prior consultation. This
time the changes resulted in the respondent having four single
days off in the first two weeks; clearly an arrangement that the
appellant knew was undesirable to the respondent.


[8]  When asked the reason, the respondent’s supervisor, Mr.
Ainsley, showed the respondent a memo from his superior Mr.
Henderson, which sent Mr. Ainsley a rather ominous message, “You
created the problem – you fix it.”  Again, the respondent was not
advised that he continued to be under investigation or that there
would be finality to the situation that had developed.


[9]  Shortly afterwards, the respondent resigned. He was never
charged and the appellant does not argue that there is evidence
to support a conclusion that he is in fact guilty.


[10] We are satisfied that the trial judge had sufficient
evidence to make the finding that the appellant’s conduct
resulted in a loss of the essential element of trust in this
employment relationship. It was clearly open to the appellant to
thoroughly investigate the alleged theft. However, it was open to
the trial judge to find that the imperfectly concealed
investigation, the lack of candour, and the last minute
unilateral changes to the schedule undermined the trust that had
existed between the appellant and the respondent.


[11] In our view, on the facts of this case, that finding of loss
of trust is capable of supporting a conclusion that the
respondent was constructively dismissed.


[12] We arrive at this conclusion without accepting the trial
judge’s finding that the rescheduling of the respondent’s days
off and supervisory days in January and February 1990 were in
themselves, breaches of an express or implied term of the
employment agreement. Rather, we find that those unilateral
scheduling changes in the context of the suspicions and rumours
involving the respondent contributed to the breach of what was a
condition of the employment trust.


[13] Finally, we are not satisfied that the trial judge erred in
concluding that the respondent’s failure to continue his
employment with the appellant until he found another job, in the
circumstances of this case, amount to a failure to mitigate.


[14] For the above reasons, the appeal is dismissed with costs.
