                      ENDORSEMENT
                                
[1]  The Toronto Dominion Bank ("the Bank") appeals from a
judgment holding it liable in tort for the non-payment of an
account owed by Atlantis to Lineal Group Inc. ("Lineal").


[2]  Cassandra Thanniah, ("Cassandra") and her husband, Freddy
Naidoo ("Freddy") were shareholders in a company called Brock
Fireplace Ltd. ("Brock"). Brock sold patio furniture at two
separate retail outlets, one in Pickering and one in North York;
each spouse operated one of the retail outlets. The financing for
Brock's inventory came from the Toronto-Dominion Bank. Although
Cassandra and Freddy ceased to live as husband and wife, they
each continued to operate a retail outlet.


[3]  In June or July 1992, Cassandra discovered that the
inventory from the retail outlet of Brock that Freddy operated
had disappeared, and Freddy had also disappeared. She learned
that Freddy had incorporated another company, Atlantis Canadian
Distributors, ("Atlantis") which also sold patio furniture and
that a subsidiary of Tridel, a builder, owed Atlantis money. On
June 1, 1992, Tridel had given Atlantis a confirmation of a
purchase order for patio furniture. The furniture was supplied by
Lineal, and delivered directly by it to various building sites
controlled by Tridel. Cassandra, however, suspected that Freddy
had taken the inventory from his Brock retail outlet and sold it
through Atlantis to Tridel. She went to the Bank with this
information. The Bank's account manager, Ms. Gauweiler, testified
that Cassandra told her that Atlantis was selling Brock's
inventory and that she needed a letter from the Bank to collect
money for the inventory that her husband had sold to Tridel. Ms.
Gauweiler made no further inquiry. She prepared a letter dated
July 22, 1992 addressed to the President of Brock Fireplace. The
letter stated in part that the proceeds of sales by Brock "or
their trading company Atlantis Canadian Distributors Inc. must be
paid to Brock Fireplace Ltd."


[4]  Cassandra called Tridel and told its representative that she
wanted the Atlantis invoice paid to Brock; she sent Tridel a copy
of the bank's letter by fax. Tridel called Ms. Gauweiler who
confirmed that the cheque should be drawn in favour of Brock.
Tridel issued a cheque dated August 14, 1992, which was deposited
to the credit of Brock on August 24, 1992, and the Bank
immediately applied this amount against the debt owing by Brock
to the Bank.


[5]  Lineal, which had supplied the furniture, remained unpaid.
It sued and obtained a default judgment against Atlantis. Lineal
also sued the Toronto-Dominion Bank. Although Lineal supplied
some patio furniture to Brock, the trial judge found that the
patio furniture sold to Tridel did not come from Brock's
inventory. The trial judge found that the Bank, "unwittingly but
intentionally put in motion a series of events which led to the
cheque being deposited to their customer's account, and then the
proceeds plus additional monies being transferred to reduce the
bank's line of credit."  He held the Bank liable for interfering
with Lineal's economic relationship with Atlantis and awarded
Lineal $23,803.97 plus interest and costs. From this judgment the
Bank now appeals.


[6]  The requirements of the tort of economic interference are
succinctly described by Corbett J., with McRae J. concurring, in
Daishowa Inc. v. Friends of the Lubicon (1996), 29 C.C.L.T. 76 at
93 (Ont. Div. Ct.) (leave to appeal to appeal to Ont. C.A.
refused April 24, 1996), Doc. CAM 17675:
                    The tort of intentional
          interference with contractual relations and
          economic interests requires the plaintiff to
          prove:
          
                    1.   An intention to injure the
          plaintiff
          
                    2.   Interference with another's
               method of gaining his or her living or
               business by illegal means (See I.B.T.,
               Local 213 v. Therien, [1960] S.C.R. 265
               at 280 and
               
                    3.   Economic loss caused thereby.
          


[7]  Although the actions of the Bank were intentional, the
actions of the Bank must be targeted against Lineal for the
intention required by the tort of economic interference to be
made out: Cheticamp Fisheries Co-operative Ltd. v. Canada
(1995), 26 C.C.L.T. (2d) 49 (N.S.C.A.) at 50 and see Fleming on
The Law of Torts, 9th ed., under the heading "Unlawful
Interference with Trade or Business" at p. 765. Atlantis, not
Lineal, was the target of the Bank's acts. On the facts of this
case, Lineal is not entitled to stand in the shoes of Atlantis.


[8]  Although the Bank's purpose was to advance Brock's interests
(and by so doing its own interests) and Lineal was injured,
Lineal does not have a cause of action against the Bank for
intentional interference with economic interests or intentional
interference with contractual relations. These torts are
intentional torts. Negligent interference with such interests
does not amount to intentional interference: Clerk and Lindsell
on Torts, 17th ed. at p. 218.


[9]  The Bank is also not liable on the basis of civil fraud.
This tort requires that there be a relationship of proximity
between the maker of the fraudulent misrepresentation and the
person who was defrauded such that there is a departure from the
duty owed by the maker of the statement to the recipient: Derry
v. Peek (1889), 14 A.C. 337 (H.L.) at 350. The statement of the
bank was not made to Lineal and there was no relationship between
them.


[10] Lineal submitted that, in the alternative, it was entitled
to judgment on the basis that the Bank had converted the proceeds
of the account receivable from Tridel in which it had an economic
interest to its own use. Atlantis was owed the account receivable
from Tridel. Lineal, on the evidence, was an unsecured creditor
of Atlantis and cannot claim an interest as such in the account
receivable. Because Lineal had no interest in the account
receivable, it cannot claim that any of its property was
converted.


[11] Lineal's claim on the basis of unjust enrichment was
properly dismissed by the trial judge. Once Tridel paid Brock,
the Bank had a juridical right to receive the funds.


[12] The appeal is allowed, the judgment in favour of Lineal is
set aside and the action as against the Bank is dismissed with
costs at trial and on appeal payable by Lineal to the Bank.
