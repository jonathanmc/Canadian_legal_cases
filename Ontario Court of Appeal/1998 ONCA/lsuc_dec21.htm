                      E N D O R S E M E N T
                                
                                
[1]  Counsel for the appellants concedes that there are too many
proceedings involving the appellants Chavali and the companies
controlled by them. He concedes further that the proceedings are
multiplicitous,  confusing and, in many instances, ill-advised.
He concludes "that they are a mess". He agreed with the
suggestion from the bench that what the appellants require in the
conduct of their litigation is a good editor.


[2]  It appears to the panel that it is in the best interests of
the appellants as well as the respondents and those involved in
the administration of justice that any litigation involving the
appellants should proceed under the aegis of the court. This is
precisely the result that is achieved by the judgment under
appeal.


[3]  In so far as the specific judgment under appeal is
concerned, Cumming J. went through the various pieces of
litigation and, save for some inconsequential misstatements of
fact, he concluded fairly that the various proceedings under
review were vexatious. He said:
                         In my view, and I so find, the
          Respondents [appellants] have persistently
          and without reasonable cause instituted
          vexatious court proceedings. I also find that
          they have persistently and without reasonable
          grounds conducted proceedings in court in a
          vexatious manner.
          
          
[4]  Cumming J. then correctly addressed the consequence of his
finding:
                         The consequence of an order
          under s. 140 [Courts of Justice Act] is that
          the Chavalis may apply for leave to the
          Court. However, the Court will serve as the
          needed screening device and gatekeeper to
          ensure that actions with a plausible claim
          can proceed while vexatious proceedings will
          be thwarted. The essential point is that a s.
          140 order does not prevent access to the
          courts. Rather, the order puts the Court in
          control of the process. The abuse of process
          and chaos seen in the proceedings to date
          will be brought under control. In reality
          (although the Chavalis would disagree) this
          will be to the gain of all parties, including
          the Chavalis. They have undoubtedly put
          considerable wasted time, money, and effort
          into an unfocussed and self-defeating
          pursuit.
          
          
[5]  Counsel objects to the stigma of having the appellants
declared vexatious litigants, but aside from that, we note that
counsel welcomes a court mandated case management of this
litigation. He submits that it would demonstrate that his clients
are not entirely to blame for what has taken place and that it
should be up to the court to determine fault as between the
parties and assist in moving the litigation forward. Counsel
wanted this panel to authorize the appellants to proceed with
three actions which he identified out of the many that are
outstanding in one form or another. However, this court is not
the appropriate forum for such an undertaking. It must be handled
on an on-going basis by a trial judge.


[6]  Accordingly, for all of the above reasons, the appeal is
dismissed with costs.
