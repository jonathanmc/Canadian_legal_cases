                          ENDORSEMENT

[1]  Mr. Steep raised two issues on the argument of this appeal.
The first was that the reasonable notice period was too long and
was based, incorrectly, on the trial judge's mischaracterization
of the length of the respondent's employment with the appellant.
The second was that this was not a case in which punitive damages
should have been awarded.


[2]  We did not call upon Mr. Gorodensky to respond to Mr.
Steep's first submission. The trial judge found that the
respondent was employed by the appellant for 17 years, with a 5
year period of absence. That finding is supported in the evidence
accepted by the trial judge. We would not interfere with it or
with the award of damages made on the basis of reasonable notice
having regard to such a period of employment.


[3]  We did hear from Mr. Gorodensky and Ms. Hoffer in response
to Mr. Steep's second submission. In our view, the award of
punitive damages can be sustained on the basis of an independent
actionable wrong. That independent actionable wrong was the
separate cause of action for misrepresentations made to the
respondent by Mr. Todd on behalf of the appellant with respect to
job security when the respondent was rehired in 1987. The trial
judge found that the respondent was induced to leave another job
in order to return to the appellant's employ. The last time he
had left that company he had made up his mind not to return, and
in fact he declined Mr. Todd's overtures twice before agreeing to
return. He finally agreed after Mr. Todd told him that he would
be a permanent employee with job security and full benefits, that
he would not have to worry about lay-offs and that he would be
employed until he retired. Although the trial judge found that
these representations did not change the contract of employment
from one of indefinite hiring to one for a fixed term, they were
inducements that led the respondent to act by changing jobs and
joining the appellant again. Having induced him to come, the
appellant then had him sign a letter with a 30-day notice clause
that was contrary to the representations and inducements made to
him and that (as the trial judge found) was inserted without the
respondent's knowledge in order to implement company policy to
minimize the cost of terminations. This was an act of bad faith
and transformed Mr. Todd's representations into
misrepresentations. When the appellant terminated the
respondent's employment summarily, without explanation and
without reasonable notice, these representations became
actionable as misrepresentations, an independent actionable wrong
that properly founded the respondent's claim for punitive
damages. We would not disturb the trial judge's decision as to
the quantum of that award.


[4]  The respondent brought a cross-appeal in which he sought an
increase in the award of damages with respect to the reasonable
notice period if the award of punitive damages was disturbed. As
we do not propose to disturb that award, it becomes unnecessary
to consider the arguments advanced with respect to the cross-
appeal.


[5]  The appeal is dismissed with costs. The cross-appeal is also
dismissed but, in the circumstances, without costs.
