                          ENDORSEMENT
[1]  In our view, there was no admissible evidence before the
jury which identified the appellant as the man who shot the
deceased. At the trial, the Crown relied upon the evidence of a
prior identification during a photographic line-up made by the
witness Testebasis on October 17, 1994, six days after the
homicide. In his charge to the jury, the trial judge treated this
as independent evidence of identification on which the jury could
rely in convicting the accused.


[2]  At the trial, Testebasis did not identify the appellant as
the man who shot the deceased. He said in his evidence in-chief
"Here I see a different man." This could mean either that the
accused at the trial was not the man Testebasis identified as the
gunman in the photographic line-up or was not the gunman he saw
on the night in question or, most likely, both of these. He did
not deny that he had, on October 17, 1994, clearly identified the
photograph as being that of the gunman. He gave an explanation
for this earlier identification and why it might have been wrong.
He said that two or three days before the homicide he had seen a
person who had passed in the store that looked something like the
person in the photograph. He said that he must have confused the
man that he saw two or three days earlier and the gunman.


[3]  He said that the accused at the trial did not look like the
gunman at the event or the person in the photograph. He testified
that the man that had the gun had white to blondish hair and that
the man in the photograph # 7 did not.


[4]  We do not think there was a proper basis for the admission
of the photographic line-up evidence. It was not consistent with
positive identification evidence given by Testebasis at trial
because there was none. Second, the witness did not testify that
he had previously made an accurate identification of the accused.
In fact, his evidence was to the contrary. The respondent submits
it was open to the jury to reject his evidence why he could not
identify the photograph as depicting the gunman. Assuming this to
be so, the result would not be that there was evidence from the
witness at the trial that he had previously made an accurate
identification.


[5]  The trial judge correctly instructed the jury:

          None of these witnesses [four eye-witnesses,
          including Testebasis, to the events
          surrounding the October 11 shooting]
          identified the accused in this court as the
          gunman.

The verdict depended entirely on the previous out of court
identification. For the reasons we have given, we do not think it
was admissible.


[6]  In the event that this evidence was admissible, we are
satisfied that any verdict based on it would be unreasonable.
Even if the jury rejected Testebasis's trial evidence, there
would still be no sworn evidence before the jury attesting to the
fact that the previous identification was accurate.


[7]  Further, we are satisfied that the evidence given by the
other three eye-witnesses, either individually or cumulatively
with all of the other evidence, was not reasonably capable of
supporting a verdict of guilt. Before setting forth the parts of
this evidence upon which the respondent particularly relies, we
note that the photographic evidence shows that the appellant was,
to use appellant's counsel's expression, "a bulletin board of
tattoos."


[8]  The witness Mylonas did not identify the appellant as the
gunman in the photographic line-up, nor could he identify anyone
in the courtroom as the gunman. He testified in-chief that the
gunman had a blue tattoo just above the index finger on his right
hand. He could not say if it was one or two because he saw it in
a "split of a second". In cross-examination he acknowledged that
he did not see what the tattoo was. A photograph of the appellant
shows tattoo letters across the whole of the back of the
appellant's right hand.


[9]  The witness Zapounidis was the owner of the cafe where the
homicide took place. He testified that he saw something on the
gunman's right or left hand. He suggested that possibly it was a
tattoo but in cross-examination he said that he could not say for
sure that it was a tattoo. He was unable to identify anyone in
the photographic line-up or in the courtroom as the gunman.


[10] The witness Katsoulis was asked to examine the photographs
on October 18, 1994. At that time he said that the gunman looked
more like photograph #1 than photograph #7 (the appellant). He
did not say either was, in fact, the gunman. At trial he said
that the gunman looked more like #7 than #1. Further, at the
trial he said that the gunman did not look like the man in
photograph #7 and with respect to the appellant at the trial
"there is nothing that I remember that I saw that night."


[11] Assuming that the failure of the appellant to testify is a
factor that could be taken into account on the issue of the
reasonableness of the verdict, in view of the weakness of the
Crown's case it should not be taken into account in this case.


[12] Accordingly, the appeal is allowed, the verdict is set
aside, and a verdict of acquittal is directed to be entered.
