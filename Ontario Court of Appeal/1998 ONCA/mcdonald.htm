ROSENBERG J.A.:
 
[1]     In   this   appeal,   the   appellant   challenges    the
constitutionality  of the four-year minimum  punishment  for  the
offence  of using a firearm while committing robbery contrary  to
s.  344(a) of the Criminal Code. The appellant submits  that  the
four-year  minimum  violates his right to  be  protected  against
cruel  and  unusual punishment as guaranteed  by  s.  12  of  the
Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms.
[2]   Counsel  for  the appellant, Ms. Pringle,  also  raises  an
important issue of statutory interpretation. The appellant  spent
over six months in pre-sentence custody. She argues that even  if
s.  344(a) is constitutional, it was open to the trial  judge  to
have sentenced the appellant to less than four years to take into
account  this pre-sentence custody in accordance with  s.  719(3)
of  the  Code. Section 719(3) provides that the court  "may  take
into  account  any  time  spent in custody"  in  determining  the
sentence to be imposed.
[3]        In  my  view, properly interpreted, s. 719(3)  of  the
Criminal  Code  allows the sentencing judge to take  pre-sentence
custody  into  account  in fixing the term  of  the  sentence  of
imprisonment  for  an  offence  under  s.  344(a).  Absent   that
interpretation   I   would   have   found   s.   344(a)   to   be
unconstitutional.
 
          THE FACTS
 
 
[4]   On  September 30, 1996, at about 9:55 p.m.,  the  appellant
entered a fast-food outlet in the City of Barrie. There was  only
one  employee  on  duty. The appellant asked  about  the  store's
closing time and was told ten o'clock. At 9:58 p.m. the appellant
re-entered  the store. The clerk asked him, "What can  I  do  for
you?"  and the appellant said, "Well, actually, you can  give  me
all  the money you have."  At the same time, the appellant lifted
his  sweatshirt to show the butt of a black gun tucked  into  the
waist  of  his  pants.  The clerk did not understand,  and  asked
"What?"   The appellant again lifted his sweater and said,  "Give
me  all the money you have."  The clerk handed the money from the
till  to  the  appellant. The appellant  took  the  money,  said,
"Thanks" and left the store. The clerk was not injured.
[5]   The  police  were  called and after a  brief  investigation
arrested  the  appellant  later that same  night.  The  appellant
readily  admitted his involvement in the offence and  the  police
recovered  all  the money, approximately $300, taken  during  the
robbery. The gun that was displayed during the robbery was  found
in  the  trunk of the appellant's car. It was a BB  gun  and  was
unloaded. The police also found a ski mask.
[6]  The appellant was twenty-one years of age at the time of the
offence.  He  had  one  prior conviction for  assault  two  years
earlier  for which he had received a suspended sentence  and  one
year  probation.  At the time of the offence, the  appellant  had
almost  completed Grade 10 and was hoping to return to a  private
school  to  finish his schooling. However, he did  not  have  the
funds  for the tuition. The appellant was unemployed at the  time
of the robbery. He claimed that the money from the robbery was to
be used for rent and other necessities.
[7]   The appellant suffers from a manic-depressive disorder.  He
had previously been treated for this disorder, but was not taking
his  medication  nor receiving medical care at the  time  of  the
offence.  He claimed that he was unable to afford the  medication
and believed he could control his medical condition through "will
power".
[8]   In a letter that he wrote to the court prior to sentencing,
the  appellant  expressed  shame and  remorse.  He  realized  the
enormous hurt he had caused his family and wrote of his  fear  of
going   to   the   penitentiary.  The  appellant  accepted   full
responsibility for his actions and stated his intention to make a
fresh start in life.
[9]   The  appellant remained in custody from  the  date  of  his
arrest  on  September 30, 1996. He pleaded guilty to the  offence
contrary to s. 344(a) of the Code on February 18, 1997. The  case
was  adjourned to permit argument on the constitutionality of the
minimum punishment.
[10]  During  submissions  before the  sentencing  judge  on  the
constitutional  question,  counsel for  the  appellant,  not  Ms.
Pringle, argued that s. 344(a) was of no force and effect because
it  authorized the imposition of cruel and unusual punishment. In
the  alternative,  counsel argued that the  appellant  should  be
given  a  constitutional exemption. Counsel did  not  argue  that
there  was  a basis apart from the Charter that would  allow  the
trial judge to reduce the sentence below four years to take  into
account the pre-sentence custody.
[11] On April 18, 1997, Libman Prov. Ct. J. delivered lengthy and
comprehensive reasons for finding s. 344(a) to be constitutional.
He  then  imposed a sentence of four years' imprisonment  on  the
appellant. In all, the appellant spent approximately six and one-
half  months in pre-sentence custody. He received no  credit  for
the  time  spent  in pre-sentence custody against  the  four-year
sentence.  Bearing  in  mind that trial judges  will  often  give
credit  of two days for each day spent in pre-trial custody,  the
sentence  imposed  could be viewed as approximating  a  five-year
sentence of imprisonment.
 
          OVERVIEW OF THE CASE
 
 
[12]  The  resolution of this case primarily centres  around  two
provisions of the Criminal Code. What is now s. 719(3) was  added
to  the Code in 1972 as part of the Bail Reform Act, R.S.C.  1970
(2nd Supp.), c. 2. It provides as follows:
 
          (3) In determining the sentence to be imposed
          on  a person convicted of an offence, a court
          may  take  into  account any  time  spent  in
          custody  by  the person as a  result  of  the
          offence.
 
 
[13]  Section 344(a) of the Criminal Code was enacted as part  of
the  Firearms  Act,  S.C.  1995, c. 39. That  Act  brought  about
significant  amendments to the firearm provisions  of  the  Code,
many  of  which have not yet been proclaimed into force. The  Act
also  increased the minimum punishment for a number  of  offences
where  a  firearm is used in the commission of the  offence.  The
provision  of concern in this appeal was proclaimed in  force  on
January 1, 1996 and reads as follows:
 
          344.  Every  person  who commits  robbery  is
          guilty of an indictable offence and liable
          (a) where a firearm is used in the commission
          of  the offence, to imprisonment for life and
          to a minimum punishment of imprisonment for a
          term of four years; and
          (b)  in  any other case, to imprisonment  for
          life.
 
 
[14]  The  constitutional  challenge to  the  minimum  punishment
provided for by s. 344(a) is based on s. 12 of the Charter:
 
          12.   Everyone  has  the  right  not  to   be
          subjected  to any cruel and unusual treatment
          or punishment.
 
 
[15]  The  Supreme Court of Canada has refined the  framework  of
analysis of a constitutional challenge based on s. 12 in a number
of  decisions. The two most important for this appeal are  R.  v.
Smith, [1987] 1 S.C.R. 1045 and R. v. Goltz, [1991] 3 S.C.R. 485.
Both cases concerned statutory minimum punishments. In Smith, the
court  found  that the seven-year minimum sentence for  importing
narcotics under the former Narcotic Control Act, R.S.C. 1985,  c.
N-1  was unconstitutional. In Goltz, the Court upheld a seven-day
minimum  punishment for driving while prohibited contrary  to  s.
88(1)(c) of the Motor Vehicle Act, R.S.B.C. 1979, c. 288.
[16]  The analysis dictated by these two cases proceeds in  three
stages. The sentencing provision will be found to infringe s.  12
if it would provide for and would actually impose a sentence that
is so excessive or grossly disproportionate as to outrage decency
in  the  particular  circumstances of  this  offender.  When  the
particular   facts   of  the  case  do  not   result   in   gross
disproportionality, the court moves to the second  stage  and  it
must  consider  whether  the impugned provision  would  impose  a
grossly  disproportionate punishment in  reasonable  hypothetical
circumstances. If so, the section will be found to violate s. 12.
Finally,  if the court is persuaded that there is a violation  of
s.  12, it must consider whether the provision can be saved as  a
reasonable limit under s. 1 of the Charter.
[17]   Three   appellate   courts  that   have   considered   the
constitutionality  of  s. 344(a), or one  of  the  other  minimum
punishment provisions enacted by the Firearms Act, have  added  a
fourth  step,  which  involves  a constitutional  exemption.  The
Quebec Court of Appeal in R. v. Lapierre (1998), 123 C.C.C.  (3d)
332  and the British Columbia Court of Appeal in R. v. Wust  (May
7,  1998) have considered the constitutionality of s. 344(a)  and
held  that  it  does  not infringe s. 12.  Similarly,  in  R.  v.
Morrisey (March 23, 1998) the Nova Scotia Court of Appeal  upheld
the  validity  of  s. 220(a), which imposes a  four-year  minimum
punishment of imprisonment for the offence of criminal negligence
causing  death  with  a  firearm.  These  courts  have,  however,
suggested  that where imposition of the four-year  minimum  would
result  in the infliction of cruel and unusual punishment on  the
particular  offender,  the  court  may  grant  that  offender   a
constitutional exemption. In Lapierre and in Morrisey, the courts
held  that the constitutional exemption might well be appropriate
where the offender had spent a considerable period of time in pre-
sentence custody.
[18]   This   appeal  then  raises  the  following   issues   for
determination:
 
          I.    Irrespective  of the  Charter  does  s.
          719(3)  of the Code permit a judge to  deduct
          time  spent in pre-sentence custody from  the
          sentence to be imposed under s. 344(a)?
          II.   Properly  interpreted, does  s.  344(a)
          violate s. 12 of the Charter?
          III. If s. 344(a) does violate s. 12, can  it
          be  saved as a reasonable limit under s. 1 of
          the Charter?
          IV.   If s. 344(a) is constitutional,  is  it
          open  to  a  court  to grant  an  offender  a
          constitutional exemption from  the  operation
          of  the  provision,  particularly  where  the
          offender has spent a lengthy period  of  time
          in pre-sentence custody?
 
 
[19]  While these appear to be four discrete issues, analysis  of
each  of  the issues is affected by the resolution of the others.
In  summary,  however,  my conclusions are  as  follows.  Section
719(3)  allows  a sentencing court to deduct time spent  in  pre-
sentence custody from the four-year minimum punishment prescribed
by  s.  344(a).  As a result, s. 344(a) does not infringe  s.  12
either   in   this  case  or  in  other  reasonable  hypothetical
circumstances.  The section is therefore valid and  there  is  no
need  to  consider  s. 1. If I am wrong and pre-sentence  custody
cannot  be taken into account, I would have found that s.  344(a)
violates  s.  12.  Finally, I do not agree that a  constitutional
exemption  is  available, even in circumstances of  lengthy  pre-
sentence custody.
 
          ANALYSIS
 
 
          I.   The Statutory Interpretation Issue
 
 
[20]  I  view  the  constitutional and interpretation  issues  as
dependent on each other. The Charter analysis of s. 344(a) cannot
be  undertaken until that provision has been properly interpreted
and  understood  within the context of the  other  relevant  Code
provisions,  particularly  s.  719.  Further,  if  one  of  these
provisions is ambiguous, it may assist the interpretive  exercise
to  consider  some  of the values enshrined in  the  Charter.  In
resolving  the  difficult interpretation  issue  raised  by  this
appeal,  I have drawn upon five rules of interpretation that  are
relevant   in  this  case.  I  begin  with  the  rule  respecting
interpretation of penal statutes.
 
          i.   Interpretation of penal statutes
 
 
[21]  Where a penal statute is ambiguous, this ambiguity must  be
resolved in favour of the accused. A recent reaffirmation of this
fundamental  rule of interpretation can be found in the  decision
of Chief Justice Lamer in R. v. McIntosh, [1995] 1 S.C.R. 686. In
that  case,  at p. 701, he referred to the "overriding  principle
governing  the interpretation of penal provisions" as  stated  by
Dickson  J.  in Marcotte v. Deputy Attorney-General  for  Canada,
[1976] 1 S.C.R. 108 at 115:
 
                Even  if  I were to conclude  that  the
          relevant  statutory provisions were ambiguous
          and  equivocal ... I would have to  find  for
          the appellant in this case. It is unnecessary
          to  emphasize the importance of  clarity  and
          certainty  when  freedom  is  at  stake.   No
          authority is needed for the proposition  that
          if  real ambiguities are found, or doubts  of
          substance  arise,  in  the  construction  and
          application   of  a  statute  affecting   the
          liberty  of  a  subject,  then  that  statute
          should  be  applied in such a  manner  as  to
          favour  the person against whom it is  sought
          to be enforced. [Emphasis added.]
          And  at  p.  702  of McIntosh,  Lamer  C.J.C.
          restated  the principle in his own  words  in
          these terms:
          It is a principle of statutory interpretation
          that where two interpretations of a provision
          which  affects the liberty of a  subject  are
          available, one of which is more favourable to
          an  accused, then the court should adopt this
          favourable  interpretation.  By   this   same
          reasoning, where such a provision is, on  its
          face, favourable to an accused, then I do not
          think  that  a  court should  engage  in  the
          interpretive process advocated by  the  Crown
          for   the  sole  purpose  of  narrowing   the
          provision  and  making it less favourable  to
          the accused. [Emphasis added.]
 
 
[22] The first step in this analysis is to interpret s. 719(3) as
it   applies  to  statutory  provisions  that  impose  a  minimum
punishment  such as s. 344(a). The relevant parts of s.  719  are
the following:
 
          719  (1)  A  sentence commences  when  it  is
          imposed,  except  where a relevant  enactment
          otherwise provides.
           (2) Any time during which a convicted person
          is  unlawfully  at large or  is  lawfully  at
          large on interim release granted pursuant  to
          any  provision of this Act does not count  as
          part  of any term of imprisonment imposed  on
          the person.
            (3)  In  determining  the  sentence  to  be
          imposed  on a person convicted of an offence,
          a  court may take into account any time spent
          in  custody by the person as a result of  the
          offence.
 
 
[23]  Section  719(3),  in  my  view,  is  clearly  open  to  the
interpretation  that  the sentencing court is  allowed  to  count
against  the sentence that might otherwise be imposed,  any  time
spent in custody by the offender "as a result of the offence". It
obviously  refers  to pre-sentence custody  since  it  is  to  be
invoked  "in determining the sentence to be imposed". It is  not,
in my view, a mere codification of a principle of sentencing, but
a provision giving a court a substantive power to relieve against
what  might  otherwise be considered a harsh  or  unfair  result,
inter  alia,  because of s. 719(1). That subsection  prohibits  a
court from backdating a sentence or allowing the sentence to take
effect  at  some future time, in the absence of express statutory
authority.1
[24]  I  take some comfort in this interpretation from  the  fact
that  in  Part XXIII, s. 719 falls under the heading  "Punishment
Generally"  rather  than  the earlier  heading  of  "Purpose  and
Principles of Sentencing". If s. 719(3) merely permits a court to
give  some  consideration to pre-sentence custody, it is  nothing
but  a principle of sentencing and one would have expected it  to
appear  in s. 718.2 with the other principles of sentencing.  If,
however, s. 719(3) gives a court a substantive power to count pre-
sentence  custody  in fixing the length of the  sentence,  it  is
properly located. Such an interpretation is also consistent  with
the immediately preceding subsection, which provides as follows:
 
          (2)  Any time during which a convicted person
          is  unlawfully  at large or  is  lawfully  at
          large on interim release granted pursuant  to
          any  provision of this Act does not count  as
          part  of any term of imprisonment imposed  on
          the person.
 
 
[25]  The  difference between s. 719(2) and 719(3)  is  that  the
former  is apparently directed to correctional officials  charged
with  the  duty  of calculating the sentence. Section  719(3)  is
directed  to  the  sentencing judge,  but  in  my  view  gives  a
substantive  power  and  does not merely  state  a  principle  of
sentencing.
[26] It is not apparent to me that s. 719(3) should not apply  to
statutory minimum sentences. This would necessitate reading words
into the subsection so that it would read as follows:
 
          In  determining the sentence to be imposed on
          a person convicted of an offence, a court may
          take  into account any time spent in  custody
          by  the  person as a result of  the  offence,
          except    where   the   relevant    enactment
          prescribes a minimum punishment.
 
 
[27]  A  similar  argument was made and rejected by  the  Supreme
Court  in  R. v. McIntosh, supra. In McIntosh, the court  had  to
consider  the self-defence provisions of s. 34 of the  Code.  The
particular  question  was whether s. 34(2),  like  s.  34(1)  was
unavailable  where the accused was the initial aggressor  in  the
conflict.  The Crown argued for a contextual approach that  would
mean  s. 34(2) was not available to the initial aggressor.  Lamer
C.J.C. rejected that approach at p. 701:
 
          [T]he  contextual approach allows the  courts
          to depart from the common grammatical meaning
          of   words  where  this  is  required  by   a
          particular context, but it does not generally
          mandate  the  courts to  read  words  into  a
          statutory  provisions. It is only when  words
          are   "reasonably  capable  of   bearing"   a
          particular   meaning   that   they   may   be
          interpreted contextually. [Emphasis added.]
 
 
[28]  In  McIntosh,  Lamer C.J.C. also examined  the  legislative
history of the self-defence provisions in attempting to find  the
proper  interpretation. An examination of the legislative history
of  s. 719 also sheds light on the proper interpretation of  that
provision.  I have already noted that what is now s.  719(3)  was
enacted as part of the Bail Reform Act. Prior to that Act,  there
was  no  statutory authority governing the effect of pre-sentence
custody.  The  predecessor  to  s.  719(1)  was  worded  somewhat
differently and at different times was found either in  the  Code
or  the Prison and Reformatories Act, R.S.C. 1927, c. 163 or  the
Penitentiary Act, 1939, (Can.), c. 6. For example, s.  3  of  the
Prison  and  Reformatories  Act  provided  that,  "The  term   of
imprisonment in pursuance of any sentence shall, unless otherwise
directed in the sentence, commence on and from the day of passing
of  such sentence."  This court held that this provision did  not
provide authority to backdate a sentence to take into account pre-
sentence  custody.  The  applicable  sentencing  principle    was
expressed  in  R. v. Sloan (1947), 87 C.C.C. 198 at  198-9  (Ont.
C.A.):
 
          This Court has had occasion to recently point
          out  in  R.  v. Patterson, 87 Can.  C.C.  86,
          [1947] O.W.N. 146, that there is no authority
          for  the  "dating back of any sentence".  The
          sentence  can only bear the date on which  it
          is  imposed  and  any  term  of  imprisonment
          contained therein cannot begin to run earlier
          than the date of the sentence itself. This is
          not  to say that the Court can not take  into
          consideration,  in  imposing  sentence,   any
          period of incarceration which the accused has
          already  undergone between the  date  of  his
          arrest and the date of the sentence, but such
          period  cannot form part of the term  imposed
          by  the  sentence. If the  Court  is  of  the
          opinion that the circumstance justifies  such
          a   course,  it  may  reduce  the   term   of
          imprisonment,   which  it   would   otherwise
          impose, by the whole or part of the period of
          imprisonment   already   served.    [Emphasis
          added.]
 
 
[29]  In 1969, Parliament made it clear through the Criminal  Law
Amendment  Act, 1968-69, c. 38 that a court had no general  power
to  backdate  a sentence by removing the words "unless  otherwise
directed in the sentence" from what had by then become s. 624  of
the Criminal Code. The amended provision was as follows:
 
          624.  (1)   A sentence commences when  it  is
          imposed,  except  where a relevant  enactment
          otherwise provides.
 
 
[30]  That  then was the statutory context prior to enactment  of
the  Bail  Reform  Act.  Before I consider  the  effect  of  that
amendment, it is appropriate to briefly examine the nature of pre-
sentence custody.
[31]  In  R.  v. Rezaie (1996), 112 C.C.C. (3d) 97 at  104  (Ont.
C.A.), Laskin J.A. explained the rationale for giving credit  for
pre-trial  custody  when fixing the length  of  the  sentence  of
imprisonment:
 
          [A] judge should not deny credit without good
          reason.  To  do  so offends  one's  sense  of
          fairness. Incarceration at any stage  of  the
          criminal  process is a denial of an accused's
          liberty. Moreover, in two respects, pre-trial
          custody  is  even  more  onerous  than  post-
          sentencing custody. First, other than  for  a
          sentence  of  life imprisonment,  legislative
          provisions   for   parole   eligibility   and
          statutory  release do not take  into  account
          time spent in custody before trial (or before
          sentencing). Second, local detention  centres
          ordinarily   do   not  provide   educational,
          retraining or rehabilitation programs  to  an
          accused  in custody waiting trial. For  these
          reasons,   pre-trial  custody   is   commonly
          referred to as "dead time", and trial judges,
          in   deciding  on  an  appropriate  sentence,
          frequently give credit for double the time an
          accused has served. [Emphasis added.]
 
 
[32]  In Detention Before Trial, A Study of Criminal Cases  Tried
in the Toronto Magistrates' Court (Toronto: University of Toronto
Press,  1965)  at p. 105, Professor Martin L. Friedland  observes
that  while it is "axiomatic that a person is not kept in custody
pending  trial  as a punishment but primarily to assure  that  he
will appear at his trial", in virtually all respects, persons  in
custody  awaiting  trial  are  indistinguishable  from  those  in
custody serving sentences. At p. 104 he quotes from the New  York
Bail Study by Professor Caleb Foote that "the most ironic finding
in  the whole study is the revelation that accused persons,  whom
our  law  presumes to be innocent and who are not to be punished,
are  confined  pending  trial under  conditions  which  are  more
oppressive  and restrictive than those applied to  convicted  and
sentenced  felons."  All this led Professor Friedland to  comment
at  p.  108  that,  "Because time spent in custody  before  trial
necessarily has the effect of acting as a punishment, the time so
spent  should  be  taken into account by the  magistrate  if  the
accused  is  convicted."  There has been  little  change  in  the
conditions under which remand prisoners are held in this province
in  the  almost  forty years since Professor  Friedland  did  his
study: see R. v. Sanchez, [1996] O.J. No. 7 (Ont. C.A.).
[33]  In  his text, The Law of Bail in Canada (Toronto: Carswell,
1992)  at p. 28, Gary Trotter summarizes the effects of pre-trial
detention in the following terms:
 
          A  number  of commentators have stressed  the
          hardships  which prisoners held without  bail
          must  endure. Remand prisoners, as  they  are
          sometimes  called,  often  spend  their  time
          awaiting trial in detention centres or  local
          jails  that are ill-suited to lengthy  stays.
          As   the   Ouimet   Report   stressed,   such
          institutions may restrict liberty  more  than
          many  institutions which house the convicted.
          Due  to overcrowding, inmate turnover and the
          problems of effectively implementing programs
          and  recreation activities, serving  time  in
          such  institutions can be quite onerous. This
          is no doubt why pre-trial detention, which is
          punctuated  by  inactivity, has  come  to  be
          known  as "dead time" and is sometimes  taken
          into  account by sentencing judges. [Emphasis
          added.]
 
 
[34]  Mr.  Trotter also notes, at p. 29, the "troubling" negative
relationship between pre-trial detention and success in court. He
referred to research showing that "compared with those who remain
at  liberty pending trial, accused who are denied bail tend to be
found  guilty more often and are more likely to receive custodial
sentences."2
[35]  Following  upon  the publication of  Professor  Friedland's
study  and  the Report of the Canadian Committee on  Corrections:
Towards   Unity:   Criminal  Justice  and  Corrections   (Ottawa:
Queen's   Printer,  March  31,  1969)  [The  Ouimet   Committee],
Parliament  enacted  the  Bail  Reform  Act,  which  resulted  in
sweeping  changes to the bail laws of this country.  As  is  well
known,  those  provisions were designed  to  ensure,  so  far  as
possible, that accused would be released pending their  trial  if
it  was  safe to do so and if they would likely appear for trial.
However,  there  would,  of course, still  be  accused  who  were
detained  in  custody prior to trial. Parliament dealt  with  one
aspect  of  that  situation by the enactment of what  is  now  s.
719(3).  In  my  view, that subsection was not only  intended  to
confirm the existing sentencing principle as explained in  R.  v.
Sloan,  but was also intended to give the judge an express  power
to  take  into  account pre-sentence custody  where  the  statute
prescribed a minimum punishment. This is made clear both  in  the
House  of Commons Debates and in a text published at the time  of
the Bail Reform Act was enacted.
[36]  During House of Commons Debates (5 February 1971) at second
reading  of  Bill C-218, Amendment of Provisions of the  Criminal
Code  relating to Arrest and Bail, the Minister of Justice,  John
Turner, stated clearly the government's intention in reference to
the new provision at p. 3118:
 
          Generally  speaking, the courts  in  deciding
          what sentence to impose on a person convicted
          of  an offence take into account the time  he
          has spent in custody awaiting trial. However,
          under  the present Criminal Code, a  sentence
          commences  only when it is imposed,  and  the
          court's hands are tied in those cases where a
          minimum term of imprisonment must be imposed.
          In  such cases, therefore, the court is bound
          to  impose not less than the minimum sentence
          even  though  the convicted person  may  have
          been  in custody awaiting trial for a  period
          in  excess of the minimum sentence.  The  new
          version  of the bill would permit the  court,
          in  a  proper  case, to take this  time  into
          account   in  imposing  sentence.   [Emphasis
          added.]
 
 
[37] This was also the understanding of John Scollin. I find  his
views  of  particular assistance since he was with the Department
of  Justice  at  the time the Bail Reform Act was introduced  and
testified  before  the Standing Committee on  Justice  and  Legal
Affairs.  In  his  text, The Bail Reform  Act:   An  Analysis  of
Amendments  to  the  Criminal Code Related  to  Bail  and  Arrest
(Toronto:  Carswell, 1972) at p. 91, he summarizes the effect  of
the new provision:
 
          This  subsection gives statutory sanction  to
          the  practice  of the courts in  taking  into
          account in determining sentence the time that
          an accused has already spent in custody as  a
          result   of   the   offence.   An   important
          extension,  however, is that the  court  will
          now  be  able to take such time into  account
          even  in  the case where there is  a  minimum
          sentence  which would otherwise not  commence
          until it is imposed. [Emphasis in original.]
 
 
[38]  Even if s. 719(3) is ambiguous and it is uncertain  whether
it  gives the judge a substantive power to take account  of  pre-
sentence custody where the Code requires imposition of a  minimum
punishment,  the rule stated in McIntosh requires the  courts  to
give  the  provision the interpretation that favours the accused.
Such  an  interpretation  is  also consistent  with  Parliament's
evident intention. The next question is whether there is anything
in  s.  344(a)  itself to indicate that s.  719(3)  can  have  no
application  to  sentences  imposed under  that  provision.  This
brings  me to a second rule of interpretation concerning conflict
between statutory enactments.
 
          ii.   Interpretation  of  statutes  to  avoid conflict
 
 
[39]  The  relationship between s. 344(a) and s. 719(3) has  been
considered  by the Quebec Court of Appeal in R. v. Alain  (1997),
119 C.C.C. (3d) 177. In that case, the Court of Appeal allowed  a
Crown  appeal from a sentence of three and one-half years imposed
for  the  offence  contrary to s. 344(a).  The  trial  judge  had
imposed  this  sentence  by  taking into  account  the  pre-trial
custody pursuant to s. 719(3). The Court of Appeal disagreed  and
increased the sentence to four years. After a review of  some  of
the  case law on the interpretation of s. 719(3) and particularly
the  decision of my colleague Mr. Justice Borins in R.  v.  Brown
(1976),  36 C.R.N.S. 246 (Ont. Co. Ct.), LeBel J.A. concluded  as
follows at p. 185:
 
          Pursuant to s. 721(1) [now s. 719(1)] of  the
          Criminal Code, a sentence begins at the  time
          that  it is imposed (see R. v. Masse,  [1996]
          R.J.Q.  564  (C.A.) at p.  567,  per  Mailhot
          J.A.).  Only  s.  746 of  the  Criminal  Code
          introduces a few derogations from  this  rule
          for  the purposes of the calculation  of  the
          period  of imprisonment for certain  offences
          involving life sentences. The general rule is
          firm. The sentence begins at the time that it
          is  imposed.  In the case at bar,  Parliament
          intended  that judicial discretion  under  s.
          344  of  the Criminal Code only be  exercised
          where  a sentence of at least four years  has
          been   imposed.  The  Crown  appeal  is  well
          founded.
 
 
[40] The proposition apparently underlying the decisions in Alain
and  in  Brown is that there is a conflict between s. 719(3)  and
the provision creating the minimum punishment, such as s. 344(a).
In  view of this conflict, the courts applied the principle  that
specific  provisions of the punishment sections  take  precedence
over the more general provision of s. 719(3). In R. v. Brown, the
court had to consider s. 5(2) of the former Narcotic Control Act,
R.S.C.  1970,  c.  N-1. That subsection set  out  the  seven-year
minimum  punishment  for the offence of importing  and  exporting
narcotics contrary to s. 5(1).3  Borins Co. Ct. J. expressed  the
difficulty of reconciling the two provisions in these terms at p.
248-9:
 
          I would agree that the language of s. 5(2) of
          the   Narcotic  Control  Act  is  clear   and
          unambiguous and that, accordingly,  I  cannot
          impose  a sentence of less than seven  years.
          The  express  provisions of s.  5(2)  of  the
          Narcotic Control Act take precedence over the
          general  provisions of s.  649(2.1)  [now  s.
          719(3)]  of the Criminal Code. Y   It  is  my
          opinion  that if I were to take into  account
          the 8 2 months which the accused has spent in
          custody as s. 649(2.1) entitles a trial judge
          to  do, and in doing so impose a sentence  of
          less   than  seven  years,  it  would  be   a
          violation of s. 5(2) of the Narcotic  Control
          Act.   Nor   do  I  perceive  any   statutory
          authority  which would enable me to  pre-date
          the sentence or in any way credit the accused
          with  the time which he has spent in custody.
          Section 649(1) of the Criminal Code is  clear
          that  sentence commences when it is  imposed.
          [Emphasis added.]
 
 
[41]  As  this  passage shows, Borins Co. Ct. J. saw  a  conflict
between  the  provisions of s. 649 [now s. 719] and  the  minimum
term  of  imprisonment mandated by s. 5(2). It may be that  Brown
can be distinguished on the basis of the different wording of  s.
5(2) which speaks of the accused being liable to "imprisonment  Y
for  not less than seven years", whereas s. 344(a) refers  to  "a
minimum punishment of imprisonment for a term of four years".
[42]  In  any event, I am not satisfied that there is a  conflict
between  s.  344(a)  and s. 719. It is a principle  of  statutory
construction  that  courts  should attempt  to  avoid  finding  a
conflict  between two pieces of legislation. Anglin J.  expressed
this principle in The Toronto Railway Company v. Paget (1909), 42
S.C.R. 488 at p. 499:
 
 
          It  is  not enough to exclude the application
          of  the  general  Act that it deals  somewhat
          differently with the same subject-matter.  It
          is   not   "inconsistent"  unless   the   two
          provisions cannot stand together.
 
 
[43]  In my view, s. 344(a) and s. 719 can stand together and  it
is  not  necessary  to  exclude the application  of  the  general
provisions  of  s.  719.  A  first  step  in  dealing  with   the
interpretation  problem is to define the terms  "punishment"  and
"sentence".  The  former  term is  used  in  s.  344(a)  and  for
convenience I will set that paragraph out again:
 
          344.  Every  person  who commits  robbery  is
          guilty of an indictable offence and liable
          (a) where a firearm is used in the commission
          of  the offence, to imprisonment for life and
          to a minimum punishment of imprisonment for a
          term of four years;  [Emphasis added.]
 
 
[44]  It  seems apparent to me that Parliament has used the  term
"punishment"  in  s.  344(a) to track the  wording  of  718.3(2).
Section 718.3 provides as follows:
 
          718.3   (1)  Where  an  enactment  prescribes
          different  degrees or kinds of punishment  in
          respect of an offence, the punishment  to  be
          imposed   is,   subject  to  the  limitations
          prescribed   in   the   enactment,   in   the
          discretion  of  the  court  that  convicts  a
          person who commits the offence.
             (2)   Where  an  enactment  prescribes   a
          punishment  in  respect of  an  offence,  the
          punishment to be imposed is, subject  to  the
          limitations  prescribed in the enactment,  in
          the  discretion of the court that convicts  a
          person  who  commits  the  offence,  but   no
          punishment is a minimum punishment unless  it
          is  declared  to  be  a  minimum  punishment.
          [Emphasis added.]
          
[45] Parliament has thus clearly expressed the intention that the
punishment imposed under s. 344(a) is a minimum punishment.  This
express  language ousts the general discretion of the  sentencing
court  under  s.  718.3(1)  to select  the  form  and  degree  of
punishment.  I am not convinced, however, that the same  language
also  precludes  a  court  from "taking into  account  any"  pre-
sentence  custody  as  directed by  s.  719(3).  In  fact,  given
Parliament's  clear  intention when [now]  s.  719(3)  was  first
enacted, if Parliament intended for that subsection not to  apply
to  s. 344(a), I would have expected to find express language  to
that effect.
[46]  A distinction must be drawn between the terms sentence  and
punishment.  The Canadian Sentencing Commission  in  its  Report,
Sentencing Reform A Canadian Approach (Ottawa:  Queen's  Printer,
February 1987) spent some time considering the meaning of the two
terms and at p. 111 came up with this definition:
 
          The  word  "sentence" comes  from  the  Latin
          "sententia",  which  means  opinion  or   the
          expression  of an opinion. Therein  lies  one
          fundamental  difference between a  punishment
          and  a  sentence. The former  is  the  actual
          infliction  of  a  deprivation,  whereas  the
          latter is a statement ordering the imposition
          of  a sanction and determining what it should
          be.
 
 
[47]  In  its discussion of the two terms, the Commission further
contrasted  sentencing and punishment by defining the latter,  at
p.  109,  as  the "imposition of severe deprivation on  a  person
found guilty of wrongdoing". The Commission left no doubt that it
considered imprisonment to be a severe deprivation. Sentencing on
the   other   hand  is  defined  at  p.  115  as  the   "judicial
determination of a legal sanction to be imposed on a person found
guilty of an offence".
[48]  I  have  already  referred to the  excerpt  from  Professor
Friedland's study in which he stated that it is axiomatic that  a
person is not held in custody pending trial as a punishment. In a
free  and democratic society accused persons are not denied  bail
to  punish them before their guilt has been determined. In R.  v.
Pearson,  [1992] 3 S.C.R. 665 at 687-88, Lamer C.J.C.  held  that
the  presumption of innocence as guaranteed by s.  11(d)  of  the
Charter  has  "no application at the tail stage of  the  criminal
process,  where  the  guilt or innocence of the  accused  is  not
determined  and  where  punishment  is  not  imposed".  [Emphasis
added.]   Similarly,  in  R.  v.  Sanchez,  supra  at  para.  23,
Austin  J.A.  recognized that the conditions under  which  remand
prisoners   are   held  arguably  could  in  some   circumstances
constitute  "punishment", even if such prisoners  are  not  being
"punished" so as to violate their rights under ss. 7  and  12  of
the Charter: R. v. Sanchez, at para. 26. However, to pretend that
pre-sentence imprisonment does not occasion a severe  deprivation
and  that  it is not punitive would result in a triumph  of  form
over  substance.  Parliament  has  recognized  this  reality   in
enacting s. 719(3). As a result of his exhaustive study  of  pre-
trial  custody in Toronto, Professor Friedland clearly  saw  pre-
trial  custody as punishment. Similarly, the Ouimet Committee  in
the  Report of the Canadian Committee on Corrections  at  p.  102
favoured  extensive  amendments to the bail  laws  because,  "The
release of the accused pending his trial avoids the infliction of
punishment on a person not yet proved guilty whom under  Canadian
law is presumed to be innocent."
[49]  My  approach in this respect is similar to  that  taken  by
Dubin J.A. in R. v. Wooff (1973), 14 C.C.C. (2d) 396 (Ont. C.A.).
In  that  case,  the  Crown appealed from the suspended  sentence
imposed  upon  the  accused for possession of narcotics  for  the
purpose  of trafficking. All members of the court agreed  on  the
governing  principle.  Except in unusual  circumstances,  a  jail
sentence  must be imposed for trafficking offences.  The  accused
argued  that  the  30  days he had spent in pre-sentence  custody
satisfied  this principle. Gale C.J.O., writing for the majority,
disagreed with the accused's submission as he was concerned  that
to  adopt  the  proposed principle could invite  abuse4  of  pre-
sentence custody. He wrote as follows at p. 398:
 
          It  was argued that the time spent in custody
          prior  to  sentence could be  regarded  as  a
          custodial  sentence in addition  to  the  two
          years  probation. I do not so regard  it.  It
          must be taken into consideration, and what  I
          propose  does take it into consideration  but
          that  is  as far as I am prepared to go  with
          respect  to the pre-sentence time in custody.
          If  countenance were given to that  argument,
          pre-sentence custody abuse would soon  occur.
          By counting that as part of the sentence, any
          person who receives that treatment might very
          well be prejudiced. [Emphasis added.]
 
 
[50]  Dubin J.A., dissenting, took a slightly different  view  of
the pre-sentence custody, and held as follows at p. 400:
 
          I  view  the substance of the sentence rather
          than its form as one of 30 days imprisonment,
          plus  probation.  I  do  not  feel  that   by
          considering it in this way I will invite  any
          abuse  of  pre-sentencing custody. Viewed  in
          that  way, therefore, the learned trail Judge
          did not err in failing to impose some term of
          imprisonment. As I have observed, the term of
          imprisonment in these cases must vary, having
          in  mind the underlying facts upon which  the
          conviction  is  founded  and  the  particular
          circumstances of the accused.  I  cannot  say
          that  it  was not open to the learned  County
          Court Judge in the exercise of his discretion
          to  impose this term of imprisonment  in  the
          circumstances  of the case which  was  before
          him. [Emphasis added.]
 
 
[51]  Whatever concerns Gale C.J.O. may have had about  abuse  of
pre-sentence custody in the circumstances of that case  can  have
no  application  where a minimum sentence is under consideration.
As  Dubin J.A. said, the court ought to look to the substance  of
the matter.
[52]  Accordingly,  in compliance with s. 344(a)  and  consistent
with  Parliament's  intent, the judge in imposing  sentence  must
ensure that the total punishment inflicted upon the offender adds
up to at least four years imprisonment. Pursuant to s. 719(1) the
sentence  runs  from the date it is imposed. In  calculating  the
total punishment and taking into account pre-sentence custody  as
authorized by s. 719(3), the judge may deduct from the four years
any  period of the pre-sentence custody that he or she  considers
appropriate.  This  final number is the sentence  that  commences
when it is imposed. In my view, any other interpretation of these
provisions  would  lead to absurd results  and  I  turn  to  that
problem now.
 
          iii.  Interpretation  of  statutes  to  avoid
          absurd results
 
 
[53]  In  R.  v.  McIntosh, supra at p. 704,  Lamer  C.J.C.  also
referred  to the rule of statutory interpretation where absurdity
is  alleged.  He quoted with approval from the decision  of  Lord
Macmillan  in  Altrincham  Electric Supply  Ltd.  v.  Sale  Urban
District Council (1936), 154 L.T. 379 at 388 (H.L.):
 
          ...  if  the  language  of  an  enactment  is
          ambiguous  and  susceptible of two  meanings,
          one  of  which is consonant with justice  and
          good  sense  while the other  would  lead  to
          extravagant  results, a  court  of  law  will
          incline to adopt the former and to reject the
          latter,   even   although  the   latter   may
          correspond  more  closely  with  the  literal
          reading  of  the  words  employed.  [Emphasis
          added.]
 
 
[54] Lamer C.J.C. also referred with approval to a judgment of La
Forest  J.A.  in New Brunswick v. Estabrooks Pontiac  Buick  Ltd.
(1982), 44 N.B.R. (2d) 201 at 210 (C.A.). La Forest J.A. made the
point  in  that case that an apparent absurdity flowing from  the
plain language of a provision cannot create an ambiguity. On  the
other hand, the fact that the words as interpreted would give  an
unreasonable  result is a good reason to closely  scrutinize  the
statute  to  "make  abundantly certain" that the  words  are  not
susceptible  of some other meaning. For, as he said,  "it  should
not   be   readily  assumed  that  the  Legislature  intends   an
unreasonable result or to perpetrate an injustice or  absurdity."
In  my  view, an interpretation of the provisions in the  present
case  that would not permit a court to take pre-sentence  custody
into account to reduce the sentence below four years would result
in an absurd and unjust result.
[55]  In  R.  v. Lapierre, supra at p. 346, Proulx J.A.  gave  an
example  something along the following lines. Assume the  accused
is  charged with an offence contrary to s. 344(a) and  is  either
refused  bail  or is unable to meet the bail set  by  the  court.
Assume  further that the accused intends to plead not guilty  and
wishes  to  exercise his right to a jury trial. Counsel  for  the
Intervener in this appeal conceded that it could take a year  for
the  accused to be tried by a jury in this province, even if  the
accused  is  in  custody.  Assume the accused  is  convicted  and
successfully appeals against that conviction and a new  trial  is
ordered.  With the delay in obtaining transcripts a further  year
elapses  before  the accused's appeal is heard and  a  new  trial
ordered.  The  new  trial is heard within  four  months  and  the
accused  is  again convicted. In all, the accused has spent  over
two years in custody.
[56] If s. 719 precludes the court from taking these periods into
account,  the  accused must now be sentenced to  a  further  four
years  in the penitentiary. It seems to me that quite apart  from
any  Charter  considerations, Parliament could not have  intended
such  an  unfair  and  unjust result.  The  answer  must  lie  in
permitting  the  sentencing judge to  make  use  of  the  express
authority  in  s.  719(3).  It may be that  the  "sentence"  only
commences  when  it  is  imposed but as I have  said  above,  the
"punishment"  began, in effect and in substance, from  the  point
when  the  offender was taken into custody as a  result  of  that
offence.  Such  an interpretation is, to use the  words  of  Lord
Macmillan, one that is consonant with justice and good sense. Put
another  way,  even  assuming  some ambiguity  in  the  interplay
between  s.  344(a) and s. 719, I am not prepared to assume  that
Parliament  intended  to  perpetrate an injustice  or  absurdity,
where  an  interpretation that would avoid  that  result  and  is
consistent  with  Parliament's intent is clearly available.  This
interpretation also has the advantage of consistency with some of
the  other provisions of the Code concerning sentencing.  I  will
briefly turn to some of those other provisions.       
 
          iv.   Reference  to other provisions  in  the
          same statute
 
 
[57]  As  Lamer  C.J.C.  said  in  R.  v.  McIntosh  at  p.   699
"interpreting  statutory provisions in context  is  a  reasonable
approach". It is therefore useful to look at other provisions  of
the  Code  that  may  shed light on the relationship  between  s.
344(a)  and s. 719. Section 718.2(b) provides that the sentencing
court  "shall"  take  into consideration the  principle  that  "a
sentence  should  be  similar  to sentences  imposed  on  similar
offenders    for   similar   offences   committed   in    similar
circumstances."   If a sentencing court is unable  to  take  into
account  pre-sentence  custody, there can  be  a  huge  disparity
between  two  accused  who  have committed  similar  offences  in
similar circumstances but where only one was able to obtain  bail
pending sentencing.
[58] The principle of fairness embodied in s. 718.2(b) is equally
offended where persons who commit different offences in different
circumstances  receive  the  identical  sentence.  For   example,
consider  two accused who commit the offence under s.  344(a)  on
the same day, are arrested on the same day and sentenced one year
later. In the one case, the accused, like this appellant,  has  a
minimal record and never brandishes the unloaded firearm.  If  he
can  receive  no  credit  for  the  pre-sentence  custody  he  is
sentenced  to  four years imprisonment. In the  other  case,  the
accused  has  a  record  for violence and actually  brandishes  a
loaded  firearm  but no one was injured and no shots  fired.  The
trial  judge considers that this much more serious offence should
attract  a  sentence of six years imprisonment. The  trial  judge
gives  this  accused credit of two days for  every  day  in  pre-
sentence  custody and imposes a sentence of four years. It  seems
to  me  that  if  the Code provisions are fairly  capable  of  an
interpretation  that  avoids  such an  inequitable  result,  that
interpretation is one that should be embraced.
[59] I should at this point deal with one other provision of  the
Criminal Code. LeBel J.A. in R. v. Alain and Proulx J.A. in R. v.
Lapierre  both made reference to s. 746 of Criminal  Code.   That
section  deals  with the effect of pre-trial  custody  on  parole
eligibility  for offenders convicted of murder and  sentenced  to
life  imprisonment. Proulx J.A. noted the effect  of  s.  746  in
these terms at p. 345:
 
          The rule that a sentence commences to run  as
          of  the  time  when  it is imposed  makes  an
          exception in the case of life sentences:   s.
          746  provides that in these cases eligibility
          for  parole is calculated as of the day  that
          the  accused  was  arrested  and  taken  into
          custody. That means, for example, that in the
          case  of a person sentenced for second degree
          murder  where  the period of eligibility  for
          parole  is  set  at  10 years,  this  minimum
          sentence will commence to run as of the  date
          the   person  was  arrested  and  taken  into
          custody.
 
 
[60]  Thus, it is argued that Parliament, having adverted to  the
problem  of  pre-trial custody in the context of  sentencing  for
murder, must have intended that this was the only circumstance in
which  pre-sentence custody can reduce a minimum sentence.  I  do
not  agree. Pre-trial custody cannot affect the minimum mandatory
sentence  for murder. That sentence is life imprisonment  and  by
definition  is  of an indeterminate length. The  sentence  cannot
change  regardless of how long the accused has been in  pre-trial
custody.  Section 746 was inserted into the Code for the purposes
of calculation of parole, not to alter the length of the sentence
and is addressed to those persons charged with the responsibility
of  calculating parole eligibility. Section 746 provides that "in
calculating the period of imprisonment served for the purposes of
section  745Y" there shall be included any time spent in  custody
prior to sentence. Section 745 provides, for example, that  in  a
case  of  first degree murder the person is to be "sentenced"  to
imprisonment  for life without eligibility for parole  until  the
person  "has served" twenty-five years of the sentence. Far  from
supporting the view that Parliament only intended to consider pre-
sentence  custody in murder cases, it seems to me that Parliament
was alive to the problem that s. 719(3) only gives the sentencing
judge  a  power to relieve against unfairness for certain minimum
sentences.  That  subsection would not be  sufficient  in  murder
cases  because the minimum sentence is life imprisonment and  the
real  issue  is  parole eligibility a matter dealt  with  by  the
Parole   Board.  It  follows  that  I  cannot  agree   with   the
interpretation  placed  upon s. 719(3) by  the  Quebec  Court  of
Appeal in R. v. Alain and R. v. Lapierre.
          
          v.   Reference to Charter values
 
 
[61]  One  final rule of interpretation may be of  assistance  in
interpreting  ss. 344(a) and 719. Where a statute is  capable  of
more  than  one  interpretation,  the  courts  should  avoid   an
interpretation  that would conflict with the  Charter:  Hills  v.
Canada  (Attorney  General), [1988]  1  S.C.R.  513  at  558  per
L'Heureux-Dubé  J.  In Slaight Communications Inc.  v.  Davidson,
[1989]  1  S.C.R. 1038 at 1078, Lamer J. expressed that principle
as follows:
 
          Although this Court must not add anything  to
          legislation  or delete anything  from  it  in
          order to make it consistent with the Charter,
          there  is no doubt in my mind that it  should
          also  not interpret legislation that is  open
          to more than one interpretation so as to make
          it inconsistent with the Charter and hence of
          no force or effect.
 
 
[62] In his very helpful submissions, Mr. Finley on behalf of the
respondent Crown referred to the problem of pre-sentence  custody
as  the  "Achilles  heel"  of  his  argument  in  favour  of  the
constitutionality  of s. 344(a). Without conceding  that  lengthy
pre-sentence  custody  combined with a four-year  sentence  could
lead  to  the imposition of cruel and unusual punishment contrary
to  s. 12 of the Charter, Mr. Finley acknowledged that some cases
would  be  troubling. Proulx J.A. in R. v. Lapierre  and  Bateman
J.A.  in  R.  v. Morrisey appear to have been of the  same  view.
However,  they  would  avoid  the  problem  by  resorting  to   a
constitutional  exemption  if  the  combination  of  pre-sentence
custody  and  the four-year sentence in a particular  case  would
otherwise lead to the imposition of cruel and unusual punishment.
Mr.  Finley argued against a constitutional exemption and for the
reasons  set out below I agree with him. However, the problem  of
pre-sentence   custody  reasserts  itself  and  jeopardizes   the
constitutionality of s. 344(a). If on the other hand,  the  court
as  a  matter  of  interpretation is able  to  take  pre-sentence
custody into account in fixing the length of the sentence then in
my view the s. 12 problem dissolves.
[63]  There is another way in which Charter values are implicated
in  the  case  of  pre-sentence custody. It is self-evident  that
holding  a person in custody before he or she has been  found  to
have committed a criminal offence affects the liberty interest of
that  person.  The  Supreme Court of Canada has  held  in  R.  v.
Pearson,  supra  and  R. v. Morales, [1992]  3  S.C.R.  711  that
detention  prior to trial in accordance with the bail  provisions
of  the Criminal Code is consistent with fundamental justice  and
not  inconsistent with the presumption of innocence as guaranteed
by  ss.  7  and  11(d) of the Charter.5  Nevertheless,  pre-trial
custody  falls more heavily upon those persons who  assert  their
innocence and insist on exercising their constitutional rights to
require  the  Crown to prove the case beyond a  reasonable  doubt
before  a jury of their peers. Those accused will spend a  longer
time in pre-trial custody than accused who choose to plead guilty
at  the  first available opportunity. They also may find it  more
difficult to defend themselves than others who have been able  to
obtain bail.
[64] Allowing time spent in pre-sentence custody to count against
the  four-year minimum cannot cure all the ills of custody  prior
to  trial. In particular, it is no solution to the accused who is
ultimately acquitted. On the other hand, it should be possible to
adopt an interpretation that does not impose upon accused persons
the added burden that the additional time in custody necessitated
by  the  exercise of their constitutional rights  will  not  even
count against the sentence if they are eventually convicted. Such
an  interpretation may lead to hasty and improvident guilty pleas
and risks undermining the legitimate exercise of rights protected
by  the  Charter.  Allowing the judge to take into  account  pre-
sentence  custody  at  least  militates  against  some  of  these
unintended consequences of the minimum sentence.
 
          vi. Calculating the amount of credit for pre-
          sentence custody
 
 
[65]  There is one final issue of interpretation: the  amount  of
credit permitted by s. 719(3). Under s. 719(3), it is left to the
sentencing court to "take into account" any pre-sentence  custody
"in  determining the sentence". The subsection does  not  provide
any  fixed  rule  as  to  how much credit should  be  taken  into
account.  For  the reasons expressed by Professor Friedland,  Mr.
Trotter  and Laskin J.A. in R. v. Rezaie, trial judges  generally
give  double credit for pre-sentence custody. I see no reason  to
depart from this practice, where appropriate, in cases of minimum
punishment, although the sentence imposed would be less than four
years.  I  appreciate that this injects a certain imprecision  in
the  sentencing.  On  the other hand, such a  lack  of  precision
already exists where the proposed sentence far exceeds four years
and  the  trial  judge  intends to give credit  for  pre-sentence
custody. The sentencing judge will, however, have to be satisfied
that when the proposed sentence and the pre-sentence custody  are
combined,  the  effective "punishment" is at  least  four  years.
Moreover,  the sentencing judge is not bound to give  credit  for
the  full  period of pre-sentence custody. And,  of  course,  the
judge  is  entitled  to impose more than the  minimum  four  year
sentence. Finally, it must be clear that the pre-sentence custody
was for the s. 344(a) offence. Otherwise it cannot be said to  be
time spent in custody "as a result of the offence" as required by
s.  719(3),  nor could it be considered part of the  "punishment"
for that offence.
[66] Having disposed of the statutory interpretation issues it is
now possible to deal with the constitutional issues raised by the
appellant. I can deal with them relatively briefly.
 
          II.   Cruel  and  Unusual Punishment  and  s. 344(a)
 
 
[67]  When s. 344(a) is properly interpreted, I am satisfied that
it  does  not constitute cruel and unusual punishment within  the
meaning of s. 12 of the Charter. As pointed out above, this issue
has been considered by the Quebec and British Columbia Courts  of
Appeal and I agree with much of their analysis.
[68]  The  test for whether a punishment is cruel and unusual  is
whether  it is grossly disproportionate to what would  have  been
appropriate.  The punishment must be so excessive as  to  outrage
standards of decency. A sentence that is merely excessive or even
unfit,  is not necessarily grossly disproportionate. Further,  it
is  unwise  to simply measure gross disproportionality against  a
current  sentencing regime. Subject to constitutional limits,  it
is  open to Parliament to create new offences or aggravated forms
of   existing  offences  with  enhanced  punishment.  Unless  the
punishment runs afoul of constitutional limits, it is not for the
courts  to debate the wisdom, or otherwise, of the new sentencing
regime.  This applies as much to minimum punishments as to  other
forms of legislated policy. The Supreme Court of Canada has  held
in  R.  v.  Smith that minimum punishments per se do not  violate
either  s. 9 or s. 12 of the Charter. The Court has also held  in
Steele v. Mountain Institution, [1990] 2 S.C.R. 1385 at 1417 that
it  will  only be on rare and unique occasions that a court  will
find a sentence so grossly disproportionate as to offend s. 12.
[69] The first stage of the s. 12 analysis requires the court  to
consider  whether the effect of the punishment would  be  grossly
disproportionate  to  what would have been appropriate  for  this
offender. In R. v. Smith, Lamer J. at p. 1073 summarized some  of
the elements of the test as follows:
 
          In  assessing whether a sentence  is  grossly
          disproportionate,  the   court   must   first
          consider  the  gravity of  the  offence,  the
          personal characteristics of the offender  and
          the  particular circumstances of the case  in
          order  to  determine what range of  sentences
          would   have  been  appropriate  to   punish,
          rehabilitate   or   deter   this   particular
          offender  or to protect the public from  this
          particular offender.
 
 
[70]  In R. v. Goltz, supra at p. 500, Gonthier J. added to  this
list  other  factors that may inform an assessment.  These  other
factors  include, "whether the punishment is necessary to achieve
a  valid  penal  purpose,  whether it is  founded  on  recognized
sentencing principles, whether there exist valid alternatives  to
the  punishment imposed, and to some extent whether a  comparison
with   punishments  imposed  for  other  crimes   in   the   same
jurisdiction reveals great disproportion."
[71]  Applying  those  principles to  this  case,  I  have  these
comments.  I have real concerns about the punishment  imposed  on
this  appellant even allowing credit for pre-sentence custody.  A
sentence  of  even three years' imprisonment is  beyond  what  is
necessary to punish, rehabilitate or deter this appellant  or  to
protect  the  public  from  this  particular  appellant.   I   am
particularly  concerned that such a sentence does not  take  into
account the impact of the appellant's mental illness.
[72] I also have reservations about putting this relatively young
man into a penitentiary setting. If it were open to this court to
review  the  propriety of this sentence on  the  usual  scale  of
appellate review as explained in R. v. M. (C.A.), [1996] 1 S.C.R.
500,  I  would  find  a  three  to  four  year  sentence  to   be
demonstrably  unfit.  However, that is  not  the  same  as  gross
disproportionality and I am not convinced that having  regard  to
the  objective  gravity of any offence involving  the  use  of  a
firearm,  even an unloaded one, that a sentence approaching  four
years  shocks  the conscience. As La Forest J.  wrote  in  R.  v.
Lyons, [1987] 2 S.C.R. 309 at 344-45, the standard under s. 12 is
not  so  exacting as to require the punishment to  be  "perfectly
suited to accommodate the moral nuances of every crime and  every
offender."
[73] The second stage in the s. 12 inquiry requires the court  to
go   beyond  the  specific  facts  of  the  case  to  assess  the
proportionality  of  the  sentence  prescribed  by  the  statute.
Gonthier J. explained this second stage in R. v. Goltz at pp. 505-
6:
 
          If  the  particular facts of the case do  not
          warrant      a     finding      of      gross
          disproportionality, there may remain  another
          aspect  to  be  examined,  namely  a  Charter
          challenge  or constitutional question  as  to
          the  validity  of  a statutory  provision  on
          grounds   of   gross  disproportionality   as
          evidenced    in    reasonable    hypothetical
          circumstances,  as opposed to far-fetched  or
          marginally imaginable cases
 
 
[74]  As I understand it, at each stage of the inquiry the  court
is  required to consider the effect of the sentence. Gonthier  J.
held as follows in R. v. Goltz at p. 513:
 
          Smith  states that the effect of the sentence
          must    also    be   measured    for    gross
          disproportionality.   The   effect   of   the
          sentence  is  ''often  a  composite  of  many
          factors and is not limited to the quantum  or
          duration  of  the sentence but  includes  its
          nature and the conditions under which  it  is
          applied'' (p. 1073). Smith indicated, by  way
          of  illustration, that 20 years for  a  first
          offence  against  property would  be  grossly
          disproportionate and would  infringe  s.  12.
          [Emphasis added.]
 
 
[75]  If  I am wrong in my interpretation of s. 719(3)  and  pre-
sentence  custody cannot be taken into account then, in my  view,
such  custody is a factor to be considered in the s. 12 analysis.
The  respondent  and  the  Intervener  argued  that  pre-sentence
custody  should  not  be  considered at  this  second  stage.  No
compelling  reason founded on principle was presented to  explain
this limitation, except that it would make the stage two analysis
imprecise.
[76]  In  R. v. LaPierre, supra, the Quebec Court of Appeal  also
took  the  view  that  the  possibility of  lengthy  pre-sentence
custody  was  not  a factor to be included as  an  element  of  a
reasonable  hypothetical.  Proulx J.A.  reasoned  as  follows  in
LaPierre at p. 346-7:
 
          In  the  opinion  of certain  persons,  as  I
          mentioned  earlier, this  question  [of  pre-
          sentence custody] is part and parcel  of  the
          question  of  the  constitutionality  of  the
          minimum  sentence.  For my  part,  I  believe
          rather,  after having earlier concluded  that
          s.   344(a)  is  constitutional,   that   the
          injustice which may flow from overly long pre-
          trial   custody   can  be   remedied   by   a
          constitutional exemption if the effect of the
          section  which  imposes the minimum  sentence
          and  for  which  no  provision  is  made  for
          consideration of the time spent in  pre-trial
          custody, renders the minimum sentence grossly
          disproportionate, and therefore  violates  s.
          12 of the Charter.
 
 
[77]  Proulx J.A. did not explain why pre-sentence custody should
not be considered as part of the constitutionality matrix. If the
court  is  required  to  take into account  the  effects  of  the
sentence  including the nature and conditions under which  it  is
applied, I fail to see why the impact of the pre-sentence custody
should  be  ignored.  To  revert  to  my  earlier  example,   the
imposition  of a further four-year sentence of imprisonment  upon
an  offender  who  has already spent over two  years  in  custody
because he chose to exercise his constitutional right to  a  jury
trial and his statutory right of appeal must be an aspect of  the
nature and conditions under which the sentence is applied. In  my
view, this is a reasonable hypothetical.
[78] In R. v. Morrisey, Bateman J.A. adopted the same approach as
did  Proulx J.A. when she considered the constitutionality of  s.
220(a),  which  imposes a four-year minimum for  the  offence  of
criminal negligence causing death with a firearm. She reasoned as
follows at p. 22:
 
          In  my view, the fact that, in certain cases,
          the  lack  of  judicial  discretion  to  give
          appropriate  credit for remand time  has  the
          potential    to   result   in    a    grossly
          disproportionate sentence does not invalidate
          the  legislation.  Where  a  valid  provision
          leads  to  a result that infringes a  Charter
          right,  a  remedy flows under s.  24  of  the
          Charter, the appropriate remedy, here,  being
          the  constitutional exemption.  As  Lamer  J.
          commented   in  Smith,  supra,  it   is   not
          legislation   that  has  the   potential   of
          operating  so as to impose cruel and  unusual
          punishment  that violates s. 12,  it  is  the
          certainty that it will so operate.
 
 
[79]  The  concern  I have with the analysis  undertaken  by  the
courts in Quebec and Nova Scotia is that it assumes one, or more,
factors can be ignored, although those factors affect the  nature
of the sentence to be imposed under the impugned provision. I can
find  no  authority for such an approach. With great  respect  to
Bateman  J.A., I do not think that the distinction Lamer J.  drew
in  R. v. Smith between the "potential" and the "certainty" of  a
violation  of  s. 12, justifies ignoring real life  factors  that
could  render  a sentence grossly disproportionate in  reasonable
hypothetical circumstances. Lamer J. wrote as follows at p.  1078
concerning  the  "certainty"  that the  seven  year  minimum  for
importing narcotics would violate s. 12:
 
          As  indicated above, the offence of importing
          enacted  by  s. 5(1) of the Narcotic  Control
          Act  covers  numerous substances  of  varying
          degrees    of   dangerousness   and   totally
          disregards the quantity of the drug imported.
          The  purpose of a given importation, such  as
          whether it is for personal consumption or for
          trafficking,  and  the  existence   or   non-
          existence   of   previous   convictions   for
          offences  of a similar nature or gravity  are
          disregarded as irrelevant. Thus, the  law  is
          such  that  it  is inevitable that,  in  some
          cases,  a verdict of guilt will lead  to  the
          imposition  of  a term of imprisonment  which
          will be grossly disproportionate.
          This  is  what offends s. 12, the  certainty,
          not  just  the potential. Absent the minimum,
          the  section  still  has  the  potential   of
          operating  so as to impose cruel and  unusual
          punishment. But that would only occur if  and
          when  a  judge chose to impose, let  us  say,
          seven  years or more on the "small offender".
          Remedy  will then flow from s. 24. It is  the
          judge's  sentence, but not the section,  that
          is  in violation of the Charter. However, the
          effect  of  the  minimum  is  to  insert  the
          certainty   that,  in  some  cases,   as   of
          conviction  the violation will occur.  It  is
          this  aspect  of  certainty  that  makes  the
          section itself a prima facie violation of  s.
          12, and the minimum must, subject to s. 1, be
          declared  of  no  force or effect.  [Emphasis
          added.]
 
 
[80]  In Smith, Lamer J. emphasized that the focus of the  s.  12
analysis  will  almost  always  be  upon  the  effects   of   the
legislation. At p. 1073, he indicated how that effect was  to  be
measured:
 
          One  must  also  measure the  effect  of  the
          sentence  actually imposed. If it is  grossly
          disproportionate  to  what  would  have  been
          appropriate,  then it infringes  s.  12.  The
          effect  of  the sentence is often a composite
          of  many  factors and is not limited  to  the
          quantum  or  duration  of  the  sentence  but
          includes its nature and the conditions  under
          which  it is applied. Sometimes by its length
          alone or by its very nature will the sentence
          be  grossly  disproportionate to the  purpose
          sought.  Sometimes it will be the  result  of
          the   combination  of  factors  which,   when
          considered in isolation, would not in and  of
          themselves       amount       to        gross
          disproportionality. For example, 20 years for
          a  first  offence against property  would  be
          grossly disproportionate, but so would  three
          months   of   imprisonment  if   the   prison
          authorities  decide it should  be  served  in
          solitary confinement. [Emphasis added.]
 
 
[81]  I  see no indication that the impact of the effects of  the
legislation  is  limited to the first stage of the  analysis  and
does  not  apply  to the reasonable hypothetical stage.  At  both
stages,  the  court  must  consider,  inter  alia,  whether   the
conditions  under which the sentence is applied  will  result  in
gross disproportionality. In my view, one of those conditions  is
that the offender may have already spent a substantial period  of
time in custody by reason of the s. 344(a) offence.
[82]  Neither pre-trial custody nor a minimum four-year  sentence
alone   would,   in   and   of  themselves,   amount   to   gross
disproportionality.   However,   I   can   envisage    reasonable
hypothetical  circumstances  where the  combination  of  the  two
factors  could  result  in  gross  disproportionality,   if   the
sentencing  judge  is not permitted to make  allowance  for  pre-
sentence custody. Those are cases of a lesser offender,  such  as
this appellant, or a person who is merely a party to the offence,
where  the  four-year minimum is excessive, but standing  on  its
own,  not  grossly  disproportionate. If the Code  precludes  the
judge  from  giving  credit for pre-sentence custody,  a  lengthy
period  of pre-sentence custody could push the sentence into  the
range of the grossly disproportionate. If the sentencing judge is
not allowed to take into account pre-sentence custody, the effect
of  the  minimum sentence is that, in some cases, a violation  of
s.  12  will certainly occur. It is the statute, not the  judge's
sentence,  that  is in violation of the Charter  if  the  statute
precludes the judge from giving credit for pre-sentence custody.
[83] However, as I have said, the sentencing court does have  the
power  to  take into account pre-sentence custody, even  if  that
would  result  in  reduction of the sentence imposed  below  four
years. In my view, any reasonable hypothetical that would outrage
standards  of decency necessarily involves a substantial  element
of  pre-sentence custody. Since properly interpreted,  s.  344(a)
allows  the  court to take pre-sentence custody into account  and
reduce  the sentence imposed, I would hold that the section  does
not  violate s. 12 of the Charter. Accordingly, it is unnecessary
to  consider  whether the section can be saved by  s.  1  of  the
Charter.
          
          III. Constitutional Exemption
 
 
[84]  In view of my conclusions concerning the interpretation  of
ss.  344(a) and 719 it is not strictly necessary for me  to  deal
with  the  constitutional exemption issue.  However,  I  think  I
should briefly deal with that question if only because the  other
appellate courts that have considered these provisions have  seen
constitutional  exemption as a means to avoid holding  s.  344(a)
unconstitutional.  For  example, in R. v. Lapierre,  Proulx  J.A.
held  that a constitutional exemption would be available  if,  by
reason  of  lengthy pre-sentence custody, the offender  would  be
subjected  to  cruel  and  unusual  punishment.  As  referred  to
earlier,  Proulx J.A. reasoned in these words in LaPierre  at  p.
346-7:
 
          In  the  opinion  of certain  persons,  as  I
          mentioned  earlier, this  question  [of  pre-
          sentence custody] is part and parcel  of  the
          question  of  the  constitutionality  of  the
          minimum  sentence.  For my  part,  I  believe
          rather,  after having earlier concluded  that
          s.   344(a)  is  constitutional,   that   the
          injustice which may flow from overly long pre-
          trial   custody   can  be   remedied   by   a
          constitutional exemption if the effect of the
          section  which  imposes the minimum  sentence
          and  for  which  no  provision  is  made  for
          consideration of the time spent in  pre-trial
          custody, renders the minimum sentence grossly
          disproportionate, and therefore  violates  s.
          12 of the Charter.
 
 
[85] The availability of constitutional exemptions generally  has
not  finally been determined in the Supreme Court of Canada. Most
recently,  Lamer  C.J.C.  dissenting  in  Rodriguez  v.   British
Columbia  (Attorney General), [1993] 3 S.C.R.  519  reviewed  the
state  of  the  law and held that a constitutional exemption  may
only  be granted during the period of a suspended declaration  of
invalidity.  The  majority  of  the  court  upheld  the  impugned
provision  against a challenge under ss. 7,  12  and  15  of  the
Charter  and  did  not deal with the question  of  constitutional
exemption.
[86]  In my view, whether a constitutional exemption is available
as  a  remedy in other circumstances, it is not available in this
province  where  a  minimum  definite  sentencing  provision   is
attacked   under  s.  12  of  the  Charter.   The   question   of
constitutional  exemption and s. 12 of the Charter  was  squarely
addressed by Finlayson J.A. in R. v. Kelly (1990), 59 C.C.C. (3d)
497  (Ont. C.A.). In that case the accused argued that the  order
prohibiting  an  offender from possessing  firearms  mandated  by
former  s.  100  of  the  Criminal Code where  the  offender  was
convicted of certain offences involving violence violated  s.  12
of  the  Charter. Finlayson J.A. pointed out that in R. v. Smith,
Lamer  J.  held that the mandatory nature of a minimum punishment
does  not per se violate s. 12. Rather, the court is required  to
consider  the individual circumstances of the particular offender
and other hypothetical circumstances to determine if the impugned
provision results in cruel and unusual punishment. Finlayson J.A.
wrote as follows at p. 514:
 
          I  appreciate that the argument of the Kellys
          was  that the Charter protected right was the
          right   to  be  free  of  cruel  and  unusual
          punishment  in  their particular  cases.  But
          this  reasoning is circular if Smith  is  the
          standard. If, as in Smith, s. 100(1) could be
          said   to   result  in  cruel   and   unusual
          punishment to a hypothetical person, such  as
          one  of the Kellys, then it casts too wide  a
          net and the entire section should be declared
          invalid.  There  can  be  no  place   for   a
          constitutional exemption.
          Having concluded that s. 100(1) does not cast
          too wide a net, I find that the complaint  of
          the Kellys is effectively reduced to the lack
          of   exceptions  to  the  prohibition  in  s.
          100(1).   In  my  opinion,  when  Smith   has
          accepted  that  the  mandatory  nature  of  a
          sentence   is   irrelevant   to   a   s.   12
          examination,  it hardly makes sense  to  hold
          that  the constitutionality of s. 100(1)  can
          be  upheld  by  ruling that it  need  not  be
          mandatory in the individual case.
 
 
[87]  There is nothing in the subsequent decision of the  Supreme
Court  of  Canada  in  R. v. Goltz to undermine  the  holding  by
Finlayson J.A. in R. v. Kelly.
 
          THE PRE-SENTENCE CUSTODY IN THIS CASE
 
 
[88]  I  can  see  numerous compelling reasons  for  taking  into
account  the  pre-sentence custody to which  this  appellant  was
subjected.  Having regard to the circumstances of  the  appellant
and of the offence, this case is properly placed at the lower end
of  the  range of seriousness. Further, as I understand  it,  the
appellant always intended to plead guilty and the delay  resulted
primarily from the time necessary to properly prepare and present
the  constitutional  argument.  Finally,  having  regard  to  the
circumstances  of that pre-sentence custody, and that  it  cannot
count  for  purposes of parole, I see no reason not to adopt  the
generally accepted practice of giving credit of two days for each
day of pre-sentence custody.
         
          DISPOSITION
 
[89]  Accordingly, I would grant leave to appeal sentence,  allow
the appeal and reduce the sentence to three years imprisonment.
