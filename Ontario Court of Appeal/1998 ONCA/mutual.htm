                     ENDORSEMENT

[1]    The plaintiffs appeal from the order of Hoilett J. made
April 16, 1998, staying these actions at the instance of the
defendants, upon the basis that British Columbia is a more
convenient forum.


[2]    There was argument both here and below as to the burden of
proof on a motion of this nature.  As the defendant Davis lives
and was served in British Columbia, if he were the only
defendant, the onus would be on the plaintiffs to establish that
Ontario is the forum conveniens.  The other defendants were
served in Ontario and have offices there.  If they were the only
defendants, the onus would be upon them.


[3]    Having regard to the test to be applied, it seems to us
that the question of the burden is largely irrelevant.  That test
was laid down by Sopinka J. on behalf of the Supreme Court of
Canada in Amchem Products Inc. v. British Columbia (WCB) [1993],
1 S.C.R. 897 at p. 921 as follows:


            ...the existence of a more appropriate
            forum must be clearly established to
            displace the forum selected by the
            plaintiff.




That language clearly places the onus on the moving party, in the
instant case, both Davis and the accounting firms.


[4]    It is not clear, however, that the motions judge saw the
test in those terms.  At p. 28 of his reasons he said:


            This motion exemplifies, in my view, the
            fact that what the court is often called
            upon to do in a motion of this kind is
            to strike that delicate balance in
            determining which of the two competing
            fora is more appropriate in
            circumstances where, clearly, the issues
            raised have some connection to each
            forum.






[5]    For a motion of this nature to succeed, a delicate balance
is not sufficient.  There must be a clear preponderance in favour
of the substitute jurisdiction proposed.


[6]    The motions judge made a thorough analysis of the nature
of the actions, the issues raised and the means whereby they
would be prosecuted and defended.  The result of that analysis,
in his view, was that British Columbia is clearly the more
convenient forum.


[7]    In the course of that analysis, he stated that:


            The laws in British Columbia governing
            joint and several liability are more
            favourable to the defendants; hence
            there is a juridical advantage to them
            in the actions proceeding in British
            Columbia.






[8]    It is argued that in recognizing a juridical advantage in
favour of the defendants he erred, that a juridical advantage is
to be considered only when it favours the plaintiff.  On the
other hand, in the course of dealing with the subject of
juridical advantage, Sopinka J. in Amchem said at p. 920:


            ...it seems to me that any juridical
            advantages to the plaintiff or defendant
            should have been considered one of the
            factors to be taken into account.






[9]    It is our view that the strength of the case for
proceeding in British Columbia as opposed to Ontario is such that
it is not necessary to resolve this issue.


[10]   We agree with the conclusion reached by the motions judge
and would therefore dismiss the appeal with costs.
Sharpe J. ad hoc  (Dissenting)




[11]   I respectfully disagree with the conclusion reached by my
colleagues and will briefly state the reasons that would lead me
to allow the appeal.
       


[12]   No issue is taken with the proposition that the correct
test is that laid down by the Supreme Court of Canada in Amchem
Products Inc. v .British Columbia (WCB), [1993] 1S.C.R. 897 at
921 per Sopinka J.: A...the existence of a more appropriate forum
must be clearly established to displace the forum selected by the
plaintiff@.  The motions judge did not assess the case in those
terms, but approached the question as a Adelicate balance in
determining which of the two competing fora is more appropriate
in circumstances where, clearly, the issues raised have some
connection to each forum@.  At no point in his reasons did the
motions judge refer to the correct test.  While a failure to
quote the relevant passage might not be fatal, the difficultly I
have is that it seems to me that the above quoted passage from
his reasons accurately reflects the approach he took, namely to
determine, on balance, which is the more convenient forum. This
is demonstrated not only by the analysis he undertook but also by
the way he states his conclusion: A...I find more compelling the
argument for concluding that British Columbia is the more
convenient forum...@.  I do not agree that it can be said that he
made a finding that British Columbia Ais clearly the more
convenient forum@.  In the absence of such a finding, it is open
to this court to consider the question, and it is my opinion that
the facts of this case fall well short of what should be required
to justify the stay sought by the defendants.




[13]   This is not an application for a change of venue where
convenience of the parties and witnesses govern.  The plaintiffs
have a legal right to sue in Ontario which can only be taken away
if another forum is shown to be clearly more appropriate.  The
main defendant was served in Ontario and is a national firm with
offices in Ontario.  The plaintiffs= head offices are in Ontario.
The dealings between the plaintiff and PNLC were conducted in
Ontario.  The financial  statements were received by the
plaintiffs in Ontario.  Damage was suffered in Ontario and it is
almost certainly the case that although the allegedly negligent
acts were done in British Columbia, the tort alleged by the
plaintiff was committed in Ontario and that Ontario law applies.




[14]   I find that these factors carry significantly more weight
than the fact that the audit work was done in British Columbia
and that it appears that most of the defendants= witnesses on
that aspect of the case will have to come to Ontario from British
Columbia.  To my mind, any argument in favour of British Columbia
is considerably weakened by the fact defendant is a national
accounting firm which prepared an audit for a company that
operated on a national scale and was financed by  Ontario-based
lenders.  The defendants were engaged in an audit of such obvious
extra-provincial import that I fail to see why the inconvenience
they assert should trump the plaintiffs= legal right to proceed
against them in Ontario.  It seems to me that the case for
Ontario jurisdiction is at least as strong, if not stronger, than
if this were an Ontario product liability case against a local
British Columbia manufacturer where, based on the principles
expressed in Moran v. Pyle National (Canada) Ltd., [1975] 1
S.C.R. 393;  Morguard Investments Ltd. v. De Savoie, [1990] 3
S.C.R. 1077, and Hunt v. T&N PLC, [1993] 4 S.C.R. 289 at 315-16,
it is almost certain that Ontario jurisdiction would be upheld in
the face of a request for a stay




[15]   I would add that I think that the motions judge also erred
on the Ajuridical advantage@ point.  While this is only one
factor to be considered, it seems to me that it should have
favoured the plaintiff.  As I read, Amchem, supra, where the
plaintiff sues in Ontario and the case has  a substantial
connection to Ontario, any loss of juridical advantage is to be
considered from the perspective of the plaintiff, not from that
of the defendant.  As I read the reasons of the motions judge, he
came at it from the opposite direction and found that the
juridical advantage factor was one favouring the defendant.  The
result is that a factor that should have weighed on the side of
the plaintiffs was incorrectly used to justify relief for the
defendants




[16]    With respect to the submission that there are conflicting
burdens of proof, one with respect to the defendants served in
Ontario and another with respect to the defendant Davis who was
served ex juris, I would adopt the resolution offered in Fidelity
Management & Research Company et al (1995), 25 O.R. (3d) 548 at
553.  In that case, Blair J. states that where one defendant is
served within the jurisdiction as of right and another is brought
in as a necessary or proper party under rule 17.02 (o), the
correct analysis is that there is a single onus on both
defendants who dispute jurisdiction to satisfy the court that
there is clearly another jurisdiction more appropriate. Here, the
defendant served ex juris is the employee of the main defendant,
a national auditing firm, and is represented before this court by
the same solicitors.  In my view, there is only one onus and it
rests with the defendants.
