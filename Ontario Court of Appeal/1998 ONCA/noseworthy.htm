GOUDGE J.A.:


[1]  This is an appeal by the defendants from the judgment of
Cosgrove J. rendered on August 16, 1996. He determined that the
plaintiff Noseworthy had been dismissed without cause from his
employment and that he should receive damages in lieu of
appropriate notice together with aggravated damages, punitive
damages, and damages for mental distress.


[2]  The defendants do not contest the finding that
Mr. Noseworthy was terminated without cause. However, they attack
all aspects of the award of damages.


[3]  The trial judgment was rendered prior to the decision of the
Supreme Court of Canada in Wallace v. United Grain Growers Ltd.,
[1997] 3 S.C.R. 701. For the reasons that follow I have concluded
that, in light of Wallace, the awards of aggravated damages,
punitive damages, and damages for mental distress cannot stand
and that the calculation of appropriate notice must be
reassessed.


[4]  Mr. Noseworthy was terminated on March 15, 1994. He was then
sixty years old. He had worked for four years as office manager
for the defendant Riverside Pontiac-Buick Ltd., an auto
dealership in Prescott, Ontario. Only one employee reported
directly to him. His annual salary was $38,480.


[5]  The defendant Birnie owned the dealership and the defendant
MacMillan was its sales manager.


[6]  Mr. Noseworthy's duties included responsibility for the
bookkeeping practices at the dealership. It was dealership policy
that only the plaintiff and the service manager Laurie Craig were
authorized to sign cheques up to $1,000. The trial judge found
that Mr. Noseworthy had permitted another employee to sign
Ms. Craig's name to a number of cheques because he reasonably
believed that the defendant MacMillan knew of this practice and
had given it his tacit approval.


[7]  The trial judge further found that on May 15, 1994, without
prior warning, the defendants confronted Mr. Noseworthy and
accused him of forging Laurie Craig's signature on the disputed
cheques. The defendant Birnie told him that they would have to
part company and that if he did not sign a letter of resignation
he would be charged with forgery. Mr. Noseworthy complied with
this demand although the defendants did not subsequently rely on
this letter as having any legal significance.


[8]  The uncontradicted psychiatric evidence was that the abrupt
termination of employment and the circumstances surrounding the
termination caused Mr. Noseworthy to suffer from traumatic stress
disorder with only a fair prognosis for recovery. Indeed, at the
time of trial Mr. Noseworthy still remained unemployed.


[9]  The trial judge found that the acceptance by Mr. Noseworthy
of the practice of substituting a signature by an unauthorized
employee was at most an error in judgment. It appears that
Mr. Birnie was unaware of this practice before the meeting of
March 15, 1994. The trial judge nevertheless characterized the
false allegations of forgery against the plaintiff and the threat
of criminal charges unless he resigned as "highhanded, repulsive,
and unacceptable by any standard of decency". He found that it
was intended to avoid giving the plaintiff adequate notice of
termination of employment. He concluded that the dismissal of
Mr. Noseworthy was without cause.


[10] His judgment as to damages was made up of the following:
          (a)  damages against the corporate defendant equivalent
          to ten months' salary representing the period of
          reasonable notice required in all the circumstances;
     
     (b)  aggravated damages against the corporate defendant
          equivalent to five months' salary. The trial judge
          expressed his finding as follows:
          
               "I have concluded that the damages for
          lack of adequate notice, because of the
          aggravating circumstances in this case,
          should increase the notice period from ten
          months to fifteen months. These factors
          include the age of the plaintiff and his
          limited prospects in a stagnant economy, the
          unfounded allegations of forgery and the
          attempt, in effect, to pressure a letter of
          resignation from the plaintiff."
          
          
     (c)  punitive damages against all defendants jointly and
          severally in the amount of $15,000;
     (d)  damages for mental distress against the defendants
          Birnie and MacMillan in an amount equivalent to one
          year's salary.
          


[11] As I have said, the defendants attack each of these
findings. I will deal with each of them in turn.


     Aggravated Damages


[12] In Vorvis v. Insurance Company of British Columbia (1989),
58 D.L.R. (4th) 193 (S.C.C.) McIntyre J., speaking for the
majority, made clear that aggravated damages are compensatory,
not punitive. He cited with approval the description of
aggravated damages given by Professor Waddams in The Law of
Damages 2nd ed. (1983) at p. 562 which includes this passage:
          
          The expression "aggravated damages", though
          it has sometimes been used interchangeably
          with punitive or exemplary damages, has more
          frequently in recent times been contrasted
          with exemplary damages. In this contrasting
          sense, aggravated damages describes an award
          that aims at compensation, but takes full
          account of the intangible injuries, such as
          distress and humiliation, that may have been
          caused by the defendant's insulting
          behaviour.
          


[13] In the context of wrongful dismissal, the issue of
aggravated damages turns not on the factors considered by the
trial judge as aggravating circumstances in the passage quoted
above but, rather, on whether the manner of dismissal caused the
plaintiff mental distress. Where that occurs damages for mental
distress are available provided that the manner of the dismissal
constitutes an independently actionable wrong on the part of the
employer. This was made clear by Iacobucci J., speaking for the
majority, in Wallace, supra at p. 734. He went on to say that
where the manner of dismissal caused mental distress falling
short of a separately actionable cause of conduct, while
aggravated damages could not be awarded there could be an
extension of the period of reasonable notice. I will return to
this concept in dealing with the period of reasonable notice that
is appropriate in this case.


[14] Here, the trial judge erred in basing his award of
aggravated damages on certain factors which he viewed to be
aggravating circumstances. Rather, he was required to assess
whether the manner of Mr. Noseworthy's dismissal caused him
mental distress and whether that constituted a separately
actionable wrong by the employer. Only if both criteria were met
could he make the award he did. There is nothing in the record
before us to suggest that the manner of dismissal was an
independent actionable wrong by the corporate defendant nor was
this argued by the plaintiff. That is enough to preclude the
award of aggravated damages, and this component of the judgment
below must be set aside.


     Punitive Damages


[15] Similarly, Wallace, supra at p. 736 makes clear that in a
wrongful dismissal action punitive damages can be awarded only
where the conduct sought to be punished by such an award
constitutes a separately actionable wrong.


[16] Here, as I have said, there was no suggestion that the
manner of dismissal of Mr. Noseworthy constituted more than a
breach of contract on the part of the employer. It could not be
said to be independently actionable conduct as against the
defendant Riverside Pontiac-Buick Ltd.


[17] Equally for the reasons set out below, the manner of
dismissal could not be said to be independently actionable
conduct by the defendants Birnie and MacMillan. It does not
constitute the tort of intentional infliction of mental
suffering.


[18] Hence, in my view there was no basis for an award of
punitive damages and that element of the judgment also must be
set aside.


     Damages for Mental Distress


[19] The trial judge assessed damages for mental distress against
the defendants Birnie and MacMillan but not against the corporate
defendant. The reasons for judgment do not reveal the basis upon
which this was done. On appeal, the only basis offered was the
tort of intentional infliction of mental suffering.


[20] Even if this cause of action is available in the employment
context, in this case the purpose of the defendants' actions in
effecting the dismissal was to avoid providing reasonable notice
to the plaintiff. This falls short of the requisite intention for
liability to be sustained on this basis. Therefore, the provision
in the judgment of damages for mental distress must also be set
aside.


     Reasonable Notice


[21] The trial judge concluded that the period of reasonable
notice required in this case was ten months. While his reasons do
not clearly set out the basis on which he reached this
conclusion, it may have been founded on the well-known principles
first laid out in Bardal v. Globe & Mail Ltd. (1960) 24 D.L.R.
(2nd) 140 (Ont. H.C.) at 145:
          
               There can be no catalogue laid down as
          to what is reasonable notice in particular
          classes of cases. The reasonableness of the
          notice must be decided with reference to each
          particular case, having regard to the
          character of the employment, the length of
          service of the servant, the age of the
          servant and the availability of similar
          employment, having regard to the experience,
          training and qualifications of the servant.

[22] Using these factors alone, I would have been inclined to
think that in the circumstances of this case ten months is
significantly too generous. However, that is not now the end of
the matter.


[23] In reaching his conclusion the trial judge appears to have
excluded consideration of the employer's bad faith in dismissing
the plaintiff and the impact of that on him. Rather, he used
these considerations (erroneously, as I have found) to underpin
his awards of aggravated damages and punitive damages.


[24] Since Wallace, supra the plaintiff is entitled to have these
factors considered as part of the assessment of reasonable
notice. Indeed the appellants' counsel conceded as much. Hence,
it is necessary that this issue be revisited in light of the
principles in Wallace.


[25] Wallace represents a significant new step in the evolution
of the way in which reasonable notice is determined. Speaking for
a majority of the Supreme Court of Canada, Iacobucci J. began his
discussion of reasonable notice by making clear that the
principles set out in Bardal, supra remain centrally relevant to
this task. He reviewed the subsequent common law to demonstrate
that Canadian courts have not treated this list as closed but
have built several additional factors onto this foundation.
Principal among them is a consideration of whether the dismissed
employee had been induced to leave previously secure employment.


[26] He then turned to an analysis of the relevance of bad faith
conduct by the employee in effecting the dismissal. He concluded
that, given the special characteristics of the employment
relationship and the type of contract governing it, the law must
recognize such conduct as a consideration in determining the
length of notice required. Particularly in light of the power
imbalance that typically exists in the employment relationship,
the fundamental importance of work in a person's life, and the
special vulnerability of an employee when termination occurs, the
employer must act fairly and in good faith in carrying out the
dismissal. He put his conclusion this way at p. 742:
          
               The point at which the employment
          relationship ruptures is the time when the
          employee is most vulnerable and hence, most
          in need of protection. In recognition of this
          need, the law ought to encourage conduct that
          minimizes the damage and dislocation (both
          economic and personal) that result from
          dismissal. … In my opinion, to ensure that
          employees receive adequate protection,
          employers ought to be held to an obligation
          of good faith and fair dealing in the manner
          of dismissal, the breach of which will be
          compensated for by adding to the length of
          the notice period.

[27] He made clear that this factor is relevant even where the
employee suffers mental distress as a result but cannot show that
the manner of dismissal affected his future job prospects
although if the latter occurs as well, the resulting extension of
the notice period would likely be considerably greater.


[28] In Wallace the employer's bad faith conduct had both these
consequences. Together with the other relevant factors this led
Iacobucci J. to uphold an award equivalent to twenty-four months'
notice which he acknowledged may be at the high end of the range.
This ultimate conclusion makes clear that the compensation for
bad faith employer conduct comes by way of a lengthened notice
period fixed within a generally accepted range.


[29] Put another way, it seems to me that Iacobucci J. does not
contemplate that the dismissed employee should recover damages
for all the harm caused by the bad faith conduct. To do so would
be to treat "bad faith discharge" as a stand-alone cause of
action either in contract or in tort, something he clearly
rejects.


[30] Fixing the notice period within a range depends on all the
relevant circumstances of the particular case. The upper end of
the range is not an absolute ceiling but is elastic enough to
increase for particularly deserving cases. Within this range, the
consequence of Wallace is that where bad faith employer conduct
exists, there will be a longer notice period than there would be
in the same case if there were no such conduct.


[31] As Iacobucci J. said in Wallace at p. 745 the trial judge in
weighing the importance of this factor must examine both the
nature of the bad faith conduct and its impact in the
circumstances. This is best done as part of a consideration of
all the relevant factors in the case rather than as a separate
exercise of determining the discrete addition to be made to the
notice period because of the employer's conduct. Given the
various factors to be considered and the endless variety of
factual contexts, reasonable notice cannot be determined as if it
were simply the product of an arithmetical formula.


[32] In part, this is a consequence of the fact that the various
factors to be considered do not appear to yield a single
organizing principle. Rather, they reflect various important
aspects of the employment relationship that deserve recognition
by the law in determining the notice required to terminate an
employee. The factors set out in Bardal, supra reflect the
employee's interest in some degree of job security and his or her
need to be cushioned against its loss. The consideration of
whether the dismissed employee has been induced away from secure
employment reflects his or her reliance and expectation
interests. The factor first laid out in Wallace, supra reflects
the employee's interest in being dealt with fairly and in good
faith at the point of dismissal.


[33] Where an employee is terminated without cause and without
notice the task of the trial judge is to examine the
circumstances of the particular case in light of these factors to
determine the quantum of damages in lieu of notice that reflects
the relative wrongfulness of the termination. The degree to which
the termination was done without respecting these important
aspects of the employment relationship will dictate where, within
the range, the required notice falls.


[34] It remains to apply the approach mandated by Wallace to the
facts of this case. Turning first to the Bardal factors,
Mr. Noseworthy's position was that of office manager for the
defendant car dealership. He had responsibility for only one
subordinate. There appears to be nothing extraordinary about his
duties. While he was sixty years of age at termination he had
only four years of service. Although the stagnant economy
undoubtedly limited his job prospects, there was no suggestion
that similar employment could not be found.


[35] Since there is no indication that Mr. Noseworthy had been
induced away from his prior employment his dismissal does not
disregard any employee reliance or expectation interest.


[36] While the employer was found to have acted in bad faith in
effecting the dismissal, neither the allegation of forgery nor
the threat of criminal charges which grounded this finding appear
to have been sustained beyond the initial meeting. It is, in my
view, far from the most egregious example of bad faith conduct.
Regrettably, however, the consequences of this conduct to
Mr. Noseworthy's mental health were significant.


[37] In all the circumstances, in my view the appropriate period
of reasonable notice is ten months. I would vary the judgment
below to provide for damages in an amount equivalent to ten
months' salary.


[38] The award of costs at trial was dependent upon a comparison
of the result with the plaintiff's offer to settle. Since it is
possible that the result of this appeal will affect that
comparison, it is appropriate that judgment as to costs, both of
the trial and of this appeal, be reserved in order to give the
parties an opportunity to provide written submissions on both
aspects of the costs issue. This is to be done within the next
thirty days.


[39] In the result, the appeal is allowed and the judgment below
is varied in accordance with these reasons. Costs of the trial
and of the appeal will be reserved to await written submissions.
