BY THE COURT:


[1]   The defendant Aden M. Shantz appeals the judgment of Molloy
J.  seeking  either  a new trial before a judge  and  jury  or  a
reduction in the damages award.  We called on the respondent, the
plaintiff Edward O'Brien, on only two issues:  first, whether the
trial judge erred in discharging the jury; and second whether the
trial  judge  erred  by  failing to take  into  account  negative
contingencies in her award of past and future loss of income.




1.   The Discharge of the Jury


[2]  This is the main ground of appeal.  After nine days of trial
and  after  both  counsel had made their closing  addresses,  the
trial  judge on the plaintiff's motion, discharged the  jury  and
gave  judgment  herself.  The appellant submits  that  the  trial
judge  erred  on  two  grounds  in  discharging  the  jury.   The
appellant argues that the trial judge erred first, by failing  to
conclude  that  the Workers' Compensation Board  ("WCB")  records
filed in evidence were admissible for the truth of their contents
as  business  records under s.35(2) of the Evidence  Act,  R.S.O.
1990, c.E-23; and second, by concluding that defence counsel (not
Mr.  Thomas)  had breached the requirements of fairness  when  he
referred to the WCB records in his closing address to the jury in
an  attempt  to  show  inconsistencies  between  the  plaintiff's
testimony at trial and statements attributed to the plaintiff  in
the  records.   Alternatively,  the  appellant  argues  that  the
fairness  concerns  prompting the plaintiff's motion  could  have
been remedied by further instructions to the jury.


[3]  We do not accept these arguments.  The trial judge was faced
with  an unusual and difficult situation.  She gave thorough  and
considered reasons for exercising her discretion to discharge the
jury.  Not only do we find no reviewable error in the exercise of
that discretion, we agree with her decision.


[4]   Plaintiff  counsel's motion to discharge the  jury  stemmed
from  defence counsel's use of the WCB records during his closing
argument  to  impeach the plaintiff's credibility.  Before  trial
both   counsel  had  given  notice  under  the  business  records
provision of the Evidence Act, s.35(2), that they intended to put
the  plaintiff's WCB records before the court.  On the first  day
of  trial,  plaintiff's counsel filed, on consent, one volume  of
approximately  400  pages  of WCB  records.   This  was  not  the
plaintiff's  entire WCB file.  Defence counsel took the  position
that  the  entire  file should be before the court,  and  on  the
fourth day of trial, additional WCB reports were filed, again  on
consent.  The total WCB file was approximately 500 pages.


[5]   At  no  time did either counsel say that the  records  were
being  filed  for the truth of their contents.  No  evidence  was
called  about  the nature of the records or the circumstances  in
which they were made.  Although s.35(2) of the Evidence Act  does
provide for an exception to the hearsay rule, the entire contents
of  the records do not constitute evidence of the truth of  their
contents by the mere fact that they are introduced  following the
service  of a notice under s.35.  Any record sought to be  relied
upon for the truth of its contents must fall within the scope  of
the  statutory exception.  See: Adderly v. Bremner, [1968] 1 O.R.
721  (H.C.).  Given the large volume of material and the  diverse
nature  of  these records, defence counsel could  not  reasonably
have   thought  that  plaintiff's  counsel  was  accepting  every
statement  in  the  records for its truth.  Moreover,  the  trial
judge  did  not understand that either counsel was conceding  the
truth of any of the WCB documents.


[6]   Defence  counsel never once referred  to  the  WCB  records
during his lengthy cross-examination of the plaintiff.  When  the
second  volume of WCB records was filed, defence counsel did  not
ask to have the plaintiff recalled for further cross-examination.
Instead, defence counsel made a tactical decision not to  examine
the  plaintiff on the WCB records because he did not want to give
plaintiff's counsel an opportunity to address any inconsistencies
in re-examination.  In closing argument, however, defence counsel
used  the records to attack the plaintiff's credibility.  He gave
the  jury copies of the records and referred to several pages  to
show  that the plaintiff had advanced two contradictory  versions
of  his medical condition, one to obtain WCB benefits, the  other
to obtain a large damages award.


[7]   In  granting the plaintiff's motion to discharge the  jury,
the  trial  judge recognized the general principle that  a  party
wishing  to  impeach the credibility of a witness must ordinarily
put  the  contradictory material to the witness in order to  give
the  witness  an opportunity to explain it.  This  principle,  at
bottom  a  principle of fairness, is reflected in  the  House  of
Lords decision Browne v. Dunn (1893), 6 R.67 (H.L.) and in ss. 20
and  21 of our Evidence Act.  The trial judge recognized that the
principle was not absolute but she concluded:


          In my opinion, both Section 21 of the Ontario
          Evidence Act and the rule in Browne  v.  Dunn
          are  applicable to the situation  before  me,
          and  both  have been violated in  spirit,  as
          well as in substance.


[8]   She  found,  as contended by plaintiff's counsel,  that  if
defence  counsel  wanted  to use the  records  to  challenge  the
plaintiff's  credibility,  he  was  obliged  to  have  given  the
plaintiff  an opportunity to explain the alleged inconsistencies.
She observed:


          On  the facts of this case, I agree with  Mr.
          O'Hagan's  position.   I  might  have   ruled
          differently  if  the alleged  inconsistencies
          were  clearly and blatantly before the  Court
          in  earlier evidence, in such a manner as  to
          cry out for some explanation by the plaintiff
          when he subsequently testified.  However, the
          inconsistencies  were  buried   within   five
          hundred  pages  of material  which  had  been
          filed in a routine manner on consent.
          In those circumstances, I think it completely
          unfair  to  Mr. O'Brien to extract  bits  and
          pieces  of  those documents, including  notes
          regarding snippets of telephone conversations
          two-and-a-half years ago, and  to  use  those
          excerpts  to  attack  his  credibility   now,
          without   giving   him  any  opportunity   to
          confirm, deny or explain.  This smacks to  me
          of  trial  by ambush, and I don't believe  it
          should   be  permitted.   It  simply  doesn't
          appear  to me to be fair or to be in  keeping
          with the principles underlying Section 21  of
          the  Evidence  Act or the Rule in  Browne  v.
          Dunn.


[9]   The  trial  judge held that after both parties  had  closed
their  case   it  was too late to redress the unfairness  to  the
plaintiff  by  recalling  him  to testify  in  reply.   Moreover,
defence counsel did not ask that the plaintiff be recalled.   She
also   held  that  the  unfairness  could  not  be  remedied   by
instructions to the jury.  On this point, she concluded:


          I  am inclined to agree with counsel for  the
          plaintiff.   It would be extremely  difficult
          for  a lay jury to sort out what evidence  is
          permitted   to  be  taken  into  account   in
          assessing credibility and what should not.  I
          am  particularly troubled by the March  23rd,
          1993  memo,  which  purportedly  evidences  a
          directly contradictory statement made by  the
          plaintiff on an issue central to the case.


[10]  Therefore, although recognizing the importance of the right
to  a  jury  trial,  the trial judge concluded  that  she  should
discharge the jury:


          I  recognize that the right to trial by  jury
          is  an  important one, and ought  not  to  be
          interfered with lightly.  I gave this  matter
          a  good  deal  of  thought, indeed  anguished
          thought.    On  balance,  I  feel  that   any
          unfairness  to  the defendant in  discharging
          the jury in these circumstances is outweighed
          by the unfairness to the plaintiff in leaving
          the  jury in.  The statements referred to  by
          Mr. Zarek are so potentially prejudicial that
          I  do not believe justice would be served for
          Mr. O'Brien if the jury remained.

[11]  We  agree  with  the trial judge's decision  and  with  her
reasons.   We  make  these four additional comments.   First,  we
reject  the  appellant's  main contention  that  the  entire  WCB
records could be used for the truth of their contents because the
records  were filed under s.35(2) of the Evidence Act.   Although
each  party served notice of his intention to file these  records
under  s.35(2),  certain prerequisites must  be  shown  to  exist
before a record can properly be admitted as proof of its contents
under that provision. The section reads as follows:


          s.35(2)   Any writing or record made  of  any
          act,  transaction,  occurrence  or  event  is
          admissible   as   evidence   of   such   act,
          transaction, occurrence or event if  made  in
          the usual and ordinary course of any business
          and  if  it  was  in the usual  and  ordinary
          course  of such business to make such writing
          or   record   at  the  time  of   such   act,
          transaction, occurrence or event or within  a
          reasonable time thereafter. [Emphasis added.]

As  we said earlier, no evidence was led about the nature of  the
records or the circumstances in which they were created.  In  our
view,  the  appellant  has  not shown that  the  WCB  records  he
intended  to use to contradict the plaintiff's testimony properly
fell within the scope of s.35(2).  Therefore, the trial judge was
correct  in concluding that the records were simply filed without
formal  proof  of their authenticity and were not admissible  for
the truth of their contents.


[12]  Second,  the  appellant's  submission  on  s.35(2)  of  the
Evidence Act ignores the fundamental unfairness that took  place.
What   was   fundamentally  unfair  was  that   defence   counsel
deliberately  refrained  from  asking  the  plaintiff  about  the
inconsistencies  while  he was in the  witness  box  and  instead
waited  to  attack his credibility when he had no opportunity  to
respond.  The appellant submits that because the WCB records were
not  independent or extrinsic evidence, having been filed by  the
plaintiff, the rule in Browne v. Dunn had no application.  In the
context  of this case, the distinction drawn by the appellant  is
irrelevant.   It is unrealistic and unfair to have  expected  the
plaintiff  to  have reviewed voluminous material  to  search  for
inconsistencies  not  brought  to  his  attention   while   being
examined.   Fairness  required that the  plaintiff  be  given  an
opportunity to explain the inconsistencies before his credibility
could be impeached by them.


[13] Third, the appellant submits that defence counsel had little
choice but to refer to the WCB records during his closing address
because he had to close first and plaintiff's counsel had already
signalled that he was going to review the records with  the  jury
by pointing out to them those records that assisted his case.  It
is  true  that  early in the trial plaintiff's counsel  told  the
court that he would be reviewing some of the WCB records with the
jury  and yet during the course of the evidence he never did  so.
Still, in our view, plaintiff's counsel's conduct does not assist
the  appellant.   It would have been no more open to  plaintiff's
counsel  to  refer to the WCB records during his closing  address
than  it was to defence counsel.  In the light of the way counsel
conducted the trial, plaintiff's counsel could not have used  the
records  to support his client's credibility and defence  counsel
could not use them to impugn the plaintiff's credibility.


[14]  Fourth, the trial judge's order discharging the jury was  a
discretionary order.  On appeal it is accorded the same deference
as  other discretionary orders.  See:  Hamstra (Guardian ad Litem
of)  v. British Columbia Rugby Union (1997), 145 D.L.R. (4th) 193
(S.C.C.).  Unless the order is unreasonable or reflects an  error
in   principle  or  in  law,  this  court  is  not  justified  in
interfering  with it.  The trial judge did not err in discharging
the  jury and, as we have already said, we agree with her  order.
Moreover,  as Major J. wrote in Hamstra at p. 203 "it  cannot  be
overstated  that  the  trial judge is in  the  best  position  to
determine  how to exercise this discretion".  As the trial  judge
properly exercised her discretion, the appellant's main ground of
appeal fails.






2.   Contingencies


[15] The trial judge awarded $90,000 for past loss of income  and
$678,200  for  future  loss of income.  She expressly  took  into
account negative contingencies in her award of past income  loss.
The  claim  for  past loss of income covers the five-year  period
from  1991  to 1995.  Because of contingencies, the  trial  judge
awarded  loss of income for only three years and at  the  reduced
annual figure of $30,000.  She said:


          I  find  as  a  fact that but for  the  motor
          vehicle  accident he would have graduated  in
          1991.   Further,  I  believe  he  would  have
          obtained employment within a reasonable  time
          thereafter.   I therefore am of  the  opinion
          that  there is some lost income between  1991
          and   1995   for   which  the  defendant   is
          responsible.   However, due to the  recession
          and  job cutbacks, it is quite possible  that
          Mr.  O'Brien would not have been employed for
          that   whole  period.   Allowing   for   this
          contingency, as well as time to  find  a  job
          and  an  entry level wage, I assess his  lost
          income claim at $30,000.00 per year for three
          years, for a total of $90,000.00.

The  negative  contingencies lowered the past income  loss  award
from $200,000 to $90,000.


[16]  In  awarding future loss of income, the trial  judge  found
that  the  plaintiff would be working today, and indeed would  be
working  until  the usual retirement age, were  it  not  for  the
accident.   She also found that neither the plaintiff's Addison's
disease nor his head injury interfered with his ability to obtain
full-time employment.  These findings limited the extent to which
negative contingencies could affect the award for future loss  of
income.


[17]  Still  the trial judge expressly factored two contingencies
into her award.  She deducted $2,000 per year for the possibility
that the plaintiff would earn some income from working part-time;
and  she  refused  to give the plaintiff any  credit  for  salary
increases  beyond the rate of inflation.  Instead she  concluded:
"Given  the  fact  that his pre-injury earnings  were  below  the
average, I have not allowed any increase for that contingency".


[18]  We  are therefore satisfied that the trial judge adequately
took  account of negative contingencies in her assessment of  the
plaintiff's loss of past and future income.  The appellant's real
argument  is not that the trial judge failed to consider negative
contingencies  but that she should have made a greater  deduction
for  them.   That argument is met by the deference  an  appellate
court  must  accord  to  a trial judge's assessment  of  damages.
Therefore, this ground of appeal also fails.


[19] Accordingly, the appeal is dismissed with costs.
