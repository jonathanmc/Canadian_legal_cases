MORDEN A.C.J.O.:

[1]   This appeal by the plaintiffs is concerned solely with  the
proper  application  of  rule 49.10(2)  of  the  Rules  of  Civil
Procedure  to  the  facts  of  this case.  This  rule,  and  rule
49.10(3), read:


          (2)  Where an offer to settle,

               (a)  is  made  by a defendant  at  least
                    seven  days before the commencement
                    of the hearing;

               (b)  is   not  withdrawn  and  does  not
                    expire  before the commencement  of
                    the hearing; and

               (c)  is not accepted by the plaintiff,

          and  the  plaintiff  obtains  a  judgment  as
          favourable  as  or less favourable  than  the
          terms  of  the offer to settle, the plaintiff
          is  entitled to party and party costs to  the
          date  the  offer was served and the defendant
          is  entitled  to party and party  costs  from
          that date, unless the court orders otherwise.

          (3)   The burden of proving that the judgment
          is as favourable as the terms of the offer to
          settle,  or more or less favourable,  as  the
          case  may be, is on the party who claims  the
          benefit of subrule (1) or (2).

[2]  The facts, in so far as they are material to the issue to be
decided  are  as follows. The plaintiff, Georgia Onisiforou,  was
injured  on  October  7, 1987, in a motor vehicle  accident.  The
other  plaintiff is her husband, Mario Onisiforou. They commenced
an  action against the defendant Clinton Rose, the driver of  the
other  vehicle  in  the  accident,  and  the  Scarborough  Public
Utilities Commission, the owner of this vehicle.


[3]   Georgia's  claim  was for damages  for  personal  injuries.
Mario's  claim  was for damages under the Family Law  Act,  1986,
S.O. 1986, c.4.


[4]   About  18 months before the trial commenced, the  defendant
served  an  offer to settle in these terms: "$50,000.00 inclusive
of   claims   and  pre-judgment  interest  for  all   plaintiffs.
Assessable  costs  are  in  addition."  The  defendants  admitted
liability  and  the  action proceeded before  a  jury.  The  jury
assessed  Georgia's damages at $30,750. After  the  deduction  of
accident  benefits  she had received and  the  addition  of  pre-
judgment  interest as determined by the trial judge, the judgment
in her favour as set forth in the formal judgment was $50,868.60.
The  jury assessed Mario's damages at nil and the formal judgment
provided   that   he  "shall  recover  no  monies   against   the
defendants".


[5]   In  comparing the judgment to the terms  of  the  offer  to
settle, the trial judge added to the net damages of $28,073  pre-
judgment interest to the date of the offer (December 7, 1993)  at
the  average rate of 10.5% on the general damages and 10% on  the
special  damages. The total came to $46,487.91 -  less  than  the
amount   of  the  defendants'  offer.  Accordingly,  under   rule
49.10(2), he awarded costs to the plaintiffs to the date  of  the
offer and costs to the defendants thereafter.


[6]   The appellants' principal argument is that the trial  judge
erred  in  concluding that the offer to settle "was of sufficient
clarity  to invoke rule 49(2)". They also submit that  the  trial
judge  erred,  with  respect to the offer,  in  calculating  pre-
judgment interest at the average rate of 10.5% on general damages
rather than the statutory rate of 13% and, further, that he erred
in  calculating  pre-judgment interest only to the  date  of  the
offer and not to the date of judgment.


[7]   In my view, the appellants are entitled to succeed on their
principal argument. I would not characterize the issue  as  being
whether  the  offer  had "sufficient clarity" but,  rather,  more
broadly, whether the respondents have shown, on the facts of this
case,  that  "the  plaintiff Georgia  obtain[ed]  a  judgment  as
favourable as or less favourable than the terms of the  offer  to
settle."  From a purely contractual point of view the  offer  was
clear. The two appellants could have accepted this offer and that
would  have  been  the  end of the action. How  they  divided  it
between them was of no concern to the defendants.


[8]   The appellants, however, did not accept the offer but  went
on  to  trial.  Georgia obtained a judgment for  $50,868.60.  Her
judgment  was  treated by the trial judge as being somewhat  less
than  $50,000. Mario did not obtain a judgment. In fact,  because
he  did  not  prove  any  damages, his  action,  in  effect,  was
dismissed.


[9]   It  is  not  possible, in these circumstances,  to  compare
Georgia's  judgment with the terms of the offer for the  purposes
of rule 49.10(2).


[10]  I  do not think that it answers the question to be resolved
to  lump  the two plaintiffs together and then say the offer  was
clear.  What  happened  here happens in many  actions.  Georgia's
claim,  on  a  separate cause of action, succeeded,  and  Mario's
claim,  albeit  asserted in a derivative action, failed.  In  the
normal  course, she would be entitled to costs and  he  would  be
liable  for  costs or, possibly, no costs would be  ordered  with
respect  to  his  claim. See Orkin, The Law  of  Costs,  2nd  ed.
(1997),  p.  2-48. No doubt, most of the time and effort  in  the
action was devoted to her claim, not his.


[11]  In these circumstances, it cannot be said that the judgment
in  this  case was as favourable as or less favourable  than  the
terms  of the offer - because there was no offer with respect  to
the claim on which Georgia obtained her judgment.


[12]  By reason of rule 49.10(3), the burden of proving that  the
judgment  is as favourable or less favourable than the  terms  of
the offer is on the respondents. This burden they have not met.


[13]  The respondents have argued that the appellants did not  at
any time request a breakdown of what was being offered as between
the  two plaintiffs. With respect, I do not see how this relieves
the  respondents of having to show, after the judgment, a set  of
facts that enables them to have the advantage of rule 49.10(2).


[14]  Reference  may  usefully be made  to  rule  49.02(1)  which
enables  a party to serve an offer to settle "any one or more  of
the claims in the proceeding". This clearly shows that within the
framework  of Rule 49 it was open to the defendants to  serve  an
offer  that could have attracted the costs consequences  of  rule
49.10(2).


[15]  The  respondents refer to a similar case under  the  former
rules,  Ross  v.  Martin, [1966] 2 O.R. 306 (H.  Ct.),  in  which
Moorehouse  J. held, with respect to a payment into  court,  that
the  failure to specify the amount of it allocated to each of two
claims was only an irregularity, and that he could still exercise
his  discretion under rule 317 to award costs after payment  into
court to the defendants.


[16] I do not think that this decision is of any more help to the
respondents   than  to  the  appellants.  The  former   governing
provision  on  costs, Rule 317, was in considerably more  general
terms than those in rule 49.10(2) and, accordingly, the reasoning
in  Ross is of little assistance in the present case. There is no
discretion  under  rule  49.10(2) respecting  costs  consequences
unless resort is had to "the court order[ing] otherwise". This is
a  narrow  residual  discretion (Niagara  Structural  Steel  (St.
Catherines) Ltd. v. W.D. Laflamme Ltd. (1987), 58 O.R.  (2d)  773
(C.A.)) and neither side in the present case submits that  it  is
relevant to the resolution of the issue before us.


[17] In light of my conclusion on the principal submission of the
appellant,  it  is  not necessary to consider  the  other  issues
raised.


[18] I would grant leave to appeal, allow the appeal, with costs,
set  aside  the costs order at trial and, in its place,  make  an
order granting the plaintiff Georgia Onisiforou her costs of  the
action  and  granting  the  defendants their  costs  against  the
plaintiff Mario Onisiforou.
