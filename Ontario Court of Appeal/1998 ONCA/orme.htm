                          ENDORSEMENT


[1]  The appellant appeals his conviction for failing to provide
a breath sample contrary to s. 254(5) of the Criminal Code.


[2]  At 4:00 p.m. on November 20, 1994, a police officer observed
the appellant behind the wheel of his vehicle, with the engine
running.   When he spoke to the appellant, the officer detected a
strong odour of alcohol and he noticed an open case of beer and
an unopened bottle of wine on the floor of the car.  The officer
asked the appellant to step out of his car and then found an
empty beer bottle underneath the driver's seat.  The officer took
the appellant to his police cruiser and had him sit in the back
of the cruiser.  At 4:04 p.m., the officer explained to the
appellant the operation of the roadside device and at 4:05 p.m.,
he made the demand for a breath sample.  After numerous attempts
(nine), the appellant produced a sample and registered a "fail".
The appellant was then taken to a police station and failed to
provide a suitable breath sample, pursuant to s. 254(3).


[3]  The trial judge provided detailed reasons for his decision.
He essentially accepted the Crown's evidence and rejected the
appellant's explanation that he was incapable of providing a
suitable breath sample.


[4]  The Summary Conviction Appeal Court judge delivered
extensive reasons in which he reviewed the evidence and all
issues raised by the appellant.  He dismissed the appeal.  Leave
was granted to appeal to this court.


[5]  It is beyond dispute, on the facts of this case, that the
police officer possessed a reasonable suspicion of the
appellant's consumption of alcohol.  The police officer took the
appellant to his cruiser, explained the operation of the roadside
screening device and formally demanded a sample of the
appellant's breath, within five minutes of the officer's first
contact with the appellant.


[6]  The appellant concedes that the terms "has the care or
control of a motor vehicle" must be interpreted in such a way as
to have some degree of past significance.  See R. v. Campbell
(1988), 44 C.C.C. (3d) 502 (Ont. C.A.) at pp. 505-6.  Unlike R.
v. Pierman, R. v. Dewald (1994), 92 C.C.C. (3d) 160 (Ont. C.A.),
where the court found an unjustifiable delay on the part of a
police officer in requiring a roadside screening device breath
sample, the time lapse between the appellant's actual care or
control of his vehicle and the demand, in the present case, was
no more than that which was reasonably necessary to enable the
officer to discharge his duties.  See also R. v. Smith (1996),
105 C.C.C. (3d) 58.


[7]  The demand made by the police officer was valid and within
the scope of s. 254(2) and the result of the screening device
could be used in considering whether the officer had reasonable
and probable grounds to make the subsequent breathalyser demand.


[8]  Although this issue was never directly addressed in the
evidence, it is our view that even without the "fail" result of
the roadside screening test, the police officer had reasonable
and probable grounds to demand a breathalyser sample, pursuant to
s. 254(3).  (The officer believed that the appellant was impaired
by the consumption of alcohol.  See p. 68 of the transcript.)


[9]  As did the Summary Conviction Court, we see no merit in the
argument that the trial judge misapprehended the requisite mens
rea for this offence.


[10] In the result, the Summary Conviction Appeal Court judge
made no error of law in dismissing the appellant's appeal.
Accordingly, this appeal is dismissed.
