                      ENDORSEMENT
                                


[1]  The appellant does not dispute that the respondent Passmore
suffered damages in the amount of $1,377,885.95. However, the
appellant argues that clause 20 of Schedule A of the Agreement of
Purchase and Sale operates to limit Passmore’s damages to the
amount of the appellant’s deposits, totalling $422,598.


[2]  Clause 20 provides:
          
          20.  In the event the Purchaser defaults on
          any of his obligations herein contained, or
          contained in the Occupancy Agreement, prior
          to closing and fails to remedy such default
          within five (5) days of his being so notified
          in writing, the Vendor, in addition to any
          other remedies this Agreement provides, may
          at its option declare this Agreement and the
          Occupancy Agreement to be at an end and any
          deposit monies together with all monies paid
          for any extras or changes shall be retained
          by the Vendor as its liquidated damages and
          not as a penalty. [Emphasis added.]
          
          

[3]  The appellant defaulted on her obligation to close the
transactions on the agreed upon date. On October 3, 1990,
Passmore’s solicitors wrote the appellant advising:
          
          Please be advised that our client accepts
          your repudiation of the Agreements of
          Purchase and Sale which are terminated and of
          no further force and effect. By reason of
          your breach of contract and repudiation of
          the Agreements of Purchase and Sale, all
          deposit money previously paid has been fofeit
          to our client as liquidated damages and not
          as a penalty. We wish to further advise that
          you will be held responsible for all
          additional costs, losses and damage which our
          client will incur as a result of or arising
          out of your breach of contract and
          repudiation of the Agreement of Purchase and
          Sale. Interest on any amount found to be
          owing by you will be claimed pursuant to the
          Courts of Justice Act, S.O. 1980), c. 11, ss.
          138-139, as amended.
          
          

[4]  The letter enclosed a draft statement of claim asserting
Passmore’s claim for the actual damages it would suffer as a
result of the appellant’s default.


[5]  At common law, one of the options open to a vendor in
Passmore’s position is to elect to accept the purchaser’s
repudiation of the agreement and sue for its damages in respect
of the breach committed. Maysen v. Clouet, [1924] A.C. 980 (H.L.)
at 985.


[6]  The appellant submits that the vendor’s only option is to
proceed under clause 20 and that as a result the vendor is
limited in its claim for damages to the amount of the deposits
and has in effect forfeited the common law right to sue for the
full amount of its loss.


[7]  We are satisfied that a proper interpretation of clause 20
did not remove the options open to Passmore at common and law and
did not therefore preclude Passmore from electing to accept the
appellant’s repudiation and sue for the full amount of its loss.


[8]  It is clear that Passmore did not opt to proceed in a manner
that would limit its claim for damages. To the contrary, the
letter of October 3, 1990 clearly set out its intention to pursue
a claim in an amount in excess of the amount of the deposits.


[9]  Accordingly, the appeal against the judgment in favour of
Passmore is dismissed with costs.


[10] The appellant also appealed the dismissal of its third party
claim against the respondent’s Kevin Lo and Sincere Realty.
O’Brien J. found that the facts of this case did not give rise to
a duty on the part of these respondents to qualify the appellant
as a purchaser or to advise her to seek legal advice. We did not
call on the respondent in respect to this part of the appeal as
we saw no merit in it. Accordingly, this branch of the appeal is
also dismissed with costs.


[11] We are not certain if all counsel agreed that costs should
be fixed in the amount of $25,000 each and for this reason we
would order that costs be assessed.
