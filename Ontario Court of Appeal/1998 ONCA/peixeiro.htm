                     ENDORSEMENT


[1]    This is an appeal with respect to costs of a motion and of
an appeal brought pursuant to ss.6.1(c), 6.2, and 7.2 of the
Courts of Justice Act, R.S.O. 1990, c.C.43.


[2]    It is common ground between the parties that the
jurisdiction of the court is properly referred to by Orkin in his
textbook on The Law of Costs, 1997, Second Edition at paragraph
603.4(1) as follows:

            It is a settled rule that on an appeal
            from the assessment officer the court is
            only concerned with questions of
            principle, and not with mere questions
            of amounts or the manner in which the
            assessment officer has exercised his
            discretion, unless the amounts are so
            inappropriate or the assessment
            officer's decision so unreasonable as to
            suggest an error in principle.

[3]    In the first instance, the motion was brought to the
General Division to determine a question of law.  The question of
law was whether or not the discoverability principle applied to
extend the two-year limitation period under the Highway Traffic
Act, R.S.O. 1990, c.H.8.  The motions judge held that it did not.
The plaintiff appealed and the Ontario Court of Appeal overruled
the decision below and held that the discoverability rule did
apply to extend the limitation period.  That decision is reported
as Peixeiro v. Haberman (1995), 25 O.R. (3d) 1 (Ont. C.A.)
(aff'd. by S.C.C. [1997] 3 S.C.R. 549).


[4]    On the costs assessment before Assessment Officer Moquin,
Mr. Azevedo, counsel for the plaintiff, apparently had minimal
dockets for the original motion and no dockets at all for the
appeal.


[5]    The assessment officer gave reasons in which she stated as
follows:

            On the assessment, the solicitor for the
            plaintiffs had filed a bill of costs
            claiming approximately 230 hours with
            respect to drafting of materials,
            research, preparation and attendance on
            the motions in the General Division.
            Time dockets submitted in support
            totaled [sic] 78.4 hours (after it was
            determined that 6.5 of docketed hours
            related to another motion and were not
            properly assessable at this assessment).
            A second bill of costs relating to the
            attendance at the Court of Appeal was
            filed as well, claiming approximately
            232 hours with respect to drafting of
            materials, research, preparation and
            attendance.  There were no time dockets
            submitted in support of this bill of
            costs.
            I am fully aware that the absence of
            dockets does not mean that the solicitor
            should not be compensated (The Law of
            Costs, 2nd Edition, Ch. 311.1(7)),
            however, I would remind counsel that at
            the assessment hearing full submissions
            were heard from both solicitors and
            evidence of the work done, as well as
            the time dockets submitted, were
            carefully considered.  All time honoured
            factors and principles of assessment
            were applied including the complexity of
            the proceeding, the degree of
            responsibility assumed, the skill and
            competence demonstrated by the
            solicitor, the importance of the issues
            to the client and the result achieved.
            Factors that can attribute to the
            expenditure of reasonably excessive
            preparation time such as experience of
            counsel, unnecessary preparation and
            preparation due to an overabundance of
            caution were combed through the dockets
            and I did determine that, under the
            given circumstances, the following was
            fair and reasonable and will remain as
            assessed:
            
                                                 
                 Preparation     Attendance      Total
                                                 
Motions in the   51.5 hours      3.5 hours       55 hours
General
Division (i.e.
motion before
Justice
Paisley +
attendance
before Justice
Borins)
                                                 
Court of         50 hours        2.5 hours       52.5 hours
Appeal


[6]    It appears from these reasons that the assessment officer
took into account the proper factors to be applied on the
assessment but, based on a review of the dockets that were there,
came to the conclusion that Mr. Azevedo had spent an inordinate
amount of time in unnecessary preparation applying an
overabundance of caution.  Consequently, she significantly
reduced the hours he claimed to have spent from 230 hours in
respect of the motion and 232 in respect of the appeal down to 55
hours for the motion and 52.5 hours for the appeal.


[7]    The appellant solicitor's position is that the assessment
officer made an error in principle by failing to appreciate the
complexity and importance of the issue and therefore the degree
of skill required to successfully prepare for and argue the
appeal. The respondent takes the position that the assessment
officer made no error in principle and that the only issue on
this appeal is as to amount.


[8]    In my view, based on the record, it cannot be said that
the assessment officer made an error in principle.  The award
included a significant amount of time to prepare for and argue
this matter. If the preparation involved something extraordinary
which the solicitor desires to demonstrate to this court on the
record, he would require dockets to do so.  The assessment of 50
hours is not one which this court can say is too low as a matter
of principle to prepare for an appeal on a question of law.


[9]    The appeal is therefore dismissed with costs.
