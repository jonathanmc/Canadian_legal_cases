                     ENDORSEMENT
                               I.
[1]  The appellants are adult relatives of Albert Perez. Mr.
Perez, while an involuntary psychiatric patient at the respondent
hospital, smashed a window with a chair, climbed out on the
window ledge and fell to his death. The appellants claim damages
under Part V of the Family Law Act, R.S.O. 1990, c. F.3.


[2]  The respondents successfully moved for summary judgment,
relying on the 6-month limitation period found in s. 8 of the
Mental Health Act, R.S.O. 1990, c. M-7 (the Act). It provides:

          All actions, prosecutions, or other
          proceedings against any person or psychiatric
          facility for anything done or omitted to be
          done in pursuance or intended pursuance of
          this Act or the regulations shall be
          commenced within six months after the act or
          omission complained of occurred and not
          afterwards.
[3]  This proceedings was not commenced within six months. The
appellants contend that s. 78 is not a bar to the action as the
allegations made by the appellants do not arise out of things
done or omitted to be done in pursuance of the Act.


[4]  Statutory limitation periods like s. 78 are strictly
construed. In Fenton v. North York Electric Commission (1996), 29
O.R. (3d) 481, Rosenberg J.A., in interpreting a similar
limitation provision, said, at p. 485:

          ... Some sensible meaning must be given to
          the phrase "in pursuance of this act." In my
          view, it refers only to those acts done in
          direct execution of a statutory duty or acts
          which are necessarily incidental to the
          carrying out of a statutory duty.
[5]  All parties agree that the approach taken in Fenton, supra,
should be applied to s. 78 of the Act.


[6]  The allegations in the statement of claim refer to:
*    a failure to take reasonable steps to ensure the safety of
     the deceased while he was an involuntary patient in the
     respondent hospital; and
*    a failure to provide adequate treatment for the deceased

while he was an involuntary patient in the respondent hospital.

[7]  Counsel undertook a detailed review of the Act and its
regulations. Read together, they clearly place a duty on
psychiatric facilities to provide services to persons suffering
from mental disorders. For patients in those facilities, those
services include observation, care and treatment. The Act
recognizes and gives statutory force to the duty to treat those
who are patients in the facility. A duty to observe, care and
treat necessarily implies a duty to take reasonable steps to
provide a safe environment for patients who are undergoing
observation, care and treatment in a psychiatric facility.


[8]  The allegations in the statement of claim refer to acts or
omissions which fall within the ambit of s 78 of the Act. The
fact that other statutes or the common law may also impose duties
on some or all of the respondents which overlap with those
imposed by the Act is of no consequence to the application of s.
78 of the Act.


[9]  The motion judge correctly held that s. 78 provided a
defence to the allegations made by the appellants.


                              II.


[10] The appellants further contend that if s. 78 of the Act
applies to their claim, it contravenes s. 15(1) of the Charter
and is rendered inoperative by s. 52 of the Constitution Act
1982. It is their position that the six-month limitation period
on allegations of negligence arising out of the care and
treatment of persons in psychiatric facilities amounts to
discrimination on account of mental disability when compared to
the 2-year limitation period applicable to similar allegations
arising out of the care and treatment in hospital facilities
other than psychiatric facilities: see Public Hospitals Act,
R.S.O. 1990, c. P.40, s.31.


[11] The constitutionality of s. 78 was not raised in the
pleadings or in the motion for summary judgment. It was raised
for the first time in a supplementary notice of appeal filed some
three years after the action was commenced. In the normal course,
appeals are not the proper forum in which to raise brand new
issues which significantly expand or alter the landscape of the
litigation. On occasion, such issues can be raised on appeal
where the party seeking to raise the new issue demonstrates that
the interests of justice require an exception to the normal and
accepted course of litigation.


[12] The appellants cannot satisfy that onus. This is not a case
like R. v. Brown (1993), 83 C.C.C. (3d) 129 (S.C.C.) where the
appellants could not reasonably have been expected to have raised
the constitutional issue in earlier proceedings. There is no
reason advanced for the failure to raise s. 15 in a timely
fashion. Nor is this a case where the constitutional issue can be
resolved without an evidentiary inquiry and findings of fact. The
ultimate constitutionality of the section may well turn on s. 1
of the Charter. The analysis required under that section demands
a proper evidentiary foundation.


[13] In the circumstances, the appellants should not be permitted
to raise s. 15 of the Charter at this late stage of the
litigation.


[14] The appeal is dismissed with costs.
