                      ENDORSEMENT
[1]  This is an appeal from a judgment of Loukidelis, J, of the
Ontario Court (General Division), dated March 11, 1997. The
appellant appeals from both the finding that he was negligent and
the assessment of damages.


[2]  The appellant, who is a solicitor, was retained by the
respondents to act for them in the purchase of a house and lands
they had agreed to purchase under the terms of an agreement of
purchase and sale. In the agreement of purchase and sale the
lands were referred to as Lots 77 and 78, plan H-811, Fenwick
Township, District of Algoma.


[3]  The problem is most easily described by simply stating that
an unopened road allowance, shown on the survey as Alberta
Street, lies between lots 77 and lots 78. Approximately sixty
percent of the house and all of the garage stand on this road
allowance. In addition, the survey shows that the respondents'
predecessors had also occupied to the water's edge, part of
another unopened road allowance, Water Street, where lot 78
fronts on it. Access to all of the land and premises is only
possible from a public road on which lot 77 fronts. Alberta
Street runs a very short distance between the road in front of
lot 77 and Water Street. However, the allowance for Water Street
runs for some distance as it follows the bank of a river that
runs through part of the lands in the plan. Much of this is
readily apparent from the survey attached to the deed (see
Appendix "A") and a portion of Plan H-811 (see Appendix "B").


[4]  When the transaction was completed, the deed to the
respondents contained a four part metes and bounds description
purporting to convey not only lots 77 and 78 but also parts of
Alberta Street and Water Street. The appellant was found to be
negligent because he certified to his clients that they had a
good and marketable title to all of the lands.


[5]  It is conceded that the respondents got a good title to lots
77 and 78. The appellant contends that his clients got a good
title to all of the lands described in the deed, relying on a
search of the abstract of title for each of the four parts in the
deed. He said the abstract for each of the two road allowances
showed a good and sufficient chain of title during a period
greater than forty years immediately preceding his search. In
short, the appellant argues that although neither abstract
recorded a conveyance of the interest of the Crown in the right
of Canada or Ontario in the unopened road allowances, grants to
his clients' predecessors in title purported to convey the same
lands and that was enough to convey clear and marketable title to
the respondents.


[6]  The lands are in unorganized territory, an area without
municipal organization. Pursuant to the Robinson-Huron Treaty of
1850, they were set aside as a reserve for the Batchewana First
Nation. They were subsequently surrendered in 1859 by a treaty
between the aboriginal owners to the Crown in right of Canada,
with the condition that any sale proceeds be held for the
aboriginal owner. An undated plan of the area, consisting of the
whole township and showing no roads, was then registered.
Subsequently, in 1861, the Crown registered a plan for that part
of the township in which these lands lie. It was the plan of the
town plot of Apoquosh. This plan laid out Alberta Street and
Water Street and the lots adjacent to them. In 1908, the Crown
applied to the court and obtained an order amending the plan;
however, the road allowances remained unaltered. Plan H-811 is
not a plan of subdivision. It is a Registrar's Composite Plan of
the town plot of Apoquosh and was approved for registration on
December 11, 1981. It includes the lots in question here and the
allowances for Water Street and Alberta Street. It is conceded
that there were valid patents from the Crown for the lands
subdivided into lots. The abstracts of title do not show that
either of the two streets was ever the subject of a patent, or
that any steps have been taken to close either of them.


[7]  We agree with the trial judge that there was sufficient on
the title to bring the matter within the exception provided by s.
112(1) of the Registry Act, R.S.O. 1990, c. R.20. Clearly, the
presence of the unclosed road allowances constitutes a claim
referred to in s. 113(5)(a)(ii) and (iii) of the Registry Act. In
the circumstances, the appeal from the finding that the appellant
was negligent fails and must be must dismissed.


[8]  The damages were assessed at $78,636. The trial judge heard
evidence that the respondents paid $105,000.00 for the property
and that it was valued at $125,000 if the owner had a good and
marketable title. However, the assessor testified that he was
unaware of a situation with the same or similar title issues that
could be used as a comparison. For his part, however, the
appellant pointed to the evidence of the recent purchase of the
house next door, on lot 76, in which case a small part of the
house and garage was perhaps built on deeded portion of the
Alberta Street allowance. Even though the purchasers were warned
of this and the fact they would get no title to the Alberta
Street land, they chose to close anyway and to pay full price.
According to the appellant, that land was marketable. But as the
appendices show, the facts there were different. Lot 76 itself is
much larger than 77 or 78, neither of which alone has a
sufficient area upon which to locate the house and, of course,
the two lots are separated by Alberta Street.


[9]  Acting on a suggested basis for assessing the damages given
in evidence by an evaluator and accepting his evidence that the
cost of purchasing a similar water front lot and moving the
respondents' house and buildings there would be $78,636, the
trial judge assessed the damages in that sum. He regarded this as
an alternative method of measuring the damages, made necessary
because of the inability to assess damages on the difference in
money between the amount paid to the vendor, and the value had he
received good title.


[10] However, in our opinion, an assessment on the basis adapted,
and in that amount, is not responsive to the evidence and the
negligence. It was overcompensation. If the respondents were to
proceed as contemplated they would end up with their house on a
new lot to which they had good title as well as the original lots
encumbered as a result of the unopened road allowances. On the
other hand, if the respondents did not proceed to buy and move
the house, they could retain or sell the present property and the
moneys. There is no suggestion the sum relates to the cost of
obtaining title to the road allowances or the diminution in value
if title cannot be obtained.


[11] The respondents led no evidence that they had ever intended
to move or that they had ever attempted to sell the property. Nor
did they lead evidence of a present intention of doing so. There
was no evidence that could support even a suggestion that the
Crown in the right of Canada or Ontario had any interest in
opening either of the road allowances or that it would serve any
public interest or reason to do so. Currently, Ontario appears to
recognize the respondents as "owners" of the lands, at least for
the purpose of taxation. A certificate of the Treasurer of the
relevant Board of Education, which was filed in evidence, states
the total taxes levied on the lands being lots 77 and 78 and part
of Alberta Street and indicates the respondents as owners. There
is no evidence that the lands are situated near or contiguous to
a reserve. Moreover, the surveys show the area beyond the road
allowances to be completely subdivided into residential lots on
patented lands, with all the lots fronting on a separate right of
way. Thus, it is quite unlikely that the respondents' occupancy
will ever be disturbed. At best, any claim in damages appears to
be minimal.


[12] While the issue was not raised by the parties, we were
concerned that the respondents had made no efforts to procure a
closing of the road allowances by the township or Crown in the
right of Ontario and or Canada, and to obtain such releases as
might be necessary. Normally, when lands are subdivided for
residential lots, it would be quite inconsistent with the sale of
the lots in the development that road allowances could be
occupied for any other purpose, except perhaps those of the
adjacent land owners. The sale of the development surely proceeds
on this basis. There is no registered plan subdivision here as
that term is understood in modern terms. But what seems clear is
that consistent with its ownership, the Crown, in 1861, for the
purpose of development through sale, put on a plan showing the
land divided into lots serviced by roads. It then proceeded to
sell the lands on that basis, granting patents for the lands on
which building was to take place. In the circumstances, it is
doubtful that there is room for a claim of a property interest in
the unopened road allowances in the original aboriginal owners,
although a claim for all or part of the compensation received
from an adjacent landowner might survive depending on the Crown's
obligation.


[13] The evidence of a witness touched on the question of whether
the closing and conveyance of the road allowances was possible at
this time, given the possibility of a claim made against them by
the original aboriginal owners. This witness, a solicitor,
produced letters that he suggested could be taken as indicating
that neither the federal government, the province, nor the Local
Roads Board were either able or prepared to move to clear the
plaintiff's title. However, that evidence was, in the main,
hearsay and, in our respectful opinion, should have little if any
weight. In our view, a bona fide attempt to resolve the title
deficiencies was a condition precedent to such an award of
damages as was made in this case.


[14] The respondents had the burden of proving their damages. The
damages suffered by the respondents was the cost of getting clear
title to the lands purportedly conveyed to them, and failing
that, the diminished value of the lands and premises because of
the title. There was very little evidence touching on these
issues and, in our respectful opinion, that evidence established
only minimal damages. On the evidence, we think the damages
should be assessed in the sum of $3,000.00.


[15] Accordingly, the appeal from the assessment of damages
succeeds and the award is reduced to $3,000.00. There will be no
order as to costs in this court. The order as to cost at trial
stand.
