                      ENDORSEMENT
                                
                                
[1]  A contractor sued a homeowner for the balance owing on a
project to refinish a basement. The statement of claim alleged a
breach of contract and did not allege a claim based on quantum
meruit. During the closing argument counsel for the plaintiff
moved to amend the statement of claim to claim quantum meruit and
made submissions based on quantum meruit. The amendment was
strenuously opposed as being inappropriate at that stage of the
proceedings but no request was made to adjourn the proceedings to
call further evidence. The trial judge reserved his decision on
the issue of whether the amendment should be granted.


[2]  In his reasons the trial judge stated in relation to the
quantum meruit claim:


                    The defendant suggests that the
          plaintiff is disentitled from relying on this
          principle of liability because it has not
          been specifically pleaded. There is no
          suggestion that he has been prejudiced by
          this failure. Throughout this litigation,
          including the three days of trial, the
          defendant has been contrasting the
          reasonableness and proof of the amount
          claimed by the plaintiff. In those
          circumstances, I see no need for an amendment
          of the pleadings to include a claim based on
          quantum meruit: see Burns v. Pocklington
          (1995), 5 C.P.C. (2d) 18 ( O.C.A.).
          Alternatively the circumstances of this case
          cry out for such relief.
          


[3]  Before us, the defendant submitted that he was prejudiced,
that he did not have an opportunity to adduce evidence as to the
value of the work done and that the trial judge did not assess
the claim for quantum meruit on the appropriate basis.


[4]  The time to raise the issue of prejudice was when the
request to amend was made.


[5]  The defendant prepared and filed a written copy of his
argument with the trial judge that had been prepared the day
before. The argument included a section entitled quantum meruit
and made submissions with respect to this claim. The defendant
was therefore aware that the plaintiff was advancing a claim
based on quantum meruit at trial.


[6]  In the circumstances we are not persuaded that there was any
unfairness to the defendant. We are also of the opinion that the
method used by the trial judge to assess the claim for quantum
meruit was one that was open to him. The appeal is dismissed with
costs.
