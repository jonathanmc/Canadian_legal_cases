ABELLA J.A.:

[1]  Adrian Ramcharitar was born on October 28, 1992. His mother,
Susan Ramcharitar, was 31 years old when Adrian was born. About
four month's before the child's birth, Susan moved to Guelph to
live with her 50 year-old sister, Molly Ramcharitar and her
sister's 56 year-old husband, Pillalamari Jagam.


[2]  The parties agreed that the child would be raised by Molly
and her husband.


[3]  After the child was born, they all lived together until
December 1992 at which time they went to Trinidad when Susan and
Molly's mother died. After the funeral, Susan remained in
Trinidad for six weeks to deal with outstanding estate matters;
Molly and her husband returned to Canada with Adrian.


[4]  When Susan returned to Canada in February 1993, she asked
her sister to return the child to her. Molly and her husband
refused.


[5]  After attempted negotiations failed, Susan applied for
custody of Adrian in June, 1993. Her sister and brother-in-law
cross-applied in July. In September 1993, an interim order was
made giving custody to Molly and her husband, with access to
Susan for three hours every other weekend.


[6]  Almost a year later, in July 1994, Molly and her husband
applied to adopt Adrian and to dispense with Susan's consent to
the adoption. The custody and adoption proceedings were
consolidated in November 1994.


[7]  The trial did not take place until March 1995. The words of
the trial judge, Associate Chief Judge Robert Walmsley are worth
repeating:


          The consolidated applications before me
          provide a cruel illustration of the
          devastating effects of delay on the rights of
          parties and parents in matters involving
          young children.

                              ...

          In this case, the passage of twenty-one
          months since the start of the custody
          application has crystallized temporary
          arrangements into permanent rights, and has
          effectively barred the court from making a
          true decision on the competing interests.

                              ...

          ... absent ill-treatment or abuse by the
          principal care-givers, Molly Ramcharitar and
          Pillalamari Jagam, the inevitable result
          would have been the firm psychological
          attachment of Adrian to Molly and
          Pillalamari, and that is in fact what
          happened.

          Adrian regards them as his parents, their
          home as his home, and while he relates well
          to Susan Ramcharitar, he does not think of
          her as his mother and indeed is not aware of
          his relationship to her. The attachment is
          confirmed by witnesses and by both of the
          professional assessors.

[8]  Based on the evidence before him, including a court-ordered
assessment by Samuel R. Luker, and a report by Dr. Nitza Perlman
who was retained by the adoptive parents, Judge Walmsley
concluded that it was in the best interests of the child to
remain with his aunt and uncle. But he was also firmly of the
view that notwithstanding the pending adoption of Adrian by Molly
and her husband, there should be access by Susan Ramcharitar. He
recited four reasons for giving her access:


          (a)  The mother has a healthy and
               comfortable relationship with the
               child and this relationship poses
               no threat to Adrian's development
               and well-being;

          (b)  Mr. Luker was in favour of
               continuing and broadening access to
               the mother as set out in his
               recommendation #4. He found that
               the custody parents were not
               opposed to access provided it was
               supervised, as they had a concern
               that the mother might abscond with
               the child;

          (c)  Dr. Perlman found that the custody
               parents were strongly in favour of
               Adrian knowing his birth mother but
               worried that if she had free access
               it might interfere with Adrian's
               sense of a secure environment;

          (d)  Finally, it must be borne in mind
               that this is a "relative" adoption.
               The adoption order may make Molly
               Ramcharitar and Pillalamari Jagam
               the legal parents of Adrian. But it
               will also make Susan Ramcharitar
               his aunt and they will all still be
               part of the same extended family.
               Further, the evidence showed that
               were it not for the extended family
               concept and a cultural tradition of
               informal adoption within the
               family, Susan Ramcharitar would not
               have felt able to turn to Molly and
               her husband for the support and
               commitment involved in raising a
               child. Clearly, access in the
               context of a "relative" adoption is
               a very different matter from access
               in the context of a stranger
               adoption. My conclusion is that
               Adrian should continue to enjoy an
               access relationship with his soon-
               to-be aunt. [Emphasis in the
               original]

[9]  In making an order for access, Associate Chief Judge
Walmsley was making an order he knew would survive the adoption
order. It was an order he considered not only jurisprudentially
available, but in fact desirable in the context of these three
adults being related to one another. In his words: "I conclude
that access orders made before a relative adoption survive the
adoption order, and that there exists in Ontario no jurisprudence
to the contrary." He referred to a number of authorities in
support of his conclusion that access and guardianship need not
be inconsistent:  S.K. and T.K. v. M.S.D. (1980), 21 R.F.L. (2d)
271 (Ontario Unified Family Court), Steinburg U.F.C.J.; Catholic
Children's Aid Society of Metropolitan Toronto v. K.H. et al.;
J.H. and M.H. v. K.H. et al. (1986), 6 R.F.L. (3d) 1 Ontario
Provincial Court (Family Division) Main, Provincial Judge; Howard
et al. v. Golding et al. (1993), 38 A.C.W.S. (3d) 1189 (Ont.
Prov. Div.); and Mario Alberto Ramirez P. by his litigation
guardian Brian Weagant and the Catholic Children's Aid Society of
Metropolitan Toronto and Angelika V. (1994), 50 A.C.W.S. (3d) 243
(Ont. Prov. Div.).


[10] If there remained any doubt about whether an access order
could survive an order for adoption, New Brunswick (Minister of
Health and Community Services) v. M.L., a judgment of the Supreme
Court of Canada, delivered October 1, 1998, definitively
articulates the possibility that in exceptional circumstances,
access and adoption orders can co-exist.


[11] Judge Walmsley's decision was appealed both by Susan
Ramcharitar and by the adoptive parents. Efforts at access
arrangements were unsuccessful and a long period of time elapsed
between visits for Susan Ramcharitar. Eventually, however, in an
effort to settle the appeal, Susan agreed to abandon her appeal
on the following bases: (1) Susan Ramcharitar would have access
to Adrian; (2) Molly Ramcharitar and Pillalamari Jagam would
adopt him. In addition, the parties agreed to mediation in order
to assist access arrangements. Minutes of Settlement giving
effect to these terms were signed on December 3, 1996.


[12] The adoption order was made on January 21, 1997.


[13] Although the access arrangements initially appeared to be
adequate, problems emerged by the spring of 1997. Efforts at
mediation were unsuccessful and an application was brought by
Susan seeking unsupervised access, police intervention to enforce
access, and mediation. Molly and her husband sought to have
access terminated. The matter was heard by O'Connor J.


[14] The expert evidence before Justice O'Connor persuaded him
that access to Adrian by Susan Ramcharitar was in the child's
best interests. The expert evidence available to this panel
confirms this conclusion. There is no doubt that the child has a
good relationship with Susan Ramcharitar and that it would be in
his interest to continue a relationship with her. He ordered
overnight access every three weekends, plus an additional day
every second weekend, in accordance with the terms of an
outstanding interim access order. He also ordered mediation and
police intervention to assist in the enforcement of access.


[15] As recently as October 27, 1998, a report from Dr. James
Deutsch, who reviewed all the documents but did not have access
to any of the parties directly, stated:


          The descriptions in Dr. Paulker's report
          suggest that birth mother demonstrated a
          sensitivity and talent for mothering and a
          good-enough relationship based on respect for
          the autonomy and separateness of Adrian. In
          contrast, there are psychological limitations
          described in the adoptive parents, along with
          observations consistent with Adrian
          representing to some extent adoptive parents'
          own aspirations or anxieties regarding
          themselves. There is a clear indication that
          the birth mother is a significant
          relationship figure in the life of Adrian,
          whether or not he fully understands that she
          is his birth mother. The descriptions of
          their interactions would indicate that the
          relationship demonstrated an ease that was
          absent from the custodial relationship and
          was likely quite positive and helpful to this
          boy. [Emphasis added.]

[16] With respect to access, Dr. Deutsch concluded:


          It remains important for Adrian to have
          continuing, predictable access to his birth
          mother. It would be detrimental if this
          access were to be cut off. This would be true
          in both the short term and the long term.

[17] His report also stresses what he views as the obligation on
the part of the adoptive mother to:


          ... address her ambivalence and to provide,
          to the best of her ability, support for this
          clearly valued child to understand and cope
          with his complex and distressing situation.
          His relationship with his biological mother
          will not go away; it cannot be wished away.
          Should its significance be denied or should
          he be deprived of it, there is significant
          risk of later issues of trust due to a sense
          of betrayal and withholding of respect for
          his autonomy.

[18] We agree, therefore, with Justice O'Connor's decision that a
positive relationship exists between Adrian and his biological
mother and that he should continue to enjoy access to her.


[19] Justice O'Connor found Molly Ramcharitar and Pillalamari
Jagam largely responsible for the frustration of access rights.
In his words:


          [The] dispute has been significantly
          contributed to by the respondents. It does
          not lie with them to seek a remedy for an
          alleged harm caused or contributing to by
          them.

[20] There was ample evidence to support this conclusion. As Dr.
Jerome Paulker indicated in his assessment, which was undertaken
pending the appeal from the decision of Associate Chief Judge
Walmsley, the adoptive parents were anxious to terminate access
and were determined to prevent Adrian from having any contact
with his biological mother. They offered no evidentiary
foundation for their desire to prevent access, either of a
physical, psychological, or practical nature.


[21] There is no doubt that Susan Ramcharitar agreed to the
Minutes of Settlement permitting the adoption of Adrian only on
the condition that she be given access. Notwithstanding this
settlement, difficulties with access quickly emerged and it was
clear that the adopting parents had every intention of attempting
to deprive Susan Ramcharitar of access. This intention cannot be
given legal effect. There is overwhelming evidence that access to
Susan is in the child's best interests. What is clearly not in
Adrian's best interests is to have his adoptive parents continue
their preoccupation with thwarting this access.


[22] We do not, however, see any basis for an order of mediation.
Mediation cannot be effective unless both sides are genuinely
open to the process. In the past five years, Molly Ramcharitar
and Pillalamari Jagam have given every indication that they are
not prepared to cooperate with mediators. Accordingly, we think
an imposed process of mediation would impose an undue burden on
Susan Ramcharitar with no likelihood of constructive results.


[23] As to the assistance of the police, this can be a traumatic
intervention for a 6 year-old child. It was requested by Susan
because of the consistent intransigence of Molly Ramcharitar and
Pillalamari Jagam in frustrating rather than facilitating access.
We expect at this stage that it is clear to them that their
obstructive conduct will not be judicially tolerated. To continue
to behave in a way which would leave Susan no alternative but to
invoke outside intervention, would reflect an unwillingness on
their part to act in the child's best interests, a reflection
that would certainly be relevant in any subsequent proceedings.


[24] The amount of access should be once every four weekends from
Friday at 6:00 p.m. until Sunday at 6:00 p.m.  The respondent
indicated her willingness to pick up and return the child.


[25] Accordingly, the order of O'Connor J. is to be varied by an
order directing that access take place once every four weekends.
The order for mediation and police intervention are set aside.
The appeal is otherwise dismissed with costs.
