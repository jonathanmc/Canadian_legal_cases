OSBORNE J.A.:



[1]  The appellant was charged with first degree murder and
convicted of second degree murder by the verdict of a jury at a
trial presided over by the Honourable Mr. Justice Meehan. He was
sentenced to imprisonment for life with a parole ineligibility
period of 10 years. He appeals his conviction. His appeal, for
the most part, centres on errors that the appellant contends were
made in the trial judge’s charge to the jury.


THE EVIDENCE


     (i)  The Shooting


[2]  On March 27, 1992, the appellant lived on the 7th floor of
an apartment building in the City of London. At about 9:30 a.m.
on March 27th he visited his friend Bill Semple who lived on the
11th floor of the same building. He spent the day in Semple’s
apartment playing cards and drinking with Semple and others who
dropped in for a visit. The appellant’s card-playing and drinking
were interrupted on two occasions when he left to obtain more
beer. In the morning, the appellant went to the beer store with
Semple and in the afternoon with Fred Howard. The appellant and
Semple were good friends who often drank together. The appellant
had met Howard for the first time on March 26, 1992.


[3]  During the afternoon of March 27, 1992, Rene Fournier, a
friend of Semple’s, came to Semple’s apartment. The appellant
knew Fournier, having met him on a few previous occasions. At
first the appellant and Fournier played cribbage while Howard
looked on. Semple was on a couch facing the table where the
appellant was playing cards.


[4]  In due course, Howard said that he wanted to play two-handed
cribbage with Fournier, however, the appellant said that he
wanted to play three-handed cribbage. When Howard would not
permit the appellant to play, the appellant slammed his fist on
the table and called Howard a “four-eyed crazy little sucker”,
and Fournier a “fat French pig.”  Fournier took offence and
slapped the appellant with his open hand. It was not a hard slap.
The appellant taunted Fournier by saying, “Is that the best
you’ve got?”  Fournier then slapped the appellant again with an
open hand. Like the first slap, this slap was not hard. The slaps
did not appear to have any particular physical effect on the
appellant.


[5]  Shortly after the second slap the appellant threatened to
kill Fournier. He went down four floors to his own apartment on
the 7th floor and removed his 22-calibre semi-automatic rifle
from under the sofa in his living room. He retrieved a box of
ammunition which was located in a tin on a window ledge. He
withdrew the magazine holder from the rifle and loaded 15 rounds
of ammunition.


[6]  Within five to ten minutes the appellant returned to
Semple’s 11th floor apartment. He entered the living room
carrying his rifle at waist level on his right side. When he
arrived Howard and Fournier were playing cards. The appellant
fired eight rounds in succession at Fournier. Two bullets struck
Fournier in his forehead, one in his neck and four in his lower
mid-chest and abdomen. The eighth shot fired missed Fournier and
hit a wall. Fournier died almost instantly.


[7]  The appellant sat down at a table while Howard was in the
process of telephoning the police. After speaking with the police
Howard left Semple’s apartment to wait for the police by the
elevator. The police and ambulance attendants arrived within
three or four minutes of Howard’s phone call.


[8]  The appellant left the apartment before the police arrived.
He went to Lino Toffolon’s apartment on the 10th floor where he
used Toffolon’s telephone to call directory assistance. Linda
Ogletree, the operator to whom the appellant spoke, testified
that the appellant said, “I need my lawyer. Get me my lawyer.
I’ve just shot someone. I need my lawyer.”  The appellant waited
on the line while Ms. Ogletree attempted to contact the lawyer
that the appellant had identified. When she could not make
contact after four or five rings, Ms. Ogletree offered to call
the police. The appellant said that the police were already on
their way and hung up the phone.


[9]  Bill Bier, an 8th floor tenant in the appellant’s apartment
building, testified that after the shooting the appellant went to
his apartment to use the telephone. He said that the appellant
opened the apartment door, put his index finger to his lips and
said, “Shh, I just shot a man. I want to use your phone.”  Bier
had no trouble understanding the appellant. He said that the
appellant appeared to be in a quandary but otherwise appeared
calm and concerned. Bier said that the appellant made a number of
telephone calls in which Bier overheard him say, “Can you help
me?” and “I shot a man.”  The appellant took a can of beer with
him when he left Bier’s apartment.


[10] At about 5:45 p.m., the appellant telephoned a community
dental student who had done some dental work for him. The dental
student’s roommate, James Takacs, answered the phone. Takacs
testified that during the phone call the appellant said:
          
          … “Hey, man, you’re not going to believe
          this, but I just blew this guy’s head right
          off … I’m not shitting you” … He said he
          grabbed his shotgun and pumped eight rounds…
          
          He said “he was a six foot four 300 pound
          motherfucker and there was no way to get to
          him” … He was just too big and he had no
          choice, so [he]went and got his shotgun and
          shot him…
          
          He described how “there was blood, coming out
          of his nose and there was blood all over the
          place… It’s kinda funny, you know, how much
          blood there is in someone.”
          
          

[11] The appellant then asked Takacs to call his lawyer. When
Takacs said that he did not know who the appellant’s lawyer was,
the appellant became angry and threatened him. Takacs said the
appellant’s mood seemed to change from being in control to being
agitated.


[12] At 6:06 p.m., the appellant telephoned the London Police and
said that he, “fucking blew the guy’s head off.”  During the
telephone conversation he identified himself to Constable Morton
and told Constable Morton what his home address was. Constable
Morton told the appellant to meet the police in front of the
apartment building. Constable Morton in turn advised the police
at the scene to expect the appellant’s arrival and he further
told them that the appellant was drunk.


[13] The police were dispatched to Semple’s apartment at 5:32
p.m. and arrived at 5:35 p.m. with ambulance personnel. When they
arrived they found the deceased sitting on a chair with blood
flowing from his ears, nose and abdomen. He had no vital signs.
The deceased was removed from the apartment by the ambulance
attendants shortly after their arrival.


[14] After making his phone calls the appellant returned to his
own apartment and picked up his jacket. He locked his door
because he knew the police would be there shortly. He then
returned to Semple’s apartment carrying an open beer. After the
appellant knocked on the door, a police officer opened the door
to see the appellant wearing his jacket and holding a beer. The
police immediately arrested the appellant. When the police asked
him where the gun was, he directed them toward the sofa. The
police quickly located the rifle under the sofa.


[15] When two police officers escorted the appellant out of the
apartment, as they approached the elevator, the appellant stated,
“I really fucked up this time; I really fucked up.”  As they went
through the lobby of the apartment building the appellant shouted
to another tenant who he knew, “I shot him Ken, I shot him.” The
tenant to whom the appellant spoke said that the appellant
appeared drunk but not as drunk as he had seen him on other
occasions. When he walked through the members of the media who
had gathered outside the apartment building, the appellant told
them to get out of the way, “… or I’ll blow your heads off.”


[16] The appellant testified he had no memory of the events of
March 27, 1992 after about noon until he found himself in the
police cruiser. He recalled seeing blood coming out of the
deceased’s mouth and of hearing the deceased making a gurgling
sound after he was shot. He also recalled seeing Howard on the
telephone. He said he had no memory of the various telephone
calls he made after the shooting.


     (ii) The Appellant’s Circumstances


[17] The appellant was 40 years old at the time of the shooting.
In 1987 he had sustained a head injury resulting in left temporal
and left frontal lobe damage. His neurological injuries resulted
in the onset of epilepsy and the development of a personality
disorder which left him impatient, impulsive and quick to anger.
He received a disability pension because of his injuries. He had
prescriptions for medication to control his epileptic seizures
and depression. In addition, he was an episodic alcoholic.


     (iii)     Alcohol Consumption


[18] A number of witnesses who observed the appellant before and
after the shooting testified that they knew that the appellant
had been drinking because they could smell alcohol on his breath
and because of the appellant’s sometimes slurred speech. However,
some of these witnesses testified that the appellant was calm,
lucid and exhibited no difficulty moving around, walking at a
normal pace or getting into the back of the police cruiser.


[19] At the request of the defence, Constable Walsh performed
breathalyzer tests on the appellant at 8:14 p.m. and 8:36 p.m. –
about three hours after the shooting. The breathalyzer readings
were .241 and .250 mg. of alcohol per 100 ml. of blood. Soon
after the appellant was in custody, he was taken to hospital
where a number of tests, including blood tests, were done. The
blood tests showed blood-alcohol levels of between .200 and .220
mg. of alcohol per 100 ml. of blood. These tests were done
sometime after 1:49 a.m. and before 3:40 a.m., on March 28, 1992.


[20] Based upon the breathalyzer and blood test results Dr.
Hirst, a toxicologist and pharmacologist called by the defence,
estimated that the appellant would have had a blood-alcohol
reading in the range of .267 to .311 mg. of alcohol per 100 ml.
of blood at the time of the shooting. He based his assessment on
a number of assumptions including the assumption the applicable
elimination rate was that of an average person. Dr. Hirst
testified that anyone with a blood-alcohol concentration of .267
to .311 would demonstrate difficulties with gross motor skills
such as balance and locomotion; he would lethargic, slow and
sedated, exhibiting problems with speech, memory, judgment and
concentration. He acknowledged that fine motor skills would be
compromised before gross motor skills.


[21] The appellant’s brother, Ernest Rathwell, testified that it
would not be unusual for the appellant to consume a case of beer
in one day. He also said that after the appellant's head injury
in 1987, his drinking became more problematic. Daniel Rathwell,
another brother, testified that the appellant drank beer, “like
it was water.”  The appellant acknowledged having a serious
drinking problem and that he was aware of the adverse effects of
mixing alcohol with the various medications that he had been
prescribed.
          
               (iv) Brain Damage


[22] Dr. Young, a neurologist, testified that the appellant had
suffered injury to his brain’s frontal and temporal lobes. The
brain damage caused the appellant’s epileptic seizures and may
have affected his ability to form long term goals, his memory and
his impulse control. His diminished impulse control left the
appellant restless and easily angered. When alcohol was involved
and the appellant became angered, he could become physically
violent.


[23] Dr. Young and Dr. Boyd, a psychiatrist, both testified to
having witnessed the appellant lose control as a result of a
minimal triggering event such as having to wait to see the
doctor. Both doctors observed obvious physical symptoms of the
appellant’s loss of control:  the appellant was red, visibly
upset and obviously angry. The more severe the appellant’s rage,
the more striking the symptoms would be. Those who saw the
appellant at the time of, or immediately after the shooting did
not describe the physical symptoms referred to by Drs. Young and
Boyd.
          
               (v)  The Appellant’s Medication


[24] Evidence, including expert evidence, was led showing that at
the time of the shooting, Thomas Rathwell, due to brain injuries,
was required to take and did take most, if not all of the
following prescribed drugs:
          (a)  Phenobarbital – 130 mg. per day
          (b)  Fluoxetine (Prozak) – 1 tablet per day
          (c)  Phenetoin (Dilantin) – 400 mg. per day
          (d)  Haloperidol – 2 tablets every 4 hours as needed
          (e)  Thioridazine – 3 to 4 tablets every 4 hours as needed
          (f)  Tylenol – as needed
          (g)  Anacin – as needed
          

[25] Dr. Hirst said in an average person the combination of
alcohol and the drugs that the appellant took, at least from time
to time, would add to the effects of alcohol in the sense that
one would see a further functional depression of thought
processes and motor skills. He noted, however, that continued use
of the prescribed medication would produce a tolerance to the
medication, specifically with respect to the depressive effect of
the medication.


THE GROUNDS OF APPEAL


[26] The appellant advances seven grounds of appeal. Six of them
centre on alleged misdirections in the charge on the intent
required for murder having regard to the appellant’s
intoxication, his brain damage, and the combined effect of
alcohol, drugs, brain damage and the deceased’s allegedly
provocative acts. The appellant also takes issue with the trial
judge’s charge on provocation.


[27] The appellant’s seventh ground of appeal relates to the
jury’s request to be provided with a dictionary. This request was
not framed as a question to the trial judge about access to a
dictionary. Rather, the request was made by a juror to a court
constable who was responsible for the jury during its
deliberations. As it turned out, the court official involved told
the jury that a dictionary would not be forthcoming.


[28] Since the appellant’s central submissions relate to the
charge on the intent required for murder, I will address that
general issue first. In doing so I will deal with aspects of the
intent issue, including intoxication (by alcohol and drugs), the
effect of the appellant’s 1987 brain injury and the combined
effect of alcohol, drugs, brain damage and provocation on the
issue whether the appellant formed the intent required for
murder. Since provocation presents a separate and distinct route
to a manslaughter verdict I will deal with it after I have dealt
with the issue of the general intent required for murder.


ANALYSIS


     (i)  The Intent Required for Murder


[29] The appellant submits that the charge on the issue of the
intent required for murder was confusing and that it would have
left the jury in doubt about the onus of proof with respect to it
and cause the jury to infer that the appellant had the intent
required for murder from the fact that he shot the deceased.


[30] In my opinion, the charge was fair and balanced. The
appellant’s experienced trial counsel (not Mr. Hughes) had no
objection on any aspect of the charge with respect to the issue
of the intent required for murder. Indeed, the appellant’s trial
counsel had only one objection to the charge and that objection
concerned the charge on first degree murder.1


[31] Near the beginning of his charge, after the trial judge had
instructed the jury that they were responsible for making
findings of fact and after he had generally reviewed what direct,
circumstantial and expert evidence were, the trial judge dealt
with the issue of identity, that is who killed  Rene Fournier.
That made sense in the circumstances, since the defence had
raised the identity issue, based to a large measure on the
appellant’s evidence that he had no memory of shooting the
appellant. In any case, evidence the appellant caused Fournier’s
death is overwhelming and the appellant conceded for the purposes
of this appeal that the fact that the appellant shot and killed
Fournier is not disputed.


[32] The trial judge then, in a very general way, alerted the
jury to the position(s) of the defence. He said:
          
          During the course of this trial you have
          heard two theories of defence. One of those
          theories of defence is drunkenness and
          intoxication and the other is provocation.
          You may return a verdict of not guilty if
          some of you have a reasonable doubt on the
          defence theory of intoxication and some of
          you have a reasonable doubt on the defence
          theory of provocation, providing that all of
          you unanimously have a reasonable doubt as to
          the guilt of the accused in regard to the
          specific charge of murder.
          
          In other words you do not have to be
          unanimous on any single defence theory but
          you have to be unanimous that there is a
          reasonable doubt as to the guilt of the
          accused in relation to the commission of
          murder.
          
          

[33] The trial judge then instructed the jury on the meaning of
homicide, culpable homicide, second degree murder and first
degree murder. I see nothing confusing about this part of the
charge. Nor, in my view, did the trial judge err in this part of
the charge. When he advised the jury of what constituted second
degree murder, the trial judge told the jury what the intent
required for murder was. The jury would have clearly understood
from the outset that the Crown was required to prove the guilt of
the appellant beyond a reasonable doubt.


[34] It was against the background of this overview that the
trial judge returned to the issue of the intent required for
murder and how the Crown might prove that intent. In this part of
the charge the trial judge referred to the “sane sober person”
common sense inference of intent:
          
          Thus, in deciding whether Thomas Rathwell
          intended or meant to cause the death of Mr.
          Fournier, you are entitled, as a matter of
          common sense, to draw an inference that a
          sane and sober person – about which I will
          have more to say later – intends the natural
          and probable consequence of his or her
          voluntary act.
          
          If you decide that Mr. Fournier’s death would
          be a natural consequence of the actions of
          Mr. Rathwell, you are entitled to conclude
          that he intended to kill him by those
          actions. However, you are not required to
          make this conclusion. You may decide that he
          did not intend to kill him, even though that
          death was a natural consequence of his
          action.
          
          In the end, you will have to consider all of
          the surrounding circumstances including what
          the accused said and did, in order to decide
          whether the Crown has proved Mr. Rathwell did
          indeed mean to cause the death of Mr.
          Fournier.
          
          Before you can find that the accused had the
          necessary criminal intent, you must be
          satisfied beyond a reasonable doubt that this
          intent was the only reasonable inference to
          be drawn from the proven facts. Remember that
          the question for you to decide is what
          Rathwell in fact intended. [Emphasis added.]
          
          

[35] Throughout the charge, the trial judge emphasized that the
jury must consider “all the surrounding circumstances” in
determining whether the Crown had proved beyond a reasonable
doubt that the appellant had the intent required for murder.


[36] The trial judge made it clear that before the jury
considered first degree murder, they had to be satisfied that the
Crown had established all of the elements of second degree murder
beyond a reasonable doubt. In the circumstances, the cardinal
element of murder that was in issue was the intent required for
murder.


[37] After he dealt with provocation (an issue to which I will
return), the trial judge charged the jury on what he
characterized as the “defence of intoxication.”  He did so by
following a two-step process. First he told the jury that they
had to be satisfied beyond a reasonable doubt that the appellant
had the capacity to form the intent required for murder having
regard to his ingestion of alcohol and second he said that the
jury had to decide whether the appellant in fact formed the
intent required for murder.2  Notwithstanding his references to
the appellant’s capacity to form the intent required for murder,
the trial judge made it clear that the critical question for the
jury was whether the appellant had the intent required for
murder. He repeatedly emphasized that the jury must consider
evidence of intoxication and all other evidence relevant to the
issue of intent. The “other evidence” included evidence of the
appellant’s brain injury, his ingestion of drugs and the acts the
appellant relied upon in support of his provocation defence. In
dealing with the rolled-up effect of the evidence in relation to
the issue of intent, the trial judge said in his charge:
          
          On the evidence you could find that because
          of a combination of the physical injury,
          alcohol and drugs, that the accused is more
          easily provoked and that he does not have the
          same control mechanism to restrain his
          emotions after drinking.
          
          …
          
          Now, if you believe or are left with a
          reasonable doubt that as a result of the
          alcohol, the phenobarbital, and his mental
          condition, he did not actually have the
          intent to kill due to this combination of
          factors, you must find him not guilty of the
          offence of murder. You would then, in the
          circumstances, if you are satisfied that he
          is the person who did the shooting, you would
          find him guilty of the lesser, included
          offence of manslaughter.
          
          

[38] Charges would generally improve if the sane sober person
boilerplate were eliminated, or at least adjusted to the
particular circumstances. In a case like this, if there is to be
a reference to the sane, sober person, the jury should be told
that the inference that a person intends the natural and probable
consequences of his acts applies only when the person being
considered is sane and sober, and, in the circumstances of this
case, in possession of otherwise intact mental faculties. In
addition, the jury should be told (as the jury was here) that all
of the evidence bearing upon the issue of intent should be
considered when the jury deals with the intent required for
murder.


[39] The charge would have been better had the trial judge
approached the common sense inference that a sane and sober
person intends the natural and probable consequences of his act
from a somewhat different perspective since, in this case, the
appellant was manifestly not sober, and his mental capacity was
in issue. 3  Thus, the sane sober person analogy did not apply to
the appellant. However, I think that the jury would have
understood that what had to be determined was the intent of the
appellant in light of his consumption of alcohol and prescription
drugs and his brain injury.  Put another way, I think that the
trial judge made it clear to the jury that the appellant was not
a sane and sober person at the time of the shooting. In addition, he
instructed the jury that the combination of the appellant’s
mental disorder, and evidence of intoxication, drug consumption
and provocation, though falling short of insanity or provocation
in law may nevertheless negate the specific intent to commit
murder and thus render manslaughter the appropriate verdict.


[40] In addition, it would have been better had the trial judge
avoided language isolating intoxication as a “defence.”  The
defence was that the appellant did not have the intent required
for murder. Some of the evidence that was relevant to that issue
was evidence suggesting that the appellant was intoxicated.
Indeed, by any normal measurement, his ingestion of alcohol was
enormous given his blood-alcohol concentration readings. The
issue in this case was not so much whether the appellant was
intoxicated, but rather whether his consumption of beer, when
taken with the other factors relevant to the issue of intent,
gave rise to a reasonable doubt on the issue whether the
appellant intended to kill Fournier (s. 229(a)(i)), or whether he
intended to cause injury to Fournier, by shooting him, and knew
that was likely to cause death (s. 229(a)(ii)). As I have said,
the charge on the issue of intent left no doubt that the Crown
had to prove beyond a reasonable doubt that the appellant had the
intent required for murder. There was evidence from which the
jury could infer that the appellant did have the required intent
notwithstanding his consumption of alcohol and drugs and his
brain damage. I see no reversible error in the charge on the
issue of intent.


[41] The appellant also submits that the trial judge’s charge
suggested that there was a different intent required for second
degree murder than for first degree murder. I see no merit in
this submission. The trial judge correctly instructed the jury on
the elements of murder as set out in s. 229(a) of the Criminal
Code and provided a copy of the relevant section (and other
relevant sections) for the jury to consider during the charge and
in their deliberations. The trial judge repeatedly told the jury
that in order to find the appellant guilty of murder they must be
satisfied beyond a reasonable doubt that the appellant meant to
cause Fournier’s death or that he meant to cause Fournier bodily
harm that he knew was likely to cause death and was reckless
whether or not death ensued. He further instructed the jury that
if they found that the Crown had proved all the elements of
murder beyond a reasonable doubt the jury was to go on and
consider whether the murder had been planned and deliberate so as
to classify it as first degree murder. In my view, the charge was
clear in explaining that the only difference between first and
second degree murder was the additional requirement for first
degree murder that the murder be planned and deliberate.


     (ii) Provocation


[42] The trial judge dealt with provocation relatively early in
his charge. In addition to providing the jury with a copy of s.
232 of the Criminal Code, the trial judge said by way of
overview:
          
          In this trial there is evidence before you
          indicating that on the date in question the
          accused was provoked into committing the
          offence of murder. I will discuss with you
          how the defence of provocation may reduce the
          offence of murder to the offence of
          manslaughter.
          
          The law provides that a killing which would
          otherwise be murder becomes manslaughter
          where the defence of provocation applies,
          even though the person who caused the death
          intended to do so. …
          
          I wish to emphasize that the defence of
          provocation applies only to murder. It only
          reduces the offence of murder to the lesser
          offence of manslaughter.
          
          
[43] In my view, the trial judge’s charge on provocation, as set
out above, is clear and correct. When the trial judge directed
the jury on the objective part of the provocation defence, he
said:
          
          The second ingredient is that the wrongful
          act or insult must be of such a nature as to
          be sufficient to deprive an ordinary person
          of the power of self-control. The reason for
          requiring the wrongful act or insult to be
          sufficient to deprive the ordinary person of
          the power of self-control is to avoid the
          injustice of allowing a person who is
          exceptionally excitable, quarrelsome or ill-
          tempered to rely on the defence of
          provocation. The objective, ordinary person
          avoids that possibility. However, you should
          use your common sense in attributing to the
          ordinary person any general characteristics
          such as sex, age or race which may be
          relevant to the provocation question. You
          must ask yourself whether the wrongful act or
          insult was of such a nature to be sufficient
          to deprive an ordinary person in the same
          situation or circumstances as the accused, of
          the power of self-control. If the answer is
          “no”, the provocation defence does not apply.
          If the answer is “yes”, you must consider the
          third ingredient of the provocation defence.
          [Emphasis added.]
          
          

[44] Later in his charge the trial judge emphasized that the jury
should consider all of the circumstances, including the
appellant’s personal characteristics, in determining whether the
appellant had lost his power of self control in response to an
unlawful act or insult. He reviewed the evidence of the allegedly
provocative acts in this part of his charge. He reminded the jury
of the medical evidence and the evidence of the appellant’s two
brothers that the appellant was a person who had a low patience
threshold. He said:
          
          If you decide that he was actually provoked,
          you must decide one final thing – was the
          provocation sudden and did the accused act
          upon it suddenly and before there was time
          for his passion to cool.
          
          The insult or wrongful act and the accused’s
          response to it must be sudden. Sudden
          provocation means the wrongful act or insult
          strikes upon a mind unprepared for it and
          sets the passion aflame.
          
          Now, in regard to those third and fourth
          ingredients, about whether the accused was
          actually provoked and whether it was sudden,
          you must look into the mind of the accused
          and assess what actually happened there.
          
          We have here a great deal of evidence as to
          the mental condition of the accused, his
          temperament, his behaviour when he has been
          drinking. All those matters are to be taken
          into consideration by you in deciding whether
          the accused was actually provoked and acted
          upon that provocation suddenly and before
          there was time for his passion to cool.
          
          There is, as well, in this particular case,
          special direction which deals with the
          cumulative effect on an ordinary person of
          provocation/ drunkenness in this type of
          offence, where you must take into
          consideration the fact that it may well be
          that people who are under the influence of
          alcohol are more easily provoked than they
          would be when they were sober.
          
          

[45] The trial judge then reviewed the evidence of Drs. Young and
Boyd who described the appellant as being “easily provoked” due
to his brain injury. They also stated that alcohol, as well as
the prescription drugs the appellant was taking, would exacerbate
that propensity. In a form of summary the trial judge said:
          
          On the evidence you could find that because
          of a combination of the physical injury,
          alcohol and drugs, that the accused is more
          easily provoked and that he does not have the
          same control mechanism to restrain his
          emotions after drinking.
          
          

[46] In my opinion, the charge on provocation was satisfactory.
The trial judge set out the applicable law and reviewed the
relevant evidence to a sufficient degree. In the end, he made it
clear to the jury that if provocation applied the jury must
reduce what would otherwise be murder to manslaughter.


[47] I acknowledge that near the end of his charge on provocation
the trial judge’s instructions to the jury were somewhat
confusing because he told the jury that if  the evidence in
support of provocation did not give rise to a reasonable doubt “…
you must still go on to consider whether on the basis of all of
the evidence the accused had the necessary intent for murder.”
This direction was obviously part of the “rolled-up” charge in
which the jury was directed to consider all of the circumstances,
including intoxication, drugs, brain injury and the provocative
acts to which the appellant was exposed when they dealt with the
intent required for murder. Nonetheless, it would have been
better had the trial judge made it clear, even in this part of
the charge, that evidence relevant to provocation should first be
considered by the jury when the jury decided whether the Crown
had proved the intent required for murder beyond a reasonable
doubt. The trial judge did this satisfactorily in the rolled-up
charge, however, he did not go on to make it abundantly clear
that the defence of provocation had to be considered if the jury
decided beyond a reasonable doubt that the appellant had the
intent required for murder. As I have earlier said when he first
dealt with provocation the trial judge made this proposition
abundantly clear. I am satisfied that any confusion arising from
the infusion of provocation into the rolled-up charge occasioned
no substantial wrong or miscarriage of justice.


[48] I see no merit in the appellant’s submission as to the
inadequacy of the charge on provocation.


     (iii)     The Dictionary


[49] During the jury’s deliberations a juror asked one of the
court constables for a dictionary. Someone notified the trial
judge, who was not then in court, of this request. The trial
judge advised the person who advised him of the dictionary
request that it was not proper to provide any books to the jury.
Somehow the dictionary request matter was brought to the
attention of the Crown Attorney who raised the issue in open
court to ensure that the jury’s request was considered in the
presence of the accused and to determine whether any further
action was required.


[50] In the course of the discussion of this issue in open court,
Crown counsel suggested that the jury could be told that if they
had any difficulty with any particular word they could submit a
question asking for a definition. The defence position was that
the jury were properly advised that they could not have a
dictionary, however, the defence complained that the trial judge
ought to have advised counsel of the jury’s request in the
presence of the appellant. Defence counsel did not accept the
Crown’s suggestion that the matter should be pursued further with
the jury. He said, “to that extent, with due respect, I think
maybe we should have been told. I am not saying anything further
or raising anything more about it.”


[51] It appears to me that the matter was not fully pursued
because of defence counsel’s position and in part because the
trial judge somehow took it that Crown counsel was making a
personal attack on him for having somewhat informally advised the
constable involved to tell the jury that they could not have a
dictionary. In any case, the appellant does not take issue with
the decision of the trial judge but now suggests that the request
for a dictionary may reflect “confusion” within the jury.


[52] There is nothing in the record to indicate that the jury was
confused and it would make no sense now to speculate about why
one or more of the jurors wanted a dictionary. This jury clearly
understood that they could ask questions.4  It seems to me that
if the jury  continued to be troubled by the meaning of a word,
or words, the jury would have simply asked the trial judge for
assistance. That did not happen. I would, therefore, not give
effect to this ground of appeal.


CONCLUSION


[56] For these reasons, I would dismiss the appeal.
