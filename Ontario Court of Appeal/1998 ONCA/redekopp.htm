                      ENDORSEMENT
                              
[1]   After  a  trial before Ouellette J. and a jury,  the  three
appellants were convicted of conspiring to import marijuana  into
Canada  and of having possession of marijuana for the purpose  of
trafficking.  They were sentenced to seven years  for  the  first
offence  and five years concurrent  for the second offence.  They
appeal their sentences on three grounds.


[2]  First, the appellants submit that the sentencing judge erred
in  accepting the evidence of Peter Friesen on the extent of  the
conspiracy.  Friesen  testified  that  the  involvement  of   the
appellants  Bartsch and Redekopp in the conspiracy  extended  not
just  to  the  delivery of marijuana in July 1995,  but  to  four
previous deliveries.


[3]   Although  the trial judge quite properly gave  the  jury  a
Vetrovec warning concerning Friesen’s evidence, he did not err in
accepting that evidence for the purpose of finding the  facts  on
sentencing. Moreover, the independent items of evidence  referred
to  at  paragraph  26  of the Crown’s factum confirmed  Friesen’s
evidence.   The   sentencing  judge’s  finding   concerning   the
involvement of Bartsch and Redekopp in the conspiracy  was  amply
supported  by  the  evidence and therefore was not  unreasonable.
This ground of appeal fails.


[4]   Second,  the  appellants submit that the  sentencing  judge
erred  in relying on the prevalence of this kind of crime in  the
Windsor  area.  We find no merit in this ground  of  appeal.  The
prevalence  of  a particular kind of crime in a  community  is  a
relevant  consideration on sentencing: see Sears1, a decision  of
this  court. Here, the evidence of Constable Avery at  trial  and
the  evidence of the customs officer James Johnston on sentencing
supported the trial judge’s finding.


[5]  Finally, the appellants submit that a seven-year sentence is
outside an acceptable range. We reject this submission for the
appellants Bartsch and Redekopp. This was a sophisticated large-
scale commercial operation for profit. The last delivery alone
had a value of between $500,000 and $1,500,000. The sentences for
Bartsch and Redekopp are not demonstrably unfit.


[6]  We take a different view, however, of the sentence for the
appellant Penner. In our opinion, the sentencing judge erred in
principle by giving Penner the same sentence as the other two
appellants. Penner was a first offender; he joined the conspiracy
only at the end; and he expressed some remorse. The Crown
acknowledges that Penner’s sentence should be reduced. In our
view, it should be reduced to four years.


[7]  For these reasons, the appeal of the appellant Penner is
allowed and his sentence is varied to one of four years. The
sentence appeals of the other two appellants, Bartsch and
Redekopp, are dismissed.
