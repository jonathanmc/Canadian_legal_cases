                     ENDORSEMENT

[1]  The appellant has worked for the respondent for over 25
years. She sued the respondent claiming that she was entitled to
compensation under a plan instituted by the respondent for the
compensation of employees who had made work-related suggestions
which were implemented by the respondent. The action proceeded
under the simplified procedure provided for in Rule 76.


[2]  The appellant contended that in November 1989 she made a
suggestion directed at reducing the costs associated with the
installation, disconnection and repair of a certain type of
telephone circuit. She was involved in this area in the course of
her employment and was familiar with the problems associated with
this type of telephone circuit. The appellant claimed that the
suggestion she made in November 1989 was eventually implemented
by the respondent in 1994 and resulted in substantial savings for
the respondent.


[3]  The respondent's position was that the procedures
implemented in 1994 had been suggested by other employees before
the appellant made her suggestion and that the suggestions made
by the other employees were under active investigation and
consideration by the respondent when the appellant made her
suggestion in November 1989. Under the terms of the compensation
plan, an employee is not entitled to compensation if a proposal
put forward is under active consideration by the company at the
time the employee made the suggestion. The respondent also
contended that it had awarded compensation under the plan to two
other employees whose suggestions had preceded that made by the
appellant. Finally, the respondent alleged that the appellant's
claim had been fully and impartially investigated under the
procedures set up by the respondent.


[4]  Both parties moved for summary judgment under Rule 76.06(1).
The respondent relied on various documents which on their face
established that the proposal put forward by the respondent was
under evaluation by the respondent in the summer of 1989. The
respondent also relied on the affidavit of a Bell manager who
swore that the documents accurately reflected what had happened.


[5]  The appellant filed an affidavit. She contended that the
respondent's documents had been altered to make it appear as
though her proposal had been under consideration before she made
her suggestion in November 1989. It was her position that the
other proposals referred to in the documents had been under
consideration prior to that date but that references to her
proposal was added to those documents at some point after
November 1989 to make it appear as though her proposal was under
consideration before November 1989. The appellant also filed
affidavits of two Bell employees who indicated that the solution
adopted by the company in 1994 employed the ideas contained in
the appellant's November 1989 suggestion. These employees had no
knowledge of whether the idea put forward by the appellant were
under investigation by the respondent prior to November 1989.


[6]  Rule 76.06(14) provides that a motion for summary judgment
should be granted unless:


          the judge is unable to decide the issues in
          the action in the absence of cross-examination; or


          it would be otherwise unjust to decide the
          issues on the motion.


[7]  A bald assertion that the documents relied on by the
respondent had been altered and do not reflect the events as they
actually occurred would not justify a refusal to grant summary
judgment under Rule 76.06(1). However, in this case, there is
more than a bald assertion. The appellant made the following
points in the course of her argument:


     (1)  The documents relied on by the respondent contain
     errors, discrepancies and inconsistencies which could lend
     credence to the appellant's contention that the documents
     were altered to "backdate" the respondent's consideration of
     the proposal which was eventually adopted in 1994. In
     particular, irregularities in the document dated July 4,
     1989 suggest that the references to the proposed solution
     identified as "alternative IV" were added to the document
     some time after the rest of the document was prepared.
     Alternative IV was the option eventually acted upon by the
     company in 1994 and is, according to the appellant,
     identical to the suggestion she put forward in November
     1989.
     (2)  The respondent's initial reaction to the suggestion
     made by the appellant in November 1989 was inconsistent with
     the position the respondent now takes. The suggestion was
     accepted for evaluation and was not rejected for quite some
     time. If, as the respondent now contends, that suggestion
     contained a proposal which was already under active
     evaluation by the respondent, the suggestion should have
     been rejected immediately as ineligible for compensation.
     (3)  There was evidence that the proposal contained in the
     appellant's suggestion was the proposal eventually adopted
     by the respondent to solve the problem.


[8]  None of these arguments establish the appellant's claim.
They do, however, raise valid questions of fact which must be
answered in order to make a proper determination of the merits of
the appellant's claim. A proper determination of these factual
questions cannot be made without cross-examination of the
appellant and those involved in the creation of the documents
relied on by the respondent.


[9]  On this record, neither party should have been granted
summary judgment under Rule 76.06(1). The appeal is allowed, the
judgment dismissing the appellant's action is set aside and an
order will go directing that the matter proceed to trial under
Rule 76.
