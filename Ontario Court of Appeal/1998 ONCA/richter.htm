                      ENDORSEMENT
                              
                             
[1]  The appellant sued for damages in the sum of U.S, $
1,758,000.00. It claimed these moneys were due August 11, 1994,
which was the settlement date upon the termination of an interest
rate currency swap transaction (contract) it had entered into
with the respondent.

[2]  By its terms, the contract would have terminated on August
11, 1994 because on that date the appellant was insolvent.
However, on that date, the appellant obtained what is referred to
as a stay order from Mr. Justice Houlden. The order was made
pursuant to the provisions of the Companies Creditors Arrangement
Act, R.S.C. 1985, c. C-36. The order restrained all counter-
parties, and that included the respondent, from terminating a
swap contract with the appellant without the appellant=s written
consent or the order of the court. But, relevant to this action,
the stay order specifically set out the procedures by which such
a contract might be terminated by the respondent after having
been given the opportunity to elect to do so by the appellant, or
by the appellant its self. The stay order provided:


               Sub-paragraph 22(b) Y Treasury may Y
          send to the relevant counter-party a notice
          providing such counter-party with the
          opportunity to terminate all of the
          Derivative Contracts between such Counter-
          party and Treasury upon making payment of the
          settlement amount owing thereunder forthwith
          to TreasuryY In the event that the other
          party to such Derivative Contract does not
          chose to exercise that right subsequent to
          service of such notice and within three days
          of such service, Treasury may assign its
          rights and obligations under all of its
          Derivative Contracts with the counter-partyY.
          
               Sub- paragraph 22(c) Y notwithstanding
          any other provision of this paragraph 22,
          Treasury shall be entitled to enforce any of
          its rights under any Derivative Contract to
          terminate such contract and to become
          entitled to any payment pursuant to such
          contract as a defaulting party.
                

[3]  On August 15, 1994, pursuant to sub-paragraph 22(b), the
appellant gave the respondent notice that it had the opportunity
to terminate the contract and pay any settlement due, failing
which the appellant might assign it rights and obligations under
the contract to an assignee of designated credit rating.

[4]  There was an issue on the pleadings and in the evidence
whether the respondent had elected to terminate the contract
pursuant to this notice.

[5]  On December 12, 1994, about four months after the appellant
had served the respondent with the notice pursuant to sub-
paragraph 22(b), the appellant served the respondent with a
notice said to be under sub-paragraph 22(c). By that notice, the
appellant purported to terminate the contract, with the
settlement date August 1994, notwithstanding interest changes in
the interim.

[6]  The evidence was that between the dates in August and
December interest rates had change greatly to the advantage to
the respondents.

[7]  The case was not tried in the usual way. The trial judge
heard no viva vocia evidence and was not called upon to make
findings of credibility. Commendably, counsel had placed before
the trial judge the relevant documents and passages from the
examinations for discovery as the evidence upon which the case
was to be decided.

[8]  As pleaded and presented to the trial judge, the matter was
primarily factual. The trial judge, in a lengthy judgment, gave
careful consideration to all of the evidence. He held that the
appellant had failed to prove its case. He found the respondent
did not make a binding election in August 1994 (or at all) to
terminate the contract, that there was no agreement to terminate
based an August 11th termination date, and that the appellant
could not, by its notice of that date, terminate the contract on
December 12 effective August 11, 1994.  There was evidence to
support these findings and conclusions. Particularly, evidence
that the appellants treated the contract as continuing after the
order of Houlden J., and after the notice of August 15, 1994, and
accordingly the respondents did too, thereby acted to their
detriment. Had they believed the contract was terminated during
August 1994, the respondents would have replaced it, thereby
managing its risk appropriately.

[9]  We are not persuaded there was any error made by the trial
judge by reason of which we should interfere.


[10] We are told that because of legislation now in place no
orders, such as that made in this case, will be made in the
future. The case is then of limited significance. Perhaps only to
those others who were also counter-parties to derivative
contracts with the appellant, each of whom we are told, received
the notice of August 15th from the appellant.

[11] The appeal is dismissed with costs.
