                     ENDORSEMENT


[1]    This is an application by the Crown for an order extending
the time for filing an appeal against the sentence imposed by
Humphrey J. on July 28, 1998 because service of the notice of
appeal on Mr. Rosenthal was made one week after the 30 day period
prescribed by Rule 4(2) of the Criminal Appeal Rules.


[2]    The test for the court is fourfold:
       (1)  Did the Crown form a bona fide intention to appeal
            within the prescribed time limit?
       (2)  Has the Crown demonstrated any lack of diligence in
            its attempts to serve the respondent? (This is
            sometimes referred to as whether the Crown has
            exercised reasonable diligence: R. v. Belanger,
            [1987] B.C.J. No. 241 (B.C.C.A.) (Q.L.), although in
            R. v. Finley (D.E.) (1995), 174 A.R. 118, the
            Alberta Court of Appeal suggested that the Crown is
            required to take all possible steps to serve.)
            (3) Even if the Crown was not reasonably diligent,
            has there been prejudice to the respondent from the
            delay?
       (4)  Is there sufficient merit in the grounds of appeal
            to warrant the granting of an extension
            notwithstanding the expiration of the time limit?
       R. v. Gruener (1979), 46 C.C.C. (2d) 88 (Ont. C.A.).


[3]    The respondent accepts that the Crown made the decision to
appeal within the time prescribed. The decision was made on
Thursday August 20. On Monday August 24, Crown counsel's
secretary contacted Detective Merrit, one of the investigating
officers, to confirm his availability to serve Mr. Rosenthal
personally. Her position is that she advised the officer of the
deadline for service and sent him the material by rush courier.
However, he says that he did not realize the urgency of the
timing, and did not open the material which was in his mail box
on August 25, until the afternoon of August 26. The deadline for
service was August 27.


[4]    It was only on August 27 at 11:15 a.m. that the officer
first tried to serve Mr. Rosenthal at his address but there was
no answer. He detailed another officer to try again which he did
at 6:00 p.m. and there was still no answer. No further attempts
were made that evening although it is conceded that service would
have been effective until 12:00 midnight.


[5]    Crown counsel does not take the position that Mr.
Rosenthal was evading service. She also concedes that the police
could have done more to try to serve Mr. Rosenthal within the
time limit, but says that what they did was reasonable. They
eventually served him on September 3, 1998 at his appointment
with his probation officer. No attempt was made to contact his
trial counsel, apparently on the theory that that might have
alerted him to try to avoid service and necessitate a motion such
as this. On September 2, the trial counsel's office advised the
police that they would accept service.


[6]    In my view, taking the lowest threshold test for the
Crown, it cannot be said that the Crown exercised reasonable
diligence to serve Mr. Rosenthal within the prescribed time
limit. Having taken until one week before the deadline to make
the decision to appeal, if there was any concern about alerting
Mr. Rosenthal then the Crown or the police were obliged to obtain
his home and work addresses and ensure that he was in town so
that when they got the papers ready, the service could be
effected. To do nothing until the last day, and then only try at
a residence at 11:00 a.m. and 6:00 p.m., not the most likely
times to find a person at home on a summer work day, cannot in
the circumstances be said to be reasonable.


[7]    As a result of these findings, there is no need to deal
with the issues of prejudice or merit.


[8]    The application is denied.
