O’CONNOR J.A.:

OVERVIEW


[1]  This appeal concerns payments made by a contractor or
subcontractor from funds received on a construction project
towards its general overhead expenses before paying amounts owing
to its subcontractors for the same project. The issue is whether
these payments reduce or eliminate the contractor’s or
subcontractor’s trust obligations to its subcontractors which are
created by s. 8 of the Construction Lien Act, R.S.O. 1990, c. 3
(the “Act”). On a motion under Rule 21(1)(a) Coo J. held that
they did not. I agree with that conclusion.


THE FACTS


[2]  The plaintiff, Rudco Insulation Ltd. (“Rudco”), sued the
corporate defendant, Toronto Sanitary Inc. (“TSI”) and the two
individual defendants Bruno Lisi (“Lisi”) and Morris Piccone
(“Piccone”) for the sum of $216,555.16 owing in contract for work
performed on three separate construction projects (“the
projects”). Lisi and Piccone are the only two officers and
directors of TSI. After the exchange of pleadings, Rudco brought
a motion under Rule 21(1)(a) for the determination of a question
of law. This appeal brought by TSI, Lisi and Piccone, is from the
order made on that motion. The defendant, Lakeshore J.
Construction Ltd. was not a party to the motion or to this
appeal.


[3]  The facts are simple and not in dispute. TSI was a
subcontractor on the projects. Rudco entered into contracts to
perform piping insulation services as a subcontractor to TSI.
After TSI received payment under its contracts and before paying
monies owing to Rudco, TSI made payments towards its general
overhead expenses. TSI became insolvent and Rudco remains unpaid.
The overhead payments, which Coo J. addressed on the motion and
which are in issue on this appeal, are set out in schedules to an
agreed statement of facts. The relevant information is as
follows:

[4]  The payments to Colbourn (an engineer on staff at TSI) and
Piccone shown in Schedule 6 were management wages for actual work
supplied by them to TSI during one of the projects and included
matters such as foreman’s work, on-site project management,
ordering materials and supplies, associated paperwork and
supervision of the trades.



[5]  All of the amounts shown in Schedules 6, 8 and 9 (“the
overhead expenses”) were pro-rated allocations to the projects of
TSI’s general overhead expenses. The overhead expenses exceeded
the amount of Rudco’s claim.



[6]  Coo J. held that the recipients of the overhead expenses
were not persons to whom trust benefits are conferred under s.
8(1) of the Act and that the payment of the overhead expenses did
not reduce TSI’s trust obligations to Rudco under s. 10 of the
Act.



[7]  Although Coo J. was not specifically asked to determine
whether the payment of overhead expenses breached TSI’s trust
obligations, the inevitable result of his finding is that they
did. The practical significance of this finding is that Lisi and
Piccone as officers and directors of TSI may personally be liable
to Rudco for the breach of trust under s. 13 of the Act.1



[8]  Rudco’s motion also sought a determination whether or not
the value of the materials taken from TSI’s inventory which were
used on the projects reduced TSI’s trust obligations. Coo J. did
not address this question in his reasons or in his order. In my
view, we do not have enough evidence to decide this issue in the
context of a Rule 21 motion.


ANALYSIS



[9]  This case turns on the interpretation of the trust
provisions set out in Part II of the Act.  The relevant sections
are as follows:
          
          8. (1) All amounts,
          
          (a)  owing to a contractor or subcontractor,
          whether or not due or payable; or
          
          (b)  received by a contractor or
          subcontractor,
          
          on account of the contract or subcontract
          price of an improvement constitute a trust
          fund for the benefit of the subcontractors
          and other persons who have supplied services
          or materials to the improvement who are owed
          amounts by the contractor or subcontractor.
          
          (2)  The contractor or subcontractor is the
          trustee of the trust fund created by
          subsection (1) and the contractor or
          subcontractor shall not appropriate or
          convert any part of the fund to the
          contractor’s or subcontractor’s own use or to
          any use inconsistent with the trust until all
          subcontractors and other persons who supply
          services or materials to the improvement are
          paid all amounts related to the improvement
          owed to them by the contractor or
          subcontractor.
          
          10. Subject to Part IV (holdbacks), every
          payment by a trustee to a person the trustee
          is liable to pay for services or materials
          supplied to the improvement discharges the
          trust of the trustee making the payment and
          the trustee’s obligations and liability as
          trustee to all beneficiaries of the trust to
          the extent of the payment made by the
          trustee.



[10] Section 8(1) creates a trust in favour of subcontractors and
other persons who have supplied services or materials to an
improvement. It is conceded that the projects were improvements,2
that the monies received by TSI from the projects were trust
funds and that Rudco, as a subcontractor, was a beneficiary of
those trusts for the monies which it was owed. Section 8(2)
precludes the trustee from using trust funds for its own purposes
or any purposes inconsistent with the trust until all of the
beneficiaries of the trust have been paid.



[11] Section 10 provides that a payment made by the trustee to a
person for services or materials supplied to the improvement
discharges the trustee’s obligation to all beneficiaries of the
trust to the extent of the payment. The persons referred to in
s. 10 to whom payment may be made are also beneficiaries of the
trust created by s. 8(1).



[12] Applying s. 10 to this case, the issue is whether the
payments made by TSI for the overhead expenses were payments to
persons who supplied services or materials to an improvement; if
so those payments would discharge TSI’s trust obligation to Rudco
to the extent of such payments; if not the payments were to
persons who were not beneficiaries and were made in breach of
TSI’s trust obligations.



[13] It is common ground that the recipients of the overhead
expenses did not supply materials to an improvement and the only
way that payments to them could discharge TSI’s trust obligations
would be if the payments were made for services supplied to an
improvement within the meaning of s. 10.

 

[14]  “Supply of services” is defined in s. 1 of the Act as
follows:
          
          “supply of services” means any work done or
          service performed upon or in respect of an
          improvement, and includes,
          
          (a)  the rental of equipment with an
          operator, and
          
          (b)  where the making of the planned
          improvement is not commenced, the supply of a
          design, plan, drawing or specification that
          in itself enhances the value of the owner’s
          interest in the land,
          
          and a corresponding expression has a
          corresponding meaning: (“prestation de
          services”)  [Emphasis added.]



[15] While the defined term “supply of services” does not exactly
coincide with the phrases “supplied services” in s. 8 or
“services supplied” in s. 10, the only reasonable interpretation
is that the three phrases refer to the same concept. Both the
ordinary meaning of the words and the concluding words of the
definition lead to this conclusion.



[16] The persons to whom the overhead expenses were paid did not
perform services upon an improvement and therefore the
interpretation of the phrase, “in respect of an improvement” in
the definition is critical. The appellants argue for a broad
interpretation under which any service that contributes either
directly or indirectly to the completion of an improvement would
be included. Without such expenses they argue, there would have
been no improvement because TSI must necessarily have incurred
the overhead expenses in order to perform its work. It would be
an unreasonable interpretation of the Act they say, if a general
contractor or a subcontractor is not entitled to pay such things
as staff payroll, office rents, insurance premiums and
accountant’s fees from project funds, as these costs arise in the
normal course of business. As a result, the appellants argue that
the persons who were paid overhead expenses attributed to the
work performed on the projects are persons who performed services
“in respect of an improvement.”




[17] The factors to be considered in statutory interpretation are
well established. In Bapoo v. Cooperators General Insurance Co.
(1998), 36 O.R. (3d) 616 (C.A.), Laskin J.A., at p. 620, set out
the factors as follows:
          
          The modern approach to statutory
          interpretation calls on courts to interpret a
          legislative provision in its total context.
          The court’s interpretation should comply with
          the legislative text, promote the legislative
          purpose and produce a reasonable and just
          meaning.


[18] In determining who is entitled to the benefit created by s.
8(1) there are two factors that deserve considerable weight. The
first is that s. 8(1) creates a preference for one class of
creditors that did not exist at common law; the second is that
the central purpose of Part II of the Act, which creates the
trust benefit, is to provide a financial preference for those
down the contractual chain on construction projects. Both of
these factors have been relied upon in a number of cases.



[19] Before considering the case law it is important to note that
the Construction Lien Act came into force in April, 1983,
repealing the Ontario Mechanics’ Lien Act, R.S.O. 1980, c. 261.
Although the language in the previous Act and the protection
afforded creditors were different in some respects, the general
purpose was the same and cases decided under the Mechanics’ Lien
Act have been referred to by courts in interpreting the Act.
Providing proper care is taken with respect to the specific
changes, references to cases under the previous Act are helpful
in interpreting the current legislation.



[20] Further, s. 14, which is in Part III of the Act, provides
lien protection to the same class of creditors who are given the
trust benefit by s. 8(1). Section 14(1) provides:
          
          14. (1)  A person who supplies services or
          materials to an improvement for an owner,
          contractor or subcontractor, has a lien upon
          the interest of the owner in the premises
          improved for the price of those services or
          materials. [Emphasis added.]



[21] In combination, ss. 8(1) and 14(1) provide for a preference
and security interest for one class of creditor not enjoyed by
other creditors of the same debtor. Cases dealing with the
entitlement to liens are therefore relevant to the interpretation
of the sections in Part II of the Act dealing with the
entitlement to trust benefits.


[22] The first principle that I draw from the cases is the
following: because the legislation creates a preference and a
security for certain creditors that did not otherwise exist at
common law, it ought to be given a strict interpretation in
determining whether a particular creditor is a person to whom the
benefit is given.



[23] In Clarkson Company Limited et al. v. Ace Lumber Limited et
al., [1963] S.C.R. 110, the Supreme Court of Canada considered a
lien claim by two companies who had rented construction equipment
to a subcontractor. The case turned on the interpretation of the
Mechanics’ Lien Act which created lien protection for “any person
who performs any work or service in respect of ... constructing a
building.”  Ritchie J., at p. 114 said:
          
               With the greatest respect, I am,
          however, of opinion that the proper approach
          to the interpretation of this statute is
          expressed in the dissenting opinion of Kelly
          J.A. where he says that:
          
               The lien commonly known as the
          mechanics’ lien was unknown to the common law
          and owes its existence in Ontario to a series
          of statutes, the latest of which is R.S.O.
          1960, c. 233. It constitutes an abrogation of
          the common law to the extent that it creates,
          in the specified circumstances, a charge upon
          the owner’s lands which would not exist but
          for the Act, and grants to one class of
          creditors a security or preference not
          enjoyed by all creditors of the same debtor;
          accordingly, while the statute may merit a
          liberal interpretation with respect to the
          rights it confers upon those to whom it
          applies, it must be given a strict
          interpretation in determining whether any
          lien-claimant is a person to whom a lien is
          given by it. [Emphasis added.]




[24] For the reasons set out in Clarkson Company Limited, this
court adopted a strict interpretation approach in George Wimpey
Canada Limited v. Peelton Mills Ltd. et al. (1982), 35 O.R. (2d)
787 (C.A.). The court held that a contractor who performed work
on a public street could not assert a lien claim against
adjoining land benefited by the work. The work was not done “in
respect of” the adjoining land. Thorson J.A. examined the history
of the legislation and noted that the words “in respect of” were
introduced into the legislation solely for the purpose of
remedying the historical problem that a lien could only be
obtained if work had been done upon the work site itself.



[25] In applying the strict interpretation approach to
entitlement, courts have described the test in a number of ways.
In 697470 Ontario Ltd. v. Presidential Developments Ltd. et al.
(1989), 69 O.R. (2d) 334 (Div. Ct.), the plaintiff subcontractor
had supplied construction management services including reviewing
tenders, selecting and co-ordinating the trades and supervising
the site superintendent. In dismissing the claim for a lien,
Rosenberg J. referred to Clarkson Company Limited above and held
at p. 337:
          
          ...the work performed by the appellant was
          not so directly related to the construction
          of the improvement to entitle the appellant
          to a lien under the Act. [Emphasis added.]




[26] In Oliver v. Muer Construction Ltd. (1985), 12 C.L.R. 1
(Ont. H.C.J.) the plaintiff solicitor represented the defendant
in obtaining severance for residential lots which substantially
increased the value of the property. In refusing a claim for a
lien, the court found that the legal services performed did not
have the degree of nexus and were too remote from the improvement
to fall within the scope of the definition of “supply of
services.”



[27] In my view, the reasoning in these cases applies in
interpreting entitlement to the trust benefit under s. 8(1) of
the Act. Importantly, a strict interpretation of s. 8(1) is
consistent with the legislature's purpose in creating the trust
benefits set out in Part II of the Act.3



[28] The most obvious purpose of Parts II and III of the Act is
to provide a financial preference and a security interest to
those down the contractual chain on construction projects.4  The
trust and lien protections reduce the risk of non-payment in the
event of insolvency by those above and undoubtedly assist those
below, often small businesses, in more readily obtaining
financing. The statute brings greater stability and security to
the construction industry than would exist at common law.



[29] The Construction Lien Act was based largely on the
recommendations of the Attorney General’s Advisory Committee on
the Draft Construction Lien Act (the “Advisory Committee”).5  The
Advisory Committee stated at p. (xxiii):
          
          The trust funds isolate contract monies, and
          require them to be used for paying suppliers
          who have contributed towards the making of an
          improvement. By making these funds a trust,
          the suppliers to a payer are given protection
          in the event that the payer becomes
          insolvent, since the monies will not then
          form part of his estate. In addition, the
          trust rights permit the tracing of contract
          funds that are used for an improper purpose.


[30] Further down on p. (xxiii) the Committee stated:
          
          By providing these rights, the lien
          legislation facilitates the granting of
          credit within the construction industry. The
          protections afforded by the Mechanics’ Lien
          Act have now become an integral factor in the
          provision of credit in that industry. Thus,
          the Act makes it easier for construction to
          take place, since it assures that credit will
          be made available. In addition, by providing
          a uniform regime of rights to all suppliers
          in the industry, that Act reduces both the
          costs of negotiating both construction
          contracts and subcontracts, and also the
          uncertainties inherent in any complex system
          of contractual relationships.




[31] If the trust provisions are interpreted as the appellant
argues, to include as beneficiaries persons who are recipients of
the overhead expenses of a contractor or subcontractor, the trust
benefit to those down the chain would be reduced or eliminated.
An expansion of the class of creditors entitled to the benefit
has the obvious result of reducing the benefit to those who are
clearly intended to be within the class.



[32] A number of courts have considered the specific issue of
overhead expenses. The preponderance of authority has held that
the payment of overhead expenses does not reduce the trust
obligations of a contractor or subcontractor. See Emco Supply v.
Gueguen et al. (1987), 25 C.L.R. 229 (Ont. H.C.J.); Heritage
Masonry Ltd. v. Building Team Ltd. (1995), 28 C.L.R. (2d) 101
(Ont. Gen. Div.) [appeal dismissed by the Divisional Court on May
27, 1996 (unreported)]; Shield Sprinkler & Fire System Ltd. v.
Fahuki Construction Inc. (1996), 31 C.L.R. (2d) 156 (Ont.
Gen.Div.).



[33] I agree with the comments of Killeen J. in Home Depot Inc.
v. Fieder Painting Inc. (1995), Kirsh’s C.L.C.F. 8.30 (Ont.
Gen.Div.) at p. 8.181:
          
          These trusts reflect the pyramidic nature of
          the payment scheme devised under the Act and
          are aimed at creating, as it were, a fortress
          of impregnability against deductions by
          either the owner or a contractor from the
          trusts imposed in favour of the respective
          beneficiaries down the line.



[34] Killeen J. went on to reason that s. 8 does not allow a
contractor, and I would add a subcontractor, to call upon those
under them to act as co-venturers or co-partners in the financing
of their overhead costs.



[35] In summary, applying a strict interpretation to the
provisions in ss. 8 and 10 of the Act and considering the purpose
of the legislation, I am of the view that Part II of the Act did
not confer trust benefits on the recipients of the overhead
expenses and payments to them did not reduce TSI’s trust
obligations to Rudco. I am satisfied that Coo J. correctly
decided the question of law under Rule 21(1)(a).


[36] I would therefore dismiss the appeal with costs.
