DOHERTY J.A.:
                               I.
[1]  The respondents were convicted by a jury of conspiracy to
traffic in cocaine. The trial judge sentenced the respondent
Vitone to an 18-month conditional sentence to be followed by 18
months probation. He sentenced the respondent Russo, who had a
related but dated criminal record, to a 23-month conditional
sentence to be followed by 23 months probation. The terms of both
conditional sentences imposed significant requirements and
restrictions on the respondents. The Crown seeks leave to appeal
both sentences.


                              II.


[2]  The charges arose out of a large-scale drug investigation
which targeted persons other than the respondents. In the course
of that investigation, intercepted communications established
that Russo had agreed to supply 4 kilograms of cocaine to a
person named Destefano who was one of the targets of the
investigation. The intercepted communications also established
that Vitone had agreed to obtain the cocaine for Russo.
Destefano was to sell the 4 kilograms to a person who was, in
fact, an undercover police agent. The respondents were in contact
with Destefano for about 2 months. They made unsuccessful efforts
to locate a supplier of the cocaine. The trial judge found that
they would not have been able to obtain 4 kilograms of cocaine as
they had promised Destefano. He further held that the police made
arrests some two days before the proposed sale to Destefano
because they knew that it was likely that the respondents would
not be able to produce 4 kilograms of cocaine. The respondents'
efforts to find a supplier continued up until Destefano's arrest.


[3]  The respondent Vitone was 29 years of age at the time of
sentencing. His pre-sentence report was positive. He is a chef
and his employer describes him as a valued employee. He has no
criminal record.


[4]  The respondent Russo was 41 years old when sentenced. He
comes from a stable family background, is married and has two
children. His pre-sentence report was also positive. Russo works
in the family business and, by all accounts, is a hardworking,
reliable contributor to that business. Russo has 5 prior criminal
convictions. However, all occurred at least 10 years prior to his
involvement in this conspiracy. Two of the offences are drug-
related, including a conviction in 1984 for which Russo received
a sentence of 9 months.


[5]  The trial judge gave thoughtful and careful reasons for
sentence. He reviewed the circumstances of the offence and the
offenders in detail. He also considered the Criminal Code
provisions governing sentences in general and the imposition of
conditional sentences in particular. He provided a thorough and
careful analysis of the case law. In the end, the trial judge
decided that a term of imprisonment within the reformatory range
was appropriate. He further held, applying the criteria in s.
742.1 of the Criminal Code, that conditional sentences were
appropriate and in keeping with the relevant principles of
sentencing.


                              III.


[6]  Section 686 of the Criminal Code directs that this court
must review the "fitness" of a sentence under appeal. Contrary to
previous authority from this court (e.g. R. v. Simmons (1973), 13
C.C.C. (2d) 65, per Brooke J.A. at 72), recent decisions of the
Supreme Court of Canada have held that the fitness review is a
limited one. An appellate court can vary the sentence imposed at
trial only where the trial judge has made an error in principle
or where the sentence imposed is outside the acceptable range of
sentence available in the circumstances: R. v. M.(C.A.), [1996] 1
S.C.R. 500; R. v. Shropshire, [1995] 4 S.C.R. 227; R. v.
McDonnell (1997), 114 C.C.C. (3d) 436 (S.C.C.).


[7]  The Crown submits that the trial judge erred in principle in
finding that the enactment of the conditional sentence provisions
effectively lowered the appropriate range of sentence so as to
render sentences of less than 2 years appropriate in
circumstances where prior to the enactment of the conditional
sentencing provisions, a sentence of 2 years or more would have
been necessary. In making this submission, the Crown relies on
the following passage from the trial judge's reasons:

          I do not know what the "starting-point"
          sentence is for conspiracy to traffic in
          cocaine; if there is one, on my reading of
          the cases it may be three years. I, as well,
          do not know whether any such "starting-point"
          will, in the fullness of time, be altered by
          the Court of Appeal of this province in
          recognition of the new sentencing regime
          introduced by Parliament in September 1996.
          However, I do not see how a "starting-point"
          can be applied in a given case without
          consideration being given to the empirical
          evidence cited in Wismayer [(1997), 115
          C.C.C. (3d) 18 (Ont. C.A.)] and to the
          principles embodied in conditional sentences.
          To do otherwise would, I believe, ignore the
          underlying shift in the philosophy of
          sentencing which is upon us. It is for this
          reason, I should think, that many pre-
          September 1996 sentencing decisions must be
          followed with caution. As I have already
          alluded to earlier in these Reasons, a pre-
          September 1996 starting-point sentence of
          three years can become a sentence of less
          than two years simply because of the sheer
          utility of s. 742.1 and the better justice it
          may afford. [Emphasis added.]
[8]  Read in isolation, this passage supports the Crown's
contention. Conditional sentences are available only where the
appropriate term of imprisonment is less than 2 years: Criminal
Code s. 742.1(a). The appropriate term of imprisonment is fixed
by reference to the fundamental purposes of sentencing described
in s. 718 and the principles of sentence set down in s. 718.1 and
s. 718.2 of the Criminal Code. If a consideration of these
purposes and principles dictates a term of imprisonment beyond 2
years, a conditional sentence is unavailable. Sentencing someone
to a conditional sentence which, of necessity, must be less than
2 years, even where that person meets the criteria of s.
742.1(b), is a fundamental error in principle if on an
application of the appropriate principles of sentencing the
proper range of sentence begins at 2 years or more.


[9]  On my reading of the entirety of the trial judge's reasons
for sentence, I do not agree that the trial judge fell into the
error alleged by the Crown. Earlier in his reasons, the trial
judge referred to R. v. Pierce (1997), 32 O.R. (3d) 321 at 336
(C.A.), leave to appeal to S.C.C. refused (1997), 34 O.R. (3d) 1
XV, where Finlayson J.A. said:

          My own view is that a simpler approach would
          be to avoid a rigid two-step process in the
          first place. Where a trial judge is asked to
          impose a conditional sentence and no minimum
          sentence is called for by the Code, surely
          the easiest method is to decide on general
          sentencing principles whether the
          circumstances of the offence and the offender
          call for a penitentiary sentence as opposed
          to a reformatory sentence. If they clearly
          call for a penitentiary sentence, there can
          be no resort to a conditional sentence. If
          they do not, or if there is some doubt on the
          matter, then the full panoply of sentencing
          guidelines should be brought into play to
          determine what should be the appropriate
          sentence. ... [Emphasis added.]
[10] The trial judge went on to say, "I have some doubt as to
whether a fit sentence should be a penitentiary or reformatory
sentence." I take this to mean that the trial judge found himself
in that area described by Finlayson J.A. in the above-quoted
passage from Pierce where a reformatory length sentence could not
be ruled out as inappropriate. Having decided that the
appropriate range of imprisonment did not exclude a reformatory
sentence, the trial judge was entitled to look at the various
sentencing options available in deciding where within that range
to fix the respondents' sentences.


[11] My conclusion that the trial judge did not fall into the
error in principle alleged on by the Crown does not end the
matter. It must still be determined, applying the purposes and
principles of sentencing set out in the Code, whether a
penitentiary term was "clearly called for" in the circumstances
of these offenders and this offence. The Crown argues that any
sentence under two years falls well outside the appropriate
range.


[12] In deciding that a penitentiary term was not required, the
trial judge quite properly put considerable emphasis on the
positive features of each respondents' character and background.
I think, however, he fell into two errors in principle when
considering the nature and gravity of the offence. First, he
emphasized the respondents' inability to actually deliver the
drugs they had agreed to deliver to Destefano. He clearly
regarded this as a mitigating factor. No doubt, this would have
been a significantly worse case had the respondents actually
obtained and delivered 4 kilograms of cocaine. I cannot regard
their failure to do so, however, as properly placed on the credit
side of the ledger for sentencing purposes. The respondents
agreed to deliver 4 kilograms of cocaine to a drug trafficker.
When they did so, they intended to make good on that promise.
Discussions to that effect went on for some 2 months. The
respondents did not withdraw from or abandon the conspiracy and
their attempts to acquire the promised cocaine continued up to
the date of Destefano's arrest. Neither operated under
extenuating circumstances like those found in R. v. Stevens
(1997), 115 C.C.C. (3d) 372 (Man. C.A.) which could be said to
mitigate their culpability. They deserve no credit for the fact
that their criminal aspirations exceeded their grasp. The trial
judge erred in principle in discounting the gravity of the
offence because the respondents were unable to deliver the
cocaine as promised.


[13] Second, the trial judge rejected the Crown's submission that
the amount of cocaine the respondents had agreed to deliver to
Destefano was an aggravating factor on sentence. He said:

          ... Since the offenders were wholly unable to
          unearth cocaine in any quantity, I do not see
          how this court can attach much weight to an
          amount that was never forthcoming.
[14] The exact nature of the criminal conspiracy entered into by
the respondents should have been a important consideration in the
imposition of sentence. These respondents were prepared to supply
a massive amount of cocaine to a person they believed to be a
drug trafficker. Their agreement contemplated a large-scale
commercial transaction in a deadly substance. While ultimately no
profit was made, there can be no doubt that profit was the
motive. These respondents were prepared to do significant damage
to others so that they could make money.


[15] Conspiracies that contemplate the sale of large amounts of a
drug like cocaine clearly pose a much more significant danger to
the community than do agreements to supply small amounts to
others for purely personal use. The amount the respondents agreed
to sell to Destefano indicates that they were ready and willing
to participate in the drug trade at a very high level. The trial
judge erred in principle in giving no weight to the magnitude of
the crime contemplated by the agreement made by the respondents.


[16] The two errors identified above caused the trial judge to
significantly underestimate the seriousness of the offence in
determining the appropriate sentence.


[17] Having found errors in principle, this court is entitled to
review the trial record and impose what it regards to be an
appropriate sentence: R. v. Rezaie (1996), 31 O.R. (3d) 713 at
719 (C.A.). This is so whether the appeal is brought by accused
or the Crown: R. v. Biancofiore (1997), 35 O.R. (3d) 782 at 789-
90 (C.A.). I think, however, that the court should be reluctant
to vary a non-custodial sentence to a custodial sentence on a
Crown appeal even where it finds error in principle unless it is
also satisfied that the sentence imposed was outside of the
acceptable range. This added limitation on appellate intervention
recognizes the added hardship brought when a custodial sentence
is imposed on appeal several months after an accused has received
a non-custodial sentence at trial.


[18] The trial judge's reasons make a good case for a conditional
sentence. Were I satisfied that a reformatory length sentence was
within the appropriate range, I would not interfere with his
decision to impose a conditional sentence. I am, however, of the
view given the seriousness of this offence, that a penitentiary
range sentence was essential to give effect to the relevant
purposes and principles of sentencing. A sentence of 3 years was
necessary to properly balance the aggravating nature of the
offence and the many mitigating factors particular to these
respondents.


[19] The respondents have successfully served some 10 months of
the sentence imposed at trial and that must be taken into
consideration in determining the appropriate sentence at this
time: R. v. Biancofiore, supra, at p.  795. I would allow the
appeal and vary both sentences to a term of imprisonment of 2
years. This does not, of course, mean that the respondents will
necessarily serve 2 years in jail. As Rosenberg J.A. pointed out
in R. v. Wismayer, supra, at p. 35, where a penitentiary term is
imposed "the question of serving any portion of the sentence in
the community is reserved to the National Parole Board." No
doubt, that agency will give careful attention to the comments of
the trial judge.


[20] I would grant leave to appeal the sentences imposed on the
respondents, allow the appeals and impose sentences of 2 years on
each respondent. That sentence begins when the respondents are
taken into custody. If necessary, a warrant may be issued for
their arrest.
