                      ENDORSEMENT
                                
                                
[1]  The appellant was convicted on her plea of guilty to
possession of a narcotic for the purposes of trafficking and
sentenced to two years in the penitentiary.  She appeals
conviction and sentence.


[2]  The appellant was not directly involved in the sale of 200
grams of heroin that was made to an undercover police officer.
He had met with the vendor and the two of them travelled by car
to the appellant=s seamstress shop.  The vendor went into the
shop and returned with the cocaine that he exchanged for $32,000.
A search of the seamstress shop revealed a supply of heroin.


[3]  In her appeal against conviction, the appellant asks that
her plea of guilty be struck out because she received improper
advice from her counsel.  She says that she had always maintained
her innocence and only pled guilty because her counsel told her
she would receive a sentence of nine years if she pleaded not
guilty and was convicted after trial but, if she pleaded guilty,
she would receive a sentence that she would be allowed to serve
in the community.


[4]  We would not give effect to this argument. The appellant had
provided written instructions to her trial counsel, which
stipulated the offence to which she would be pleading guilty, the
potential consequences of her plea, and the basis upon which her
plea of guilty would be entered. She did not repudiate these
instructions during the protracted sentencing proceedings, made
up of three separate hearings over a twelve week period, despite
the fact that the trial judge initiated a detailed dialogue as to
the nature and extent of her knowledge of what she was pleading
to.


[5]  Accordingly, we are in no doubt that she was well aware of
the seriousness of the charges against her and that she knew she
was facing a substantial custodial sentence.  The appeal against
conviction is dismissed.


[6]  As to sentence, we are all of the opinion that the sentence
imposed is excessive. The trial judge appears to have thought
that it was irrelevant that the appellant was wilfully blind to
the nature of the narcotic involved, as opposed to having
knowledge that it was heroin. This is not correct. Although the
appellant had to be sentenced as being in possession of heroin
for the purpose of trafficking, the fact that she did not know it
was heroin was a mitigating factor. This factor, together with
her passive role in the commission of the offence and the fact
that she received no real benefit from it, justifies a reduction
in her sentence.


[7]  Accordingly, leave to appeal sentence is granted and the
sentence is reduced to six months imprisonment.
