                     ENDORSEMENT

[1]    The Crown applied for leave to appeal from the decision of
MacDougall J., upholding the respondent's acquittal by Provincial
Court Judge Main on a charge of operating a boat while the
concentration of alcohol in his blood exceeded the legal limit,
contrary to s.253(b) of the Criminal Code.  We denied leave to
appeal as the Crown had failed to perfect the appeal in a timely
fashion.


[2]    In the vast majority of cases where leave to appeal is
refused, we will not comment on the merits of the proposed
appeal.  However, in rare cases where we believe that the
decision below concerns a significant issue and does not accord
with established authority, we will comment on the merits of the
proposed appeal.  In our view, this is one of those rare cases.


[3]    The respondent was acquitted on the ground that each
sample of his breath was not taken "as soon as practicable after
the time when the offence was alleged to have been committed", as
required by s.258(1)(c)(ii) of the Criminal Code.


[4]    The police intercepted the respondent at 6:55 p.m. while
he was operating his boat on a lake.  At 7:03 p.m. the appellant
was arrested and advised of his Charter rights.  At 7:05 p.m. the
police and the respondent began a short journey to the town dock
where the respondent was turned over to a police officer at 7:20
p.m. to be taken to the Midland detachment of the O.P.P. so that
samples of his breath could be obtained.  The first sample was
obtained at 7:34 p.m., and the second sample, at 7:54 p.m.  It is
apparent that only 31 minutes elapsed between the respondent's
arrest on the lake and when the first sample was obtained.


[5]    The trial judge was concerned with the failure of the
Crown to provide evidence to explain what happened during the
fourteen minutes from the time the respondent left the dock and
the first sample was obtained.  Because the Crown had failed to
explain what occurred during this brief period and his view that
he was "entitled" to know what happened, he concluded that he was
unable to "paint in the entire picture of everything that
transpired in that interval".  Accordingly, he found that the
Crown had failed to satisfy the requirements of s.258(1)(c)(ii)
and acquitted the respondent.


[6]    The reasoning of the trial judge was affirmed by the
appeal judge.  MacDougall J. made reference to the decisions of
this court in R. v. Phillips (1988), 42 C.C.C. (3d) 150 and R. v.
Payne (1990), 56 C.C.C. (3d) 548 in which it was held that the
test of practicability is reasonableness and that the court must
be satisfied that the conduct of the police in the interval
between the arrest and the taking of the breath sample was
reasonable, having regard to all the circumstances.  However, he
then went on to conclude:

            In order for the court to determine
            whether the delay was reasonable having
            regard to all the circumstances,
            evidence must be introduced describing
            what was happening throughout the time
            period between the time of arrest and
            the breathalyzer test being administered
            in order for the court to determine if
            the police officer's conduct was
            reasonable.
            ...
            Because the trial judge found that there
            was a 14 minute unexplained delay, the
            judge was therefore entitled to find
            that there was a reasonable doubt that
            the tests were taken as soon as
            practicable.
                                     [emphasis added]

[7]    It would have been our view had we granted leave to appeal
that both the trial judge and the appeal judge misconstrued the
meaning of "as soon as practicable" and in imposing an
evidentiary burden on the prosecution to account for every minute
between the time of arrest and the administration of the first
test, rather than focusing on whether the police had acted
reasonably, and expeditiously, in all the circumstances.  Their
decisions were contrary to an established line of authority.  In
addition to the Phillips case and the Payne case, see, for
example, R. v. McCarthy (1981), 64 C.C.C. (2d) 280 (Newf. C.A.);
R. v. Carter (1981), 59 C.C.C. (2d) 450 (Sask. C.A.); R. v.
Carter (1980), 55 C.C.C. (2d) 405 (B.C.C.A.); R. v. Rasmussen
(1981), 64 C.C.C. (2d) 304 (B.C.C.A.).


[8]    The following comments of Craig J.A. in R. v. Cambrin
(1982), 1 C.C.C. (3d) 59 at 62 (B.C.C.A.) have direct application
to this appeal:
            The framers of the legislation never
            intended that the prosecution would have
            to account for each minute which elapsed
            between the time of the arrival of the
            accused and the taking of the first
            test.  As Mr. Justice Macdonald, in
            giving the judgment of this court, in R.
            v. Carter, pointed out, the test adopted
            by the appeal judge in that case, and
            apparently adopted by the appeal judge
            in this case [at p.407] "would require a
            trial Judge to find that the condition
            specified in para. (c)(ii) had not been
            met without evidence explaining a delay
            of as little as five minutes".
