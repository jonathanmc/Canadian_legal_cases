BY THE COURT:

[1]  This is an appeal by Great-West Lifeco Inc. ("Lifeco") and
The Great-West Life Assurance Company from the judgment of
Lederman J. in which he determined the rights of a dissenting
shareholder who, after a successful take-over bid, was forced to
sell his shares to the successful offeror pursuant to s. 206 of
the Canada Business Corporations Act, R.S.C. 1985, c. C-44 (the
"Act").

[2]  The relevant provisions of s. 206 of the Act are as follows:
          206. (3) Notice – An offeror may acquire
          shares held by a dissenting offeree by
          sending by registered mail within sixty days
          after the date of termination of the take-
          over bid and in any event within one hundred
          and eighty days after the date of the take-
          over bid, an offeror's notice to each
          dissenting offeree and to the Director
          stating that
          
            . . .
            
            (c)  a dissenting offeree is required to elect
               (i)  to transfer his shares to the offeror
				    on the terms on which the offeror acquired
	 			    the shares of the offerees who accepted the
                    take-over bid, or
                    
               (ii) to demand payment of the fair value of his
			        shares in accordance with subsections (9)
			        to (18) by notifying the offeror within
					twenty days after he receives the offeror's
					notice;


Facts:
[3]  The appellants’ take-over bid for the outstanding shares of
London Insurance Group ("LIG") provided several options. For the
purpose of this appeal, only two options are relevant: a cash
option of $34 ("Option 1") and a share transfer option for Lifeco
common shares ("Option 2"). The share transfer option was limited
to a maximum number of shares. If the maximum number of Lifeco
common shares provided for in Option 2 was reached, the total
number of available Lifeco common shares would be allocated pro
rata to the shareholders who chose this option, with the balance
of their consideration to be paid in cash at the rate of $34.00
per LIG common share. The prorating was to be determined at the
date of the initial take-up of shares deposited to the bid.


[4]  The appellants made a successful take-over bid for the
outstanding LIG shares.


[5]  The respondent, a sophisticated arbitrageur, purchased LIG
shares after the announcement of the take-over bid and continued
to purchase LIG shares after the take-up on the bid. In the end,
the respondent's LIG shares became subject to the compulsory
acquisition provisions. Relying on s. 206(3)(c)(i) he sought to
exercise Option 2.


[6]  The appellants took the position that Option 2 was no longer
available to the respondent because the maximum number of Lifeco
common shares offered under Option 2 had been exceeded by those
offerees who had accepted the take-over bid. The appellants
therefore notified the respondent that he had a choice between
Option 1 or the fair value of his shares in accordance with s.
206(3)(c)(ii).


[7]  The respondent brought an application for a declaration that
he was entitled to redeem his shares on the same basis as the
offerees who had transferred their shares pursuant to Option 2.


[8]  The motions judge concluded that a dissenting shareholder,
such as the respondent, should not be prejudiced and that he was
entitled to choose from all the possible forms of consideration
given to offerees who elected to receive Lifeco common shares
under the bid. He ordered that the respondent was entitled to
receive the combination of Lifeco common shares and cash for his
LIG shares as provided to accepting shareholders opting for
Option 2


Issues:
[9]  On this appeal, the grounds of appeal relating to the
failure of the respondent to deposit his LIG shares in accordance
with the Act, the interest and dividends awarded by the motions
judge on the shares and the proper time to crystalize the value
of the LIG shares for the purpose of Option 2, have all been
abandoned.


[10] The only remaining issue is the proper interpretation of the
words "on the terms on which the offeror acquired the shares of
the offerees who accepted the take-over bid" as contained in
s. 206(3)(c)(i).


Analysis:
[11] Counsel for the appellants argued that the word "terms" in
s. 206(3)(c)(i) meant that the same options, including all of the
limits placed on them, must be offered to dissenting offerees.
This interpretation does not accurately reflect the wording of
s. 206(3)(c)(i). It is not simply the terms on which the offer
was made that are relevant; it is the terms on which the offeror
acquired the shares that are important for the purposes of
s. 206(3)(c)(i).


[12] Counsel also questioned the motions judge's description of
the respondent's option as the "maximum benefit ever available to
any shareholder under a bid". In our view, nothing turns on the
motion judge’s choice of words to describe the options available
to the respondent. He clearly held that, based upon the wording
of the statute, the respondent was entitled to elect to transfer
his shares on the same terms on which the offeror acquired the
shares of the offerees who accepted the take-over bid.  These
terms included the share transfer option, subject to the same pro
rata distribution of the Lifeco shares received by the accepting
offerees. We agree with the motions judge.


[13] In reaching this conclusion, the motions judge relied on the
decision in Re Carlton Holdings Ltd. [1971] 2 All E.R. 1082 (Ch.
D.). In Re Carlton the court interpreted the meaning of the same
"terms" requirement in the context of a take-over bid with
alternative options of consideration. The court held that
dissenting offerees must be given the same choices that accepting
offerees had under the offer, even though some of the choices
available under the bid were limited in time and no longer
available.


[14] The motions judge was correct in interpreting s. 206(3)(c)
as requiring an offeror who exercises the right of compulsory
acquisition, to provide dissenting offerees with all the choices
upon which the offeror acquired the shares of the accepting
offerees. On the plain wording of s. 206(3)(c), the acquisition
must be on the same terms that the offeror acquired the shares
from accepting offerees.


[15] On the basis of the appellants' argument, the dissenting
offerees would not be entitled to Lifeco common shares if the
maximum number stated in the offer was exhausted. The effect of
this interpretation is to render one particular option of the
offer unavailable to the dissenting offerees. To recognize such
limitations, would remove the protection clearly envisaged by the
Act and leave dissenting shareholders and indeed all shareholders
subject to undue pressure in the face of a take-over bid. This
would be inconsistent with the purpose of s. 206(3)(c) which is
to protect the dissenting shareholder from prejudice.


[16] The interpretation adopted by the motions judge is the only
one that can accomplish the purpose of s. 206(3)(c). A dissenting
shareholder is required to elect to transfer his shares "on the
terms on which the offeror acquired the shares of the offerees
who accepted the take-over bid" or demand payment of the fair
value of his shares. A dissenting shareholder cannot be penalized
as a result of his failure to tender his shares to a bid; he must
receive treatment that is no less favourable than that received
by an accepting shareholder. While the intervening vagaries of
the market may well assist a shareholder in his choice, that
choice cannot be limited on compulsory acquisition as intended by
the appellants. Further, the limitation on the number of shares
available was of the appellants' own making. The resulting
difficulty was clearly foreseeable.


[17] Hence, the motions judge correctly concluded that
s. 206(3)(c) entitled dissenting offerees to choose the same form
of consideration as that given to the accepting offerees under
the offer. This would not alter the terms of the offer, but would
fairly compensate a dissenting offeree for what is, in effect,
the expropriation of his shares.


[18] The appeal is dismissed with costs.
