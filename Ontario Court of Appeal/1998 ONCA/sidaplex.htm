ROSENBERG J.A.:

 

[1]               This is an appeal and cross-appeal from the judgment of Blair J. whose reasons are now reported at 131 D.L.R. (4th) 399.  Frank Lin appeals from the order that he pay a sum owing to Sidaplex-Plastic Suppliers, Inc.  Mr. Lin's company, The Elta Group Inc., owed this money to Sidaplex.  Blair J. made this order by applying the oppression remedy provisions of the Ontario Business Corporation Act, R.S.O. 1990, c. B.16, s. 248.  Sidaplex cross-appeals from the order dismissing its application under the Bulk Sales Act, R.S.O. 1990, c. B.14 to set aside a bulk sale by Elta to Kimoto Canada Inc.

 

THE FACTS

 

[2]               The facts underlying the appeal and cross-appeal are set out in the reported reasons of Blair J.  He summarized the facts as follows at pp. 401 and 402:

 

The corporate parties are all companies engaged in the production and sale of plastics and film in the graphic arts industry. Sidaplex‑Plastic Suppliers, Inc. is a judgment creditor of the defendant, the Elta Group Inc. The judgment, in the amount of $97,076.30, was obtained on consent and as part of an overall arrangement whereby a further claim by Sidaplex involving approximately $10,000 would be tried together with an outstanding action by Elta against Sidaplex claiming substantial damages for breach of fiduciary duty and breach of contract. Although the terms of the judgment itself provided that the moneys were to be paid in cash to the solicitors for Sidaplex, the parties entered into an agreement whereby the judgment would instead be secured by an irrevocable letter of credit in favour of Sidaplex, to be automatically renewed pending the disposition of the issues remaining outstanding between the parties.

 

A letter of credit was arranged by Elta, through its bank, but instead of being renewable automatically, the letter of credit was for a fixed term. It expired on February 21, 1995. As the application for the letter of credit had sought an instrument that would be renewable automatically, the parties apparently assumed that such was the case. The letter of credit itself had been forwarded to Elta's solicitors who, in turn, forwarded it to Sidaplex's solicitors. No one apparently noticed that it was for a fixed term. Through what appears to be sheer inadvertence, the letter of credit was not renewed.

 

Thus, Sidaplex's security for its judgment lapsed with the expiration of the letter of credit.

 

In the meantime, Elta entered into an agreement whereby it sold the bulk of its assets to the defendant Kimoto Canada Inc., a competitor and sometimes supplier of both Sidaplex and Elta. The proceeds of sale ‑‑ something in excess of $533,000 ‑‑ were utilized to eliminate Elta's indebtedness of $320,000 at the bank, and at the same time eliminate the liability of the defendant Frank Lin ‑‑ Elta's sole shareholder, director and officer ‑‑ to the bank on his guarantee of that indebtedness. The balance of the sale proceeds was used in one fashion or another to pay off other Elta indebtedness, including two other letters of credit totalling approximately $168,000.

 

The sale did not comply with the provisions of the Bulk Sales Act. Neither Elta nor Kimoto filed particulars of the asset sale with the court, nor did they obtain an order exempting the application of the Act. Kimoto did not demand a statement of creditors pursuant to s. 4.

 

Elta is no longer carrying on business in the graphic arts field. It has no assets.

 

The Oppression Remedy

 

[3]               Blair J. held that Sidaplex, as a creditor, had status to bring an application as a complainant under s. 248 of the Ontario Business Corporations Act.  He held that the failure to renew the letter of credit was an omission which effected a result that was prejudicial to Sidaplex.  Sidaplex had lost the security that assured payment of its judgment at a time when all of the other creditors, including unsecured creditors, had been taken care of.  This included Mr. Lin, the sole shareholder, officer and director of Elta.  Mr. Lin had been released from a substantial personal guarantee.  Notwithstanding that there was no bad faith or lack of probity on the part of Elta, the circumstances amounted to an unfair prejudice or an unfair disregard of Sidaplex's interest.  After considering all of the circumstances Blair J. held that Sidaplex was entitled to a remedy under s. 248.  Further, this was an appropriate case to make an order against the director, in this case Mr. Lin, personally.  Blair J. summarized his reasons for making the order as follows at pp. 405 to 407:

 

Courts have made orders against directors personally, in oppression remedy cases: see, for example, Canadian Opera Co. v. Euro‑American Motor Cars, supra; Prime Computer of Canada Ltd. v. Jeffrey, supra; Tropxe Investments Inc. v. Ursus Securities Corp., [1993] O.J. No. 1736 (QL) (Gen. Div.) [summarized 41 A.C.W.S. (3d) 1140]. These cases, in particular, have involved small, closely held corporations, where the director whose conduct was attacked has been the sole controlling owner of the corporation and its sole and directing mind; and where the conduct in question has redounded directly to the benefit of that person.

 

Such is the case here. Mr. Lin is the sole shareholder, director and officer of Elta. He is the one who has benefited personally from the events that have transpired because he has been relieved of his substantial exposure under his personal guarantee of Elta's indebtedness to the bank. Sidaplex, on the other hand, appears to be the only loser. All other creditors have been paid. Sidaplex has been deprived of the security for which it bargained, and on which it relied, and left with a "paper" judgment when it should not have been. That security ‑‑ the letter of credit ‑‑ while not directly guaranteed by Mr. Lin personally, was based upon the company's line of credit at the bank, which was in turn buttressed by Mr. Lin's guarantee. The evidence is that the bank looked primarily to that guarantee for its security, and the bank's testimony, at least, is that it would have been prepared to renew the letter of credit had Mr. Lin made the request and had he been prepared to support the renewal with his guarantee.

 

Lawyers and judges tend to worry and fuss a great deal about whether or not a given set of circumstances permits the piercing of the "corporate veil". They do so for legitimate reasons pertaining to corporate law. While personal liability of a director in an oppression remedy situation may be founded upon such a base ‑‑ as it was in the authorities referred to above ‑‑ the issue, in my view, is not so much one of piercing the corporate veil as it is a question of the overall application of s. 248(2) of the OBCA and the interplay between its various provisions.

 

When "oppressive" conduct (in the broad sense), has been found to have occurred under s. 248, the Court has a very broad discretionary power to "make an order to rectify the matters complained of". That broad discretionary power, under s. 248(3) is to "make any interim or final order it thinks fit", including:

 

(j)        an order compensating an aggrieved person;

 

In its targeting of the kinds of conduct encompassed by the oppression remedy provision of the Act, the legislature has focused specifically upon the acts or omissions of the corporation (s. 248(2)(a)), the business or affairs of the corporation (s. 248(2)(b)), and the exercise of the powers of the directors (s. 248(2)(c)). In a small, closely held corporation such as Elta, it is the director who is in the position of a Mr. Lin, who is the source of all such conduct. When the power of the director is exercised in a fashion which causes an act or omission of the corporation which effects an unfairly prejudicial result, or a result which unfairly disregards the interests of the complainant ‑‑ or which causes the business or affairs of the corporation to be conducted in a manner which has the same effect ‑‑ those powers themselves have been "exercised in a manner" which is caught by the section, in my opinion. Liability therefore lies directly with the director, under the section, in appropriate cases.

 

This, in my view, is one of those cases. The proper way in which to rectify the matters complained of in this case is to make an order directing that Mr. Lin pay to Sidaplex the amount of Elta's judgment debt that should have been continually secured by the letter of credit, but which was not. That amount, as I have indicated, is $97,076.36, together with accrued interest. I so order.

 

[4]               The Court did not call upon counsel for Sidaplex to respond to the appeal by Mr. Lin.  As pointed out by Galligan J.A. in Naneff v. Con-Crete Holdings Ltd. (1995), 23 O.R. (3d) 481 at 486-87 (C.A.), s. 248(3) empowers a court upon a finding of oppression to make any order "it thinks fit".  This gives the court at first instance a broad discretion and the appellate court a limited power of review.  The appellate court is entitled to interfere only where it is established that the court at first instance has erred in principle or its decision is otherwise unjust.  In our view, counsel for Mr. Lin had not demonstrated any error in principle on the part of Blair J. and we were not persuaded that the decision was otherwise unjust.  Blair J. considered all of the relevant circumstances and reached a conclusion that was clearly open on the facts.  As counsel for Mr. Lin conceded, it is no longer necessary to prove bad faith or want of probity to show a right to a remedy under s. 248:  see Brant Investments Ltd. v. KeepRite Inc. (1991), 3 O.R. (3d) 289 at 311 (C.A.).  Accordingly, the appeal by Mr. Lin is dismissed with costs.

 

The Bulk Sales Act

 

[5]               There was no dispute that the sale by Elta to Kimoto was a bulk sale within the meaning of the Bulk Sales Act and did not comply with the provisions of the Act.  Under s. 4 of the Act, subject to certain exceptions that do not apply here, the buyer before paying or delivering to the seller any part of the proceeds of a bulk sale is required to demand and receive from the seller a statement verified by the affidavit of the seller in Form 1.  This statement must show the names and addresses of the secured and unsecured trade creditors and the amount of the indebtedness or liability due.  Kimoto neither demanded, nor received, such a statement.  Under s. 16(1) of the Act the sale in bulk is voidable unless the buyer has complied with the Act.  Under s. 17(1) a proceeding to set aside, or have declared void, a sale in bulk may be brought or taken by a "creditor".  If a bulk sale is set aside, or declared void, s. 16(2) provides that the buyer is personally liable to account to the creditors of the seller for the value of the stock taken in possession from the sale.

 

[6]               Kimoto made two arguments before Blair J.  First, it submitted that Sidaplex had waived compliance with the Act by its conduct.  Secondly, it submitted that the remedial provisions of s. 16(2) constituted a penalty with respect to which relief should be afforded in the circumstances.  Blair J. held that there was no merit in the second argument.  As to the first argument, he held that while a creditor might be able to waive compliance with the Act by conduct, there was a conflict in the evidence between the principals of Kimoto and Sidaplex as to what was discussed between them.  He could not resolve the conflict on the basis of the affidavit evidence so as to determine the waiver issue.  Nevertheless, Blair J. dismissed the application by Sidaplex and held that the remedial provisions of the Act were not available to assist it.  His reasoning was as follows at pp. 407 and 408:

 

Sidaplex is a secured creditor in this context ‑‑ or, at least was so at the time ‑‑ having substituted its right under the judgment to have the cash amount of the judgment paid to its solicitors for its security in the letter of credit. It was not, however, a secured creditor for which adequate provision for payment is required to be made by a buyer receiving a statement of creditors, under the Act. Such provision, and payment, is to be made with respect to claims of secured creditors "that are or become due and payable upon completion of the sale": Bulk Sales Act, s. 8(1)(c). Sidaplex's claim did not fall into this category. While its claim had been crystallized in terms of liability and amount by the judgment, it was not "due and payable" until after the disposition of the pending trial dealing [with] the additional $10,000 dispute and the claims of Elta against Sidaplex. That this was so is confirmed by the evidence noted above to the effect that Sidaplex was not looking to Kimoto or to the assets of Elta, but to the letter of credit, for satisfaction of its judgment.

 

Accordingly, the claim against Kimoto pursuant to the Bulk Sales Act, must be dismissed.

 

[7]               The relevant provisions of the Bulk Sales Act are the following:

 

1. In this Act,

 

"creditor" means any creditor, including an unsecured trade creditor and a secured trade creditor;

 

"sale in bulk" means a sale of stock in bulk out of the usual course of business or trade of the seller;

 

"secured trade creditor" means a person to whom a seller is indebted, whether or not the debt is due,

 

(a)   for stock, money or services furnished for the purpose of enabling the seller to carry on business, or

 

(b)   for rental of premises in or from which the seller carries on business,

 

and who holds security or is entitled to a preference in respect of a claim;

 

"unsecured trade creditor" means a person to whom a seller is indebted for stock, money or services furnished for the purpose of enabling the seller to carry on a business, whether or not the debt is due, and who holds no security or who is entitled to no preference in respect of a claim.

 

4.(1) The buyer, before paying or delivering to the seller any part of the proceeds of the sale, other than the part mentioned in section 6, shall demand of and receive from the seller, and the seller shall deliver to the buyer, a statement verified by the affidavit of the seller in Form 1.

 

(2) The statement shall show the names and addresses of the unsecured trade creditors and the secured trade creditors of the seller and the amount of the indebtedness or liability due, owing, payable, or accruing due, or to become due and payable, by the seller to each of them, and, with respect to the claims of the secured trade creditors, the nature of their security and whether their claims are due or, in the event of sale, become due on the date fixed for the completion of the sale.

 

8.(1) Where the buyer has received the statement mentioned in section 4, the buyer may pay or deliver the proceeds of the sale to the seller and thereupon acquire the property of the seller in the stock in bulk,

 

(a)   if the statement mentioned in section 4 discloses that the claims of the unsecured trade creditors of the seller do not exceed a total of $2,500 and that the claims of the secured trade creditors of the seller do not exceed a total of $2,500 and the buyer has no notice that the claims of the unsecured trade creditors of the seller exceed a total of $2,500 and that the claims of the secured trade creditors of the seller exceed a total of $2,500; or

 

(b)   if the seller delivers a statement verified by the seller's affidavit showing that the claims of all unsecured trade creditors and all secured trade creditors of the seller of which the buyer has notice have been paid in full; or

 

(c)   if adequate provision has been made for the immediate payment in full of all claims of the unsecured trade creditors of the seller of which the buyer has notice and of all claims of secured trade creditors of the seller that are or become due and payable upon completion of the sale of which the buyer has notice, so long as their claims are paid in full forthwith after completion of the sale, but where any such creditor has delivered a waiver in Form 2 no provision need be made for the immediate payment of the creditor's claim.

 

(2) Where the buyer has received the statement mentioned in section 4, the buyer may pay or deliver the proceeds of the sale to the trustee and thereupon acquire the property of the seller in the stock in bulk, if the seller delivers to the buyer,

 

(a)   the consent to the sale in Form 3 of unsecured trade creditors of the seller representing not less than 60 per cent in number and amount of the claims that exceed $50 of all the unsecured trade creditors of the seller of whose claims the buyer has notice; and

 

(b)   an affidavit of the seller deposing that the seller delivered or caused to be delivered to all of the seller's unsecured trade creditors and secured trade creditors personally or by registered mail addressed to them at their last known addresses at least fourteen days before the date fixed for the completion of the sale copies of the contract of the sale in bulk, the statement mentioned in subsection 4 (1), and the statement of affairs in Form 4, and deposing that the affairs of the seller as disclosed in the statement of affairs have not materially changed since it was made.

 

(3) Duplicate originals of the documents mentioned in clause (2)(b) shall be attached as exhibits to the affidavit mentioned therein.

 

9.(1) Where a sale in bulk is being completed under subsection 8(2), a trustee shall be appointed,

 

(a)   by the seller with the consent in Form 3 of the seller's unsecured trade creditors representing not less than 60 per cent in number and amount of the claims that exceed $50 of the unsecured trade creditors as shown by the statement mentioned in section 4; or

 

(b)   by a judge upon the application of any person interested where the unsecured trade creditors of the seller representing not less than 60 per cent in number and amount of the claims that exceed $50 as shown by the statement mentioned in section 4 have consented to the sale in bulk but have not consented to the appointment of a trustee, or where the trustee appointed under clause (a) is unable or unwilling to act.

 

11.(1) Within five days after the completion of a sale in bulk, the buyer shall file in the office of the local registrar of the court an affidavit setting out the particulars of the sale, including the subject‑matter thereof and the name and address of the trustee, if any, and exhibiting duplicate originals of the statement mentioned in section 4, the statement, if any, mentioned in clause 8(1)(b), the waivers, if any, mentioned in clause 8(1)(c) and the consent and affidavit, if any, mentioned in subsection 8(2).

 

12.(1) Where the proceeds of the sale are paid or delivered to a trustee under section 10, the trustee is a trustee for the general benefit of the creditors of the seller and shall distribute the proceeds of the sale among the creditors of the seller, and, in making the distribution, all creditors' claims shall be proved in like manner and are subject to like contestation before a judge and, subject to section 13, are entitled to like priorities as in the case of a distribution under the Bankruptcy Act (Canada), as amended or re‑enacted from time to time, and shall be determined as of the date of the completion of the sale.

 

16.(1) A sale in bulk is voidable unless the buyer has complied with this Act.

 

(2) If a sale in bulk has been set aside or declared void and the buyer has received or taken possession of the stock in bulk, the buyer is personally liable to account to the creditors of the seller for the value thereof, including all money, security and property realized or taken by the buyer from, out of, or on account of, the sale or other disposition by the buyer of the stock in bulk. R.S.O. 1990, c. B.14, s. 16.

 

17.(1) An action or proceeding to set aside or have declared void a sale in bulk may be brought or taken by a creditor of the seller, and, if the seller is adjudged bankrupt, by the trustee of the seller's estate.

 

(2) No action shall be brought or proceeding taken in respect of real property included in a sale in bulk if the real property has been sold, transferred, charged or mortgaged to a purchaser, transferee, chargee or mortgagee in good faith for valuable consideration without actual notice of non‑compliance with the Act by the buyer.

 

18. In an action or proceeding in which a sale in bulk is attacked or comes in question, whether directly or indirectly, the burden of proof that this Act has been complied with is upon the person upholding the sale in bulk.

 

19.  No action shall be brought or proceeding taken to set aside or have declared void a sale in bulk for failure to comply with this Act unless the action is brought or the proceeding is taken either before the documents are filed under section 11 or within six months after the date on which the documents were filed under section 11.  [Emphasis added.]

 

[8]               There was no dispute that this was a bulk sale within the meaning of the Act.  In my view, it is also clear that Sidaplex was a secured trade creditor.  Although most of its claim against Elta had crystallized in a judgment, it still fell within the definition of trade creditor in s. 1 being a person "to whom a seller is indebted, whether or not the debt is due, (a) for stock, money or services furnished for the purpose of enabling the seller to carry on business".  I also agree with Blair J. that Sidaplex was not a secured trade creditor for whom provision would have had to be made under s. 8(1)(c), if Kimoto and Elta had complied with s. 4, since the debt to Sidaplex did not become "due and payable upon completion of the sale".  Rather, in accordance with the arrangement between Sidaplex and Elta the monies owing to Sidaplex were to be held in trust until resolution of the action commenced by Elta against Sidaplex.  This arrangement was later varied to substitute a letter of credit.  However, the evidence seems clear that the same terms applied.  Sidaplex could only call upon the letter of credit once the other action had been disposed of.  That did not occur until June 1995 when Dyson J. gave judgment to Sidaplex on its outstanding claim for the sum of $10,285.94 and dismissed Elta's claim against Sidaplex.

 

[9]               While I agree that Sidaplex was not a creditor for whom provision needed to be made under s. 8(1)(c) of the Act, that in my view does not dispose of the issue.  Section 8 creates two ways in which the buyer may complete the bulk sale.  Under subsection (1), the buyer may pay the seller directly where, in effect, provision has been made for the trade creditors or where their claims do not exceed a certain amount.  Under subsection (2) the buyer may pay the proceeds of the bulk sale to a trustee.  It is then the trustee's responsibility to distribute the proceeds to all the creditors, not just the trade creditors: s. 12.

 

[10]           However, resort to either method under s. 8 depends upon the buyer having received the s. 4 statement.  That was not done in this case.  In my view, the fact that had Kimoto complied with s. 4, it could have completed the sale under s. 8(1) without making provision for Sidaplex does not deprive Sidaplex of a remedy.  Under s. 17(1), an action or proceeding to set aside or have declared void a sale in bulk may be brought or taken by "a creditor".  Thus, even though a creditor is not a person for whom provision need be made under s. 8, it is entitled to apply to set aside the sale.  As there is no doubt that Sidaplex was a creditor of the seller Elta, it was entitled to apply under s. 17 to have the sale set aside.

 

[11]           Section 16 gives the creditor its remedy where the buyer has not complied with the Act.  Section 16(2) makes the buyer personally liable to account to "the creditors" of the seller for the value of the stock taken into possession under the sale.  Again, the remedy is not limited to trade creditors for whom provision should have been made under s. 8.  However, the circumstances of this case raise two further issues:

 

i.   Does a court to whom an application is made under s. 17 have a discretion to refuse to set aside the bulk sale notwithstanding there has been no compliance with the Act?

 

ii.   Can a creditor waive its right to relief under s. 16(2) of the Act?

 

i.  Discretion to Refuse to Set Aside a Bulk Sale

 

[12]           There does not appear to be any authority on the question of whether a court has a discretion to refuse to set aside a sale where there has been non-compliance with the Act.  The legislation does not expressly give the court such a discretion.  The use of the term "voidable" in s. 16 merely indicates that the sale is valid until set aside and does not of itself suggest that the court has a discretion. 

 

[13]           The purpose of the Act is to protect creditors of the vendor by ensuring that the proceeds of the sale are used, pro rata, if necessary, to pay their debts.  As such it is legislation for the benefits of trade creditors.  It places the responsibility on the purchaser to engage the mechanisms of the Act to ensure payment of the vendor's creditors.  Should the purchaser fail to do so, absent a waiver of the benefits of the Act by the creditors, the penalty that the purchaser must pay if the sale proceeds are not used to pay the creditors, is to pay the creditors what the vendor should have paid them.  Throughout, of course, the creditors still retain their remedies against the vendor, who, in effect, is the primary debtor.

 

[14]           A proceeding to have a bulk sale declared void may be brought by any creditor of the seller.  The proceeding, however, is in the nature of a representative action for the benefit of all of the creditors of the vendor.  See Busuttil v. Diamond T Trucks (Toronto) Ltd. et al., [1969] 1 O.R. 245 at 253 (C.A.).  The conduct of the particular applicant in perhaps delaying making the application, absent intervention of a limitation period, is not in my view relevant to whether the bulk sale should be declared void.  Accordingly, except where there has only been some technical non-compliance, the court does not have a discretion to refuse to declare a sale void.

 

[15]           This was not a case of only technical non-compliance.  There was no compliance whatsoever.  Accordingly, the bulk set must be declared void. 

 

ii.   Waiver

 

[16]           Even if the bulk sale is declared void, that does not determine whether any particular creditor is entitled to a remedy under s. 16(2).  Kimoto argues that Sidaplex waived compliance with the Act and thus its rights under s. 16(2).  Sidaplex argues that while a creditor can waive compliance with the Act, the waiver must be in accordance with s. 8(1)(c) by delivering a waiver in Form 2.  That was not done in this case.  For convenience, I will repeat the terms of s. 8(1)(c).

 

8.(1) Where the buyer has received the statement mentioned in section 4, the buyer may pay or deliver the proceeds of the sale to the seller and thereupon acquire the property of the seller in the stock in bulk,

 

...

(c)   if adequate provision has been made for the immediate payment in full of all claims of the unsecured trade creditors of the seller of which the buyer has notice and of all claims of secured trade creditors of the seller that are or become due and payable upon completion of the sale of which the buyer has notice, so long as their claims are paid in full forthwith after completion of the sale, but where any such creditor has delivered a waiver in Form 2 no provision need be made for the immediate payment of the creditor's claim. [Emphasis added.]

 

[17]           The Form 2 waiver referred to in this paragraph is in the following terms:

 

 I, ..............................................................................., of the ............................................... of ..................................., in the .................................. of ................................, a secured (or unsecured) trade creditor of the above‑named seller, hereby waive the provisions of the Bulk Sales Act that require that adequate provision be made for the immediate payment in full of my claim forthwith after completion of the sale, and I hereby acknowledge and agree that the buyer may pay or deliver the proceeds of the sale to the seller and thereupon acquire the property of the seller in the stock without making provision for the immediate payment of my claim and that any right to recover payment of my claim may, unless otherwise agreed, be asserted against the seller only. [Emphasis added.]

 

[18]           As stated earlier, Sidaplex was not a trade creditor for whom provision would have had to be made under s. 8(1)(c).  Therefore, the written waiver in Form 2 would not have had any application.  In any event, the fact that the legislation allows for a written waiver in certain cases does not preclude the buyer from asserting that the creditor is not entitled to relief under the Act because of its conduct.  I have not been persuaded that the principle laid down in Corporation of the City of Toronto v. Russell, [1908] A.C. 493 at 500 (P.C.) cannot apply to the Bulk Sales Act.  In that case, the Privy Council held that a land owner could waive the notice required under the Assessment Act:

 

Their Lordships concur with both the Canadian Courts in thinking that the notice must, if given, be given to the owner of the lands, though it is not so expressed in the statute.  But the notice, by warning the owner of what is about to take place, can only serve the purpose of enabling him either (1.) to oppose the sale as illegal or improper; or (2.) to attend the sale and bid at it, and see that it is regularly conducted; or (3) to redeem his land by payment of the taxes due.  These being things entirely for his own benefit, he can undoubtedly waive the notice:  Great Easter Ry. Co. v. Goldsmid (1884), 9 App. Cas. 927, at p. 936 (H.L.).  The question is, Has he waived it?  In other words, is there evidence from which it may fairly be inferred that he consented to dispense with the notice?  [Emphasis added.]

 

[19]           If a trier of fact were to find that Sidaplex agreed that Kimoto could complete the bulk sale without making provision for the immediate payment of its claim and agreed that any right of recovery would be asserted against Elta only, Sidaplex is not entitled to make a claim under the Act and is not entitled to any relief.  The agreement for the sale of the assets of Elta to Kimoto was made on May 11, 1994.  The principals of Sidaplex learned of the asset sale to Kimoto later that month.  They arranged to meet with the principals of Kimoto and a meeting took place on June 9, 1994.  It is alleged as well that there were further contacts between Sidaplex and Kimoto.  The assets were apparently transferred to Kimoto later in June 1994.  Kimoto made a series of payments to Elta, the last being in November 1994.  Sidaplex launched its application to set aside the sale in September 1995.

 

[20]           Both Kimoto and Sidaplex filed affidavits in which Mr. Moore of Kimoto and Mr. Murray of Sidaplex set out their recollections of the June 9, 1994 meeting.  Suffice it to say that there is a serious dispute between the parties as to the purpose of the meeting and the nature of the discussions.  Blair J. was unable to resolve the waiver issue on the application and this court is in no better position to do so.  There must be a trial to resolve that issue.  Kimoto will have the burden of proving that Sidaplex is not entitled to relief.

 

Disposition

 

[21]           Accordingly, I would dismiss the appeal by Mr. Lin with costs.  I would allow the cross-appeal by Sidaplex from the dismissal of its application under the Bulk Sales Act, set aside the judgment of Blair J., declare the bulk sale by Elta to Kimoto void and pursuant to Rule 38.10 direct a trial of the issue whether Sidaplex waived its entitlement to assert a claim for a remedy under s. 16(2) of the Act.  It appears from the material that Sidaplex is the only creditor of Elta that may have a claim under the Act.  However, as was done in Busuttil, I would direct that any creditor of Elta may apply to be added as a party in this action.

 

[22]           In the circumstances, I would reserve the issue of costs of the cross-appeal and of this part of the application before Blair J. to the judge hearing the trial.
