CHARRON J.A.:

[1]               This proceeding concerns the limitation period for actions brought under s. 61(1) of the Family Law Act (“the FLA”) for the recovery of a plaintiff’s pecuniary loss resulting from the wrongful death of a family member. The defendants appeal against the dismissal of their motion to dismiss the infant plaintiffs’ FLA claims as statute-barred. The plaintiff Joanna Smith cross-appeals against the dismissal of her FLA claim as statute-barred.

A.        History of the Proceedings

[2]               The late Michael S.W. Smith was a medical doctor who was licensed to practice in Ontario from 1964 until his license was suspended by the appellant, The College of Physicians and Surgeons of Ontario (“the College”), on December 2, 1992. Dr. Smith committed suicide on December 21, 1992.  On April 28, 1995, two years and four months after his death, his widow, the respondent Joanna Smith, brought an action against the College and other named defendants for the following relief:

a)         as executrix of the estate of Michael S.W. Smith, seeking declaratory relief, damages for wrongful death, damages for loss of income from December 2, 1992, until December 21, 1992, special damages and punitive damages;

b)        in her personal capacity, seeking declaratory relief, punitive damages, and damages pursuant to the FLA for loss of income and support resulting from the wrongful death, and for loss of guidance, care and companionship; and

c)         as litigation guardian for the four minor children of the late Michael S.W. Smith, claiming declaratory relief, punitive damages and damages pursuant to the FLA for loss of income and support resulting from the wrongful death and for loss of guidance, care and companionship.

[3]               A motion was brought by the defendants under rule 21.01(1)(a) of the Rules of Civil Procedure for the  determination of certain questions of law raised by the pleadings. Most questions pertained to limitation periods. A cross-motion was brought by the plaintiffs seeking, amongst other relief, an extension of the applicable limitation periods.

[4]               The first question for determination by the motions judge was whether Joanna Smith’s action as executrix of the estate of Michael S.W. Smith was statute-barred. It was conceded that that part of the action was governed by the provisions of the Trustee Act and that it was brought outside the two-year limitation period prescribed under s. 38(3) of that statute. However, the plaintiffs sought an extension of the limitation period based on special circumstances. The relevant provisions of the Trustee Act read as follows:

s. 38(1) Except in cases of libel and slander, the executor or administrator of any deceased person may maintain an action for all torts or injuries to the person or to the property of the deceased in the same manner and with the same rights and remedies as the deceased would, if living, have been entitled to do, and the damages when recovered shall form part of the personal estate of the deceased but if death results from such injuries no damages shall be allowed for the death or for the loss of the expectation of life, but this proviso is not in derogation of any rights conferred by Part V of the Family Law Act.

...

(3) An action under this section shall not be brought after the expiration of two years from the death of the deceased.

The motions judge held that he had no discretion to extend this time limit. Consequently, he dismissed Joanna Smith’s action as executrix of the estate. No appeal is taken from this order.

[5]               The next question was whether the claims brought by Joanna Smith and by the infants under s. 61 of the FLA are “derivative” in nature. In other words, must they fall as a consequence of the expiration of the limitation period under the Trustee Act, or do they stand alone to be governed solely by their own limitation period under the FLA? The relevant provisions of the FLA are the following:

s. 61 (1) If a person is injured or killed by the fault or neglect of another under circumstances where the person is entitled to recover damages, or would have been entitled if not killed, the spouse, as defined in Part III (Support Obligations), children, grandchildren, parents, grandparents, brothers and sisters of the person are entitled to recover their pecuniary loss resulting from the injury or death from the person from whom the person injured or killed is entitled to recover or would have been entitled if not killed, and to maintain an action for the purpose in a court of competent jurisdiction.

...

(4) No action shall be brought under subsection (1) after the expiration of two years from the time the cause of action arose.

The action referred to in s. 61(1), which the person injured or killed by the wrongdoing of another is entitled to bring or would have been entitled to bring had he or she not been killed, will be referred to for the purpose of this appeal as “the principal action.”

[6]               The motions judge held that the FLA claims stood “on their own merits” and were not subject to the limitation period governing the principal action. Rather, they were governed solely by the two year limitation period set out under s. 61(4) of the FLA. The defendants submit that the motions judge erred in finding that the FLA claims can stand alone without regard to the limitation period governing the principal action.

[7]               The third question to be determined by the motions judge was whether the infants’ FLA claims were prescribed by s. 61(4) of the FLA. The motions judge held that, by reason of s. 47 of the Limitations Act, the limitation period did not start to run until each infant attained the age of majority. Section 47 provides that:

[w]here a person entitled to bring an action mentioned in section 45 or 46 is at the time the cause of action accrues a minor, . . .  the period within which the action may be brought shall be reckoned from the date when such person became of full age or of sound mind.

Consequently, because of the infants’ ages, the motions judge held that their claim was not statute-barred. The defendants take the position that s.47 of the Limitations Act does not apply to the infants’ FLA claims because s.47 only applies to actions “mentioned in section 45 or 46". Neither of these sections specifically refer to the FLA right of action.[1]

[8]               The next question was whether Joanna Smith’s FLA claim would be allowed to proceed. It was statute-barred under s. 61(4), having been brought more than two years after the cause of action arose, but was subject to the exercise of the court’s discretion under s. 2(8) of the FLA to extend the limitation period:

s. 2(8) The court may, on motion, extend a time prescribed by this Act if it is satisfied that,

(a)      there are apparent grounds for relief;

(b)       relief is unavailable because of delay that has been incurred in good faith; and

(c)       no person will suffer substantial prejudice by reason of the delay.

The motions judge held that Joanna Smith did not bring herself within the provisions of s. 2(8)(b) and, consequently, dismissed that part of her action. Joanna Smith cross-appeals from this dismissal of her FLA claim.

[9]               Finally, the motions judge held that the claim for declaratory relief could not stand and dismissed the action in this respect. The plaintiffs appealed from this dismissal but subsequently abandoned that part of their appeal.

B.        Position of the Parties

[10]           The defendants take the position that the motions judge erred in finding that the FLA claims could stand alone without regard to the limitation period governing the principal action. They submit that this question has been authoritatively settled by this court and by the Supreme Court of Canada. They submit that the right of action created by s.61 of the FLA and its predecessor, s. 60 of the Family Law Reform Act (“the FLRA”), has been consistently described as derivative and held not to be separate from the principal cause of action for the purpose of determining the applicable limitation period. Consequently, it has been held that, if the principal cause of action is statute-barred, the derivative action cannot proceed.

[11]           Alternatively, if the infants’ FLA claims are to be governed solely by the two-year limitation period under s.61(4) of the FLA, the defendants argue that the claims are nonetheless statute-barred because they were commenced more than two years after the cause of action arose. They submit that s.61 FLA claims fall outside the scope of s.47 of the Limitations Act and that, consequently, the infant plaintiffs cannot benefit from the extension provided under s.47.

[12]           On the question whether the FLA claims are derivative of the principal action or whether they stand alone, the plaintiffs presented two different positions. In his oral argument on the appeal, counsel for the plaintiffs supported the approach adopted by the motions judge and argued that the FLA claims were not subject to the limitation period governing the principal action. However, the plaintiffs take a different approach in their factum.  It is conceded in the factum that, on a plain reading of s. 61(1) of the FLA, if the statutory limitation period has expired for the principal right of action and no principal action has been commenced, the derivative claims also expire notwithstanding the provisions of s. 2(8) of the FLA and notwithstanding s. 47 of the Limitations Act. The plaintiffs state, however, that the limitation period for the principal right of action in this case is not the two-year period under s. 38(3) of the Trustee Act but rather the limitation period which would have governed an action commenced by Dr. Smith for the same wrong had he not died. It is common ground that this latter period would be six years by virtue of s. 45(1)(g) of the Limitations Act. I will consider the position adopted in the factum as an alternative approach on this issue.

C.        The Issues

[13]           The following issues are raised on the appeal:[2]

1.         In any given case, if the principal cause of action is statute-barred, are the FLA claims also barred?[3]

2.         If the answer to question one is yes, what limitation period governs the principal cause of action in this case?

3.         If the answer to question one is no, does s.47 of the Limitations Act serve to extend the limitation period governing the infants’ FLA claims?

[14]           A fourth issue is raised on the cross-appeal:

4.         Did the motions judge err in refusing to extend the limitation period for Joanna Smith’s FLA claim?

D.        Analysis

[15]           The defendants rely mainly on the following authorities in support of their position that the FLA claims are subject to the limitation period governing the principal action: Swain Estate v. Lake of Woods District Hospital (1992), 9 C.P.C. (3d) 169 (Ont. C.A.), leave to appeal refused (1993), 19 C.P.C. (3d) 25; Martin v. Listowel Memorial Hospital (1992), 9 O.R. (3d) 65 (C.A.), leave to appeal refused (1993), 19 C.P.C. (3d) 23; Von Cramm v. Riverside Hospital (1986), 32 D.L.R. (4th) 314 (Ont. C. A.) and Murphy v. Welsh, [1993] 2 S.C.R. 1069.

[16]           The motions judge distinguished the authorities relied upon by the defendants on the basis that they were decided under the FLRA and not the FLA. He further distinguished the Supreme Court of Canada decision in Murphy on the basis that it involved injury and not death. He followed the reasoning of the Ontario Divisional Court in Toner v. Cherrington (1993), 104 D.L.R. (4th) 32, and held that the respondents’ FLA claims could “stand on their own merits” and were governed solely by the prescription period under the FLA.

[17]            It is my view that the authorities relied upon by the defendants are authoritative on the first question and that it must be answered affirmatively. Despite the attractiveness of the analysis contained in Toner, the motions judge was bound to follow earlier decisions of this court as well as the Supreme Court of Canada decision in Murphy v. Welsh and to conclude that the FLA claims are barred by the expiration of the limitation period governing the principal action. For reasons that follow, I do not think that these authorities can be distinguished on the basis that they were decided under the FLRA. Nor do I think that it matters on this issue that the FLA claims are founded on the wrongful death of a family member as opposed to injury.

[18]           I am also of the view that this court’s decision in Swain is authoritative on the second issue and that it must be concluded that the limitation period governing the principal action in this case is the two-year period set out in s. 38(3) of the Trustee Act and not the six-year period which would have governed an action taken by Dr. Smith had he survived.

[19]           In view of these conclusions, I do not find it necessary to deal with the third issue or with the cross-appeal.

1.         If the principal cause of action is statute-barred, are the FLA claims also barred?

[20]           The plaintiffs do not dispute that a claim for the pecuniary loss resulting from the wrongful death of a family member brought under the FLRA is barred by the expiration of the limitation period governing the principal action. They agree that the authorities relied upon by the defendants authoritatively settle the issue. They contend, however, that these authorities can be distinguished on the basis that they were decided under the FLRA as opposed to the FLA.

[21]           The FLRA was introduced in 1978. S.60 provided that a plaintiff was entitled to recover pecuniary loss resulting from a wrong done to a family member. On March 1, 1986, the FLRA was repealed and was replaced by the FLA. For convenience, the parallel provisions under both statutes are annexed to this judgment.

[22]           It is important to note that the FLA provision that creates the dependant’s right of action is virtually identical to s.60(1) in the FLRA. The right of action under the FLRA is different from that found in the FLA only in the following respects.

·The FLRA provides in s. 60(4) that “not more than one action lies under subsection (1) for and in respect of the same occurrence.” This requirement is not found in the FLA.

·The FLRA provides that, where an action is commenced under s. 60(1), the plaintiff is required to name and join the claim of any other person entitled to bring an action under s. 60(1) in respect of the same occurrence. When the action is brought in respect of a person who is killed, it has to be commenced by the representative of the estate or, where there is no representative or no such action is brought, by all or any of the persons for whose benefit the action would have been brought by the estate representative. These requirements are found in s. 61 and s. 62 of the FLRA and no comparable provisions are contained in the FLA.

·The FLA includes the recovery of funeral expenses in the list of recoverable damages whereas the FLRA has no such provision.

[23]           Hence, under the FLRA, only one action, joining all possible claimants, could be brought for the recovery of pecuniary loss resulting from injuries to or the death of a family member caused by a person’s wrongful act. Under the FLA, one or more actions can now be brought under s.61(1) by individual claimants. Under both statutes, however, there is no prerequisite that any claimant’s action be joined with the principal action or that the principal action be commenced before the right to recover the pecuniary loss can be asserted.

[24]           The plaintiffs, as did the motions judge, rely on the reasoning of the Divisional Court in Toner in support of their contention that the expiry of the limitation period for the principal action is of no consequence in the determination of the timeliness of the FLA claims. The relevant facts in Toner are as follows.

[25]           As a result of a motor vehicle collision which occurred on August 7, 1983, the parents of two infants were killed and the two infants sustained personal injuries. An action was commenced on April 2, 1987 well past the two-year limitation period under s.180(1) of the Highway Traffic Act. The action by the infants was for damages for the personal injuries they sustained and for damages under the FLA resulting from the wrongful death of their parents.[4] No principal action had been commenced on behalf of the deceased parents pursuant to the Trustee Act and it was conceded that any such principal action was prescribed prior to the commencement of the infants’ action under the FLA. Consequently, the same issue arose in Toner as in this case, that is whether the infants’ FLA claims were barred as a consequence of the principal action being barred. Carruthers J., in writing for the court, held that they were not (at 42-3):

There appears to be no dispute that the cause of action available to a dependant under s.61(1) FLA is founded upon the entitlement of the principal to recover damages from the tortfeasor within the factual situation surrounding his or her injury or death.

It is only to this extent that the dependants’ cause of action has been variously described in the authorities to which counsel has referred as being “derivative,” “vicarious,” not “separate from” or “distinct from,” or as a “claim under” that of the principal. It is clear that once created through the fulfilment of the pre-condition or requirement of s.61(1), the dependants have a cause of action separate from that of the principal. Thereafter it has a life of its own, not tied to the cause of action of the principal.

...

And, in my opinion, the fact that the claim of the mother’s estate became statute-barred after two years following her death has no bearing upon the cause of action of [the two infants], on account of which by s.61(1) FLA they are able to “maintain an action” of their own.

Thus, in my view, for the purpose of applying the applicable limitation period, the position of [the infants] stands on its own. Either it is now statute-barred, or by reason of the application of s.47 of the Limitations Act, the two-year period of the FLA does not begin to run until the infants have attained their majority.

It is my opinion that s.47 does apply and that, accordingly, the infants’ cause of action under s.61(1) of the FLA has not been statute-barred.

[26]           In arriving at the latter conclusion that s.47 applied to the FLA claims, Carruthers J. distinguished the reasoning in previous decisions of this court on the basis that they were decided under the FLRA regime. However, no analysis is provided on whether the FLA claims are subject to the limitation period governing the principal action. The defendants argue that, whatever the basis for the decision, Toner has been implicitly overruled by the Supreme Court of Canada in the subsequent case of Murphy v. Welsh.

[27]           In Murphy, the appellant Jamie Murphy was injured in a motor vehicle accident in June, 1984. He was eight years old at the time of the accident. His mother, the appellant Sharon Murphy, was injured in the same accident. A statement of claim was issued on July 11, 1986, more than two years after the accident. The statement of claim named both Jamie and his mother as plaintiffs and included a derivative action by Jamie Murphy under the FLA.

[28]           The action in Murphy was subject to the two-year limitation period under s. 180(1) of the Highway Traffic Act (“the HTA”). The main issue was whether s. 47 of the Limitations Act served to extend the limitation period under s. 180(1) for the infant Jamie’s action for damages for personal injuries. A subsidiary issue was raised, if the infant’s claim can proceed, can the mother’s claim for damages for personal injuries be added in light of “special circumstances.” The issue whether Jamie’s FLA claim for damages resulting from injuries to his mother was statute-barred was not specifically raised. However, the Supreme Court disposed of the matter as follows.

[29]           On the main issue, the court held that s. 47 did serve to extend the limitation period for Jamie’s claim for damages for personal injuries to two years after he attained the age of majority. This claim therefore was not statute-barred. On the subsidiary issue, the court held that, even if the mother’s claim were to be added to the statement of claim, the action was still out of time for her. Sharon Murphy’s claim was therefore statute-barred. The court then concluded as follows(at 1082):

Sharon Murphy’s claim is statute barred; as a result, Jamie Murphy’s derivative claim under the Family Law Act also falls. [Emphasis added.]

[30]           Quite clearly, this latter conclusion would not have been made if the Supreme Court had been of the view that the limitation period governing the principal action had no effect upon the infant’s derivative claim. If the court had been of the view that Jamie’s FLA action stood “on its own merits” and was governed solely by the limitation period in the FLA, a third question would have had to be answered, that is whether s. 47 of the Limitations Act extended the two-year limitation period set out in the FLA. However, this issue was not even raised. Jamie’s FLA action was brought with respect to the injuries sustained by his mother in the accident. Once the mother’s action for damages, the principal action, was found to be statute-barred, the court simply concluded that the same result followed for Jamie’s derivative action.

[31]           The motions judge in this case referred to the decision in Murphy and distinguished it on the basis that “the claim in Murphy was under the FLRA and not the FLA as in Toner”. Counsel explained at the hearing of the appeal that the motions judge misapprehended this fact because he was provided with a copy of the decision in Murphy as reported in (1993), 106 D.L.R. (4th) 404. This publication erroneously refers to the FLRA in the text of the reasons, as opposed to the FLA, as appears in the official report.

[32]           In fact, as noted above, the accident in Murphy occurred in June 1984. At that time, the FLRA was in force. However, the action was commenced on July 11, 1986 after the enactment of the FLA. While the parties in Murphy would be governed by the FLRA in so far as any substantive rights are concerned, the action would have been governed by the procedural requirements set out in the FLA. See s. 14(1)(c) and s.14(2)(b) of the Interpretation Act, R.S.O. 1990, c. I.11. The distinction between the two statutes was not discussed in Murphy. No issue appears to have been raised on this point. In the circumstances, I do not think it would be useful to determine whether the questions raised in Murphy affected substantive or procedural rights and whether the FLRA or the FLA governed the issue.

[33]           What is noteworthy, in my view, is that the court clearly adopted the principle that Jamie’s action for pecuniary loss was derivative in nature in this sense - it failed if the principal action was statute-barred. However, the question which led to this conclusion does not seem to have been specifically raised in Murphy. Therefore, it may be useful to review the main appellate decisions on this point and determine if these decisions were instructed by provisions unique to the FLRA.

[34]           In Martin v. Listowel Memorial Hospital, the infant plaintiff was born prematurely in 1981 and suffered severe brain damage allegedly caused by negligence in his medical treatment during and following his birth. He sued for damages through a litigation guardian. Members of his family also sought damages under the FLRA. The action was commenced in a timely fashion against the hospitals involved but it was not until seven years after the birth that the plaintiffs sought to add certain doctors as defendants. The doctors argued that any action against them was prescribed by the one-year limitation period under s. 17 of the Health Disciplines Act. On a preliminary motion, it was held that s. 47 of the Limitations Act served to extend the limitation period and it was ordered that the doctors be added as defendants. The order was confirmed on appeal to this court. The court agreed that the limitation period did not start to run until the infant’s eighteenth birthday despite the appointment of a litigation guardian.

[35]           Of significance to this case was the further ruling of this court that the derivative claims of the other plaintiffs were not statute-barred. Arbour J.A., in writing for the court, stated as follows (at 72):

...to hold the derivative claimants to a shorter limitation period than the one available to the person injured, and upon whose injuries the derivative claims are based, would ignore the true derivative nature of the claims. In Von Cramm v. Riverside Hospital of Ottawa (1986), 56 O.R. (2d) 700, 32 D.L.R. (4th) 314, Cory J.A., speaking for this court, held that a Family Law Reform Act derivative claim cannot be commenced after the expiration of the limitation period applicable to the injured person.  The court held that Family Law Reform Act claims stand in contrast with claims made under the Fatal Accidents Act, R.S.O. 1970, c. 164 (now repealed by S.O. 1978, c. 2, s. 79).  Under the latter, it was the death itself that gave rise to the beneficiaries' cause of action.  Referring to the action under the Family Law Reform Act, Cory J.A. said, at p. 703:

The action brought on behalf of Maria Von Cramm's son and daughter is a derivative one which could be brought either in the case of her injuries or her death. The derivative action is based upon and founded upon the injury which the late Maria Von Cramm suffered.  The son and daughter cannot have a cause of action which is distinct from and separate from the cause of action of their mother.  We agree with the conclusion expressed by Griffiths J. in Re Butler Trucking Co. et al. and Brydges et al. (1984), 46 O.R. (2d) 686 at p. 689, 10 D.L.R. (4th) 275 at p. 278:

In our opinion, the right of action of the parents, brother and sisters of the deceased person under s. 60 of the Family Law Reform Act is purely a derivative action depending on the entitlement of the deceased to personally maintain an action for damages in the circumstances of the accident if he had not been killed.  In the decision of Fera et al. v. Uguccioni (1980), 29 O.R. (2d) 65, 112 D.L.R. (3d) 367, 18 R.F.L. (2d) 391 (H.C.J.), Anderson J. held that the right of action of dependants of an injured or deceased person under s. 60 of the Family Law Reform Act is "clearly derivative or vicarious".

These authorities do not squarely dispose of what can be viewed as the reverse situation: can the derivative action expire before the expiry date of the principal action?  The issue, of course, does not arise in the case of death.  It arises only in cases where the person injured enjoys a longer limitation period than the two years provided for in s. 60(4) of the Family Law Reform Act.  In my view, s. 60(4) cannot be read to force a derivative action to proceed before or in the absence of a main action by the person injured.  Even though this conclusion may seem to do violence to the apparently plain language of the subsection, the proper interpretation, in my opinion, is to limit the scope of s. 60(4) to cases of death, and to tie the limitation period, in cases of injuries, to the limitation applicable to the main action by the person injured.  This is not only consistent with the rationale in Von Cramm, supra, but also promotes the sound policy goal of orderly and non-repetitive trials of actions.

[36]           In my view, the finding in Toner that, “for the purpose of applying the applicable limitation period, ... the derivative action stands alone” is clearly inconsistent with the reasoning in Martin v. Listowel Memorial Hospital. It is also inconsistent with the reasoning in the decision of Von Cramm v. Riverside Hospital of Ottawa referred to by Arbour J.A. in the above-noted passage. In Von Cramm, the principal action was subject to a two year limitation applicable to public hospitals and it was statute-barred at the time the FLRA action was brought by the children of the injured party. The Court considered the language of s.60(1) of the FLRA and held in the passage quoted above that the children’s action was also statute-barred.

[37]           As noted earlier, the language of s.61(1) of the FLA is virtually identical to its predecessor. The question raised in this case whether the limitation period governing the principal action follows the derivative action is the same as that raised in the above-noted cases under the FLRA. The policy considerations discussed in those cases apply equally to FLA claims. I see no basis for distinguishing these authorities on the basis that they were decided under the earlier regime. The amendments made to the legislation do not affect the interpretation of the relevant statutory provision. A reference to the distinction between the two statutes was actually made in Swain Estate v. Lake of the Woods Hospital where Arbour J.A., in writing for the court, observed as follows (at p.181):

The infant son of the deceased Phyllis Swain, as well as other adult members of her family, are advancing derivative claims, ostensibly under the Family Law Act, 1986. Although this has not been raised by either party, it appears that everything about this action occurred before the coming into force of the Family Law Act, 1986, on March 1, 1986, except the actual issuing of the statement of claim. Accordingly, the claim might more properly be one under the Family Law Reform Act.

However, this observation does not affect my reasons or my conclusion as to the derivative claimants’ entitlement to the benefit of the amendment that I think must be granted to the administrator of the estate of Phyllis Swain. For the reasons given in Martin, the derivative claims follow the main action and therefore receive the benefit of the extension I have concluded must be granted to Phyllis Swain’s administrators. [Emphasis added.]

[38]           For these reasons, I conclude that the motions judge erred in finding that the FLA claims stood “on their own merits” for the purpose of determining the limitation period. He was bound by established authority to conclude that they followed the principal action. No basis for departing from established authority has been advanced on this appeal. The issue then becomes what limitation period governs the principal action in this case.

2.         What limitation period governs the principal action?

[39]           As noted earlier, the plaintiffs take the position that the limitation period for the principal right of action in this case is not the two-year period under s. 38(3) of the Trustee Act. Rather, they argue that the governing limitation period is that which would have governed an action commenced by Dr. Smith for the same wrong had he not died. This latter period would be six years by virtue of s. 45(1)(g) of the Limitations Act. Since their action was commenced within this time period, they submit that it is not statute-barred.[5] In support of this argument, they rely on the wording of s.61(1) which gives the plaintiffs a right of action in circumstances where the deceased person “would have been entitled [to recover damages] if not killed.” It is submitted that the words “if not killed” in effect supersede s.38(3) of the Trustee Act that provides for a two year limitation period for any action commenced by the estate of a deceased person for a wrong done to the deceased person.

[40]           In my view, this argument is foreclosed by the decision of this court in Swain Estate. In Swain Estate, the plaintiffs argued that they should have the benefit of the extended limitation period which would have benefitted the deceased infant had she not died. In support of this argument, the plaintiffs relied on the wording found under s.38(1) of the Trustee Act which gave them a right to maintain an action for the injuries to the deceased “in the same manner and with the same rights and remedies as the deceased would, if living, have been entitled to do.” They argued that those words were wide enough to incorporate the limitation period that the deceased infant would have been subjected to had she lived. This court held that this argument could not succeed in view of “the inescapable effect” of what is now s.38(3). The court concluded (at p.176):

If the minor - or anybody else for that matter- dies before the expiration of that limitation period, s.38 of the Trustee Act takes effect and, for the reasons expressed above, the administrator or executor must commence the action within two years of the death, regardless of any longer time which would have remained to the deceased to bring an action had he or she lived.

[41]           In my view, it cannot be disputed that the principal action in this case is subject to the Trustee Act. Consequently, as in Swain Estate, the plaintiffs cannot avoid s.38(3). The court must conclude that the applicable limitation period for the principal action is two years.

E.        Conclusion

[42]           Dr. Smith’s right of action arose on December 2, 1992 at the time he was suspended by the College. The limitation period which applied to any right of action which he may have had was six years. By reason of his death on December 21, 1992, this limitation period was truncated to two years from his death. The action, which included both the principal action and the FLA claims, was not commenced until April 28, 1995, more than two years after his death. The principal action was therefore statute-barred at that time. In accordance with Murphy v. Welsh and decisions of this court on this issue, the FLA claims brought by either the widow of the deceased or his children are also statute barred, regardless of the provisions of s.47 of the Limitations Act or s.2(8) of the FLA.

[43]           I would allow the appeal, set aside the order of the motions judge and dismiss the action. I would also dismiss the cross-appeal as moot. I would not make any order as to costs.

61.-(1)  If a person is injured or killed by the fault or neglect of another under circumstances where the person is entitled to recover damages, or would have been entitled if not killed, the spouse, as defined in Part III (Support Obligations), children, grandchildren, parents, grandparents, brothers and sisters of the person are entitled to recover their pecuniary loss resulting from the injury or death from the person from whom the person injured or killed is entitled to recover or would have been entitled if not killed, and to maintain an action for the purpose in a court of competent jurisdiction.

(e)    an amount to compensate for the loss of guidance, care and companionship that the claimant might reasonably have expected to receive from the person if the injury or death had not occurred.

(3)   In an action under subsection (1), the right to damages is subject to any apportionment of damages due to contributory fault or neglect of the person who was injured or killed.
