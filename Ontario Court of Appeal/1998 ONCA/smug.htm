                     ENDORSEMENT
[1]    The appellant requires leave of this court to appeal on a
question of law under s.839 of the Criminal Code and rule 31 of
the Criminal Appeal Rules before he can seek a stay of the
driving prohibition.  Crown counsel concedes that leave ought to
be granted on ground 1 dealing with the breach of s.10(b) of the
Charter but that the other two grounds referred to the errors of
the trial judge and not of the summary conviction  appeal court
judge.  Defence counsel seeks leave to amend the notice of appeal
and to allow the panel hearing the appeal to decide the leave
issue on the other two grounds.  I agree that leave is granted on
the Charter issue as a question of law which issue was decided by
the summary conviction appeal judge in a brief written
endorsement of June 29, 1998 and that the balance of the issues
are left to the panel after amendment of the notice of appeal by
the appellant.


[2]    On the issue of the stay of the licence suspension, the
test is set out in the annotation to the Criminal Appeal Rules at
paragraph 31.1 as follows:

            The burden is on the appellant to show
            that a stay of the order should be
            granted.  The appellant must show that
            the appeal is not frivolous, that
            continuation of the driving prohibition
            pending appeal is not necessary in the
            public interest, and that to grant the
            stay would not detrimentally affect the
            confidence of the public in the
            effective enforcement and administration
            of justice:  R. v. Jay and MacLean
            (1987),66 Nfld. & P.E.I.R. 84, 50 M.V.R.
            137 (P.E.I.S.C.).  See also R. v. Smith
            (1993), 58 W.A.C. 202, 50 M.V.R. (2d)
            307 (B.C.C.A.); R. v. Quesnel (1995), 11
            M.V.R. (3d) 89 (B.C.C.A.).


[3]    The argument that a stay would not be in the public
interest is based on two main factors.  One, the fact that this
particular conviction is Mr. Smug's second conviction on a
drinking/driving offence committed after he served a 12-month
licence suspension for the first offence in 1993 and second that
he is still a recovering alcoholic.  The arguments in favour of
granting the stay are that there has been a stay in effect
pending the summary conviction appeal for two years and he has
had no drinking/driving incidents with the police during that
time.


[4]    I am not convinced that the appellant has satisfied the
burden in this case.  Although the appellant has done well over
the last two years to attend Alcoholics Anonymous and not to have
any further drinking/driving charges, the fact remains that after
a 12-month suspension, the incident leading to this charge
occurred seven months later.  At trial, the appellant
acknowledged that he drank four beers plus two double vodkas
before getting into his car to drive.  He is still a recovering
alcoholic and not completely alcohol free.  I am not satisfied
that the driving suspension is not necessary in the public
interest.


[5]    I have determined from the Registrar that this appeal can
be heard by the court on January 11, 1999.  There are also dates
available in February, 1999.  When the original stay was granted,
the appellant was facing a two-year wait for the summary
conviction appeal to be heard.  Now he faces only a two and one-
half month wait to be without his licence.


[6]    Therefore, the stay is not granted but the appeal is
ordered expedited and subject to the availability of defence
counsel, the appeal can be heard on January 11 or in February
1999.
