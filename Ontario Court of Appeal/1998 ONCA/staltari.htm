                          ENDORSEMENT

[1]  This is an appeal from a judgment of Chilcott J. dismissing
the appellant's claim for 50% of the commission on a sale of real
property in Ottawa, Ontario.


[2]  The respondent had a listing agreement for the sale of the
property. The listing agreement provided for an exclusive
marketing period to the listing agent of ten days from the
completion of a marketing brochure, and thereafter for the
commission to be shared equally between selling and listing
brokers. However, it also stated that the vendor would not be
liable to any broker other than the listing broker, and that the
listing broker would indemnify the vendor against any claim from
a "co-operating" broker.


[3]  The appellant knew of the project and the listing agreement,
and informed the respondent that it wished to introduce
prospective purchasers to the property. In response, the
respondent sent a letter to the appellant setting out the terms
upon which the appellant could market the property. The only
portion of that letter which could be construed as any form of
contractual offer reads:

          ....commencing Monday, October 10th, we will
          consider prospect registrations from
          recognized commercial real estate brokers,
          including yourself, of clients not already
          contacted in writing or by phone by Colliers
          [the respondent], with respect to our
          exclusive listing of Amesbrooke Development.
          Any such registration of a prospective
          purchaser by an outside broker, must be
          approved by Colliers in advance of
          confidential information regarding the
          subject property being provided to such
          prospects.
          

[4]  Although it does not seem to have been of concern to the
appellant, it should be noted that there is no provision in the
listing agreement for registration with the respondent of
prospective clients. In addition, the listing agreement requires
the respondent to co-operate with other agents in marketing the
property.


[5]  On October ll, the day after the expiration of the exclusive
marketing period, the appellant faxed a letter to the respondent
asking that SGM Investments Ltd. be registered as a prospect. The
respondent provided written confirmation of the registration of
SGM that same day.


[6]  The next day, October 12, Mr. Staltari of the appellant
corporation met with Mr. Levinson, the president of SGM, to
discuss the property. On his return from his meeting with Mr.
Levinson, a faxed letter from the respondent was awaiting him
rescinding the registration of SGM as his prospect, because SGM
had already been contacted about the property by the respondent.
Apparently, there was more than one principal of SGM and the
trial judge found that a different representative had been
contacted by the respondent before October 11. There was evidence
to support that conclusion and we would not interfere with it.


[7]  If there was a contract between the parties, it was based on
the offer from the respondent that prospects would be registered
if they had not been previously contacted by the respondent. The
acceptance of the offer was the appellant's letter requesting
registration of SGM. The respondent's written confirmation merely
confirmed the acceptance.


[8]  The real question is not whether the court could grant
relief from the respondent's mistake when it first confirmed the
registration of SGM but, rather, whether the appellant's
acceptance fulfilled the terms of the offer -- i.e., whether the
prospect named in its letter, SGM, was a prospect not already
contacted by the respondent. Because of the finding of the trial
judge that SGM had already been contacted by the respondent prior
to the end of the exclusive marketing period, the acceptance by
the plaintiff did not comply with the pre-condition of the offer.


[9]  When the respondent first confirmed the registration, it did
so on the basis that the acceptance did comply. As soon as it
found out that SGM had already been contacted by Colliers, the
error of the respondent in its original registration of SGM as a
prospective client of the appellant was very quickly remedied,
and caused no damage to the appellant. Therefore, there was no
valid contract, and no actionable estoppel by conduct.


[10] The trial judge awarded solicitor and client costs to the
respondent from the date of an offer from it to settle the
action. Rule 49 does not provide for solicitor and client costs
in a situation such as this, and they should be awarded in cases
only where there are very special circumstances; none exists in
this case.


[11] The judgment is varied only to the extent of striking out
the provision dealing with costs and substituting therefor an
award of costs throughout on a party and party basis. In all
other respects the appeal is dismissed with costs.
