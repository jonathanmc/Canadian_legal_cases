ABELLA J.A.:
[1]  Gordon Stuckless pleaded guilty before Watt J. to 24 counts
of indecent and sexual assault relating to an almost twenty-year
history of assaulting boys between the ages of 10 and 15.  His
total sentence was two years less a day, to be followed by
probation for three years.  The Crown appeals this sentence.
[2]  There were hundreds of incidents.  They occurred in Toronto
between 1968 and 1988 when Stuckless was working for Maple Leaf
Gardens as an assistant equipment manager, for Park Public School
and Lake Wilcox Public School as a teacher's assistant, and for a
number of local minor hockey and lacrosse teams as a volunteer
coach.  At the time of sentencing, Stuckless was 48 years old.
[3]  The details of the offences to which Stuckless pleaded
guilty are set out in an Agreed Statement of Facts, which
describes the methodology used by Stuckless as follows:
          ... He would begin by befriending them.  In
          some cases he would also befriend the parents
          of the victims, thus gaining their trust.
          The parents would allow the victims to go to
          hockey games and movies with the accused.  In
          other cases, he would befriend the victims
          and gain their trust by allowing them into
          Maple Leaf Gardens for Toronto Maple Leaf
          practices, and for Toronto Toros and Toronto
          Marlies Hockey games.  He would take the
          victims to movies, buy them meals or give
          them hockey sticks or other memorabilia.
          Many of the children whom Stuckless abused
          came from low income families in Regent Park.
[4]  The sexual conduct with the boys included fondling them;
performing oral sex on them; having them perform oral sex on him;
masturbating them; having them masturbate him; masturbating in
front of them; mutual masturbation; rubbing against them until he
ejaculated; having the boys perform sexual acts with each other
in front of him and his Maple Leaf Gardens supervisor, George
Hanna, while he and Hanna masturbated; having the boys perform
oral sex on George Hanna; and participating with Hanna in group
sex with the boys.
[5]  The relationships with the boys lasted anywhere from several
days to several months or several years.  They took place in a
variety of locations, including Maple Leaf Gardens, the boys'
homes, movie theatres, classrooms, showers, cars, Cherry Beach,
and Stuckless' home.
[6]  The various impacts of the abuse on the victims included
depression, loneliness, trauma, shame, pain, guilt, nightmares,
helplessness, fear, loss of confidence, rage and self-hatred.
Some quit school, developed  a dependency on drugs and alcohol,
had difficulties in developing and maintaining personal
relationships, or required extensive counselling, all of which
impaired their ability to get or keep a job.  Many felt their
lives had been ruined.  There were several suicide attempts.
[7]  Pursuant to R. v. Garcia and Silva, [1970] 3 C.C.C. 124
(Ont. C.A.), additional facts were taken into account for
purposes of sentencing.  These facts related to one count of
sexual assault involving 7 victims, covering a period from 1978-
1987 in Toronto; and two counts of sexual assault involving a
single complainant in Newfoundland, covering a period from 1992-
1995.  Stuckless pleaded guilty to these offences in March, 1996
and was sentenced to 14 months, followed by a period of
probation.

[19] Having reviewed the nature of the offences and the offender,
Watt J. then undertook an examination of the relevant sentencing
principles.  He reiterated the objectives of sentencing outlined
in s. 718 of the Criminal Code, R.S.C. 1985, c. C-46, as am. by
S.C. 1995, c. 22, s.6, including denunciation, deterrence,
separation from society of offenders where necessary,
rehabilitation, responsibility, and harm to victims.  He noted
that sentences should be reduced or increased, depending on
whether there are mitigating or aggravating circumstances
relating to the offence or the offender (s. 718.2(a)), and that
abusing a position of trust or authority is an aggravating
circumstance (s. 718.2(a)(iii)).
[20] He noted that it is a fundamental principle that the
sentence imposed be proportionate to the gravity of the offence
and the degree of responsibility of the offender (s. 718.1).  He
also invoked the "totality" principle, whereby in cases involving
multiple offences, the sentencing judge must first determine the
gravamen of the conduct underlying the offences, then decide what
total sentence would most appropriately reflect the essence of
the overall conduct (R. v. Jewell and Gramlick (1995), 83 O.A.C.
81 at pp. 88-9).
[21] Watt J. also took into account the following factors:  that

ANALYSIS
[31] There is no specific formula for balancing the sentencing
factors in s. 718 of the Criminal Code.  As Lamer C.J.C. stated
in M. (C.A.), supra, at p. 566:
          The determination of a just and appropriate
          sentence is a delicate art which attempts to
          balance carefully the societal goals of
          sentencing against the moral blameworthiness
          of the offender and the circumstances of the
          offence, while at all times taking into
          account the needs and current conditions of
          and in the community.
[32] In his extensive reasons, the sentencing judge made
reference to all the factors and principles he considered
relevant.  He also acknowledged that his task involved not merely
knowing what to take into account, but how to balance those
factors in a way which yields an appropriate sentence.
[33] I appreciate that sentencing is a matter of discretion, and
that a sentencing judge ought to be accorded a substantial
measure of deference.  In my view, however, the sentence imposed
by Watt J. was manifestly unfit (R. v. Shropshire, [1995] 4
S.C.R. 227;  R. v. M. (C.A.), [1996] 1 S.C.R. 500).
[34] The unreasonableness of the sentence flowed, in my view,
from two errors in principle:  the sentencing judge's view that
the component of force or violence was absent and the offences
were, therefore, on the "lower end of the spectrum" of sexual
assaults; and his conclusion that since general deterrence has no
role in sentencing pedophiles the rehabilitative factor is
correspondingly to be given primary consideration.
[35] These errors resulted in a reduced emphasis on the many
aggravating factors in this case, namely:  the pre-meditation
involved; the number of victims and years of victimization; the
history of failed treatment and denial of responsibility by
Stuckless; the abuse of trust and confidence of vulnerable boys;
the profound impact on the victims; and the fragility of the
prognosis regarding rehabilitative prospects over time.
[36] The result was a sentence outside the "acceptable range"
that was, consequently, clearly inadequate (R. v. Pepin (1990),
98 N.S.R. (2d) 238 at p. 251 (N.S.C.A.)) and demonstrably unfit
(R. v. M.C.A., supra at p. 569).
The Characterization of the Offences
[37] Watt J. characterized these offences as attracting an
aggregate sentence in the lower end of the range.  In this, he
misappreciated how serious these offences were (R. v. Henein
(1980), 53 C.C.C. (2d) 257 at p. 267 (Ont. C.A.)).  Watt J.
identified the gravamen of the charges as consisting of repeated
acts of "fondling, fellatio and masturbation upon and with
children whose participation had been obtained by breaches of a
position of trust or authority."   This is undoubtedly true, but
this technical characterization fails to capture the full nature
and scope of the offences.
[38] While Watt J. felt himself constrained not to calculate "the
full dimensions of the iceberg", he was nonetheless presented
with a factual context of staggering proportions.  These were not
unconnected, isolated acts of sexual or indecent assault, they
were part of a systematic twenty year pattern of unrelenting
predatory and exploitative sexual conduct involving children.
[39] In fact, Watt J. referred to an "overall scheme of
defilement" on several occasions in his reasons, but this scope
appears to have played little role ultimately in measuring the
seriousness of the offences.  Instead, Watt J. consistently
referred to the seriousness of the conduct by adding the
attenuating observation that there was no violence, force,
weapons, or penetration.
[40] It is true that there were no beatings or weapons used by
Stuckless, but that is because his strategy involved the
exploitation of trust, not the use of overt force.  All the boys
were either in a relationship of trust with Stuckless because he
was their coach or teacher, or they came to trust him because he
nurtured their confidence in order to ease the route to the
inevitable sexual abuse.
[41] Rather than threats, Stuckless used the irresistible lure of
Maple Leaf Gardens to entice many of these vulnerable boys into
his web, offering them access to its otherwise unattainable
amenities in order to reduce their resistance to his sexual
assaults.  It does not diminish the seriousness of these offences
that Stuckless used the magnetic aura of Maple Leaf Gardens
rather than a more overtly forceful form of manipulation to prey
on these children.
[42] The absence of penetration does not automatically relegate
the sexual abuse of children to the "lower range" of sexual
offences.  There is no question that "additional force",
"collateral crimes", and penetration are aggravating factors.
But their absence does not thereby transform them into mitigating
circumstances, nor neutralize the other aggravating factors found
in this case:  the abuse of trust, the number of victims, the
frequency of the assaults and their devastating impact on the
lives of the victims.  These offences were, individually and
collectively, unconscionable.  Any characterization which
purports to diminish their magnitude, is unacceptable.
[43] I am unable to find a single reported appellate decision in
recent years which does not view the sexual abuse of children as
extremely serious, whether or not there is penetration.  In
saying there was no violence "apart from the coercion implicit in
the relationship between the parties" (emphasis added) or "beyond
what was implicit in the interference with the physical integrity
of the complainant", Watt J. misappreciated how serious these
offences were.
[44] Sexual abuse is an act of violence.  When committed against
children, the violence is both physical and profoundly
psychological.  It is coercive and exploitative conduct, and
represents the use of compulsion against someone who is
defenceless.  As Moldaver J. stated in R. v. McF., released April
27, 1992, unreported, (Ont. Gen. Div.):
          I cannot conclude that [the accused] should
          be treated in a more lenient fashion simply
          because he refrained from the use of threats
          of physical harm or the infliction of limited
          and measured amounts of force.
          ... I must again reiterate the feelings that
          I have expressed in similar cases where the
          lack of serious physical harm has been
          advanced as a factor to be considered in
          mitigation.  The crimes of incest and sexual
          assault are inherently violent.  They can and
          often do have a crippling effect upon the
          psychological and emotional well-being of the
          victim.  Conduct which brutalizes the mind
          can be far more devastating, painful and long-
          lasting than conduct which causes injury to
          the body.  (Emphasis added.)
[45] Stuckless' relentless sexual exploitation of children
involving multiple victims and offences over a predatory two
decade odyssey, unfettered by conscience and facilitated by the
manipulation of trust, is reprehensible.  For the sentencing
judge to conclude that "the crimes of Gordon Stuckless are nearer
to the lower end of the spectrum of behaviour comprehended by the
legal definitions which apply", warranting sentences "towards the
lower middle end of the range", is an error in principle.  The
totality of the sentences imposed in this case is not
proportionate to the gravamen of the offences, and does not
adequately reflect their seriousness (R. v. Jewell and Gramlick,
supra;  R. v. M.(G.) (1992), 77 C.C.C. (3d) 310 (Ont. C.A.);  R.
v. Peterson (1996), 106 C.C.C. 9(3d) 64 (Ont. C.A.);  R. v.
Chadbourne, [1996] O.J. No. 2591 (Ont. C.A.);  R. v. Aird,
unreported, January 3, 1991, (Ont. C.A.);  R. v. Kenny (1994),
393 A.P.R. 199 (Nfld. C.A.);  R. v. Ralph (1993), 331 A.P.R. 220
(Nfld. C.A.);  R. v. Cooper (1994), 39 B.C.A.C. 227 (C.A.);  R.
v. A.(F.S.) (1996), 109 C.C.C. (3d) 427 (N.B.C.A.);  R. v.
Monaghan, [1988] B.C.J. No. 2582 (B.C.C.A.);  R. v. Bennett,
[1986] B.C.J. No. 645 (B.C.C.A.);  R. v. Cadden, [1989] B.C.J.
No. 1573 (B.C.C.A.)).
The Role of General Deterrence and Rehabilitation
[46] The second error in principle, in my view, is the
determinative role accorded by Watt J. to the possibility of
rehabilitation, and his corollary conclusion that general
deterrence is of no relevance in cases involving pedophiles.
[47] Watt J.'s reliance, in particular, on the 1979 case of
Robertson, supra, was problematic.  Robertson was part of a line
of cases holding that pedophilia was more properly dealt with
through suspended sentences and a period of treatment, on the
theory that incarceration does little to rehabilitate or deter
the offender.  The Crown in Robertson, in fact, did not even seek
a term of imprisonment at trial.  But Robertson was explicitly
rejected by this court one year later in Henein, supra, and
subsequent cases (R. v. Clubb (1984), 20 O.A.C. 157;  R. v.
Cambil (1983), 1 O.A.C. 139;  R. v. Nye (1988), 43 C.C.C. (3d)
180 (Ont. C.A.);  R. v. Veysey (1989), 34 O.A.C. 73;  R. v.
H.(W.) (1993), 84 C.C.C. (3d) 465 (Ont. C.A.)).
[48] In Henein, this court dealt with a Crown appeal from a
sentence of three years probation where the accused was convicted
of five counts of gross indecency involving boys aged 11 to 13,
with the abuse spanning an 11-month period. The trial judge
imposed a suspended sentence on the basis that he was bound by
Robertson, even though he was inclined to impose a period of
incarceration.  MacKinnon A.C.J.O. allowed the appeal, explicitly
rejecting the Robertson approach, at pp. 267-68:
          ... it was urged upon us that the case law
          has developed to the extent that it now
          supports a conclusion that almost all such
          cases as the instant one call for a suspended
          sentence plus treatment for a lengthy
          probationary period.
          I do not agree that such a disposition is
          inevitably right for these cases and that the
          abhorrence of the public of such conduct and
          the inherent gravity of the offence itself
          must always be disregarded in the interests
          of the accused, because, ultimately, the
          interests of the accused in his
          rehabilitation and that of society coincide.
          From a review of the recent cases it can be
          seen that custodial sentences in some cases
          are appropriate notwithstanding the accused
          suffers from paedophilia regard being had to
          the gravity of the particular conduct which
          forms the subject of the offence or offences
          ... .
                              ...
          In my view, on the facts of this case there
          must be a period of imprisonment imposed.
          The offences were serious and incarceration
          can be a specific deterrence to this man as
          well as properly expressing the abhorrence by
          the public and repudiation by the Court of
          this conduct.
[49] Watt J. made no reference to Henein or its jurisprudential
descendants.
[50] His reliance on R. v. B., supra, is similarly troubling.
While he correctly states that the court concluded that excluding
the possibility of rehabilitation in sentencing is an error, he
neglects to observe that the court's final disposition was to
dismiss the appeal against sentence because the gravity of the
offence outweighed the benefits of rehabilitation.  At pp. 309-
10, the court stated:
          The main aim of sentencing is the protection
          of society which is more likely to be
          accomplished by the rehabilitation of the
          appellant than by an overly severe
          denunciatory sentence.  Nevertheless, in view
          of the appellant's background and the gravity
          of his offence, the sentence of eight years
          cannot be considered to have been unfit when
          it was imposed.  (Emphasis added.)
[51] Watt J. appears to have disregarded the need for a
denunciatory sentence reflecting Stuckless' "moral culpability"
and society's outrage.  Chief Justice Lamer, in M.  (C.A.),
supra, at p. 558, reiterated the significance of these factors in
the determination of an appropriate sentence as follows:
          Retribution requires that a judicial sentence
          properly reflect the moral blameworthiness of
          that particular offender.  The objective of
          denunciation mandates that a sentence should
          also communicate society's condemnation of
          that particular offender's conduct.  In
          short, a sentence with a denunciatory element
          represents a symbolic, collective statement
          that the offender's conduct should be
          punished for encroaching on our society's
          basic code of values as enshrined within our
          substantive criminal law.  As Lord Justice
          Lawton stated in R. v. Sargeant (1974), 60
          Cr. App. R. 74, at p. 77:  "society, through
          the courts, must show its abhorrence of
          particular types of crime, and the only way
          in which the courts can show this is by the
          sentences they pass".  (Emphasis in
          original.)
(See also R. v. Biancofiore (1997), 119 C.C.C. (3d) 344 at p. 357
(Ont. C.A.) and s. 781.1 of the Criminal Code which states:  "A
sentence must be proportionate to the gravity of the offence and
the degree of responsibility of the offender.").
[52] Watt J. cited no basis for disregarding the relevant
jurisprudence, and no evidentiary foundation for his conclusion
that imprisonment will not deter the offender or others who are
pedophiles.  It is, with respect, a quantum leap to deduce that
since pedophiles have an ingrained, lifelong sexual propensity,
they are not deterred by imprisonment.  Neither Dr. Collins, nor
anyone else, gave evidence about the effect of imprisonment on
the longterm prospects for the rehabilitation of pedophiles.
[53] Absent evidence, it is difficult to justify the categorical
conclusion by Watt J. that pedophiles, unlike others in society,
are unlikely to be deterred by a term of imprisonment imposed on
other pedophiles.  This would mean that a pedophile could, as
Gordon Stuckless did, sexually exploit children for a generation
without fear that the consequences will be in any way
proportional to the crimes committed.
[54] These were not isolated acts worthy of compassionate review.
They represented a deliberate, manipulative, destructive, and
shameless pattern of sexual abuse.  Pedophilia is an explanation,
not a defence.  Society is entitled to protection no less from
pedophiles than from those who sexually abuse children without
this tendency.  General deterrence is a concept which seeks, in
part, to protect the public by signalling, through imprisonment,
a potential consequence to others of the condemned conduct.
There is no basis for concluding that it has, or ought to have, a
reduced role in the sentencing of pedophiles.
[55] This does not mean that rehabilitation, or the circumstances
of the particular offender, are unimportant in sentencing sexual
abusers of children; it means, rather, that when one takes
rehabilitation into account, it must be in the context of the
seriousness, number and duration of the offences committed.  Even
Stuckless' social worker at the Ontario Correctional Institute,
who gave extremely positive evidence about Stuckless' progress in
the relapse prevention programme, acknowledged how much more
difficult it is to treat someone with a "long pattern of
ingrained behaviour" who commits multiple offences.
[56] Not only, however, was there no evidence to support the
sentencing judge's conclusion that general deterrence is not a
relevant factor, or is, at best, a "flexible" factor in
sentencing pedophiles, there was no evidence that Stuckless' own
rehabilitation was best satisfied by a reformatory term.  It is
undoubtedly true that the six months Stuckless served in the
Ontario Correctional Institute helped him to recognize that he
ought to change.  But it is worth noting that as late as February
3, 1997, Stuckless still preferred to rely on counselling rather
than taking the chemical injections.  It was only after Dr.
Collins urged him to reconsider, that he finally agreed he would
start the medication on February 11th.
[57] Dr. Collins' evidence was that until 1996, there was no
evidence of Stuckless' motivation to change his behaviour.  Until
then, Stuckless steadfastly denied that he was a pedophile.  He
had already served a brief period of incarceration followed by
probation for his 1988 conviction for indecent assault.  Yet he
re-offended in 1992 and, according to the agreed facts at the
sentencing hearing, was still committing such acts in 1995.  This
evidence far from demonstrates that Stuckless had rehabilitated
himself since committing these offences.
[58] Dr. Collins' evidence was that since there was no cure, to
be effective in reducing recidivism, the medication required
accompanying counselling and an ongoing willingness to cooperate.
The evidence was that Stuckless' motivation appeared to have
improved substantially as a result of his imprisonment and
treatment following his 1996 conviction.  There is no evidence,
however, from Stuckless or any expert on his behalf, on how, or
whether, a longer term of imprisonment would effect this
motivation when he was released.
[59] There is no evidence that early release plus probation would
ensure his continuing motivation any more than would a
significant term of imprisonment, particularly where the
behaviour is, as here, longstanding and the offences
multitudinous.
[60] The almost exclusive reliance by Watt J. on the
rehabilitative model was, accordingly, an error in principle
based on the relevant jurisprudence and the evidence.
[61] The public has an undeniable interest in protecting itself
through the rehabilitation of Stuckless.  But in the absence of
convincing evidence that a reduced, blended sentence would offer
that outcome, the public's interest in protecting its children
from sexual abuse and exploitation is entitled to greater
deference.

CONCLUSION
[62] The sentence imposed on Gordon Stuckless was not "just and
appropriate."  The sentencing judge's errors in principle caused
him to devise an aggregate sentence that, in total, was well
below what is appropriate for offences of this gravity, extent
and duration.  The sentence, considered in its entirety, is
demonstrably unfit.
[63] I agree that these are not offences which attract the
maximum of 10 years, but they are nonetheless of sufficient
gravity to warrant a substantial penitentiary term.  In my view,
given Stuckless' recent efforts at rehabilitation, a fit sentence
would be a total of 6 years.  I agree with Watt J. that Stuckless
should be credited with 12 months for his pre-sentence custody,
making 5 years the remaining sentence to be served.
[64] Accordingly, I would grant leave to appeal sentence, and
allow the appeal by varying the sentence imposed on Gordon
Stuckless to 5 years as follows:  3 years on counts 7, 23, 30,
31, 35, 45, 46, 53 and 54; 4 years on counts 32 and 33; and 5
years on all other counts.  The sentences are to be served
concurrently.
AUSTIN J.A. (concurring in the result):

[65] It has been said that the most difficult task faced by a
judge is that of sentencing.  I agree with that statement.  This
case illustrates its truth.
[66] Gordon Stuckless pleaded guilty on September 8, 1997, to
indecently assaulting and sexually assaulting 24 boys, ages ten
to fifteen, over a period of almost twenty years.  He was
sentenced on October 27, 1997, to two years less a day, plus
three years' probation.
[67] The Crown appeals this sentence.  Abella J.A. would allow
the appeal, set aside the order below, and vary the sentence to
one netting five years after taking into account pre-sentence
custody.  I agree with that result but for reasons which differ
in some respects.
[68] Abella J.A. found that the trial judge made two errors in
principle.  The first was his view that:
          The crimes of Gordon Stuckless are nearer to
          the lower end of the spectrum of behaviour
          comprehended by the legal definitions which
          apply.
The second was that, as she put it:
          ... since general deterrence has no role in
          sentencing pedophiles, the rehabilitative
          factor is correspondingly to be given primary
          consideration.


[69] I agree that the trial judge committed the first of these
errors.  I also agree with the remarks of Moldaver J. in R. v.
McF., quoted by Abella J.A. in her reasons.  The psychological
damage occasioned in this case precludes any possibility of a
finding that these offences were at the lower end of the range.
[70] Before dealing with the second error, it is necessary to
consider the sentence imposed below.  That sentence was one of
imprisonment for two years less a day, plus three years
probation.
[71] Abella J.A. was critical of the reliance of the judge below
on the decision of this court in R. v. Robertson (1979), 46
C.C.C. (2d) 573 and his failure to consider and be guided by R.
v. Henein (1980), 53 C.C.C. (2d) 257 (Ont. C.A.).
[72] I am not persuaded that the judge below was misled by
Robertson. Nor am I persuaded that he gave undue importance to
rehabilitation at the expense of deterrence.
[73] Rather, it seems to me that the primary objective of the
sentence was protection of the public.  I rely in this regard
upon the following statement of the judge below:

          ... The goal of sentencing is not achieved
          when the offender walks out of jail at the
          end of the term, untreated.


[74] One of the terms of probation was that the respondent submit
to "injection or other administration of such sex-drive reducing
and/or other medication as Dr. Collins or his designate may
prescribe in order to control the accused's sexual preferences
for pre-pubescent or adolescent children."
[75] Protection of the public was to be achieved, first, by two
years' incarceration, and then, on a more permanent basis, by the
administration of a sex-drive reducing medication. Dr. Collins
referred to this as "chemical castration."
[76] Chemical castration is not an option normally available to a
trial judge. It became available here as a term of probation on
the consent of the respondent through the submissions of his
counsel.
[77] Probation is not available where the sentence is for a term
of imprisonment for two years or more. Accordingly, if the court
below wished to act on the offer of chemical castration, it would
be limited to the imposition of either a fine, or a sentence not
in excess of two years less a day. A sentence of imprisonment in
excess of two years could not have probation added to it and
therefore could not include chemical castration.
[78] In my view, the court below was faced with this dilemma: the
offences in issue demanded a denunciatory sentence in excess of
two years less a day, but chemical castration would provide
longer term protection for the public.
[79] In opting for longer term protection for the public, the
judge below erred in two respects. The first error was that he
put himself in a position in which he would be unable to impose a
sentence paying due regard to denunciation and deterrence,
specific and general. The second error was that he relied too
heavily on the evidence with respect to the medication in
question.
[80] That evidence was the testimony of Dr. Collins, and it
provided very little information as to the precise nature of the
medication, how it works, and the effect of it on persons such as
the respondent. Apart from the fact that it was to be
administered by injection, that it reduces sex drive and
fantasies, and that the respondent had been cleared for it by an
endocrinologist, little else was revealed. The evidence does not
indicate how often the medication was to be administered or its
possible side effects. Nor is there any evidence as to how long
the injections were to continue, that is, for a year, or five
years, or for life.
[81] The respondent never actually received any of the
medication. There is no evidence as to how, or if, he will
respond to it. Apart from Dr. Collins' statement that "it's the
best control that we have available," there is no evidence as to
its effectiveness. There is no assurance that the medication will
have the desired effect or, in the alternative, will have it to
the extent or degree necessary to protect the public.
[82] Having regard to the vagueness and uncertainty of the
evidence, it was, in my view, an error for the judge below to
rely on it in imposing the sentence he did.
[83] I would allow the appeal and impose the sentence proposed by
Abella J.A.
