                     ENDORSEMENT
[1]  There are two appeals before this court. One is an appeal
from the order of Lane J. dated January 19, 1998 dismissing the
appellant's motion for an order staying the application launched
by the respondent in the Ontario Court (Gen. Div.) (the stay
appeal). The other appeal is an appeal from an order of Lane J.
dated January 28, 1998 whereby he stayed certain parts of the
respondent's application, but also enjoined the appellant from
removing the respondent from Canada pending the outcome of the
respondent's application for judicial review in the Federal Court
(the injunction appeal).


[2]  The injunction appeal was originally brought with leave in
the Divisional Court, and was ordered transferred to this court
pursuant to s. 6(2) and s. 6(3) of the Courts of Justice Act. It
is agreed by the parties that if this court does not have
jurisdiction to hear the stay appeal, then it cannot hear the
injunction appeal since an appeal can be transferred from the
Divisional Court to this court under s. 6(2) of the Courts of
Justice Act only where an appeal lies to this court "in the same
proceedings."


[3]  This court's jurisdiction to hear the stay appeal depends on
whether the order of Lane J. refusing the stay is a final or
interlocutory order. This court has jurisdiction under s. 6(1)(b)
of the Courts of Justice Act only if the order is a final order.
The respondent contends that the order of January 19, 1998
refusing the stay was an interlocutory order.


[4]  The respondent was declared to be a security risk by
certificates issued by two Ministers of the Crown. That finding
was upheld as "reasonable" in the Federal Court after a lengthy
proceeding. The respondent has been ordered removed from Canada
back to his homeland of Sri Lanka. The respondent is an outspoken
supporter of the Tamil independence movement. He claims that if
he is deported back to Sri Lanka, he will in all likelihood be
imprisoned, tortured and perhaps even killed by government and
police authorities because of his political beliefs. The
respondent sought leave under the applicable legislation to bring
a judicial review application challenging the constitutionality
of the deportation order and the legislation on which it was
based. While his application for leave to commence a judicial
review application was outstanding, the respondent moved before a
judge of the Federal Court trial division for a stay of the
deportation order. It was his position that if he was deported
back to Sri Lanka pending his judicial review application, any
remedy which he might eventually obtain would be of no assistance
to him. His motion for a stay was dismissed ((1998), 41 F.T.R.
152). There is no appeal available from that refusal in the
Federal Court. Subsequent to the stay application being refused,
leave was granted to commence the judicial review application.
That application will proceed on its merits in the Federal Court
early in 1999.


[5]  Immediately after his application for a stay was dismissed
in the Federal Court, the respondent commenced proceedings by way
of an application in the Ontario Court (Gen. Div.). He sought a
declaration that the deportation order and the underlying
legislation was unconstitutional and an order enjoining the
appellant from removing him from the country pending the outcome
of his application. The declaratory relief sought by the
respondent in the proceedings in the Ontario Court (Gen. Div.) is
in effect the same relief he seeks in the judicial review
proceedings in the Federal Court. The injunction sought by the
respondent would serve the same purpose as the stay which had
been sought and refused in the Federal Court.


[6]  The appellant brought a motion within the respondent's
application for an order staying the proceedings in the Ontario
Court (Gen. Div.). The applicant took the position that the
matters had been dealt with and should continue to be dealt with
in the Federal Court. On January 18th, Lane J. dismissed the
motion for a stay holding that in the circumstances the interests
of justice required that he "hear the motion on its merits." The
appellant launched an appeal from that order to this court (the
stay appeal). On January 23, 1998, Lane J. heard further argument
on the respondent's application. On January 28th, he stayed the
declaratory component of the application brought by the
respondent, but granted injunctive relief restraining the
appellant from removing the respondent from Canada until 10 days
after his application for judicial review in the Federal Court
had been determined. The appellant sought leave to appeal that
injunction to the Divisional Court. Leave was granted and, as
indicated above, the appeal was eventually transferred to this
court.


[7]  It can be difficult to determine whether an order is final
or interlocutory for appeal purposes. Clearly, the refusal to
grant the stay did not bring the proceedings in the Ontario Court
(Gen. Div.) to an end, but as the appellant correctly points out,
orders may be final even though they do not bring proceedings to
an end. An order which finally disposes of a substantive right
within a proceeding is a final order: Ball v. Donais (1993), 13
O.R. (3d) 322 (C.A.).


[8]  On the motion for the stay before Lane J., the appellant
argued that he should stay the proceedings in the Ontario Court
(Gen. Div.) as the Federal Court was the appropriate forum. In
making that submission, the appellant relied on provisions in the
Immigration Act and the decision of the Supreme Court of Canada
in Reza v. Canada, [1994] 2 S.C.R. 394. It was not suggested that
the Ontario Court (Gen. Div.) did not have jurisdiction to
entertain the application brought by the respondent, but rather
that a principled application of that discretion required that it
be exercised in favour of granting a stay of proceedings without
a consideration of the specifics of the particular application
brought by the respondent.


[9]  As we read the endorsement of Lane J., he did not come to
any final decision on January 18, 1998 as to whether the
proceedings brought by the respondent should be stayed. Rather,
he declined to make that decision without first assessing the
merits of the specific claims made by the respondent. In short,
he declined to preemptively deny the respondent access to the
Ontario Court (Gen. Div.).


[10] This interpretation of his endorsement is confirmed by his
order of January 28th. After hearing further argument, Lane J. in
fact stayed many aspects of the claim. He also issued the
injunctive relief sought by the respondent.


[11] While it may be said that the appellant's entitlement to a
stay of the proceedings raised issues which involved the
appellant's substantive rights, it cannot be said that the
refusal to grant the stay on January 19th without an inquiry into
the merits of the respondent's application finally determined the
appellant's right to a stay. We find the distinction drawn by the
authors Sopinka and Gelowitz in The Conduct of an Appeal (1993)
at p. 16 apropos. They write:

          Where such orders have a terminating effect
          on an issue or on the exposure of a party,
          they plainly "dispose of the rights of the
          parties" and are appropriately treated as
          final. Where such orders set the stage for a
          determination on the merits, they do not
          "dispose of the rights of the parties" and
          are appropriately treated as interlocutory.
          [Emphasis added.]

[12] The order of Lane J. refusing to stay the proceedings was an
interlocutory order and no appeal lies to this court from that
order.


[13] The stay appeal must be quashed. We direct, pursuant to s.
110(1) of the Courts of Justice Act, that the stay appeal be
transferred to the Divisional Court and be treated as a perfected
motion for leave to appeal to that court pursuant to Rule 62.02.
As the injunction appeal cannot stand alone in this court, we
would direct that it be returned to the Divisional Court.


[14] The respondent is entitled to his costs as they arise out of
the motion to transfer the injunction appeal to this court. The
appellant is also entitled to costs referable to the preparation
of, and attendance at the oral argument before this court. Costs
associated with the preparation of the material, including the
facta, should be left to the Divisional Court.
