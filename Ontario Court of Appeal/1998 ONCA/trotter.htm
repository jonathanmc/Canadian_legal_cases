                      ENDORSEMENT
                                


[1]  This was a difficult essentially fact-driven case. The trial
judge reviewed the governing authority, the decision of the
Supreme Court of Canada in Peter v. Beblow (1993), 44 R.F.L. (3d)
329, and concluded that there had been an unjust enrichment and
that this was a proper case for a constructive trust.. The
appellant has not demonstrated any error in the trial judge’s
application of the tests set out in that case for determining
whether there has been an unjust enrichment and the choice of
remedy.


[2]  In our view, there was ample evidence to support a finding
of unjust enrichment. The appellant concedes that there was a
benefit. The evidence supports the trial judge's findings of a
corresponding deprivation and the absence of any juristic reason
for the enrichment, as those elements are explained by McLachlin
J. in Peter v. Beblow. Further, we would not interfere with the
trial judge’s decision that the contribution by the respondent
gave rise to a constructive trust. This is not a case of a “minor
or indirect contribution”: Peter v. Beblow at p. 343.


[3]  Having found that the respondent’s contribution was
sufficiently substantial and direct to entitle her to a
constructive trust, it was for the trial judge to determine the
extent of the constructive trust: Pettkus v. Becker, [1980] 2
S.C.R. 834 at 852-3. On the evidence, the trial judge concluded
that the respondent was entitled to one-half of the increased
equity in the matrimonial home between 1974 and 1984 and one-half
of the savings and R.R.S.P.’s. We did not understand the
appellant to be arguing that if this was a proper case for a
constructive trust, the trial judge erred in his determination of
the extent of the constructive trust.


[4]  Accordingly, the appeal is dismissed.


[5]  With respect to the cross-appeal, the respondent abandoned
all but one of the grounds. The respondent submitted that the
trial judge erred in failing to award a constructive trust in
half of the increase in the equity in the matrimonial home for
the entire period of cohabitation. The trial judge said as
follows:
          
          While I agree with the [appellant’s] position
          that the divorce terminated [the
          respondent’s] rights with respect to the
          matrimonial home, I feel that I am entitled
          to look at the full period of cohabitation
          and, in particular, 9 ½ of 15 years. I am
          satisfied that by virtue of a constructive
          trust, I have little difficulty in finding
          that the applicant is entitled to ½ of the
          increased equity in the matrimonial home
          between 1974 and 1984.

[6]       While this portion of the reasons is not free from
difficulty, in our view, the trial judge did not make the error
attributed to him by the respondent of failing to take into
account the period of the marriage in determining the extent of
the constructive trust. It appears to us that the trial judge was
doing no more than attempting to arrive at a fair approximation
of the value of the respondent’s efforts as reflected in the
family assets: Peter v. Beblow at p. 347. Given this approach,
the order with respect to the house cannot be looked at in
isolation and without regard to the orders made by the trial
judge in relation to the savings and the R.R.S.P.’s.


[7]       Accordingly, the cross-appeal is dismissed.


[8]  As success was divided, we make no order for costs.
