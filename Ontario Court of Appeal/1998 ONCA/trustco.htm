                     ENDORSEMENT

[1]    Mr. Tandon was an investor in a limited partnership which
purchased a condominium project as a tax shelter. Mr. Tandon
obtained a loan from the plaintiff  Canada Trustco in order to
finance his purchase of  his limited partnership interest.  He
had executed (through an agent) a promissory note to Canada
Trustco and had provided post-dated cheques.  He had also taken
the tax deductions to which he was entitled as a limited partner.


[2]    Through an error Canada Trustco maintained Mr. Tandon's
name on a list of borrowers who had not actually been approved
for loans and therefore his limited partnership interest had not
been converted from the blanket mortgage on the entire
condominium project to an individual mortgage on his own unit.


[3]    Canada Trustco issued notice of power of sale in respect
of the blanket mortgage and sent that notice to a number of
defaulting limited partnership investors as well as to Mr. Tandon
at a time when he was not in default.  After receiving this
notice Mr. Tandon stopped payment on his next post-dated cheque
and correspondence ensued between Canada Trustco and his
solicitor.  Very shortly Canada Trustco realized its error and
offered to remove Mr. Tandon from the blanket mortgage and give
him his individual mortgage on his unit upon his bringing his
account into good standing.  However, Mr. Tandon instead chose to
take the position that Canada Trustco had breached the loan
agreement it had with him.  He accepted that breach and elected
to treat the contract as at an end.


[4]    The trial judge concluded that Mr. Tandon was entitled to
treat the contract as terminated.  The trial judge further held
that as the contract was terminated Mr. Tandon was not only
relieved of any further obligation to pay interest on the loan,
he also did not have to repay the principal.


[5]    In our view, this conclusion constitutes an error in law.
We leave aside the issue of whether the fact that after realizing
its error Canada Trust offered to reinstate Mr. Tandon, indicated
that Canada Trustco was not in fact repudiating its contract with
him.


[6]    If Canada Trustco's conduct in accelerating the loan of
Mr. Tandon when he was not in default was a breach amounting to a
repudiation by Canada Trustco, then Mr. Tandon as the borrower
could accept the breach and treat the contract as terminated by
repaying the principal.  In that way the mortgage security, or in
this case, the portion of the blanket mortgage which reflected
Mr. Tandon's interest is discharged.  The obligation from which
Mr. Tandon is relieved is the obligation to continue to pay
interest or any other fees or costs to Canada Trustco in respect
of the loan.


[7]    There may be situations where a lender's actions are such
that they indicate that the lender is electing to take the
security in full satisfaction of any obligation of the borrower
to repay the loan.  However, there is nothing in the facts of
this case that would indicate such an intention.


[8]    The money was advanced by Canada Trustco so that there was
no breach of that part of the agreement.  It is therefore
entitled to have that principal money returned.  Had Mr. Tandon
wished, he could have elected to insist on further performance of
the contract, that is to insist that the loan remain outstanding
and that he be entitled to repay it in accordance with the
original terms.  He may have elected to do that had he had a
favourable interest rate or for other reasons.  In this case he
did not elect to do that.


[9]    He purported to elect to treat the loan agreement as at an
end but he did not do so effectively because he did not tender
the principal.


[10]   The appeal of the appellant Canada Trustco is therefore
allowed and judgment shall go for the amount of principal plus
interest at the rate set out in the promissory note with costs of
the trial and of the appeal on a solicitor and client basis in
accordance with the terms of the note.
