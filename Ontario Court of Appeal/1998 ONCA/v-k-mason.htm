FINLAYSON  J.A.:

[1]  This is a motion by the plaintiff to quash as interlocutory
an appeal from the order of Sharpe J. sitting as a motions court
judge. Sharpe J. dismissed the motion for summary judgment
brought by the defendant under Rule 20 of the Rules of Civil
Procedure ("Rules").


[2]  The pleadings disclose that the plaintiff V.K. Mason
Construction Ltd. ("Mason") entered into a contract with the
Canadian Imperial Bank of Commerce and the CIBC Development
Corporation (collectively "CIBC") to renovate the Commerce Court
building. The contract required Mason, as general contractor, to
obtain from each of its subcontractors a performance bond naming
the subcontractor as the "principal", Mason as the "obligee", and
the defendant Canadian General Insurance Group Limited ("Canadian
General") as the "surety". CIBC was not a named party to the
bond.


[3]  One of the subcontractors, Watts and Henderson (Alberta)
Ltd. ("Watts"), defaulted on its contract. Mason arranged for
Watts' work to be performed and was paid or will be paid in full
by CIBC. Mason now sues Canadian General for $1,500,000
representing the cost of completing Watts' subcontract together
with Mason's fee attributable to that work.


[4]  Canadian General raised a number of substantive defenses in
its pleading, but relied upon only one in its motion for summary
judgment to dismiss Mason's action in its entirety. This defence
asserts that Mason has been paid by CIBC for all amounts that it
is claiming in the action and therefore has not incurred any loss
or damage for which Canadian General is liable.


[5]  The motions judge, in very full reasons, analyzed the
documents involved and the largely undisputed facts in this case
and dismissed the motion. He stated:
                    In my view, the contractual
          relationship between CIBC and Mason,
          including the undisputed facts concerning
          industry practice and the specific
          understanding those parties reached
          concerning the bond, constituted Mason as
          trustee for CIBC with regard to Mason's
          rights under the bond.
          
[6]  He further said:
                    Given the contractual setting
          already described and the purpose of the bond
          within that contractual framework, it seems
          to me to be an inevitable conclusion that
          Canadian General must be taken to have
          impliedly waived any rights it might
          otherwise have by way of subrogation against
          CIBC. Alternatively, I find that it would be
          inequitable to permit Canadian General to
          assert a right of subrogation in the
          circumstances.
          
Analysis
[7]  The applicable summary judgment rules are:
                    20.01 (3) A defendant may, after
          delivering a statement of defence, move with
          supporting affidavit material or other
          evidence for summary judgment dismissing all
          or part of the claim in the statement of
          claim.
               Y
                    20.04 (2) Where the court is
          satisfied that there is no genuine issue for
          trial with respect to a claim or defence, the
          court shall grant summary judgment
          accordingly.
          
[8]  The parties are in agreement that the only issue on this
motion is whether the order of Sharpe J. is interlocutory or
final. If the order is final, this court must hear the
defendant's appeal pursuant to s. 6(1)(b) of the Courts of
Justice Act, R.S.O. 1990, c. C.43, as amended ("Act"). If it is
interlocutory, then under s. 19(1) of the Act, the appeal lies to
the Divisional Court with leave of that court.


[9]  It is conceded that the order of Sharpe J., as entered, is
interlocutory in form. It simply dismisses the defendant's motion
for summary judgment. In my view, the order's substance is
restricted to the determination that there is a genuine issue for
trial on a material fact or facts. In the language of Hendrickson
v. Kallio, [1932] O.R. 675 at 678 (C.A.), "the merits of the case
remain to be determined". The plaintiff's action must proceed to
trial.


[10] The defendant/appellant points out that, in dismissing the
motion for summary judgment, the motions court judge made
numerous findings against the defendant. It claims that these
findings, unless successfully challenged on appeal, would be res
judicata or raise issue estoppel at the trial of the main action.
I have referred above to two such findings. The first finding,
that Mason has status to sue Canadian General as trustee of CIBC,
is central to the decision to dismiss the summary judgment
motion. However, the second finding, regarding the equities of
granting Canadian General subrogation rights is only one of a
number of findings made by the motions court judge that were not
central to the narrow issue before him although it and other
issues were argued in support of the motion for summary judgment.


[11] As I stated earlier, the sole issue before the motions court
judge was whether there was no genuine issue for trial in light
of the defendant's assertion that Mason had suffered no loss or
damage by reason of Watts' default. Canadian General submits that
that the application of res judicata or issue estoppel to these
findings in the reasons delivered by the motions court judge
converts what is in form an interlocutory order into a final one.
In an unintended emphasis to this point, counsel for the
appellant candidly conceded that if the motions court judge had
dismissed the defendant's motion for summary judgment without
reasons, the order clearly would be interlocutory.


[12] In my opinion, the difficulty that the parties have in
summary judgment appeals stems from a tendency to confuse motions
under Rule 20 with those under Rules 21 and 22. Rule 21
authorizes a motion for the determination of a question of law
disclosed in a pleading and Rule 22 allows parties to a
proceeding to concur in stating a question of law to the court.
It is generally accepted that orders made under Rules 21 and 22
determining questions of law are final: see Wigle v. Allstate
Insurance Co. of Canada (1984), 49 O.R. (2d) 101 (C.A.) and Ball
v. Donais (1993), 13 O.R. (3d) 322 (C.A.). In those cases, the
material facts were not in dispute and the determination of a
question of law was sufficient therefore to finally determine the
entire proceedings, or a substantial part thereof. The orders in
these cases were also final because they ended the proceedings or
a substantial part thereof: see Buck Brothers Ltd. v. Frontenac
Builders Ltd. (1994), 19 O.R. (3d) 97. In the case on appeal, the
motion brought under Rule 20 was dismissed. The matter must still
proceed to trial at which time the defence considered on the
summary judgment motion, along with a number of other defences,
will be heard and finally determined.


[13] Appeals from orders dismissing motions for summary judgment
have, in the past, been heard by our court without analysis of
whether the order appealed from was final or interlocutory. See,
for example, Bluestone v. Enroute Restaurants Inc. (1994), 18
O.R. (3d) 481; and Dunlop Construction Inc. (Receiver or) v.
Flavelle Holdings Inc. (1996), 31 O.R. (3d) 58. In two other
cases, however, this court has addressed this specific issue:
see Hoffman Products Ltd. v. Karr et al. (1990), 71 O.R. (2d) 734
(Ont. C.A.) and Re: Place Concorde East Limited Partnership v.
Shelter Corporation of Canada Limited and others,  [1997] OJ.
4909. In both of these cases, this court considered the reasons
underlying the order dismissing the motion for summary judgment
and decided that there was no reason to depart from the
conventional view that the decisions under appeal were
interlocutory. In addition, both cases held that res judicata or
issue estoppel would not apply to bind a trial judge to the
motions court judge's findings. For example, in Hoffman Products,
supra, the court found that "the findings of the learned motions
court judge are not binding at trial". Similar language was
employed in Place Concorde, supra, where the court held that:

                    The motions judge in his reasons
          said: "In my view, it would be unfair to
          strike the investors' claim of equitable set-
          off at this juncture". In the context of the
          motions before him, which were for summary
          judgment, we do not regard this as expressing
          a judgment on a question of law which finally
          binds the parties in this proceeding. We
          regard any other statements in his reasons
          respecting the legal issue involved as being
          made in this context.
          
[14] When parties argue whether orders of this nature are final
or interlocutory, they appear to lose sight of the fact that
there is a remedy where the order is interlocutory, although
admittedly, it is to another court and requires leave. It was
apparent from the argument in this appeal that the appellant's
real concern was not where to appeal the motions court judge's
order allowing the action to proceed to trial. Rather, the real
concern was that, unless overturned on appeal, the findings
underlying this order would be binding on the judge who
ultimately tried the action, either because the findings are res
judicata or raise issue estoppel, or because, in this particular
case, they are so detailed and incisive that the trial judge,
even if he or she does not feel obliged in law to follow them,
will show them great deference and adopt them as persuasive. The
latter consideration may be a sound reason for allowing leave to
appeal, but it is of no concern to this court. As to res judicata
or issue estoppel in the context of a motion for summary judgment
that is dismissed, I do not think that the motions court judge's
reasons should be taken as anything more than his explanation for
finding that there is a genuine issue for trial. It is the
finding that there is a genuine issue for trial that is res
judicata.


[15] In assessing the problem whether an order dismissing such a
motion for summary judgment is final or interlocutory, I do not
find much assistance in case law decided prior to January 1,
1985, when Rule 20 came into force. I am particularly mindful of
what the Supreme Court of Canada said in Diamond v. Western
Realty Co., [1924] 2 D.L.R. 922 at 929 to the effect that a court
may look at the reasons for judgment as distinguished from the
formal judgment itself to ascertain the scope of the decision.
That case involved an appeal from a referee's report. However, an
earlier referee's report in the same case had already been
appealed and the judge determining that appeal had made certain
findings. The court found that issue estoppel precluded the
appellant from raising an issue on the second appeal that had
already been determined in the appeal from the earlier report.
The Supreme Court held that, although the judge's findings in the
initial report were interlocutory in the sense that the reference
was on-going, res judicata nonetheless applied. However, the
legal process giving rise to res judicata in that case is clearly
different from that of a motion for summary judgment under Rule
20. I believe that the best approach in the present case is
therefore to look at the legal process that Rule 20 creates and
determine if the process is intended to result in final
determinations regardless of the outcome of the motion for
summary judgment.


[16] The leading case in this jurisdiction on summary judgments
is Irving Ungerman Ltd. v. Galanis (1991), 4 O.R. (3d) 545
(C.A.). Morden A.C.J.O., giving the judgment for the court, noted
(at p. 549) that Rule 20 "substantially expanded the potential
for summary judgment beyond that provided in the former Rules of
Practice". He pointed out that the key provision in the new
practice was Rule 20.04(2), supra, which introduces the concept
of a "genuine issue for trial". Later he discussed the meaning of
the phrase (at p. 551):
                       It is safe to say that "genuine"
          means not spurious and, more specifically,
          that the words "for trial" assist in showing
          the meaning of the term. If the evidence on a
          motion for summary judgment satisfies the
          court that there is no issue of fact which
          requires a trial for its resolution, the
          requirements of the rule have been met. It
          must be clear that a trial is unnecessary.
          The burden is on the moving party to satisfy
          the court that the requirements of the rule
          have been met. Further, it is important to
          keep in mind that the court's function is not
          to resolve an issue of fact but to determine
          whether a genuine issue of fact exists.
          [Emphasis in the original.]
          
[17] Ungerman and a number of other summary judgment cases were
recently reviewed by this court in Dawson v. Rexcraft Storage and
Warehouse Inc., [1998] O.J. 3240 (C.A.). While I do not believe
the court changed the law from that set out in Ungerman, the
extensive analysis by Borins J.A. as to the difficulties motions
court judges encounter in determining whether the record before
them discloses a genuine issue for trial, emphasized the
restricted scope of the judicial process created by Rule 20. The
crux of Borins J.A.'s analysis was summarized by him at para.18:
                    In my view, the difficulty
          encountered by motions judges arises not so
          much because of any real problem in
          appreciating that the inquiry must focus on a
          genuine issue of material fact, but because
          of uncertainty concerning the role of a
          motions judge and that of a trial judge. Not
          infrequently, it is apparent from their
          reasons for judgment that some motions judges
          have come to regard a motion for summary
          judgment as an adequate substitute for a
          trial. In my view, this is incorrect and does
          not reflect the true purpose of Rule 20. This
          confusion of roles usually arises in the more
          difficult cases in which the parties have
          presented conflicting evidence relevant to a
          material fact.
          
Borins J.A. underscored this analysis in holding that a motions
court judge should avoid or at least exercise caution, in making
findings of credibility, drawing inferences from underlying facts
and in weighing evidence.


[18] The notion that an order, dismissing a motion for summary
judgment because there is a genuine issue for trial, can give
rise to a plea of res judicata is contrary to the basic premise
of such a plea. The underlying principle supporting a plea of res
judicata is that it is desirable that litigation should end and
that it is undesirable that a person should be vexatiously
pursued in litigation in regard to a matter that has already been
decided: see Society of Medical Officers of Health v. Hope,
[1959] 1 Q.B. 462, affirmed [1960] A.C. 551. An order that the
matter must proceed to trial hardly qualifies under this
analysis.


[19] Conceptually, it may be difficult to accept that where a
plaintiff or defendant moves for summary judgment and succeeds,
the appeal from that summary judgment is to this court as of
right whereas if the moving party fails to obtain a summary
judgment, the order dismissing the motion is to the Divisional
Court, and only with leave of that court. However, the
distinction has a long tradition in the law. The summary judgment
finally determines the issue or issues between the parties and
gives rise to a plea of res judicata in subsequent proceedings on
the same issue or issues. An order dismissing a motion for
summary judgment, on the other hand, determines only that there
is a genuine issue for trial and the issue or issues have not
been finally resolved. The remedy of appeal from such an order is
restricted by the requirement for leave because the order
reflects a failed attempt to reach a final resolution. It is
incidental to the object of the litigation. If the unsuccessful
party on such a motion obtains leave to appeal and succeeds on
appeal in persuading the Divisional Court that the motions court
judge was in error in denying the motion for judgment, the
Divisional Court can give such a judgment. That judgment of the
Divisional Court is clearly final and subject to appeal to this
court.


[20] Accordingly, for the reasons set out above, I would quash
this appeal. However, as the law in this area was somewhat
unsettled, I would not make any order as to costs.
