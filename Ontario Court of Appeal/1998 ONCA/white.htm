DOHERTY J.A.:

[1]  The appellant was convicted by a jury of attempted murder
and sentenced to 9 years in the penitentiary. An alternate charge
of aggravated assault was stayed. The appellant appeals
conviction and sentence.


[2]  The conviction appeal turns on the applicability of the
curative proviso as the Crown concedes that the trial judge made
two errors in law in his instructions to the jury.


[3]  The appellant is unrepresented. When the appeal was argued,
the Crown acknowledged the errors in the charge. The court could
not determine whether the curative proviso should be applied
without a full transcript of the trial. The Crown obtained that
transcript and provided further written submissions. The court
was also assisted by extensive written submissions prepared on
behalf of the appellant by the Correctional Law Project of the
faculty of law at Queen's University. Unfortunately, and through
no fault of any of the parties involved, obtaining the
transcripts and additional submissions delayed the determination
of this appeal by some 13 months.


[4]  The complainant and the appellant were both in their early
twenties. They had known each other for about six years. There
was no evidence of any ongoing animosity between them. On the
night of the stabbing, they happened to meet in a bar where the
complainant worked. He was not working that night. When the
complainant saw the appellant speaking to his aunts, he
approached the appellant and told him not to "hit on" them as
they were married women. He also asked the appellant to pay him
for certain collect telephone calls which the appellant had
charged to the complainant's number. The discussion became
argumentative and, on one version of events, led to some pushing
and shoving. The complainant testified that he did not want to
fight in the bar because he was afraid that he would lose his
job.


[5]  According to the complainant, he returned to the dance floor
a short time after he spoke to the appellant. He felt someone cut
his neck from behind. He turned, saw the appellant, kicked out at
him and chased him across part of the dance floor and up a short
flight of stairs to the elevated area of the bar. The complainant
caught up to the appellant at the top of stairs. They struggled
as they moved along the elevated area toward another longer
flight of stairs. Blows were exchanged. The complainant suddenly
felt very weak and saw blood gushing from his neck. He eventually
made his way up the second flight of stairs and to the manager's
office where he collapsed.


[6]  The complainant was rushed to the hospital and emergency
surgery was performed. He had a 10 cm long slash across his neck.
His jugular vein had been severed. Obviously, his injury was very
serious.


[7]  There were three issues raised at trial:
‚    was it the appellant who stabbed the complainant?
‚    did the appellant act in self-defence?
‚    did the appellant have the intent necessary to commit an
attempted murder?

[8]  The jury clearly decided the first issue against the
appellant. There was ample evidence to support that conclusion
and neither of the errors in the charge to the jury could have
affected that finding. I proceed on the basis that it was the
appellant who slashed the complainant's throat.


[9]  The availability of self-defence depended in part on where
the stabbing occurred. If the complainant was stabbed from behind
by the appellant on the dance floor, then there was no
evidentiary basis for self-defence. If, however, the jury was not
satisfied that the stabbing took place on the dance floor and
concluded that it occurred, or could have occurred, when the
complainant and the appellant were struggling on the elevated
area of the bar, then self-defence was a live issue.


[10] The other witnesses who observed the altercation gave
conflicting accounts of what happened. Support could be found in
different parts of their testimony for the contention that the
stabbing occurred on the dance floor, or for the contention that
it occurred at the top of the stairs on the elevated part of the
bar. Michael Ward, a friend of the complainant, testified that he
saw the appellant come up behind the complainant on the dance
floor and make a slashing motion across the complainant's neck.
He then saw the appellant put a bloodied knife into his pocket
immediately before he was chased up the stairs by the
complainant.


[11] Christos Bezas saw the altercation on the dance floor, but
did not see the appellant make a slashing motion across the
complainant's neck. He saw the complainant chase the appellant up
the stairs to the elevated area, catch him and hit him 2 to 4
times. Mr. Bezas then saw the complainant grab his neck and
appear to be in shock.


[12] Kenneth Busby also saw the altercation at the top of the
stairs. He saw the complainant pushing the appellant backward
against the rail as they moved along the elevated area. Mr. Busby
testified that, as the appellant was being pushed at close
quarters by the complainant, he struck the complainant with an
awkward backhand blow in the area of the head. The appellant
appeared to have something in his hand when he hit the
complainant. Mr. Busby indicated that the struggle continued and
moved along the elevated area towards the second longer flight of
stairs. The complainant then realized he was bleeding profusely.


[13] The complainant's blood was found on the dance floor very
near the short flight of stairs leading to the elevated area, and
on the elevated area. Some of the bloodstains were in droplet
form. There was evidence that patrons of the bar had moved from
the elevated area to the dance floor after the stabbing had
occurred. There was also medical evidence concerning the blood
flow which might be expected from an injury like that suffered by
the complainant.


[14] There was no expert evidence to assist the jury in deciding
what inferences, if any, could reasonably be drawn from the
nature and location of the bloodstains.


[15] The appellant did not testify.


The Charge to the Jury

[16] The trial judge told the jury that "self-defence may be
raised in this case depending on how you view the facts." He went
on to tell the jury that self-defence was only available if the
jury could "find as a fact" that the complainant was stabbed when
he and the appellant were struggling on the elevated area of the
bar after the complainant had chased the appellant from the dance
floor.


[17] The Crown acknowledges that this instruction improperly put
an onus on the appellant to convince the jury that the stabbing
had occurred on the elevated area of the bar. The jury should
have been told that self-defence was a live issue unless the
Crown satisfied them that the appellant had stabbed the
complainant on the dance floor before the complainant pursued the
appellant up the stairs.


[18] The second error committed by the trial judge occurred in
his instructions on the evidentiary value of the after-the-fact
evidence. There was evidence that the appellant fled the scene
and evidence that he lied to two persons concerning the events of
that evening. The trial judge told the jury that evidence of
flight and evidence of false statements:


          ... may be considered and weighed by you in
          connection with all of the other evidence in
          this case in deciding the guilt or innocence
          of the accused.

[19] The trial judge did not place any limitation on the use the
jury could make of the after-the-fact conduct evidence. He should
have told the jury that the evidence was relevant to the question
of identity and to the issue of self-defence, but that it had no
relevance to the other issues in the case. The jury should have
been told that if they were satisfied that the appellant was the
perpetrator of the assault and did not act in self-defence, his
after-the-fact conduct could not assist in determining whether he
was guilty of attempted murder as opposed to aggravated assault.
The trial judge should have told the jury that the after-the-fact
conduct could not be considered in deciding whether the appellant
intended to kill the complainant. The trial judge's failure to
give these limiting instructions amounts to an error in law: R.
v. Marinaro (1994), 95 C.C.C. (3d) 74, per Dubin C.J.O. in
dissent at p. 82, rev'd (for reasons of Dubin C.J.O.), [1996] 1
S.C.R. 462.


The Curative Proviso (s. 686(1)(b)(iii))

[20] Having found errors in law, the onus shifts to the Crown to
demonstrate that those errors caused no substantial wrong or
miscarriage of justice. To meet that onus, the Crown must show
that there is no reasonable possibility that the verdict would
have been different had the error not been made: R. v. Bevan,
[1993] 2 S.C.R. 599.


[21] The Crown's no substantial wrong argument as it applies to
the self-defence misdirection requires a more detailed review of
the evidence, including the photographs and sketches tendered by
the Crown. A trail of the complainant's blood was found beginning
at the bottom of the stairs leading from the dance floor to the
elevated drinking area and ending up in the manager's office on
the next floor. The complainant's blood was found in six places
on the dance floor. All six were within about 7 inches of the
stairs leading to the elevated area. Four of the six blood stains
were in droplet form. The other two stains appeared smeared. The
complainant's blood was found in droplet form in three places on
the elevated area at or near the top of the stairs. The elevated
area is about 2 feet above the dance floor and there are three
steps in the stairs leading from the dance floor to the elevated
area. There was no direct evidence of the distances between any
of the bloodstains. Based on the photographs, it would appear
that all nine bloodstains were within about five to eight feet of
each other.


[22] The Crown submits that the complainant's blood on the dance
floor, particularly the blood in droplet form, establishes that
he was bleeding when he was chasing the appellant and before he
caught him at the top of the stairs. This, says the Crown,
clearly establishes that the appellant stabbed the complainant on
the dance floor The defence claims that the complainant's blood
found on the dance floor could have been tracked there by the
patrons of the bar after the stabbing occurred. The defence also
submits that the blood droplets found at the bottom of the stairs
are arguably only a few feet from where the appellant and
complainant struggled at the top of the stairs. The defence
contends that the location of the those blood droplets is not
necessarily inconsistent with the complainant having been stabbed
at the top of the short flight of stairs.


[23] The vascular surgeon who treated the complainant was asked
about the expected blood flow when a major vein is severed. He
said:


          A.   If one considers arteries which are high-
          pressure blood vessels versus veins which are
          low-pressure blood vessels, it stands to
          reason that if you cut an artery and it
          remains open it can bleed much more profusely
          and much more impressively. A vein has a
          tendency to bleed and then collapse. If it's
          a major vein, like the jugular vein, it can
          continue to bleed. If you're dealing with an
          injury which has a lot of soft tissue
          overlying a vein, blood can accumulate within
          a pool underneath that soft tissue, and when
          it reaches a pressure sufficient to extrude
          the blood, the blood leaves the body. That's
          as best as I can describe it in terms of
          mechanism and amount of bleeding.

The doctor could not say whether the complainant bled immediately
upon being stabbed.

[24] In cross-examination, the doctor was asked about the
difference between bleeding from an artery and bleeding from a
vein. He said that when an artery was cut the blood tended to
"spurt out", whereas when a vein was cut the blood tended to
"ooze out." He went on, however, to testify:


          ... Mr. Fesiuk's injury was a laceration in
          the front of the jugular vein, and because it
          was not a huge hole, it was a smaller hole,
          it is not unlike putting your thumb on garden
          hose. If you make the hole smaller at the
          same pressure it will spurt more, so his vein
          was spurting actually at the time, even
          though it was a vein. [Emphasis added.]

[25] The doctor was not asked how far the blood might spurt and
he was not asked specifically about any of the bloodstains found
on the dance floor or on the elevated area at the top of the
stairs.


[26] My review of the evidence, including the photographs and
diagrams, suggests that the evidence is more consistent with the
Crown's position than with the appellant's position. To the
untrained eye, at least four of the bloodstains on the dance
floor appear to have been produced by blood dropping directly on
the floor as opposed to being tracked onto the floor by patrons
of the bar. However, absent expert evidence explaining the
significance of the location and appearance of the bloodstains
and, in light of the possibility that the complainant's blood may
have spurted some distance when he started to bleed, I cannot say
that the physical evidence renders the appellant's position
untenable.  I cannot say that any reasonable trier of fact would
have concluded that the complainant was stabbed on the dance
floor. A reasonable jury, considering the physical evidence and
the conflicting testimony, could well be uncertain as to whether
the stabbing occurred on the dance floor or on the elevated area
at the top of the stairs. If a jury was left with that
uncertainty, they had to consider self-defence. The trial judge's
instruction foreclosed that consideration. The curative proviso
cannot be applied as the Crown has not convinced me that the
result would necessarily have been the same had the jury not been
misdirected as to the availability of self-defence.


[27] My conclusion that the misdirection as the availability of
self-defence constitutes reversible error necessitates the
quashing of the appellant's conviction. I will, however, address
the Crown's contention that the non-direction as to the limited
evidentiary value of the after-the-fact conduct was harmless
error.


[28] The Crown submits that the appellant's intention to kill was
"obvious given the nature of the wound and the total absence of
any explanation." This submission assumes that the stabbing
occurred on the dance floor before the physical altercation
between the complainant and the appellant on the elevated area of
the bar. If the stabbing occurred on the dance floor, the Crown's
argument that the appellant must have intended to kill the
complainant is a compelling one, although I am not certain that
it is the only reasonable inference available. A reasonable jury,
properly instructed, may have inferred some lesser intention,
such as an intention to cause serious bodily harm knowing death
was likely to ensue. Proof of any intention short of an actual
intention to kill will not suffice to prove a charge of attempted
murder: R. v. Ancio, [1984] 1 S.C.R. 225.


[29] In any event, for the reasons set out above, I do not think
that the application of the curative proviso can be approached on
the basis that the complainant must have been stabbed on the
dance floor. Once it is acknowledged that a reasonable jury could
have been uncertain as to where the stabbing occurred, then the
argument that the only reasonable inference to be drawn from the
nature of the wound is that the appellant intended to kill the
complainant loses all force. If, for example, the jury could not
exclude the possibility that the wound was inflicted when the
appellant struck out awkwardly at the complainant as the two
grappled and the complainant pushed the appellant backwards
against a wall, as described by Mr. Busby, then it would be open
to infer some intent other than an intent to kill the
complainant.


[30] On the evidence adduced in this case, the non-direction as
to the limited use of evidence of after-the-fact conduct
constituted misdirection and reversible error.


[31] I would quash the conviction. I would also set aside the
conditional stay on the charge of aggravated assault and order a
new trial on both counts.
