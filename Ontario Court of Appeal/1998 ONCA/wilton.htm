                      ENDORSEMENT


[1]  The trial judge found that the defendant was negligent in
his diagnosis, treatment and care of Mr. Wilton. In particular,
he found that the defendant had failed to take the appropriate
patient history, that he had failed to do another ECG, that he
failed to give an appropriate diagnosis and that he failed to
give appropriate discharge advice. The defendant has not appealed
these findings of negligence.


[2]  The trial judge next addressed the issue of causation.
Applying the principles in Lawson v. Laferriere (1991), 78 D.L.R.
(4th) 609 (S.C.C.) he concluded that it was not reasonable to
presume a causal link between the defendant’s negligence and Mr.
Wilton’s death because there was “a demonstration or indication
to the contrary.”  He found that Mr. Wilton’s rare condition of a
90% stenosis in the left main artery with left ventricular
hypertrophy was of such a serious nature that his death was
inevitable. He found that there was nothing that the defendant
did or failed to do that was in any way the cause of Mr. Wilton’s
death.


[3]  In reaching these conclusions he made four critical findings
of fact. First, it was probable that further ECG’s and serial
enzyme tests after admission to the hospital would not have been
significant in diagnosing unstable angina. Second, the only way
of determining the existence of the 90% stenosis in the left main
artery which would have led to the essential by-pass surgery
would be by way of angiogram and this would not have occurred
until after the fatal episode on October 12th.  Third, the most
probable scenario if Mr. Wilton had been admitted to the hospital
on October 10th would have been that the normal tests would not
have resulted in a diagnosis of unstable angina or any diagnosis
which would have resulted in Mr. Wilton remaining in hospital
after October 11th or at the latest on the morning of October
12th. Finally, he accepted the evidence of Drs. Roth and James
that in 1987 Mr. Wilton probably would not have been given
aspirin but even if he had it would not have been given in time
to make a difference. Mr. Wilton would have died in any event.


[4]  The trial judge carefully considered the conflicting expert
evidence with respect to each of these matters. He was
significantly influenced by the critical nature of Mr. Wilton’s
condition and the fact that a 90% stenosis of the left main
artery meant that even a small thrombosis would be fatal.


[5]  This is an appeal that turns solely on the findings of fact
made by the trial judge who had the advantage of hearing the
witnesses and weighing their evidence over the course of a six
day trial.


[6]  While there was conflicting evidence on many of the matters
on which the trial judge made the findings of fact, we are of the
view that he had sufficient evidence upon which to do so. The
effect of those findings when taken together is that even if the
defendant had not been negligent and Mr. Wilton had been properly
diagnosed and treated, his condition was of such a serious nature
that he would have died in any event.


[7]  The appeal is therefore dismissed. In the circumstances
there will be no order as to costs. We do not have to deal with
the cross-appeal relating to the mortality issue. Counsel for the
respondent advised that he was abandoning the cross-appeal with
respect to the cost order made below. We wish to thank both
counsel for their very clear and able submissions.
