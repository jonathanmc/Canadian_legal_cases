                           ENDORSEMENT


[1]  On September 29, 1998, I ordered the appellants to post
security for costs of this appeal. Since the respondent, as
moving party on the motion, was successful, I concluded that
costs should be follow the event. After seeking counsels’
submissions on the issue of fixing costs, I was satisfied that
the costs of the motion should be fixed. In the end I fixed costs
of the motion at $1,750.00 payable by the appellant to the
respondent. The costs part of the order as entered reads as
follows:
          
          2. And this court further orders that the
          appellants Oscar Fisher, Jan-Jeff Investments
          Limited, Clarwa Investments Limited pay to
          the respondent (plaintiff) Lynda Wine costs
          of this motion in the amount of $1,750.
          
          

[2]  The only issue before me at this at this time is what is the
effect of the language of my endorsement and the order entered
further to it in respect of the timing of the payment of the
costs fixed at $1,750.00. The appellant takes the position that
the order contains no adverb such as “forthwith” that would
determine when the $1,750 fixed costs are to be paid. The
appellant relies upon Cory J.’s judgment in Banke Electronics
Ltd. v. Olvan Tool & Die Inc. (1981), 32 O.R. (2d) 630 (H.C.) in
support of the submission that absent language such as “costs
payable forthwith”, the costs of an interlocutory motion should
be payable at the conclusion of the litigation between the
parties after the costs of the proceeding are assessed. The
respondent contends that fixed costs should be payable forthwith
unless the contrary is indicated.


[3]  In Orkin, The Law of Costs 2nd ed., the author notes (at
para. 402) that, as a general rule, costs of interlocutory
proceedings are not payable until the conclusion of the
litigation unless the court directs in some way that they be paid
at an earlier time. This general rule is, however, premised on
the assumption that costs of an interlocutory proceeding will be
assessed.


[4]  This motion for directions squarely raises the issue when
the costs of an interlocutory proceeding are payable, if those
costs are fixed and there is nothing in the language of the order
to indicate whether the fixed costs are payable forthwith, or at
the conclusion of the main proceeding.


[5]  The authority to fix costs of a proceeding is not new.
Indeed, old Rule 659 provides that the court may order “payment
of a sum in gross” instead of having costs assessed. The 1985
rules provide explicit authority to fix costs. Rule 57.01(3)
states:
          
          In awarding costs, the court may fix all or
          part of the costs with or without reference
          to the Tariffs, instead of referring them for
          assessment, and where the costs are not
          fixed, they may be assessed under Rule 58.
          [Emphasis added.]
          
          

[6]  Although the court has long had the authority to fix costs,
it would appear from a review of the cases that that power was
not frequently exercised until the 1990’s. In Apotex Inc. v. Egis
Pharmaceuticals (1990), 2 O.R. (3d) 126, Suppl. Reasons at
(1990), 4 O.R. (3d) 321, Henry J. referred to fixing costs as a
“developing judicial policy”. It is not necessary here to discuss
the rationale for fixing costs or the process to be followed in
determining whether costs should be fixed and in fixing them. As
I have said the only issue to be decided here is when costs of an
interlocutory motion that have been fixed are payable, absent
specific language addressing that issue.


[7]  It is apparent that the objectives underlying fixing costs,
as opposed to directing their assessment, are to eliminate the
transaction costs and attendant delay involved in having the
costs assessed. If a judge decides to fix costs, but those costs
are not payable until the end of the proceeding, the objective of
minimizing the delay involved in obtaining an appointment for
assessment will be a non-factor as the costs of the proceeding,
whoever wins, will be subject to an assessment, unless, of
course, the costs of the main proceeding are fixed. Thus, if
fixed costs of interlocutory proceedings are not to be payable
forthwith, it seems to me that there is less reason to fix them
in the first place. Fixing the costs of an interlocutory motion
involves additional judicial time to determine the quantum of the
fixed costs.1  It thus seems to me on balance that where costs
are fixed those costs should be payable forthwith unless there
is, in the discretion of the motions judge, a proper basis (such
as preventing a meritorious action from going to trial) not to
require immediate payment. However, in order to avoid confusion,
the order should clearly indicate that this is so. In my opinion,
the rule in Banke Electronics with respect to the payment of
costs ought to be followed where costs are fixed. Absent language
to the contrary, fixed costs of interlocutory proceedings should
not be payable forthwith. This will avoid one payment rule for
fixed costs and another where costs are not fixed. As a matter of
the general approach to be taken where costs of interlocutory
motions are fixed, in my view, absent good reason not to require
those costs to be paid forthwith, the order should specifically
require payment to be forthwith.


[8]  There was no “payment forthwith” direction in my endorsement
on the respondent’s motion for security for costs or in the
entered order. Thus, it seems to me that the costs of the motion
are not payable until the appeal has been completed.


[9]  There should be no costs of this motion for directions.
