AUSTIN J.A.:


[1]                           This matter concerns rent increases imposed during the period June 1, 1988, to May 31, 1995. The Rent Officer found that this period exceeded the review period permitted by the Rent Control Act, S.O. 1992, c. 11, s. 30(12), namely, six years before the date of filing of the tenant's application. As a result, the review was restricted to the period March 1, 1989, to February 27, 1995, plus the period after the application was filed.

[2]                           The general issue was whether the notices of increase given complied with the relevant Acts. These were the Residential Rent Regulation Act, R.S.O. 1990, c. R.29 ("the R.R.R. Act"), which was applicable until August 9, 1992, and the Rent Control Act, S.O. 1992, c. 11 ("the R.C. Act"), which was applicable thereafter.

[3]                           The notice given for the rent increase commencing March 1, 1993, is duplicated on appendix 1 to these reasons. It was agreed that the other notices were in the same form. There were seven apartment units in the building. The notice was not addressed to any particular tenant. Approximately sixty days before the increase was to come into effect, according to the notice, it was posted on the box where the tenants placed their rental payments.

[4]                           In compliance with these notices, the tenant paid rent as follows:

June 1, 1988                                            -     $309.00

July 1, 1988 - February 28, 1989          -     310.00 per month

March 1, 1989 - February 28, 1990      -     324.00 per month

March 1, 1990 - February 28, 1991      -     339.00 per month    

March 1, 1991 - February 29, 1992      -     357.00 per month

March 1, 1992 - January 31, 1993        -     378.00 per month

[5]                           All of these increases were in issue. The Rent Officer found all to be illegal by reason of the failure of the landlord to provide the ninety-day notice of increase required by the applicable legislation.

[6]                           The result of this finding was that the tenant had paid $4,632 more in rent than the landlord was entitled to receive. The landlord was ordered to repay that amount to the tenant, together with interest of $151.29 (9% of $4,632 from February 27, 1995, the date of filing of the application, to July 13, 1995, the day before the date of the rent order).

[7]                           The Divisional Court found that the notices of rent increases were not effective according to their terms (60 days after posting), but were effective 90 days after given, with the result that the excess payment over the period in question was $68. The landlord was ordered to deduct that amount from the next month's rent.

[9]                           Both Acts prescribe a form for the giving of notice of a rent increase. Those forms are set out as appendices 2 and 3 to these reasons.

[10]                       The first three increases fall to be considered in relation to the provisions of the R.R.R. Act. Clearly, those notices do not comply with s. 5(1) of that Act in that they:

(a)             are not in the prescribed form;

(b)             they do not express the increase in terms of both dollars and percentage of the current rent; and

(c)             do not give 90 days notice of the increase, all as required by s. 5(1).

[11]                       The remaining increases fall for consideration under ss. 7 and 91 and 98 of the R.C. Act. On their face those notices do not comply with s. 7 in that they:

(a)             are not in the prescribed form;

(b)             do not contain the information required by the prescribed form, including current rent, increased rent, guideline for rent increases, relationship of increase to guideline, and maximum rent chargeable; and

(c)             do not give 90 days notice of the increase.

[12]                       Although in slightly different terms, s. 5(2) of the R.R.R. Act and s. 7(5) of the R.C. Act provide that an increase in rent is void where the notice required by the Act has not been given. In my view, "void" in those sections means empty, spent and of no effect. Had the Legislature intended the result achieved by the decision of the Divisional Court, it could have stated that "no increase shall become effective until ninety days from the giving of notice," or words to that effect. In my opinion, the intention of the Legislature was to void the increase, not merely to delay it.


[13]                       Whether s. 98 of the R.C. Act applies only to the later increases or to all of them, it does not, in my view, assist the landlord because the notices given fall woefully short of "substantial compliance" with the forms prescribed by the Acts.

[14]                       In my view, neither s. 49 of the R.R.R. Act, nor s. 91 of the R.C. Act is of any assistance. The landlord's position is that he simply followed a practice established by his predecessor. That may provide an explanation, but not a justification for the kind of notice given.

[15]                       The courts have held consistently that, without notice in accordance with statutory requirements, rent increases are void, in the sense that they are without legal effect and not merely postponed. See Re Symons and Alexander (1986), 55 O.R. (2d) 395 at 397-98 (Sup. Ct.); Re Devitt and Sarochyn (1976), 12 O.R. (2d) 652 at 655 (Div. Ct.); aff'd. (1977), 14 O.R. (2d) 135 (C.A.); Re Kasprzycki and Abel (1986), 55 O.R. (2d) 143 (Dist. Ct.); Re Di Petta and Mardarowicz (1989), 69 O.R. (2d) 143 (Dist. Ct.).

[16]                       In Re Massicotte Bros. Holdings Ltd. and Beales, released January 26, 1996, a different panel of the Divisional Court considered a fact situation similar to that in this appeal and reached an opposite conclusion. In that case, the landlord provided a notice of rent increase 47 days in advance of the effective date. When the tenant refused to pay the increase, the landlord applied to the Ontario Court (General Division) for a termination of the tenancy and the trial judge found that there had been substantial compliance with the notice of rent increase. The Divisional Court, however, did not find that the notice became valid at another date but stated, in allowing the tenant's appeal:

The clear implication of s. 5(1) of the R.R.R.A is that a notice of increase in rent in the case of a periodic tenancy must state that the increase will be effective not earlier than the end of a period occurring at least 90 days after the giving of the notice. The notice given by the landlord in this case in which there was a monthly tenancy provided that the increase would be effective about 47 days after the date of the notice. The notice did not comply with s. 5(1). Under s. 5(2) the increase is void.


[17]                       In my view, a fundamental objective of both Acts is to clearly define the amount and the circumstances in which rents may be increased by a landlord. Consistent with this objective, the legislation provides that a tenant shall be given at least 90 days notice, in the prescribed form, of a landlord's intention to increase the rent. As the Divisional Court held in Re Devitt and Sarochyn, supra, the purpose of the 90-day period is to allow a tenant sufficient time to accept the increase, or to serve the required 60-day notice to terminate the tenancy.

[18]                       This interpretation is not only in accordance with the language of the two Acts, but is also consistent with the policy objective the legislation is designed to achieve. The general purpose of the R.R.R.Act and the R.C.Act is to regulate and control residential rents. For example, under the R.C.Act:

Ë          no landlord shall charge rent that exceeds the amount permitted under the Act or as ordered by a rent officer (s. 5(2));

Ë          landlords may not raise rents above a prescribed amount without first making an application to a rent officer (s. 11(1));

Ë          rent increases above the prescribed amount may only be allowed by a rent officer in specific, defined circumstances, for example, where the landlord can demonstrate extraordinary operating expenses or eligible capital expendistures (ss. 13-18);

Ë          landlords may raise the rent only once in any 12-month period (s. 6);

Ë          landlords must provide 90 days notice in the prescribed form before imposing any rent increase (s. 7).


[19]                       In my view, the reasoning of the Divisional Court effectively disregards the clear language of s. 7(1)(2)(5) and of the R.C. Act. Adherence to the requirements of this legislation means that there will be a level playing field for all residential landlords and tenants. The form prescribed by s. 7(2) serves an important notice-giving function and leaves a tenant with no doubt of the date of the intended rent increase, the amount of the increase, the calculation of the increase and the components of the new rent. As well, it provides additional important information. To replace the form with a notice similar to the one used by the landlord in this appeal is to ignore s. 7(2). The purpose of s. 7(5), which voids a rent increase where a landlord has failed to comply with s. 7(1) and (2), is to encourage compliance by the landlords with these provisions. With respect, if the Divisional Court's reasoning were adopted, there would be a greatly reduced incentive to landlords to comply with these important notice requirements, as a landlord could give less than 90 days notice and ignore the prescribed form with the knowledge that if a tenant objected, at worst, only a short period of the increase would be disallowed.

[20]                       I would allow the appeal, set aside the order of the Divisional Court and restore the order of the Rent Officer.

[21]                       The order granting leave to appeal provides that there are to be no costs of this appeal. Accordingly, there will be no order as to costs.
