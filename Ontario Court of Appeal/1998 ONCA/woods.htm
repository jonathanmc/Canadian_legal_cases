                      ENDORSEMENT
[1]   The  test  for  unconscionability is a high  one.   On  the
evidence,  the  trial judge was not persuaded that the  threshold
was  met  and we see no basis for interfering with his conclusion
that  no  amount  should be awarded higher than the  equalization
payment.   Nor  can  we  see any basis for interfering  with  his
conclusion   that   the  respondent,  while   enjoying   a   good
relationship   with  Tanya,  did  not  demonstrate   a   “settled
attention” within the meaning of the Act.  This is a question  of
fact and on the facts before him the trial judge was entitled  to
reach the conclusion he did.


[2]  The question of whether the $65,000 was a gift or a loan was
also  based  on findings of fact and credibility.  We cannot  say
that  the  trial judge was wrong to conclude this amount  was  in
fact  a  gift to the appellant from her mother.  We are, however,
of  the  view that he erred in his conclusion that the  appellant
ought  to  pay  occupation rent and account for  rents  received.
Given  the  conduct  of  the  respondent  since  the  separation,
including  the  fact  that the appellant  received  virtually  no
financial assistance from the respondent, it would inequitable to
require her to pay occupation rent or account for rents received.
By not looking at the surrounding circumstances since separation,
and  the extent to which the respondent’s conduct interfered with
the  marketability  of the home, the trial  judge  erred  in  the
exercise of his discretion in connection with these two issues.


[3]  Accordingly, the appeal is allowed to the extent of deleting
paragraphs 3 and 4 of the formal order.  Otherwise the appeal  is
dismissed.


[4]  In the circumstances, although representations ought to have
been made, the conclusion with respect to costs was not in error.
Accordingly, the cross-appeal is also dismissed.  There  will  be
no costs of the appeal or the cross-appeal.
