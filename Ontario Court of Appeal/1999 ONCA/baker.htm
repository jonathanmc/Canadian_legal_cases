                          ENDORSEMENT

[1]  The appellant seeks to appeal from an order a) denying leave
to amend his Statement of Claim b) refusing to determine the
admissibility in evidence at trial of correspondence between
counsel and c) striking out portions of his pleading. The
respondent brings a motion to quash the appeal on several
grounds.


[2]  In our view, the order denying leave to amend the pleadings
is clearly interlocutory in nature and this court has no
jurisdiction to entertain an appeal from that decision.


[3]  While we are inclined to the view that the appeal with
respect to the question of admissibility of evidence is without
merit as this is a matter for the trial judge to decide, we do
not need to address the question of merit because the order in
this respect is also interlocutory in nature and no appeal
therefrom lies to this court.


[4]  It is also our view that the appeal from the order striking
out parts of the Statement of Claim should be quashed.


[5]  Pleadings referring to a duty to act fairly are relevant to
the cause of action for damages for wrongful dismissal as
determined in Wallace v. United Grain Growers Limited, [1997] 3
S.C.R. 701. However, as the Supreme Court determined in Wallace,
there is no independent cause of action for Abad faith
discharge@.


[6]  Consequently, the order striking out the references to a
duty to act fairly, insofar as the pleadings related to the cause
of action for damages for wrongful dismissal, is interlocutory in
nature and this court has no jurisdiction to entertain the
appeal. Insofar as the pleadings purported to raise a separate
cause of action based on a duty to act fairly, the order is
clearly correct and any appeal from the order in that respect is
devoid of merit.


[7]  In the result, the motion to quash must succeed. The motion
is allowed with costs and the appeal is quashed.


[8]  In the circumstances, there is no need to deal with the
cross-motion seeking an extension of time. It is dismissed
without costs.
