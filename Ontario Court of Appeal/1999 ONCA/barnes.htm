COURT OF APPEAL FOR ONTARIO
RE:            HER MAJESTY THE QUEEN (Respondent) v.
               JEFFREY BARNES (Appellant)

HEARD:         January 7, 1999

                      ENDORSEMENT
                                

[1]  The appellant appeals his conviction and sentence by a court
composed of Dunn J. and a jury at Brampton. At the conclusion of
argument of this appeal, the court indicated that the appeal from
conviction for dangerous driving causing death and causing bodily
harm was dismissed, that the appeal from conviction for impaired
operation was allowed and the conviction set aside, and that the
appeal from sentence was allowed and the sentence reduced to two
years less one day imprisonment. These are the reasons for that
disposition.
          
          1.   The application for a mistrial


[2]  Prior to trial, Crown counsel decided not to tender any
evidence of the results of testing of the appellant’s blood
alcohol level. It appears that the Crown had determined that the
results would be ruled inadmissible because of a violation of the
appellant’s rights under s. 10(b) of the Charter of Rights and
Freedoms. In his opening address, counsel for the Crown at trial,
Mr. Raftery, set out the evidence he anticipated would come from
civilian witnesses as to the appellant’s statement of impairment.
He then said the following:
          
          The police arrived, and, because of certain
          rulings, you are not going to hear much in
          the way of actual police first hand evidence.

[3]       Following the opening address, defence counsel
immediately moved for a mistrial. Crown counsel acknowledged that
the remark was improper but submitted that any prejudice to the
appellant could be cured by an appropriate instruction to the
jury. The trial judge agreed and dismissed the motion for a
mistrial.


[4]       The remark by Crown counsel was inaccurate and highly
improper. There had been no rulings of any kind. More
importantly, it was entirely wrong for Crown counsel to suggest
to the jury that they would be deprived of hearing certain
evidence because of legal rulings by the trial judge. Given that
the proceedings were at such an early stage, the trial judge, out
of an abundance of caution, might well have declared a mistrial.
However, the trial judge was of the view that any prejudice could
be cured by an appropriate instruction to the jury and we have
not been persuaded that he improperly exercised his discretion to
refuse the mistrial.
          
          2.   The inadmissible hearsay


[5]       Two civilian witnesses testified that the appellant
seemed to be drunk. They were extensively cross-examined to the
effect that they had not told this to the investigating officer.
Both witnesses claimed that they had told the officer, Constable
Mullen, but that he had apparently not included these
observations in their statements. For example, the following
occurred with the witness Grewal:
          
          Q.   And, so you are saying that at that time
          you never told him why you thought that, you
          never told him what you observed, in other
          words, that night?
          
          A.   No that’s why I just, only just told him
          he seemed to be drunk, he said, when he was
          writing report, he said, Oh, we’ll find out
          [the] proper way, you know whatever, blood or
          breathalyser. [Emphasis added.]

[6]       In the absence of the jury, defence counsel again moved
for a mistrial. Crown counsel resisted the application and
submitted that any prejudice could be cured by an appropriate
jury instruction. The trial judge agreed and dismissed the
application.


[7]       Much the same thing occurred with the witness Levy. In
re-examination, he was asked if, when he took the statement,
Constable Mullen had directed the witness’ attention to any
particular area. Levy replied:
          
          The gentleman was drunk and driving and the
          police even tested him, the police even
          tested him and said— [Emphasis added.]

[8]       Crown counsel then cut the witness off and instructed
the witness not to discuss what the police did. Defence counsel
asked for a mistrial, which was again refused. The trial judge
gave these reasons:
          
          Counsel, as I say again, I can’t agree that
          there is a potential for injustice in the
          matter. The other side is the thing that
          would occur to me. There’s a potential that
          it won’t work to the benefit of the accused
          but I, so far I haven’t had any concerns
          especially, hopefully, knowing that the jury
          know what they know, what evidence they must
          weigh, and, when I finish the charge, they
          will know even better. [Emphasis added.]

[9]       We would not interfere with the trial judge’s decision
refusing a mistrial. The trial judge was in the best position to
determine the effect of the impugned answers. We also note that
it is not entirely clear that the comments, especially those by
the witness Grewal, were inadmissible. Defence counsel was
pursuing Grewal on his alleged failure to tell the officer of his
observations as to the appellant’s condition at the scene. This
was an allegation of recent fabrication. The witness was entitled
to rebut the allegation by testifying that he had in fact told
the officer and to provide at least some of the context in which
he had done so. The comments were admissible for that limited
purpose. They were obviously not admissible, however, as proof
that the police had conducted tests or that the tests had proved
that the appellant was impaired. Accordingly, it would have been
preferable if the witnesses had been instructed, in the absence
of the jury, not to refer to any testing by the police.


[10] Although the trial judge indicated that he would cure any
possible prejudice in his jury instructions, he failed to do so.
On three occasions, the trial judge instructed the jury that they
were to be governed “solely by the evidence introduced at the
trial and by absolutely nothing else”. In the circumstances of
this case that was not sufficient. The problem was that the jury
had heard some “evidence introduced at the trial” that was either
not admissible or was admissible only for a limited purpose.
Particularly in light of Crown counsel’s opening comments, it was
essential that the trial judge direct the jury in the clearest of
terms that there was no evidence of any testing by the police.
The jury should have been told to ignore the hearsay comments by
the witnesses Levy and Grewal who obviously would have had no
first-hand knowledge of any investigation performed by the
police.


[11] In view of this error, the conviction for impaired operation
cannot stand. Ordinarily, the appropriate disposition would be a
new trial. However, the admissible evidence of impairment was
slight. The appellant had been injured in the accident and the
observations of the civilian witnesses are consistent with the
effects of the accident. In the circumstances, we would allow the
appeal, set aside the conviction for impaired operation and enter
an acquittal. In view of this disposition, it is unnecessary to
deal with the submission that, in any event, this verdict is
perverse.


[12] We are satisfied that this error did not affect the validity
of the convictions for dangerous driving causing death and
causing bodily harm. The jury acquitted the appellant of the
offences of impaired operation causing death and causing bodily
harm and convicted only of impaired operation simpliciter. The
trial judge instructed the jurors that to convict of the full
offences they need only find that the appellant’s impairment was
a contributing cause of death, outside the de minimus range. By
its verdict, the jury was obviously not satisfied that any
impairment contributed to the accident, which was solely
attributable to the other causes such as excessive speed. The
evidence that the collision was caused by the appellant’s
dangerous driving was overwhelming in this case.
          
          3.   The Crown’s closing address

[13] The appellant submits that Crown counsel’s jury address was
improper by suggesting that there was some onus on the defence to
call Constable Mullen and that not having done so the jury could
infer that his evidence would not contradict the witnesses Grewal
and Levy. By the end of the trial, there was no evidence to
contradict Grewal and Levy in their assertions that they had told
Mullen about their observations of the appellant at the scene. It
was open to Crown counsel to point that out to the jury. We have
not been persuaded that the comments by Crown counsel were
improper or misleading. In particular, we are not satisfied that
Mullen’s preliminary hearing testimony conclusively establishes
that Grewal and Levy did not tell him about the appellant’s
condition. This part of the jury address is at worst confusing.
[14] Crown counsel also said the following in his address:
          
          As counsel for the Crown I don’t have an
          individual client. I have a duty in placing
          before you evidence upon which you must
          render your decision. If the Crown wanted to
          keep out evidence…the Crown did not have to
          call, for instance, a witness who would say,
          “No, I didn’t hear slurring of speech.”  But
          the Crown did, because the Crown’s duty here
          is to place before you—let’s put it this way:
          The Crown’s ultimate duty to society is to
          see that justice is done, not representing an
          accused. I have tried to do so in a manner
          which is consistent with that duty that has
          to be done.

[15] This kind of comment should be avoided. It may be
misinterpreted by the jury and leave the jury with the impression
that the Crown would not have brought the prosecution if the
accused were not guilty. The comments also unfairly diminish the
role of defence counsel. As Hoyt C.J.N.B. observed in R. v. Pitt
(1996), 109 C.C.C. (3d) 488 (N.B.C.A.), leave to appeal to
Supreme Court of Canada refused (1997), 211 N.R. 319n., at p. 504
“Crown counsel’s performance should render a description of his
or her role unnecessary”. However, as in Pitt, we are not
satisfied that the comments prejudiced the appellant’s trial.
          
          4.   Improper remarks by Crown counsel


[16] On several occasions, while defence counsel was attempting
to cross-examine Crown witnesses, Mr. Raftery interjected with
what can only be characterized as smart-aleck comments. These
comments were unhelpful and demeaning of his office and the trial
judge ought to have warned counsel to desist. However, these
inappropriate interjections did not prejudice the appellant’s
case nor deprive him of a fair trial.
          
          5.   Charge to the jury on reasonable doubt


[17] This case was tried prior to the release of the decision of
the Supreme Court of Canada in R. v. Lifchus (1997), 118 C.C.C.
(3d) 1 and thus does not entirely conform with the model charge
proposed in that decision. The appellant submits that there was
misdirection because the trial judge directed the jury that proof
beyond a reasonable doubt is attained when the jurors feel “sure”
or “certain” of the accused’s guilt but that mathematical
certainty is not essential. Counsel for the appellant argues that
this direction is fatal since it was not coupled with a direction
that proof beyond a reasonable doubt requires more than “probable
guilt”. He relies upon the dissenting reasons of Twaddle J.A. in
R. v. Starr (1998), 123 C.C.C. (3d) 145 (Man. C.A.). [An appeal
to the Supreme Court of Canada based on the dissent was heard on
December 3, 1998 and judgment reserved.]


[18] We would not give effect to this ground of appeal. The
charge to the jury in this case conforms to the charge in common
use in this province prior to Lifchus. This court has upheld the
use of these directions on several occasions:  See, for example,
R. v. Arsenault, [1997] O.J. No. 3977 (Q.L.), leave to appeal to
Supreme Court of Canada dismissed April 2, 1998 and R. v.
Atkinson, [1997] O.J. No. 4788 (QL). We are not persuaded that
the jury was misled as to the burden of proof on the Crown or of
the meaning of reasonable doubt.


[19] Accordingly, the appeal from conviction for dangerous
operation causing death and causing bodily harm is dismissed.
          
          6.   The sentence appeal


[20] The appellant was sentenced to three years' imprisonment on
the dangerous operation charges. These were serious offences and,
in view of the appellant’s prior record, deserving of a
substantial prison sentence. However, in our view, the trial
judge erred in principle in imposing sentence upon the appellant
on the basis that the “dangerous driving that caused the injury
and caused the death [was] alcohol related”. By acquitting the
appellant of the full offences of impaired operation causing
death and bodily harm, the jury rejected this theory and the
trial judge was bound by this finding:  R. v. Brown, [1991] 2
S.C.R. 518. In our view, an appropriate sentence is two years
less one day imprisonment.


[21] The appellant submitted that if this court imposed a
reformatory sentence, we should make the sentence a conditional
sentence of imprisonment. In our view, the appellant has not
satisfied the condition that “serving the sentence in the
community would not endanger the safety of the community” within
the meaning of s. 742.1 of the Criminal Code. The appellant has
prior convictions for Criminal Code driving offences and was
convicted of several speeding offences while awaiting trial on
these charges. Excessive speed was the principal factor in the
collision that occurred in this case. Accordingly, this is not an
appropriate case for a conditional sentence.
          
          Disposition


[22] In summary, the appeal from conviction for impaired
operation is allowed, the conviction quashed and an acquittal
entered. The appeal from conviction is otherwise dismissed. Leave
to appeal sentence is granted, the appeal from sentence is
allowed and the sentence reduced to two years less one day
imprisonment concurrent on both counts. The driving prohibition
will stand.
