                      ENDORSEMENT
                                

[1]  Contrary to the submissions of counsel for the appellant,
this is not a traditional identification case where the court is
concerned about the identification of the accused by a stranger
with a limited opportunity to observe the accused.
[2]  This case revolved around the credibility of a co-
conspirator who testified in detail as to the nature of the
conspiracy and the involvement of the appellant in it. He had
known the appellant since childhood.
[3]  The impugned evidence of the undercover police officer and a
surveillance officer was supportive of the appellant’s
involvement in the conspiracy. To the extent that there were
frailties in their evidence as to identification, the trial
judge’s charge to the jury was adequate in reminding them of the
problems inherent in identification cases.
[4]  Counsel on appeal raised other objections to the charge but
they were not made at trial. They related to a failure of the
trial judge to follow the instructions of the Supreme Court of
Canada in R. v. Lifchus (1997), 118 C.C.C. (3d) 1 and in R. v. W.
(D.) (1991), 63 C.C.C. (3d) at 397. In our view, there is no
substance to these objections.
[5]  As to sentence, the amount of heroin involved in this
conspiracy was 280 grams. There was evidence to support the trial
judge’s finding that the appellant was the head of the group
involved in this conspiracy. The appellant had been convicted
before in 1993 for the same offence and received a sentence of
fourteen years in addition to pre-trial custody. In our view, the
present sentence of ten years is eminently fit in the
circumstances.
[6]  Accordingly, the appeal against conviction is dismissed,
leave to appeal sentence is granted, but the appeal against
sentence is dismissed.
