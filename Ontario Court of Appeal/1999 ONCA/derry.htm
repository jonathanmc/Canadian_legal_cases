BY THE COURT:

[1]  The appellant Derry & Tomken Business Centre Inc. (ADerry@)
appeals from the summary dismissal of its action against the
respondent Kingslea Development Limited (AKingslea@). In its
action against Kingslea, Derry seeks an order of specific
performance requiring Kingslea to convey certain lands to it. In
the same action, the appellant seeks damages against the Canadian
Imperial Bank of Commerce (ACIBC@).  Kingslea moved for summary
judgment dismissing the action and obtained judgment accordingly.
Although the CIBC appeared on the motion and on this appeal as an
interested party, the summary judgment under appeal applies to
Kingslea only.


[2]  This action concerns certain conditions registered on title
by Derry in 1986 with respect to property it owned at the time,
including the land in question in this action. These conditions
purported to be a building scheme and included Derry=s right of
first refusal to purchase the land in the event of certain
happenings. In December 1993, Derry transferred the land (and
other property) to CIBC pursuant to a settlement agreement
between Derry, CIBC and other persons related to Derry. The
transfer to CIBC, duly registered, did not state that it was
subject to the conditions registered in 1986.


[3]  By subsequent transfer dated July 14, 1997, CIBC transferred
the land to Kingslea without notice to Derry. Kingslea, although
aware of the conditions on title as registered in 1986, did not
inquire from Derry but, rather, obtained on closing a statutory
declaration from CIBC indicating that all conditions had been
complied with to date.


[4]  The motions judge granted summary judgment dismissing the
action against Kingslea on three bases. He held that:
     (1)  the land is not so unique as to entitle Derry to a
          remedy of specific performance;
     (2)  Derry effectively released its claims on the land by
          the transfer to CIBC as provided in s. 5(1)1.v. of the
          Land Registration Reform Act, R.S.O. 1990,  c. L.4 ;
          and
     (3)  the conditions registered by Derry did not effectively
          bind the land and ought not to have been registered
          under the Land Titles Act.
On this appeal, Derry disputes all three findings.


[5]  In our view, the motions judge was correct in finding that
the transfer to CIBC effectively released all claims on the land,
including any right of first refusal. Section 5(1) of the Land
Registration Reform Act, R.S.O. 1990, c. L.4, provides as
follows:


          5.(1) A transfer in the prescribed form shall
          be deemed to include the following covenants
          and release by the transferor, for the
          transferor and the transferor=s successors,
          to and with the transferee and persons
          deriving title under the transferee:

          1.   In a transfer of freehold or leasehold
               land by the beneficial owner for
               valuable consideration, unless the
               transfer is expressed to be a quitclaim:

               v.   That the transferor releases to the
                    transferee all the transferor=s
                    existing claims on the land, except
                    as the transfer provides and the
                    records of the land registry office
                    disclose.[Emphasis added.]

The transfer from Derry to CIBC provides for no exception. Hence,
pursuant to s. 5(1)1.v., it is deemed to include a release by
Derry to the CIBC of all of Derry=s Aexisting claims on the
land@.


[6]  Derry takes the position that its right of first refusal is
not a Aclaim on land@ within the meaning of s. 5(1)1.v. and
relies on Canadian Long Island Petroleum Ltd v. Irving
Industries, [1974] 6 W.W.R. 385 (S.C.C.) in support of its
contention. In our view, the latter case does not assist the
appellant. While the Supreme Court in Canadian Long Island held
that a right of first refusal does not constitute an Ainterest@
in land, the words Aexisting claims on the land@ in s. 5(1)1.v.
have a wider meaning and, in our view, do include a right of
first refusal. Reference need only be made to the following
definitions in The Dicitionary of Canadian Law (Carswell:
Toronto, 1991:
     Claim:      A right, title, interest, encumbrance or
                 demand of any kind affecting land.


     Interest in land:(i) An estate in land less than an
                 estate in fee simple; and (ii) any
                 interest, right, easement, or right-of-
                 way, in, to, or over, land other than the
                 interest of the owner of an estate in fee
                 simple.


[7]  It is common ground between the parties that, whatever
equities may exist between Derry and the CIBC, Kingslea can only
be bound by what is on title. Since the transfer from Derry to
the CIBC did not expressly reserve any right arising from the
1986 conditions registered on title, Kinglea was entitled to rely
on it as a release of all existing claims by Derry.


[8]  In light of our conclusion on this issue, we do not find it
necessary to deal with the other findings made by the motions
judge. In particular, we express no opinion on any matter
concerning Derry=s claim against the CIBC.


[9]  The appeal is dismissed with costs.
