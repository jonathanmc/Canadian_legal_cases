ENDORSEMENT

[1] The appellant appeals his convictions for sexual assault and for sexual touching. The facts in overview are as follows:

[2] In the spring of 1993 the complainant was 13 years old and living in Toronto with her mother. The appellant was her mother's boyfriend who spent a lot of time at the complainant's house, and whom both the complainant and the appellant viewed as her step-father. Towards the end of 1992, the appellant started to touch the complainant by patting her on the bum as she went upstairs. Some time after that, he started to kiss her, and try to fondle her breasts. His conduct became more and more sexually intrusive, until around about May, 1993 he had sex with her against her will. The touching continued on and off, until the appellant made a second attempt to have intercourse with the complainant, but was interrupted when the complainant's cousin came to the door. In July 1993 the complainant moved to Timmins to live with her aunt. The complainant's relationship with her mother was, to say the least, strained. Towards the end of 1993, the complainant's mother telephoned her, and accused her of wanting her boyfriend, and of ruining her life. The complainant was upset by the call, and shortly thereafter disclosed the assaults by the appellant to her aunt. The appellant denied the accusations. It was the clear position of the defence that the complainant had fabricated the allegations in an attempt to get back at her mother, who had mistreated her for years, and who was now threatening to send her back to Jamaica.

[3] The appellant raised ten grounds of appeal against conviction in his factum. Without dealing with each in detail, we feel confident in stating that, for the most part, they relate to rulings made by the trial judge with respect to the admissibility of evidence and in imposing restrictions on the manner in which both counsel for the Crown and the defence conducted their cross- examinations. It is not the function of this court to critique the performance of a trial judge, particularly in areas that involve an exercise of discretion. Any objection we would give effect to in this area would have to carry substance. None of the objections here falls within that category.

[4] It is also contended that there were errors in the trial judge's instructions to the jury. We feel it necessary to identify only one, and that was the submission that the trial judge failed to follow the instructions later laid down by the Supreme Court of Canada in R. v. Lifchus (1997), 118 C.C.C. (3rd) 1. We have considered the charge on this issue as a whole and note that it conforms to charges in common use in this province prior to Lifchus. The jury was adequately instructed on the meaning of “reasonable doubt” and we are not prepared to give effect to this ground of appeal.

[5] In the last analysis, while there are other grounds relating to the jury charge, we are satisfied that the appellant received a fair trial and that the charge to the jury was balanced and impartial.

[6] A final ground of appeal raised as to conviction relates to the reasonableness of the jury's verdict. The appellant testified and his evidence was clearly rejected. However, his testimony and other evidence adduced in cross-examination put forward fully the theory of the defence, particularly that there was animosity between the complainant and her mother and that the complainant turned on the appellant when she perceived that he had stopped supporting her against her mother. This is not an unreasonable verdict.

[7] As to sentence, the trial judge committed no error in principle nor was the sentence manifestly unfit. It was acknowledged that the appellant was in a position of trust with respect to the complainant and the trial judge found as a fact that he was aware of her vulnerability as a young person with a difficult past. He addressed the applicable principles of sentencing, focusing his concern on the principle of general deterrence. Notwithstanding the absence of any record, the sentence of four years in the penitentiary was within the appropriate range.

[8] Accordingly, the appeal against conviction is dismissed, leave to appeal sentence is granted but the appeal against sentence is dismissed.
