ABELLA J.A.:

[1] On February 5, 1988, National Bank of Canada issued an Irrevocable Stand-By Letter of Credit in the amount of $2,500,000. in favour of the Simcoe & Erie General Insurance Company. In February 1996, Simcoe and Erie changed its name to GAN General Insurance Company.

[2] The Letter of Credit was issued by the Bank at the request of its customer Lavalin Inc. as security for Lavalin's obligations to GAN under a professional liability policy. The issuance of the insurance policy was conditional on such a Letter of Credit being provided by a Canadian chartered bank.

[3] The Letter of Credit, issued to Simcoe Erie General Insurance Company by National Bank of Canada, states:

IRREVOCABLE STAND-BY LETTER OF CREDIT NO. SAGE - 1516

February 5, 1988

Dear Sirs:

At the request of and for account of our customer, LAVALIN INC., we hereby establish in your favour our irrevocable Stand-By Letter of Credit No. SAGE-1516 for a sum not exceeding in the aggregate CAD $500,000.00 (FIVE HUNDRED THOUSAND DOLLARS).

This Credit shall be available to you by your signed and endorsed sight draft drawn on NATIONAL BANK OF CANADA when supported by your signed written demand certifying that the amount drawn is due and payable to you by LAVALIN INC. in connection with their obligations to you pursuant to the professional liability policy No. L56075 and its renewal (s) or replacement (s) thereof (hereinafter referred to as "the Policy").

It is understood the Bank is obligated under this Credit, to the payment of monies only and not the performance of services or otherwise and we hereby agree that we shall honour your demand for payment without enquiring whether you have a right as between yourselves and LAVALIN INC. to make such demand and without recognizing any claims of LAVALIN INC..

Partial drawings are permitted.

This Stand-By Letter of Credit is valid up to October 15, 1992 and will expire at 5 pm on that day; however, it is a condition of this Stand-By Letter of Credit that it shall be automatically extended for further periods of one year from the present or any future expiration dates hereof, unless at least thirty days prior to any such expiry date we notify you, in writing, by registered mail, that we elect not to have this Stand-By Letter of Credit renewed for any such additional period. Upon receipt of such notice, you may claim up to the amount available under this letter of credit by means of your sight draft on us, accompanied by your signed certificate certifying that your obligations under the Policy are still outstanding and that the proceeds of your drafts by which payment is demanded under this credit will be retained and used by you to meet any payments which you might thereafter be required to make under the Policy. Your certificate should also contain your undertaking that in the event that your obligations under the Policy the amount claimed under this credit, you will refund to us the amount received by you under this credit less any amount paid by you in respect of your obligations under the Policy and/or any premiums due to you by LAVALIN INC. in respect of said Policy.

Drafts drawn under this credit are to be endorsed hereon by ourselves and must be accompanied by the original of this credit. [Emphasis added.] [Emphasis added.]

We hereby agree that drafts drawn under this credit will be duly honoured on presentation provided that all terms and conditions of this credit have been complied with and the drafts are presented to the NATIONAL BANK OF CANADA at 600 de la Gauchetière West, Montréal, Quebec, on or before 5 pm on the respective expiry dates as mentioned above.

Except so far as otherwise expressly stated this Stand-By Letter of Credit is subject to the Uniform Customs and Practice for Documentary Credits - 1983 Revision - International Chamber of Commerce, Publication No. 400.

NATIONAL BANK OF CANADA

[4] On July 14, 1997, in accordance with the Letter of Credit, GAN presented the Bank with a signed and endorsed Sight Draft drawn on the Bank and supported by a written Demand certifying that $2,500,000. was owing to GAN by Lavalin.

[5] Because the original Letter of Credit could not be found when the demand for payment was made, GAN also gave the Bank two additional documents: (i) an Indemnification in favour of the Bank to protect it from any claims which might subsequently arise from its payment of the money; and (ii) an affidavit from GAN's corporate counsel confirming that the original Letter of Credit could not be found but that it had not been sold, transferred or otherwise assigned.

[6] The Bank refused to make payment because it was not presented with the original Letter of Credit. It took the position that it was entitled to strict compliance with every term of the Letter of Credit, and that a true copy of the Letter of Credit does not strictly comply with the requirement that it be presented with the original. The Bank does not dispute the accuracy or legitimacy of the copy; nor does it deny that the Letter of Credit had been automatically renewed every year since 1992 and was therefore valid and in force at the time GAN requested payment.

[7] GAN's application for a declaration that the Bank was obligated to pay the $2,500,000. notwithstanding the absence of the original Letter of Credit was successful. Lax J. ordered the Bank to pay the full sum upon delivery by GAN of an indemnity and an insurance bond of indemnity. The Bank appeals from that judgment.

[8] There is no doubt that the Letter of Credit states that "the original of this credit" must accompany sight drafts presented for payment under the Letter of Credit. The issue, however, is whether the presentation of a true copy rather than the original Letter of Credit, voids the Bank's obligations under it.

[9] It is my view that Lax J. reached the correct result and that the Bank's obligation to honour the demand for payment was not affected by GAN's failure to produce the original Letter of Credit.

[10] There is no doubt that banks must protect themselves from the possibility of fraud or from liability arising from breaches in the terms and conditions of the credit. Article 15 of the Uniform Customs and Practice for Documentary Credits reinforces this commercial truism:

Banks must examine all documents with reasonable care to ascertain that they appear on their face to be in accordance with the terms and conditions of the credit. Documents which appear on their face to be inconsistent with one another will be considered as not appearing on their face to be in accordance with the terms and conditions of the credit.

[11] But in this case we are not dealing with a document which lacks authenticity, or with a demand for payment which is inconsistent with the terms and conditions of the credit. On the contrary, there is no challenge to the authenticity of the copy, nor any denial that, on its face, the Bank is obliged to pay $2,500,000. to GAN under the Letter of Credit.

[12] The Bank, in my view, is not entitled to succeed based on its argued test of "strict compliance." Strict compliance applies to those documentary terms and conditions that go to the essence of the credit agreement, those which are "sufficiently material to justify a refusal of payment": Bank of Nova Scotia v. Angelica-Whitewear Ltd., [1987] 1 S.C.R. 59 at 96 per Le Dain J.

[13] The essence of this particular agreement is found in its second paragraph:

This Credit shall be available to you by your signed and endorsed sight draft drawn on NATIONAL BANK OF CANADA when supported by your signed written demand certifying that the amount drawn is due and payable to you by LAVALIN INC. in connection with their obligations to you pursuant to the professional liability policy No. L56075 and its renewal(s) or replacement(s) thereof (hereinafter referred to as "the Policy").

[14] There is no basis for the Bank insisting on strict compliance with the requirement for the original document. A true copy rather than the original is, in this case, more in the nature of a minor discrepancy than a material breach of a fundamental provision. This was a document prepared by the Bank and, as such, one with which the Bank was fully familiar. The Bank knew from the true copy everything it needed to know to be satisfied that the requirements of the credit had been met. It was clear on its face: Angelica-Whitewear at 98.

[15] The requirement of the original Letter of Credit is not a fundamental term or condition of the credit, and, as stated in Angelica-Whitewear, "not sufficiently material to justify a refusal of payment." There is therefore no requirement of strict compliance with this term. The Bank is only entitled to substantive compliance with respect to this document. In my view, the production of an undisputed true copy of the Letter of Credit generously meets that threshold.

[16] While not determinative, it is worth noting that there is virtually no prejudice or risk to the Bank in this case. An authentic copy of the Letter of Credit was available. GAN offered to indemnify the Bank, and even to provide a bond of indemnity issued by Chubb Insurance Company of Canada. Moreover, the likelihood of the original being found and successfully presented was negligible given that it could only be presented for payment at one branch of the Bank, and only by GAN, accompanied by the documents required to justify payment.

[17] To find otherwise permits the Bank to avoid its liabilities based on a "technicality", a recipe for commercial unreliability and uncertainty. On the other hand, sanctioning the use of an authentic copy, and insulating the Bank's vulnerability with an indemnity agreement, permits the fulfilment of the commercial agreement reached by the parties when they negotiated the Letter of Credit. The essence of that agreement was that when GAN presented a sight draft drawn on National Bank of Canada, along with a signed written demand certifying that the amount due relates to Lavalin's obligation under the policy, the Bank would pay.

[18] GAN did in fact present those documents to the Bank and was therefore in compliance with the Letter of Credit. It was, accordingly, entitled to payment in the amount requested. The Bank's refusal to honour the Letter of Credit was, in the circumstances, officious and entirely unwarranted.

[19] The appeal is dismissed with costs.
