                          ENDORSEMENT
[1]  Gerry's Distributors Limited ("Gerry's") brought this
action, by its receiver and manager, Richter & Partners Inc., for
the recovery of rebates owed to it by Itwal Limited ("Itwal"). At
trial, Itwal conceded that it owed Gerry's the sum of $413,878 in
rebates. However, Itwal claimed a right of set-off in the amount
of $455,201 in respect of payments it made to Gerry's suppliers
after Gerry's went into receivership and into bankruptcy. Brennan
J. held that Itwal was entitled to rely on the defence of
equitable set-off and dismissed the action. Gerry's appeals
against the dismissal of the action.


[2]  Itwal is a buying group established by its members to allow
them to benefit from the discounts and rebates offered to volume
purchasers of goods. Gerry's was one of the members of the group.


[3]  It is common ground between the parties that there were
three types of transactions that gave rise to a volume rebate
entitlement:
1.   "warehouse shipments" whereby Gerry's would order suppliers'
     goods directly from Itwal and receive an invoice from Itwal;
2.   " drop shipments" whereby Gerry's ordered the goods directly
     from the supplier, the supplier invoiced Itwal and Itwal in
     turn invoiced Gerry's; and
3.   "direct shipments" whereby Gerry's ordered goods directly
     from the supplier and paid the supplier directly; the
     suppliers would periodically report to Itwal on the direct
     shipments so that the appropriate rebates could be
     calculated.


[4]  Gerry's agrees that Itwal has a right of set-off in respect
to unpaid invoices from Itwal to Gerry's for both warehouse and
drop shipments. However, it disputes that the right extends to
suppliers paid by Itwal for direct shipments to Gerry's. Two
arguments are advanced in support of its position.


[5]  First, it is submitted that Itwal has no claim against
Gerry's. Gerry's claims that Itwal was under no legal obligation
to pay Gerry's unpaid suppliers. It is argued that Itwal should
not be credited for voluntary payments it made for the good of
continuing members but to the detriment of Gerry's creditors.


[6]  Second, in the event that the court takes the view that
Itwal has a valid cross-claim, it is submitted that the rebates
owed to Gerry's did not flow from Itwal's payment of Gerry's
debts to suppliers and that the connection between the cross-
claims is insufficient to give rise to equitable set-off.
Alternatively, it is submitted that only the payments of those
invoices that relate directly to the rebates owing should be
credited, an amount of approximately $24,000.


[7]  Three general requirements must be met before equitable set-
off can successfully be raised to reduce, or extinguish, a
defendant's liability:
1.   Each party must have a claim against the other. The claims
     can be liquidated or unliquidated. The issue raised in this
     case is whether Itwal has a valid claim against Gerry's.
2.   There must be mutuality. Mutuality has been defined in
     various ways. For the purpose of this appeal, it is
     sufficient to refer to Telford v. Holt (1987), 41 D.L.R.
     (4th) 385 (S.C.C.) where the Supreme Court (at 393) adopted
     the following words on the meaning of mutuality A[t]hat the
     plaintiff, at the commencement of the suit was and still is
     indebted to the defendant in an amount equal to the
     plaintiff's claim..." The Court noted that under this
     definition any assignment would destroy mutuality and hence
     destroy the possiblity of set-off at law. It is well
     established however that the requirement of mutuality is
     relaxed in the case of equitable set-off. Gerry's raised an
     issue on the question of mutuality in its factum but did not
     pursue the matter at the hearing. In our view, the facts of
     this case do not raise any concerns with respect to this
     requirement.
3.   The cross-claims must be sufficiently connected. The second
     issue raised on the appeal relates to this requirement.


Does Itwal have a valid claim against Gerry's for reimbursement
of monies paid to Gerry's suppliers?


[8]  Although the trial judge did not expressly address this
question in his reasons, it is manifest from his judgment that he
took the view that Gerry's was liable to reimburse Itwal for the
payments made to Gerry's suppliers.  In our view, this finding is
supported both in law and on the evidence.


[9]  It is clear from the evidence that rebates were earned by
the members on shipments obtained directly from the suppliers as
well as on shipments that went through Itwal in one form or
another. The evidence is uncontroverted that the direct shipments
formed part of the overall scheme to obtain the volume rebates.
Itwal's representative testified that suppliers looked to Itwal
for payment on direct payments when a member experienced
financial difficulties. This arrangement formed part of the
overall scheme. In some cases, the arrangement was reduced to
writing. In other cases, including the case at hand, it was not.
No evidence was called to dispute this testimony.


[10] Based on this evidence, we see no reason to interfere with
the trial judge's finding that Itwal had a valid claim against
Gerry's for payments made on its behalf.


Is there a sufficient connection between the cross claims to give
rise to equitable set-off?


[11] The trial judge held that Gerry's entitlement to the rebates
was "dependent upon completion of the transactions including
paying for the goods. Not having paid, Gerry's is not entitled."
Gerry's submits that this finding is wrong because Itwal
acknowledged that it owed the rebates to Gerry's. Further, it is
submitted that the evidence did not show that the rebates were
payable only on payment of the goods.


[12] We do not read the trial judge's reasons in the strict sense
urged upon us by Gerry's. We interpret the reasons as meaning
that both the benefit to the members in the form of rebates and
the corresponding obligation to pay for the goods formed part of
the scheme. The rebates cannot be considered independently from
the payment of the goods. The only conclusion supported by common
sense is that, in the overall scheme, rebates would not be
available to members in the absence of payment for the goods.


[13] In our view, the trial judge's finding that the claims were
sufficiently connected with each other to give rise to a right of
equitable set-off is supported by the evidence. Further, the
equities favour Itwal. Gerry's, and consequently its creditors,
received the benefit of the goods purchased from the suppliers.
It also received the benefit of the non-spayment of the invoices.
As the trial judge concluded, it would be unconscionable if
Gerry's were entitled to collect the rebates in addition to the
benefit already received. We agree with his conclusion.


[14] The appeal is dismissed with costs.
