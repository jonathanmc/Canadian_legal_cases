                      ENDORSEMENT
[1]  This is an action for damages for wrongful dismissal. The
defendant employer appeals from the judgment granted in favour of
the employee for $26,145 damages and costs of $13,000.
[2]  The appeal has three aspects:  fresh evidence, interventions
by the trial judge, and the quantum of damages. In our opinion,
it is only necessary to deal at any length with the matter of
interventions.
[3]  The employer’s position is that the employee was discharged
for cause. The cause is either that she stole a large of sum or
she was part of a scheme involving theft from the employer or as
store manager she did less than was required of her to prevent
the thefts.
[4]  The defendant’s complaint is that from the outset of the
trial, the trial judge manifested his disbelief and non-
acceptance of any of these defences. This position was
demonstrated by his frequent intervention in the defence’s
examination and cross-examination of witnesses. The result of
such interventions, it is argued, was the impression on the part
of the defendant that the trial judge had closed his mind to the
possibility that a defence could be established and that the
outcome was pre-determined.
[5]  Counsel for the appellant made specific reference to 48
interventions. Some of these could be construed as requests or
suggestions for clarification or as humorous asides. Most of the
interventions, however, could not be explained away.

[22] The law is clear “that not only should justice be done, but
should manifestly and undoubtedly be seen to be done”. R. v.
Sussex Judges; ex parte McCarthy 1924 1 K.B. 256 per Lord Hewitt
at p.259.
[23] In R. v. Valley (1986), 26 C.C.C. (3d) 207, Martin J.A. said
at p.232:
          
          Interventions  by  the  judge  creating   the
          appearance of an unfair trial may be of  more
          than  one type and the appearance of  a  fair
          trial  may  be destroyed by a combination  of
          different types of intervention. The ultimate
          question  to  be answered is not whether  the
          accused  was  in  fact  prejudiced   by   the
          interventions but whether he might reasonably
          consider that he had not had a fair trial  or
          whether  a reasonably minded person  who  had
          been   present  throughout  the  trial  would
          consider that the accused had not had a  fair
          trial.
[24] In R.D.S. v. Her Majesty The Queen [1997], 3 S.C.R. 484,
L’Heureux-Dube and McLachlin J. said at p.503:
          
          Notwithstanding  the  strong  presumption  of
          impartiality  that applies  to  judges,  they
          will   nevertheless  be   held   to   certain
          stringent  standards  regarding  bias  –   “a
          reasonable apprehension that the judge  might
          not  act  in an entirely impartial manner  is
          ground for disqualification”.
[25] At pp. 524 and 533, Cory J. said:
          
          Fairness  and  impartiality  must   be   both
          subjectively    present    and    objectively
          demonstrated  to the informed and  reasonable
          observer.  If  the words or  actions  of  the
          presiding  judge  give rise to  a  reasonable
          apprehension  of  bias to  the  informed  and
          reasonable  observer, this  will  render  the
          trial unfair.
          
          …
          
          It is right and proper that judges be held to
          the  highest standards of impartiality  since
          they   will  have  to  determine   the   most
          fundamentally important rights of the parties
          appearing  before them. This is true  whether
          the  legal dispute arises between citizen and
          citizen or between the citizen and the state.
          Every  comment  that a judge makes  from  the
          bench   is  weighed  and  evaluated  by   the
          community as well as the parties. Judges must
          be  conscious  of this constant weighing  and
          make  every effort to achieve neutrality  and
          fairness  in carrying out their duties.  This
          must be a cardinal rule of judicial conduct.
[26] These comments apply whether the proceeding is criminal or
civil. A review of the comments of the trial judge in this case,
comments which occurred predominantly while defence counsel was
on his feet, persuades us that the defendant and its counsel
would have had a reasonable expectation of bias. As said in
Sorger et al v. Bank of Nova Scotia et al (1998) 39 O.R. (3d) 1
at p.2:
          
          There  is  a  reasonable  apprehension  of  a
          judicial  mind  which  was  closed   to   the
          appellant’s  case  rather  than   one   which
          addressed   that  case  with   fairness   and
          impartiality.
[27] In these circumstances, it is not necessary to deal with the
questions of fresh evidence or damages. It may be said in
passing, however, that the trial judge’s enthusiasm for the
plaintiff’s case may have been reflected in the assessment of
damages.
[28] The appeal is allowed. The judgment below is set aside and
new trial directed. The appellant is entitled to its costs of
this appeal, if asked.
