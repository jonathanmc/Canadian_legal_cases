McKINLAY J.A.:


 [1] This is an appeal of a declaratory order of The Honourable
Mr. Justice Spence dated October 8, 1997.  The matter was argued
by way of motion based on an agreed statement of fact.


 [2] The trial judge succinctly explains the background and
nature of the issue before him and before us as follows:

                         The issue in this case arises
          in the following way. By a transfer of the
          trademark in 1991 from Lawrason’s to Min-
          Chem, Min-Chem acquired the trademark. Min-
          Chem therefore ceased to hold the security
          interest in the trademark which it had
          earlier held under a General Security
          Agreement. Counsel accepted that this
          consequence flowed from the transfer. The
          issue in the case arises from the transfer in
          1991 having been followed, some time after
          1994, by the order of this court which
          rendered that transfer void.

                         The issue is as follows. By
          reason of the transfer of the trademark to
          Min-Chem having been rendered void, may Min-
          Chem assert that the general security
          interest which it formerly held in the
          trademark has in effect been revived so that
          it takes priority over any recovery by the s.
          38 Creditors in respect of the sale of the
          trademark at public auction? Or, on the
          contrary, does the rendering void of the
          transfer of the trademark to Min-Chem simply
          leave it in the position it was in
          immediately beforehand, i.e. that it no
          longer holds security on the trademark
          because the security interest was terminated
          by virtue of Min-Chem’s acquisition of the
          trademark itself from Lawrason’s.
          
 [3] The agreed statement of facts is quoted below:
                    Hydrotech Chemical Corporation
          (“Hydrotech”) carries on business as a
          manufacturer and supplier of chemicals. Min-
          Chem Canada Ltd. (“Min-Chem”) carries on
          business as a distributor of chemicals and
          related products. Lawrason’s Chemicals Ltd.
          (“Lawrason’s”) was an Ontario company which
          also carried on business as a distributor of
          chemicals and related products. It is
          bankrupt.
                    Between 1988 and 1991, Min-Chem
          loaned in total the sum of approximately
          (CDN.) $1.4 million to Lawrason’s. All of
          these loan advances were secured by way of a
          General Security Agreement duly registered
          under the Personal Property Security Act
          against all of Lawrason’s assets. The
          security held by Min-Chem in Lawrason’s
          included all trademarks. Lawrason’s owned the
          trademark “Aquabrome”. “Aquabrome” was a
          trademark used with respect to Lawrason’s
          bromine products for swimming pools.
                    On the 18th day of January, 1991,
          Lawrason’s transferred the “Aquabrome”
          trademark to Min-Chem. The consideration was
          nominal (“$5.00 and other good and valuable
          consideration”).
                    On December 18, 1991, a Petition in
          Bankruptcy relating to Lawrason’s was filed
          by Hydrotech. Subsequently, a Receiving Order
          was made and as such the effective date of
          the bankruptcy of Lawrason’s is December 18,
          1991. The remaining assets of Lawrason’s were
          sold by Min-Chem under its General Security
          Agreement at a public auction on February 8,
          1992.
                    Min-Chem takes the position that it
          is still owed substantial funds by
          Lawrason’s. Hydrotech takes the position that
          Min-Chem is not owed any monies by
          Lawrason’s, but that there was an over-
          realization by Min-Chem.
                    On July 26, 1994, Hydrotech
          obtained an Order under Section 38 of the
          Bankruptcy and Insolvency Act. It obtained an
          assignment of the rights that the trustee in
          Bankruptcy of Lawrason’s might have against
          Min-Chem to pursue Min-Chem:
                    (a)  for over-realization;
                    (b)  to claim an Order setting
          aside the transfer of the trademark
          “Aquabrome” to Min-Chem.
                    With respect to the Section 38
          action, the parties have consented, through
          counsel, to an Order finding the transfer of
          the trademark “Aquabrome” from Lawrason’s to
          Min-Chem on January 18, 1991 fraudulent and
          void as against the creditors of Lawrason’s,
          under Section 2 of the Fraudulent Conveyances
          Act, R.S.O. 1990. The parties also agreed
          that the trademark should be sold at public
          auction. The Order was consented to without
          prejudice to the claims of both parties to
          the proceeds of the sale of the trademark.
          
 [4] Section 2 of the Fraudulent Conveyances Act, R.S.O. 1990, c.
F.29 reads:
               2.  Every conveyance of real property or
          personal property and every bond, suit,
          judgment and execution heretofore or
          hereafter made with intent to defeat, hinder,
          delay or defraud creditors or others of their
          just and lawful actions, suits, debts,
          accounts, damages, penalties or forfeitures
          are void as against such persons and their
          assigns.

 [5] The appellant takes the position that, although the
transaction was agreed by the parties to be fraudulent and void
pursuant to s. 2 of the Fraudulent Conveyances Act, it is still a
valid and subsisting transfer between the original parties to it.
The motions judge held, to the contrary, that the transaction was
void for all purposes and that, as a result, the position of the
respondent as a secured creditor, which obtained before the
transfer of the trademark, was revived as a result of the voiding
of the transfer.  In coming to that conclusion, he followed the
decision of White J. in Bank of Montreal v. Bray (1993), 12 O.R.
(3d) 545 (Gen. Div.).  That decision has since been reversed by
this court, as reported in (1997), 36 O.R. (3d) 99.


 [6] Bank of Montreal v. Bray was a case involving a transfer by
a husband to his wife of his joint tenancy interest in real
property.  The bank held a guarantee from the husband for debts
of a company incorporated by him to the extent of $750,000.  The
bank brought an action pursuant to s. 2 of the Fraudulent
Conveyances Act to set aside the guarantor's transfer to his
wife.  The husband died before the bank could obtain an execution
against him.  The action was defended on the basis, inter alia,
that the status quo ante could not be revived (that is, that the
joint tenancy could not be reinstated because of the death of the
husband) and that, therefore, the conveyance to the wife could
not be set aside.  This court was of the view that the revival of
the joint tenancy was not necessary, since the transfer as
between the husband and the wife had the effect of severing the
joint tenancy, and the effect of the transfer as between them was
not altered by an order voiding the transaction as against
creditors.  Rosenberg J.A., speaking for all of the court on this
issue, stated at pages l06 and l07:

                         There is little ambiguity in
          s. 2 of the Act. Transactions made with
          intent to defraud creditors are void as
          against those creditors. There is nothing in
          s. 2 that renders the transaction void as
          against the parties to the transaction. This
          issue was settled by the Supreme Court of
          Canada in Donohoe v. Hull Bros. & Co. (1895),
          24 S.C.R. 683.
                               . . . .
                     As between the parties to it, the
          transaction is valid and is only void as
          against the creditors. Accordingly, in my
          view,  the conveyance by Mr. Bray to his wife
          was valid as between them.
          
 [7] The respondent argues that this case is different because
the appellant proceeded under s. 38 of the Bankruptcy and
Insolvency Act, R.S.C. 1985, c. B-3, taking an assignment of the
rights of the trustee in bankruptcy.  It argues that the trustee
could have sued to set aside the transfer, and the benefit
derived from the suit would have been for the benefit of all
creditors of the bankrupt.  A judgment in favour of the trustee,
the respondent says, would have the effect of reviving its
security interest.  It gives no authority for that proposition
other than the decision of the motions judge in this case, and
the motions judge, as stated above, relied on the trial decision
in Bank of Montreal v. Bray.


 [8] If the trustee had sued and recovered pursuant to the
provisions of s. 2 of the Fraudulent Conveyances Act, he could
only have sued on behalf of those creditors whose interests were
defeated, hindered, delayed or defrauded by the transfer of the
trademark.  However, because of the specific provisions of s.
38(3), the benefit derived from the action would belong to the
estate to be shared by all creditors, and not just those whose
interests were affected.  As between the parties to the
transaction, the transfer would be a good and valid one.  That
being the case, there could be no revival of the respondent's
security interest in the trademark: it could not be the owner of
and a secured creditor in the same property.  Nonetheless, in my
opinion, it would be entitled to share in the estate as an
unsecured creditor.


 [9] As a result, the appeal is allowed and the order of Spence
J. is set aside with party and party costs here and below.  The
appellant is entitled to an order declaring that the respondent,
Min-Chem Canada Ltd., does not have a security interest in the
proceeds of the sale of the Aquabrome trademark.  Pursuant to the
provisions of s. 38 of the Bankruptcy and Insolvency Act, the
appellant, Hydrotech Chemical Corporation, and any creditors who
joined in the action, are entitled to recover against the
proceeds of the sale of the trade-mark the total amount of their
claims in the bankruptcy of Lawrason's Chemicals Ltd. plus any
costs of the motion and of this appeal not recovered from the
respondent.  If there is any shortfall, the appellant will rank
to the extent of the shortfall as an unsecured creditor in the
bankruptcy estate.  The respondent is entitled to claim as an
unsecured creditor for any amount of the indebtedness to it not
recovered on realization of other assets over which it held or
holds security.
