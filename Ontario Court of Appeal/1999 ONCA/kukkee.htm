                     ENDORSEMENT
[1]   In  this  personal  injury action, the  jury  assessed  the
plaintiff’s damages as follows:
            General Damages          $ 95,000.00
            Past Income Loss                 nil
            Future Income Loss       $ 25,000.00
            Future Care Costs (after
            October 7, 2003)         $ 20,884.08
            Total		             $140,884.08


[2]   As  the jury found the plaintiff to be 35% responsible  for
the  accident, these amounts were reduced by the trial  judge  to
$91,574.65.


[3]   The  jury  was clearly instructed in answer to  a  question
posed during the jury’s deliberations that in assessing the  loss
of  income claim, Canada Pension Plan disability benefits had  to
be  deducted  from any award made in the plaintiff’s favour.  The
Divisional Court’s judgment in Cugliari v. White (1996), 31  O.R.
(3d)  42  (since  affirmed (1998), 38 O.R. (3d) 641  (C.A.))  was
released  after the jury had given its verdict but  before  final
judgment was entered. Cugliari holds that, contrary to the  trial
judge’s  instruction to the jury, Canada Pension Plan  disability
benefits  should  not be deducted as income replacement  benefits
pursuant  to the Insurance Act, R.S.O. 1990, c. I-8, s. 267(1)(c)
from a damages award. After the jury had rendered its verdict but
before   judgment  was  entered,  the  trial  judge   entertained
submissions  with  respect to the impact of Cugliari.  The  trial
judge decided that as the jury had been explicitly instructed  to
make  its  award on the basis that Canada Pension Plan disability
benefits  were  to  be deducted, it was appropriate  to  add  the
present value of those benefits, namely $99,759.49, to the  award
the  jury  had  made.  After taking into account  the  65%  fault
factor,  he  found  that  the  plaintiff   was  entitled  to   an
additional $64,843.67 for a total award of $156,418.32.
[4]   The  trial judge also found that the defendant was entitled
to  a  deduction of the value of the no-fault benefits  from  the
award pursuant to the Insurance Act, s. 267. Those benefits  were
for  past loss of income and for future care costs to October  7,
2003. The total value of these benefits was $143,908.41, of which
$29,647.49  was  for  past income loss and  $114,261.00  was  for
future care costs. As the jury had been instructed to deduct  the
value  of the benefits paid for past income loss, the trial judge
found that the defendant had already received the benefit of that
deduction.  In the action, the plaintiff had made  no  claim  for
future  care  costs for the period to October  7,  2003  as  that
period  was covered under the no-fault provisions of his  policy.
The  trial judge found that the appropriate adjustment was to add
to  the award 65% of $114,261.00, or $74,273.00, the value of the
no-fault  benefits for that period, but then to deduct the  value
of  the  no  fault benefits to which the plaintiff  is  entitled,
$114,261.  The  net effect of this adjustment was to  reduce  the
award by 35% of the value of the future care costs.


[5]   In  the  end,  the  plaintiff received  a  total  award  of
$116,430.32. The trial judge held that there should be  no  award
of pre-judgment interest.


[6]   Finally, the trial judge imposed as a term of the  judgment
the following:
3.  THIS COURT ORDERS AND ADJUDGES that with respect to
            future  benefits to which the Plaintiff,
            John Kukkee, may become entitled and not
            already  accounted for in this Judgment,
            those amounts will be held in trust  for
            the  Defendants  by the Plaintiff,  John
            Kukkee,  until the Defendants have  been
            compensated  to the full extent  of  the
            Judgment.
       



[7]   Both  parties  made  a  number of concessions  during  oral
argument,  reducing the issues to be decided on this  appeal  and
cross-appeal.  The defendant concedes that as  a  result  of  the
decision of this Court in Bannon v. McNeely (1998), 38 O.R.  (3d)
659,  released after the trial judgment in the case at  bar,  the
value of 35% of the future care costs to the year 2003 should not
have  been deducted from the award as the plaintiff made no claim
for  those costs in the action. The defendant also concedes  that
the plaintiff is entitled to pre-judgment interest on the general
damage  award at the rate of 5% from the date of the accident  to
the date of trial. The defendant also now concedes that the trial
judge’s costs order is appropriate.


[8]   The defendant submits, however, that the trial judge  erred
in  adding  back the value of the Canada Pension Plan  disability
benefits.  Mr. Rogers argues that it is apparent from the  nature
of  the round figure award of $25,000 for future income loss that
the  jury did not approach the assessment of this head of damages
as  a  matter  of  arithmetic. He submits that there  can  be  no
assurance that the jury’s award for future income loss  took  the
value  of  the  Canada  Pension  Plan  disability  benefits  into
account.  We  do  not  accept  that  submission.  The  jury   was
instructed  in  the clearest terms to deduct the value  of  these
benefits  from  its  award.  In our view,  the  trial  judge  was
entitled  to  assume  that the jury had  assessed  this  head  of
damages  in accordance with the clear direction he gave.  As  the
jury’s  assessment could properly be considered as the  value  of
the  future income loss net of the Canada Pension Plan disability
benefits, the judgment as entered by the trial judge was entirely
consistent with the answer given by the jury in its verdict.

[9]  On behalf of the plaintiff, Mr. Stevens concedes that if  he
succeeds on the Canada Pension Plan point, the amount to be added
back should be adjusted in light of the jury’s nil assessment for
past  loss  of  income. To the date of trial, the  plaintiff  had
received  $5,652.00 in Canada Pension disability benefits.  While
the  jury may have taken this amount into account in arriving  at
its  zero  award  for past loss, that was far from  certain,  and
accordingly, on the basis of the concession made, the award is to
be  reduced  by  $5,652.00. Similarly, Mr. Stevens conceded  that
there should be a further deduction in the defendant’s favour  of
$29,647.49,  the  amount of no-fault income replacement  accident
benefits received by the plaintiff up to date of trial.

[10]  In view of these concessions and findings, the amount of the
award is to be varied on the following basis:
       Judgment
                                                         $116,430.32
       Add:   35% of no fault
       benefits for future care costs                   $  39,988.00

                                                        $ 156,418.32
  Subtract: CPP benefits to date of trial $5,652.00
            No fault income replacement
            payments to date of trial     $29,647.49
                                          $35,299.49      $35,299.49
  Judgment as varied                                     $121,118.83


[11]   Finally, as full account has been taken for future benefits
to  which  the  plaintiff may be entitled, the provision  of  the
judgment imposing a trust on those benefits must be deleted.


[12]  The net result is the judgment is to be varied as follows:
       1.The amount of the award is $121,118.83;


       2.The  plaintiff  is awarded prejudgment  interest
       at the rate of 5% from the date of the accident to
       the date of trial on the general damages of 65% of
       $ 95,000.00, or 61,750.00; and


       3.Paragraph 3 of the judgment is deleted.


[13]   The plaintiff shall have his costs of the appeal and cross-
appeal.
