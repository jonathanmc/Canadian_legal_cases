On Appeal from the judgment of G. Ferguson J. dated March 19, 1997


AUSTIN J.A.:
[1]  The appellant, a psychologist, appeals from his conviction
on March 19, 1997, by G. Ferguson J., sitting without a jury, on
two charges of sexual assault contrary to s. 271 of the Criminal
Code, and in particular, of having had sexual intercourse with
two patients without their consent.  Dr. Matheson also applies
for leave and, if granted, appeals from the sentence of one year
imprisonment on each count, to be served consecutively, imposed
on May 12, 1997.


OVERVIEW
[2]  The appellant was the chief psychologist at Etobicoke
General Hospital.  The complainants, whose names are subject to a
non-publication order, will be referred to simply as “X” and “Y”.
[3]  X was 47 years old, married and had a grown son when she
began therapy with the appellant on an out-patient basis.  She
was his patient from March 1987 to March or April 1989.  Sexual
intercourse began at her home and later occurred at the
appellant’s office and home and at her office.  The sexual
relationship continued for about three and one-half years after
the doctor/patient relationship ended.  The charge included only
the period of March 1, 1987 to April 1, 1989.
[4]  Y was a 29 year old registered nurse, married and the mother
of two children when she became a private patient of the
appellant in March 1992.  She remained his patient for seven
months.  From June to August 1992, they engaged in a sexual
relationship.
THE ISSUES ON APPEAL
[5]  The appeal raises the following issues:
          
          1.   Whether the trial judge erred in editing the records of
               persons who treated the complainants after the appellant;
          
          2.   Whether  one of such errors involved the trial  judge’s
               failure to note the expression “brain washing”;
          
          3.   Whether the evidence of Dr. Gail Robinson was admissible
               expert evidence;
          
          4.   Whether the verdict was unreasonable having regard to the
               parts played by health professionals and lawyers after the times
               covered by the charges; and
          
          5.   Whether  the  consent or submission of the complainants
               to  sexual  activities was procured or induced  by  the
               exercise  of  authority  within  the  meaning   of   s.
               265(3)(d) of the Criminal Code.
DISPOSITION OF GROUNDS 1 TO 4
[6]  The first two grounds were dealt with together as a
preliminary matter.  The expression “brain washing” did not
appear anywhere in the evidence.  The trial judge used it towards
the end of his reasons for conviction.  The appellant’s concern
was that it may have been in the notes or records of the health
professionals who had treated the complainants subsequent to the
periods set out in the charges.  Had that been the case, it was
the appellant’s position that that note or record should have
been disclosed but was not.
[7]  After hearing arguments on this matter, the court recessed
to consider it.  We were satisfied that the expression was used
by the trial judge as a form of shorthand and that there was no
basis for a review of his editing of those notes and records.
Those grounds were accordingly rejected from the bench.
[8]  Dr. Gail Robinson testified for the prosecution at the
preliminary hearing and at trial.  She is a full professor of
psychiatry, obstetrics and gynecology at the University of
Toronto.  Dr. Robinson has written and lectured widely on the
subject of the sexual exploitation of female patients by health
professionals and on the issue of therapist/patient sexual
misconduct.  In my view, her evidence met the criteria set out in
R. v. Mohan (1994), 89 C.C.C. (3d) 402 (S.C.C.), namely,
relevance, necessity to assist the trier of fact, absence of any
exclusionary rule and proper qualification of the expert.  It
should be noted that the defence called its own experts.
[9]  The unreasonable verdict argument appears to depend in part
upon the remaining ground, namely, the interpretation of
“exercise of authority”, and the proposition that although both
complainants consented to sexual activities, they were later
persuaded by their therapists, lawyers and the material they read
that “they couldn’t have consented”.  The influence of the
therapists, lawyers and readings fell to the trial judge to
consider and weigh.  I am not persuaded that he erred in
rejecting this proposition.
[10] Accordingly, in my view, the first four grounds of appeal
listed in paragraph [5] of these reasons must fail.
[11] The interpretation of “exercise of authority” will be dealt
with next.  In my view, it is the only ground of any substance.

LAW
[53] Absent consent, there can be no doubt that the events in
issue were assaults.  The sole defence was that all sexual
activity was consensual.  The onus was on the prosecution to
prove that any consent was vitiated by the exercise of authority.
The position taken on behalf of the appellant was that he was not
in a position of authority with respect to either complainant and
that their consents were not induced by the exercise of
authority.
[54] The relevant sections of the Criminal Code are ss. 271 and
265.  They are as follows:
          
          271.      (1)       Every one who commits a sexual
                    assault is guilty of
          
                              (a)  an indictable offence and
                         is  liable  to imprisonment  for  a
                         term not exceeding ten years; or
          
                               (b)  an offence punishable on
                         summary  conviction and  liable  to
                         imprisonment   for   a   term   not
                         exceeding eighteen months.
          
          …
          
          265. (1)  A person commits an assault when
          
                               (a)   without the consent  of
                         another  person, he  applies  force
                         intentionally    to   that    other
                         person, directly or indirectly;
          
                              (b)  he attempts or threatens,
                         by  an  act or a gesture, to  apply
                         force to another person, if he has,
                         or  causes  that  other  person  to
                         believe   upon  reasonable  grounds
                         that  he  has, present  ability  to
                         effect his purpose; or
               
                         (c)    while   openly  wearing   or
                         carrying  a weapon or an  imitation
                         thereof,  he  accosts  or   impedes
                         another person or begs.
          
                   (2)   This  section applies to all  forms
                   of  assault,  including  sexual  assault,
                   sexual assault with a weapon, threats  to
                   a  third party or causing bodily harm and
                   aggravated sexual assault.
          
                   (3)   For  the purposes of this  section,
                   no   consent   is  obtained   where   the
                   complainant  submits or does  not  resist
                   by reason of
          
                    (a)  the application of force
                         to  the  complainant or to a person
                         other than the complainant;
                    
                    (b)  threats or fear of the application of force to the
                         complainant or to a person other than the complainant;
                    
                    (c)  fraud; or
                    
                    (d)  the exercise of authority.    [Emphasis added]
[55] “Authority” is defined in The Oxford English Dictionary, 2nd
ed., Vol. 1 (Oxford:  Claredon Press, 1989) at p. 798 as follows:
          
          authority:    1.   Power  or  right   to   enforce
          obedience; moral or legal supremacy; the right  to
          command, or give an ultimate decision.  2. Derived
          or  delegated  power; conferred  right  or  title;
          authorization.  3. Those in authority; the body or
          person  exercising power or command.  4. Power  to
          influence  the  conduct  and  actions  of  others;
          personal or practical influence.  5.  Power  over,
          or  title  to influence, the opinions  of  others;
          authoritative  opinion;  weight  of  judgment   or
          opinion;  intellectual  influence.   6.  Power  to
          inspire    belief,   title   to    be    believed;
          authoritative  statement;  weight  of   testimony.
          Sometimes weakened to:  Authorship, testimony.  7.
          The quotation or book acknowledged, or alleged, to
          settle  a  question or give conclusive  testimony.
          8.a.  The  person  whose opinion or  testimony  is
          accepted; the author of an accepted statement.  b.
          One whose opinion of or upon a subject is entitled
          to   be  accepted;  an  expert  in  any  question.
          [Emphasis added]


LEGISLATIVE HISTORY OF SECTION 265(3)(d) OF THE CRIMINAL CODE
[56] Exercise of authority was introduced to the statutory law of
assault in Canada as part of Bill C-127, An Act to Amend the
Criminal Code (Sexual Offences and Offences Against the Person),
which came into force on January 4, 1983.  The current
s.265(3)(d) was s.244(3)(d) in Bill C-127.
[57] In 1982, a history of Bill C-127, Sexual Offences (Ottawa:
Law and Government Division, Research Branch, Library of
Parliament, 1982), was prepared by Donald Macdonald for the
Standing Committee on Legal and Constitutional Affairs.  At p. 4,
Macdonald notes that the effect of s.244(3) was to significantly
change the circumstances in which consent to sexual activity is
not genuine.  At the time, the predecessor to s. 244(3), s.
143(b) of the Criminal Code, stated:
          
               A male person commits rape when he has sexual
               intercourse with a female person who  is  not
               his wife,
          
               …
          
               (b)  with her consent if the consent
                    
                    (i)  is extorted by threats or fear of bodily harm,
                    
                    (ii) is obtained by personating her husband, or
                    
                    (iii)is obtained by false and fraudulent representations as
                         to the nature and quality of the act.
[58] Macdonald’s comments on the scope of the changes
contemplated by s. 244(3) are limited to the following at p. 5:
          
          The  necessity for fear of bodily harm is dropped;
          the  application or fear of “force” to the  victim
          or   another  person  is  sufficient  to   vitiate
          consent.   The qualification as to the  nature  of
          fraudulently  obtained consent will no  longer  be
          necessary  and  a  new element,  the  exercise  of
          authority, is added.  [Emphasis added]
[59] In An Information Paper on Bill C-127 (Ottawa:  January
1983) released by the Minister of Justice and Attorney General of
Canada to coincide with Bill C-127’s coming into force, it was
noted that the impetus for Bill C-127 was the Law Reform
Commission of Canada’s Report on Sexual Offences, released in
1978.  In that Report, the Law Reform Commission proposed
sweeping changes to the law on sexual offences in Canada.  The
Report recognized that any changes to the criminal law in this
area should reflect a number of objectives, including:
          
                    1.     Protection   of  the   individual
                    integrity   of  the  person  from   non-
                    consensual sexual contact; and
          
                    2.    Protection  of  children  and  the
                    psychologically vulnerable  from  sexual
                    contact.
[60] Once again, the Information Paper on Bill C-127 makes only
passing mention of s.244(3)(d).  At p. 2, it states:  “Section
244(3) outlines the circumstances where no consent to the assault
or sexual assault is obtained….The new Section 244 sets out in
subsection 3 the factors which vitiate consent to any form of
assault”.
[61] In The New Offences Against the Person:  The Provisions of
Bill C-27 (Toronto:  Butterworths, 1984), David Watt (now the
Honourable Mr. Justice Watt) includes a discussion of s.244(3).
At p.132, he writes:
          
          Section  244(3) lists a number of factors,  which,
          though  they  may  cause V. to submit  or  not  to
          resist  D.’s conduct, will not amount  to  consent
          sufficient  in  law to negate that aspect  of  the
          external circumstances of any form of assault.
[62] Watt provides a more detailed analysis of s.244(3)(d) at pp.
220-21:
          
          The  final  element whose existence  will  vitiate
          V.’s  consent  arises when such consent  has  been
          obtained   by  the  exercise  of  authority.    No
          specific  relationships are  listed  as  excluding
          consent so that the issue in each case will become
          whether   the  authority  allegedly  negated   the
          voluntary character of the consent given.
          
          On  occasion, the exercise of D. of authority over
          V.  may  deprive the latter of a true appreciation
          of  all the risks involved as for example where D.
          uses  the employment relationship to induce V.,  a
          subordinate, to have sexual intercourse with  him.
          On  other  occasions,  however,  the  exercise  of
          authority  may  be  restricted  in  scope   by   a
          statutory provision, as for example s. 43  of  the
          Code which provides:
          
                    43.   Every  schoolteacher,  parent   or
                    person standing in the place of a parent
                    is  justified in using force by  way  of
                    correction toward a pupil or  child,  as
                    the  case may be, who is under his care,
                    if  the  force does not exceed  what  is
                    reasonable under the circumstances.
          
          …
          
          It  is  submitted that when it is asserted that  a
          consent,  apparently given, has been  vitiated  by
          the  exercise  of  authority,  the  circumstantial
          facts  ought  to  be first examined  to  determine
          whether  D.,  by  law, custom or usage,  is  in  a
          position  of authority vis-à-vis V.  In the  event
          that  it  is  determined that such a  relationship
          existed, it must next be ascertained whether, as a
          matter  of  fact,  V.’s  submission  or  lack   of
          resistance was founded upon such relationship.  An
          affirmative response upon both issues will  render
          legally  ineffectual  V.’s  consent  and  hold  D.
          liable  to  the  extent of his conduct.   Upon  it
          being  asserted that V. consented to D.’s conduct,
          it  will be incumbent upon the prosecution,  where
          it  is alleged that V.’s consent has been vitiated
          by  exercise of authority, to prove the  existence
          of  the  authority relationship,  its  nature  and
          extent, and that V.’s consent has been vitiated by
          the exercise of such authority.
[63] It could be argued on behalf of the appellant that the
analysis suggested by Watt was never undertaken by the trial
judge.  That is, that there was never any determination as to:
(a) whether the appellant, by law, custom or usage, was in a
position of authority vis-à-vis the complainants; (b) whether the
appellant exercised his authority over the complainants; and (c)
whether the complainants consented or submitted by reason of the
exercise of authority by the appellant.
[64] In Sexual Assault (Toronto:  Carswell, 1984), Professor
Christine Boyle discusses the changes to the law of sexual
assault under Bill C-127.  She explains s. 244(3)(d) and the
“exercise of authority” as a basis for vitiating consent at pp.
70-71:
          
          This  is  a  new  factor, so  little  guidance  is
          available.  The obvious issues are when one person
          can  be said to be in a position of authority over
          another,  and when that authority can be  said  to
          have been exercised.
          
          …
          
          One  would  expect the definition of authority  to
          depend  on  the rationale for the rule itself  but
          that is of limited assistance.  The meaning of the
          word   in   this   context…will  depend   on   the
          judiciary’s  view  of  the scope  of  the  morally
          reprehensible conduct which ought to  attract  the
          attention   of  the  criminal  law….[T]he   Oxford
          English Dictionary indicates that authority means,
          inter   alia,  the  power  or  right  to   enforce
          obedience;  the power to influence the conduct  or
          actions   of   others;   personal   or   practical
          influence.  The possibility therefore exists  that
          an extremely broad definition of authority will be
          adopted by the courts.
          
          …
          
          Employers  (including persons put in positions  of
          authority  by  them), teachers,  police  officers,
          immigration  officers, judges, all have  power  to
          enforce  obedience  and a fortiori  the  power  to
          influence  the  conduct  or  actions  of   others.
          Husbands  who  insist on being  the  head  of  the
          household might well be in trouble here, if judged
          according to their view of reality….This provision
          may   well  cover  the  not-unknown  case  of  the
          professor  who engages in sexual activity  with  a
          student.   Doctor/nurse romances may  be  somewhat
          risky.
[65] The central issue raised by this appeal is as to the meaning
of the word “authority” as used by Parliament in enacting
s.265(3)(d).
[66] The appellant argues that “authority” in the present context
means “power or right to enforce obedience”.  He argues that as
the complainants’ psychologist he had neither the power nor the
right to enforce obedience.  Accordingly, his conduct does not
fall within the ambit of s. 265(3)(d).
[67] It is further argued that “authority relationships” are
numerous, e.g. parent and child, teacher and pupil, employer and
employee and superior and junior ranks in military and other
organizations.  In each of these organizations, there is a power
or right to enforce obedience in some respect – but that is not
true of the doctor/patient relationship nor of the
therapist/patient relationship.  The doctor may prescribe, but he
or she has no power or right to enforce obedience.
[68] The fourth meaning ascribed by the dictionary to
“authority”, and that advanced by the respondent, is “power to
influence the conduct and actions of others; personal or
practical influence”.  Taking the example of a doctor prescribing
a medication or a course of treatment, he or she does have the
power to influence his or her patients by way of persuasion.
[69] A review of the jurisprudence reveals examples of both the
interpretations of “authority” set out in paragraphs [66] and
[68].
JURISPRUDENCE
[70] In R. v. Cadden, [1986] B.C.J. No. 2778 (Co. Ct.) (QL
Systems), an elementary school teacher was charged with sexual
assault and gross indecency.  Although consent was irrelevant by
virtue of the ages of the complainants, s.265(3)(d) was
considered. Arkell C.C. J. said:
          
          It  is abundantly clear from the evidence that the
          Accused,  with  the  authority as  their  teacher,
          manipulated the complainants as his students,  and
          they  were  subject  to his direction.   [Emphasis
          added]
[71] Arkell C.C.J. then discussed s.43 of the Criminal Code,
which is quoted in paragraph [62] above, and noted:
          
          This section is an indication of the position  and
          authority held by a school teacher.
          
          In the Concise Oxford Dictionary, 6th Edition, the
          definition  of  force  there  refers  to  strength
          exerted on a person by coercion or compulsion.  It
          refers  to  mental  or moral strength,  influence,
          controlling   power,  efficacy,   and   power   to
          convince.   The  definition  as  a  verb   is   to
          constrain,  compel,  to compel  a  person  to  act
          prematurely or adopt a policy unwillingly.
          
          Here  the  Accused, as a teacher,  by  his  subtle
          threats, had the potential to put into effect, his
          authority   either  through  fear   or   coercion,
          intimidation  or  direct  compulsion.   From   his
          exercise of authority these boys were required  to
          perform   the   sexual  acts   of   fellatio   and
          masturbation  upon him.  To submit that  this  was
          consensual sexual activity by the students is  not
          valid  since  no  consent can be obtained  by  the
          exercise of authority.  The Accused had the power,
          or  right, to enforce obedience as a teacher,  and
          the power to influence the conduct and actions  of
          the students.  [Emphasis added]
[72] The conviction in Cadden was affirmed by the British
Columbia Court of Appeal [(1989), 48 C.C.C. (3d) 122].  The
issues in that court dealt with what constituted an assault and
did not touch upon the matter of authority.
[73] In R. v. S. (C.E.) (1988), 84 N.S.R. (2d) 30 (Co. Ct.), the
accused was convicted of sexually assaulting a 15-year-old girl
who was, by virtue of a common law relationship, his
stepdaughter.  The court found that she submitted to the sexual
activity in question by reason of “the exercise of authority” by
the accused.  The trial judge said at pp. 33-34:
          
          I  find  that  the accused was in  a  position  of
          authority with respect to the complainant.  As she
          said  in  her evidence the accused was the  father
          figure  in  the  home.  He was the father  of  her
          little   sister  and  she  regarded  him  as   her
          stepfather.  She had been informed that since  she
          was  living  in his home she had to  live  by  his
          rules.   The  accused  exercised  discipline   and
          control over her.  Clearly the accused was in loco
          parentis with respect to the complainant.
          
          The  final  question  is whether  the  complainant
          submitted  to the sexual acts as a result  of  the
          exercise of authority by the accused?
          
          …
          
          In  my view…the persistence of the accused in  his
          requests  to this young girl of fifteen years  for
          sexual   favours  and  his  continuing  to   apply
          pressure to her while demonstrating anger,  for  a
          period  of ten to fifteen minutes in circumstances
          where  she  had virtually no means of  escape  and
          where  no other alternatives were apparently  open
          to  her  and her being fearful of the consequences
          of  refusing,  amounted  to  an  exercise  of  the
          accused’s  parental authority, which  resulted  in
          the complainant submitting to the sexual act.   In
          these  circumstances I find that  no  consent  was
          obtained  because  the  complainant  submitted  by
          reason,  of  the  exercise  of  authority  by  the
          accused within the meaning of section 244(3)(d) of
          the Criminal Code.  [Emphasis added]
[74] On appeal, the conviction was set aside and a new trial
ordered on the admission of fresh evidence in which the
complainant said she had really consented but refused to swear an
affidavit to that effect.
[75] It should be noted that in R. v. S. (C.E.) the trial judge
followed the analysis suggested in Watt’s commentary.
[76] In R. v. Tremblay, [1988] O.J. No. 2436 (Prov. Ct.) (QL
Systems), the charge was sexual assault by a “boss” or purported
“boss”.  The accused told the complainant, a fellow employee,
that when he became owner of the firm in October, there would be
layoffs, but he would fire girls other than her.  The trial judge
found that the accused was not in fact the “boss” of the
complainant.  However, the trial judge also found that the
complainant believed the accused was her boss and had engaged in
sexual activity with him as a direct consequence of this belief.
The trial judge went on to deal with the question of a purported
“boss” and concluded that:
          
          The  accused  assumed  the  mantle  of  authority,
          exercised  it improperly for his illicit purposes,
          and thus, he cannot now set up his actual lack  of
          authority  as  a  defence, on these  facts  he  is
          guilty.
[77] In R. v.  Singh, [1992] O.J. No. 219 (C.A.) (QL Systems),
this court dismissed a Crown appeal as to sentence.  The brief
report of this case indicates that the respondent doctor pleaded
guilty to six counts of indecent assault and ten counts of sexual
assault involving brief touching of his female patients on their
breasts through their clothing.  The endorsement of this court
states in part that:
          
          The  respondent’s  authority as a  medical  doctor
          constituted an abuse of his position of  authority
          as  a  medical doctor and, in some cases, a  gross
          breach of trust.  [Emphasis added]
No issue appears to have been raised as to whether or not the
respondent was in a position of authority with respect to the
complainants.
[78] In R. v. Levandier, [1992] N.S.J. No. 593 (Co. Ct.) (QL
Systems), a police officer was charged with the sexual assault of
a young civilian.  It was argued by the prosecution that any
consent was vitiated by the use of force, threats, fraud or the
exercise of authority.  The trial judge concluded on the facts
that:
          
          The only possible obtaining of consent through one
          of  those  enumerated  factors,  is  the  possible
          exercise of authority.
          
          There  is  no evidence before this court that  the
          accused exercised his authority as a Peace Officer
          in  order  to have the complainant remain  in  the
          police  vehicle or engage in the conduct which  is
          the subject matter of these charges.
In the result, the charges were dismissed.
[79] Norberg v. Wynrib, [1992] 2 S.C.R. 226 is a closely related
civil matter in which a medical doctor exchanged prescriptions
for drugs to which his patient was addicted, for sex.  The action
for damages against Wynrib on the grounds of sexual assault,
negligence, breach of fiduciary duty and breach of contract was
dismissed at trial.  The dismissal was affirmed by the majority
in the British Columbia Court of Appeal upon the ground ex turpi
causa non oritur actio.  The Supreme Court of Canada was also
divided in its reasoning.  On the issue of sexual assault, La
Forest, Gonthier and Cory JJ. decided that the plaintiff’s
consent was vitiated by the imbalance of power between doctor and
patient, her vulnerability and his exploitation of that
vulnerability.  L’Heureux-Dubé and McLachlin JJ. declared the
doctor/patient relationship to be fiduciary and that the action
lay for breach of that relationship, in this case, by the doctor.
Sopinka J. held that while her consent to sexual activity was not
vitiated by the circumstances, fairness or the doctrine of
unconscionability required that the transaction be set aside.
His approach was to acknowledge the validity of the consent but
to go on to consider the doctor’s conduct in the light of his
professional duty to his patient.
[80] La Forest J. spoke for a plurality on the subject of
consent.  After dealing at length with consent in the context of
an imbalance of power between the parties involved in tort and in
contract, he went on to consider the same factors in the criminal
field starting at pp. 250-51:
          
          There  is some support in the criminal law for  an
          approach  that  takes  into account  the  relative
          positions  of the parties.  This can be seen  from
          the definition of assault, which includes assault,
          sexual assault, under s. 265 of the Criminal Code,
          R.S.C., 1985, c.C-46.
[81] After reproducing s. 265, La Forest J. commented on the
concept of “authority” as a basis for vitiating consent under s.
265(3)(d).  Specifically, he said at p. 252:
          
          The general notion of submission to an “authority”
          figure  indicates an inequality of  power  between
          the  parties  such that the existence  of  genuine
          consent  is  questionable.  Section 265(3)  is  an
          expression   of   the   fact   that   in   certain
          circumstances,  considerations  of  public  policy
          will  negate  the legal validity of consent  as  a
          defence to a charge of assault.
[82] La Forest J. proceeded to consider the cases of W.(B.) v.
Mellor, [1989] B.C.J. No. 1393 (S.C.) (QL Systems) and Lyth v.
Dagg (1988), 56 C.C.L.T. 25 (B.C.S.C.) in which the defence of
consent was rejected where sexual relations took place in the
context of an unequal power relationship, in the former case
between a doctor and his patient and in the latter case between a
teacher and his 15-year-old student.  In both cases, the ability
to dominate and influence was equated with the existence of an
unequal power relationship.  La Forest J. discussed these
concepts as indices of power at pp. 255-56:
          
          An  ability  to  “dominate and influence”  is  not
          restricted  to  the student-teacher  relationship.
          Professor  Coleman outlines a number of situations
          which  she calls “power dependency” relationships:
          see    Coleman,    “Sex   in   Power    Dependency
          Relationships:   Taking Unfair  Advantage  of  the
          ‘Fair’  Sex” (1988), 53 Alb. L. Rev. 95.  Included
          in    these    relationships   are   parent-child,
          psychotherapist-patient, physician-patient, clergy-
          patient,  professor-student, attorney-client,  and
          employer-employee.  She asserts that “consent”  to
          a  sexual  relationship in such  relationships  is
          inherently suspect.  She notes, at p.96:
          
               The   common   element  in  power  dependency
               relationships  is an underlying  personal  or
               professional  association  which  creates   a
               significant   power  imbalance  between   the
               parties.
          
               Exploitation   occurs  when  the   “powerful”
               person  abuses the position of  authority  by
               inducing the “dependent” person into a sexual
               relationship, thereby causing harm.
          
          …
          
          If the type of sexual relationship at issue is one
          that  is  sufficiently  divergent  from  community
          standards of conduct, this may alert the court  to
          the possibility of exploitation. [Emphasis added]
[83] La Forest J. then considered whether consent was vitiated in
the case under consideration at pp. 256-61:
          
          The  trial judge held that the appellant’s implied
          consent to the sexual activity was voluntary.  Dr.
          Wynrib,  he  stated, exercised neither  force  nor
          threats  of force and the appellant’s capacity  to
          consent  was  not impaired by her drug  use.   The
          Court   of   Appeal  agreed  that  the   appellant
          voluntarily  engaged  in  the  sexual  encounters.
          However,  it  must be asked if the  appellant  was
          truly  in  a  position to make a free choice.   It
          seems   clear  to  me  that  there  was  a  marked
          inequality  in  the  respective  powers   of   the
          parties.  The appellant was a young woman  with  a
          limited   education.   More  important,  she   was
          addicted  to  the  heavy use of tranquilizers  and
          painkillers.  On this ground alone it can be  said
          that  there  was an inequality in the position  of
          the  parties arising out of the appellant’s  need.
          The  appellant’s  drug dependence  diminished  her
          ability  to make a real choice.  Although she  did
          not  wish  to engage in sexual activity  with  Dr.
          Wynrib,  her  reluctance was  overwhelmed  by  the
          driving  force of her addiction and the unsettling
          prospect   of  a  painful,  unsupervised  chemical
          withdrawal.
          
          …
          
          On  the other side of the equation was an elderly,
          male  professional – the appellant’s  doctor.   An
          unequal distribution of power is frequently a part
          of  the  doctor-patient relationship.   As  it  is
          stated  in The Final Report of the Task  Force  on
          Sexual  Abuse  of  Patients, An  Independent  Task
          Force  Commissioned by The College  of  Physicians
          and   Surgeons  of  Ontario  (November  25,  1991)
          (Chair:  Marilou McPhedran), at p. 11:
          
               Patients  seek the help of doctors when  they
               are  in  a  vulnerable state – when they  are
               sick,  when  they are needy,  when  they  are
               uncertain about what needs to be done.
          
               The  unequal  distribution of  power  in  the
               physician-patient     relationship      makes
               opportunities  for  sexual exploitation  more
               possible  than in other relationships.   This
               vulnerability gives physicians the  power  to
               exact  sexual compliance.  Physical force  or
               weapons   are   not  necessary  because   the
               physician’s  power  comes  from  having   the
               knowledge and being trusted by patients.
          
          In  this  case, Dr. Wynrib knew that the appellant
          was  vulnerable  and driven by her compulsion  for
          drugs.   It  is likely that he knew  or  at  least
          strongly  suspected  that she was  dependant  upon
          Fiorinal before she admitted her addiction to him.
          It  was  he who ferreted out that she was addicted
          to drugs.  As a doctor, the respondent knew how to
          assist  the  appellant medically and he  knew  (or
          should have known) that she could not “just  quit”
          taking drugs without treatment.
          
          …
          
          The  respondent’s medical knowledge and  knowledge
          of  the  appellant’s addiction, combined with  his
          authority to prescribe drugs, gave him power  over
          her.   It  was  he who suggested the sex-for-drugs
          arrangement.
          
          However,  it  must  still be asked  if  there  was
          exploitation.   In  my  opinion  there  was.   Dr.
          Herbert  of  the  Department of  Family  Practice,
          Faculty   of   Medicine,  University  of   British
          Columbia, expressed the opinion that “a reasonable
          practitioner would have taken steps to attempt  to
          help  Ms.  Norberg  end  her  addiction  by,   for
          example, suggesting drug counselling, or,  at  the
          very least, by discontinuing her prescriptions  of
          Fiorinal”.   However, Dr. Wynrib did not  use  his
          medical  knowledge and expertise  to  address  the
          appellant’s  addiction.  Instead,  he  abused  his
          power  over  her and exploited the information  he
          obtained concerning her weakness to pursue his own
          personal interests.  It seems to me that a sex-for-
          drugs  arrangement initiated by a doctor with  his
          drug  addict  patient is a relationship  which  is
          divergent  from what the community would  consider
          acceptable.
          
          …
          
          There  is  also  a body of opinion  which  regards
          sexual  contact in any doctor-patient relationship
          as exploitative.  In the opinion of the Task Force
          on Sexual Abuse of Patients, supra, at p.12:
          
               Due  to  the position of power the  physician
               brings  to  the  doctor-patient relationship,
               there  are NO circumstances – NONE – in which
               sexual  activity  between a physician  and  a
               patient   is  acceptable.   Sexual   activity
               between   a  patient  and  a  doctor   ALWAYS
               represents sexual abuse, regardless  of  what
               rationalization or belief system  the  doctor
               chooses to use to excuse it.  Doctors need to
               recognize  that they have power  and  status,
               and  that  there may be times when a  patient
               will test the boundaries between them.  It is
               ALWAYS  the doctor’s responsibility  to  know
               what  is  appropriate and never to cross  the
               line into sexual activity.
          
          Indeed, the Hippocratic Oath indicates that sexual
          contact between a doctor and his or her patient is
          fundamentally improper:
          
               In  every  house where I come I  will  enter
               only  for  the good of my patients,  keeping
               myself  far  from all intentional  ill-doing
               and   seduction,  and  especially  from  the
               pleasures of love with women or with men, be
               they free or slaves.
          
               (Dorland’s   Illustrated  Medical  Dictionary
               (27th ed. 1988), at p.768.)
          
          These observations were directed at the regulation
          of  the  doctor-patient relationship, rather  than
          civil  liability  and I need  not  consider  their
          precise  implications in the latter context.   For
          we are not here dealing with just a doctor-patient
          relationship    but    a    doctor-drug     addict
          relationship,  and  it  was  not  just  a   sexual
          relationship  but  a  sex-for-drugs  relationship.
          These  circumstances suggest that the  appellant’s
          consent  was not genuine for the purposes  of  the
          law.
          
          …
          
          To  summarize, in my view, the defence of  consent
          cannot succeed in the circumstances of this  case.
          The appellant had a medical problem – an addiction
          to  Fiorinal.   Dr.  Wynrib had knowledge  of  the
          problem.   As  a doctor, he had knowledge  of  the

The appellant maintains that the addition of s. 273.1 has
broadened the scope of criminal conduct.  Accordingly, simply
because the doctor/patient relationship is one of trust involving
an imbalance of power, this does not mean that sexual activity
between doctor/patient will involve an “exercise of authority”
within the meaning of s. 265(3)(d).
[110]     This argument depends upon “authority” being
interpreted as it was by Beauregard J.A. in Saint-Laurent.  As
noted in paragraph [103] above, this interpretation has been
rejected by the Supreme Court of Canada.
[111]     The appellant argued that when interpreting the words
“exercise of authority” this court should apply the rule of
statutory construction that provides that if a penal provision is
reasonably capable of two interpretations, the court should adopt
the interpretation that is most favourable to the accused.  This
rule of strict construction, however, only becomes applicable
when attempts at the interpretation suggested by s. 12 of the
Interpretation Act fail to resolve the uncertainty.
[112]     In R. v. Hasselwander (1993), 81 C.C.C. (3d) 471, Cory
J. set out the principle as follows, at p.478:
          
          Thus,  the  rule  of strict construction  becomes
          applicable  only  when attempts  at  the  neutral
          interpretation   suggested   by   s.12   of   the
          Interpretation  Act still leave reasonable  doubt
          as  to  the meaning or scope of the text  of  the
          statute.  As Professor Côté has pointed out, this
          means  that  even with penal statutes,  the  real
          intention of the legislature must be sought,  and
          the meaning compatible with its goals applied.
[113]     For the reasons above, I am satisfied that the purpose
of s. 265(3)(d) is to criminalize coerced sexual relations rather
than to proscribe sexual relations within specific categories of
relationships.  Viewed in that light, the meaning of s. 265(3)(d)
is clear and there is no need to resort to the restrictive rule
of construction relied upon by the appellant.
DISPOSITION
[114]     Whether or not the appellant had a right to command or
to enforce obedience, he clearly had the power to influence the
conduct and actions of others.  He also, clearly, exercised that
power for his own benefit and interest and against the interests
of his patients X and Y.  Accordingly, I see no merit in this
last ground of appeal and I would dismiss the appeal against
conviction.
SENTENCE
[115]     On May 12, 1997, the appellant was sentenced to one
year imprisonment on each count to be served consecutively.  At
the time of sentencing, the appellant was 48 years of age.  He
had been married and divorced three times.  He has two children
from his second marriage.
[116]     He did not testify at his trial.
[117]     He pleaded guilty to charges of sexual misconduct
brought by the Ontario Board of Examiners in Psychology and
surrendered his licence to practice as a psychologist.
[118]     Since April 1994, he has resided in Victoria, B.C. with
Dr. Tana Dineen, a licenced psychologist and author.  They run a
bed and breakfast.
[119]     The Crown’s position at trial was that the sentence
should be three years.  The defence position was that a suspended
sentence with probation would be sufficient; or, at most, a
conditional sentence of three to six months should be imposed.
[120]     The trial judge found that “there was a blatant long-
term breach of trust by a highly intelligent and experienced
psychologist regarding two patients, who as a result of his
unprofessional and criminal activity, have suffered harm”.
[121]     He further found that the appellant had a fiduciary
relationship with the victims and he deliberately groomed and
manipulated them to satisfy his lust, knowing that doing so would
harm them.  The trial judge noted that any remorse expressed by
the appellant was for the loss of his licence, not for his
criminal acts.
[122]     The trial judge found that a conditional sentence would
not be consistent with the fundamental purposes and principles of
sentencing.
[123]     I agree with all of the above conclusions.  The gravity
of the offences demands incarceration.  The extent of the
denunciation required removes a conditional sentence as a
possibility.  I would grant leave to appeal the sentence but
dismiss that appeal.
