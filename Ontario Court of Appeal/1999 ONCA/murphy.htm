                     ENDORSEMENT


[1]   The issue in this case was the proper interpretation  of  a
separation   agreement  and,  in  particular,  what  rights   and
obligations  arose  from the clause dealing  with  the  appellant
husband’s pension from Ontario Hydro.


[2]   In her action, the respondent wife was claiming for amounts
not paid to her in respect of the appellant husband’s pension. It
appears  to  be  common  ground  between  the  parties  that   if
maintained,  the  judgment  for the amount  found  owing  to  the
respondent  also  effectively determines the ongoing  rights  and
obligations of the parties under the agreement.
[1]
[3]   It  was the appellant’s position at trial that the November
28, 1984 separation agreement did not reflect the true intentions
of  the  parties  and that it should be rectified  to  include  a
provision that the respondent’s entitlement to any proceeds  from
his  pension would terminate upon her remarrying or entering into
a common law relationship for a period of 30 days or more.


[4]  The trial judge refused to give effect to this argument, and
granted  judgment to the respondent. We believe  that  the  trial
judge  committed two errors which require the judgment to be  set
aside and a new trial ordered.


[5]   The  evidence of the appellant was that the  “Revisions  to
Proposed  Separation Agreement”, signed by both parties, resolved
all  the  outstanding issues between them and that  very  shortly
thereafter, the agreement, upon which the respondent relies,  was
executed.  The appellant’s version is supported by the letter  of
November 20, 1984 from his solicitor and was not disputed by  the
respondent. Despite the uncontradicted evidence of the  appellant
that the outstanding issues between the parties were resolved  by
the  “Revisions  to  Proposed Separation  Agreement”,  the  trial
judge, in refusing to grant rectification, appears to have  based
his  decision, at least in part, upon the assumption  that  there
were further negotiations:


          I  don’t  have any evidence about the context
          in  which that negotiation took place  and  I
          think that’s important because I have no idea
          what  horse  trading went  on  between  these
          parties.  It may well be that as at the  time
          Exhibit  Five  [the  “Revisions  to  Proposed
          Separation  Agreement”] was signed  that  was
          the  substance or at least a portion  of  the
          substance   of  the  bargain  between   these
          people.  But a month later or so,  they  sign
          the new document and it’s different. How am I
          to  tell  whether  or  not  some  compromises
          weren’t  made  and  some changes  in  various
          other entitlements or positions unless I know
          the   entire   course  of  history   or   the
          negotiations  and I don’t have  the  evidence
          from the lawyers.

     [6]  In our view, on the basis of the record before him, the
trial  judge  was not entitled to engage in the speculation  that
there were further negotiations that could have altered the terms
reflected by the “Revisions to Proposed Separation Agreement”.


[7]   The  trial judge also stated that if he were to accept  the
evidence  of  the  appellant, he would, in effect,  be  making  a
finding  that  the appellant’s solicitor “committed malpractice”.
As  the  solicitor had not testified and was not otherwise before
the  court, the trial judge concluded that he could not make that
finding:
          [1]  I’m not prepared to make any finding of fact which might
          lead to that conclusion in the absence of the lawyer having an
          opportunity to defend himself.

     [8]   In  our  view, the trial judge imposed an  unwarranted
constraint  on the right of the parties to have the case  decided
on  the  basis  of  the admissible evidence.  A  finding  in  the
appellant’s favour, had that been the result, would have  had  no
adverse consequences for the solicitor who could not be bound  by
the  result  and  who  had no right to be heard  in  the  present
proceedings.


[9]   In view of the issues and evidence as a whole, we find that
these  errors are sufficient to cast doubt on the judgment  under
review.   As  the  case  gives  rise  to  difficult   issues   of
interpretation  which turn, in part at least, on credibility,  it
would  not  be  appropriate  for this  court  to  substitute  its
determination for that of the trial judge.  Accordingly, we order
a new trial.


[10]  The  matter of costs, both of the first trial and  of  this
appeal,  should be left to the discretion of the trial  judge  on
the new trial.
