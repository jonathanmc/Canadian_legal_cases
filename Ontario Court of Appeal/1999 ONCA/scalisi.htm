                      ENDORSEMENT
                                
[1]  The appellant has raised two grounds of appeal.


[2]   With respect to the first ground, the interventions of  the
trial judge fell far short of those which would permit this court
to  interfere.   See  Shoppers Mortgage and Loan  Corporation  v.
Health  First  Wellington Square Ltd. v. Health First  Wellington
Square  Ltd.  (1995), 23 O.R. (3d) 362 (Ont. C.A.).  Further,  in
our  view,  neither these interventions, nor the  record  in  its
entirety,  substantiate  the serious allegation  that  the  trial
judge  exhibited  bias  against the appellant  as  early  as  the
testimony  of  the  first witness, nor did his  conduct  raise  a
reasonable apprehension of bias against the appellant as  defined
by the authorities, such as R. v. S.(R.D.), [1997] 3 S.C.R. 484.


[3]   With  respect to the second ground of appeal, in this  case
the  appellant  had  the  burden to prove  that  Yolanda  Scalisi
deliberately  set the fire.  This was a substantial burden  given
the gravity of the conduct alleged.  The ultimate question to  be
decided was whether the appellant had proved she deliberately set
the  fire. While acknowledging that her smoking may have been the
cause  of  the  fire,  Ms. Scalisi testified  that  she  did  not
deliberately  set it.  The trial judge believed her testimony  as
he was entitled to do.  In our view, it has not been demonstrated
that  he erred in concluding that the appellant did not meet  its
burden of proof.


[4]  Therefore, the appeal is dismissed with costs.
