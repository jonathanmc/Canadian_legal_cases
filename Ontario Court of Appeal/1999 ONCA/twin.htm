                      ENDORSEMENT
[1]  For the purposes of this appeal, it is unnecessary to decide
whether Ontario, having chosen, in its capacity as owner of the
project, to use the bid depository process, owed a duty of care
to subcontractors such as Twin City to take reasonable steps to
ensure the integrity of the bid depository process.


[2]  Assuming that such a duty existed, either generally as a
result of using the bid depository process or on the specific
facts of this case, we are satisfied that in taking the steps it
did, Ontario complied with this duty.


[3]  Contrary to the views expressed by the trial judge, in order
to fulfil its obligation Ontario was not required to investigate
or adjudicate the complex legal and factual issues arising out of
the dispute between Bradsil and Twin City. Nor, in our opinion,
was Ontario obliged to mediate the dispute between Bradsil and
Twin City with a view to working out a solution acceptable to
both parties.


[4]  What Ontario did do was to require Bradsil to try to work
out an agreement with Twin City, failing which Bradsil could
replace Twin City with one of the subcontractors that had bid on
the project at a price not less than that submitted by Twin City.


[5]  In our opinion, this was a reasonable response designed to
preserve the integrity of the bid depository process. It sought
to overcome the mischief of bid-shopping by precluding Bradsil
from entering a new subcontract at a price lower than that
submitted by Twin City. This measure effectively removed any
incentive on the part of a general contractor, such as Bradsil,
to  circumvent the rules governing the bidding process.


[6]  The trial judge was also of the view that Ontario was
negligent in failing to ensure Bradsil’s compliance with the
condition that the new subcontract price not be less than that
submitted by Twin City. With respect, we disagree.


[7]  Much as it would have been preferable for Ontario to follow
up with Bradsil, in our opinion, it was not obliged to do so.
Ontario was entitled to rely upon Bradsil’s unqualified assurance
that the new subcontract price would not be less than the price
submitted by Twin City. To the extent, if any, that Bradsil
breached this condition, Ontario may have been justified in
reducing the amount it was obliged to pay Bradsil under the
contract. This, however, was a matter between Ontario and
Bradsil. It had nothing to do with Twin City.


[8]  Finally, we take issue with the trial judge’s finding that
Bradsil’s initial tender was informal and as such, it should have
been disqualified.


[9]  On its face, Bradsil’s tender was unqualified. The problem
of Bradsil’s alleged duplicity was only brought to light after
the closing date for tenders had passed. To say that Ontario
should, at that point, have disqualified the bid as informal
would have required Ontario to become embroiled in the legal and
factual dispute between Bradsil and Twin City, something it was
not required to do.


[10] In sum, we have not been persuaded that there was any basis
upon which Ontario could be found liable to Twin City.
Accordingly, the appeal is allowed, the judgment is set aside,
and in its place judgment is granted dismissing Twin City’s claim
against Ontario.


[11] Ontario is entitled to its costs, both at trial and on
appeal.
