Synopsis of The Chippewas of Sarnia Band v. Canada (A.G.)
 
Judgment

 

 

Summary of the decision

From June 19th to 29th, 2000, a five-judge panel of the Court of Appeal for Ontario, composed of Associate Chief Justice Osborne and Justices Finlayson, Doherty, Charron, and Sharpe, considered appeals arising from a series of motions for summary judgment related to an aboriginal land claim over a four square mile parcel of land in and near the City of Sarnia.  The land is presently occupied by over 2,000 businesses, organizations, and individuals.  On April 30, 1999, the motions judge, Ontario Superior Court Justice Archie Campbell, ruled that a 1853 Crown patent purporting to transfer the lands from the Chippewas of Sarnia to a non-aboriginal party was invalid.  On equitable grounds, however, he dismissed the Chippewas’ claim against the present owners of the disputed lands, and declared that they held their lands free and clear of any Chippewa aboriginal or treaty right.  However, the Band’s action for damages against the Crown did not form part of the summary judgment motion and could continue.  All parties appealed.

The unanimous Court of Appeal upholds the decision below that the Chippewa lands were never properly surrendered to the Crown.  However, the Court holds that as the 1853 patent has been relied on as valid for over 150 years, it should not now be set aside.  By virtue of their subsequent actions, the Chippewas effectively accepted the sale of their lands and cannot now challenge the validity of the transaction or be entitled to the return of the lands.  Therefore, the Chippewas are left with their claim in damages against the Crown.

Factual background

Prior to 1825, much of southwestern Ontario consisted of unceded lands of the Chippewas.  In 1827, the Chippewas entered into Treaty 29 with the British Crown, finalizing an earlier agreement whereby the Chippewas surrendered some 2.2 million acres of land to King George IV.  However, the Chippewas retained four small reserves for themselves, including the “Upper Reserve” which then encompassed the disputed lands.

By 1839, Malcolm Cameron, a businessman, politician, and land speculator, had developed a close working relationship with Joshua Wawanosh, the regional Head Chief of the Chippewas.  Cameron was interested in the purchase of a large part of the Upper Reserve, and was permitted by government officials to negotiate with the Chippewas to this end.  He was advised that any bargain reached would be subject to ultimate Crown approval.

Cameron met with the Chippewa chiefs on the Upper Reserve and negotiated a sale of the lands.  Those chiefs later called on Indian Department officials to propose to the government on their behalf the sale of lands “off the rear of the Upper St. Clair Reserve”.  There is every reason to believe that the chiefs spoke on behalf of the Chippewas for the purposes of the transaction.  Contrary to the proper legal procedures then in place, however, no formal surrender of the Chippewa lands was ever obtained.

No evidence was proffered from which to infer that the lack of surrender was due to any conspiracy to deprive the Chippewas of their land.  Rather, it appears that the requirement that there be a valid surrender was merely forgotten amidst the administrative chaos that reigned over the Indian Department of the day.  Regardless of this oversight, it is clear that by the fall of 1843 the Chippewas were aware of the full extent of the lands involved in the sale, yet lodged no complaints of irregularities with the Indian Department.

In the fourteen years following the sale discussions, Cameron sold portions of the disputed lands to settlers who then began to occupy and develop the land.  Furthermore, disputes regarding road allowances through the reserve illustrated that the Chippewas had accepted the Cameron transaction as an effective transfer of land, despite the absence of a surrender.  In 1853, letters patent were finally granted by the Crown to Cameron.

Current legal proceedings

In 1995, after they determined that there was no formal surrender of the land, the Chippewas commenced an action against the governments of Ontario and Canada, and the current commercial and residential landowners.  The Chippewas sought a declaration of their right of possession over the disputed lands, in addition to monetary damages for trespass and breach of the Crown’s fiduciary duty.  Prior to trial, the Government of Canada moved for summary judgment, claiming that there was no factual or legal basis for the Chippewas’ claim.  The landowners brought a similar motion.  In response, the Chippewas brought a cross-motion for a declaration that no valid defence to their claim had been raised, and therefore that the claim should succeed.

The motions judge held that the disputed lands had never been validly surrendered by the Chippewas, and that the patent the Crown had issued to Cameron in 1853 was therefore invalid.  The motions judge held further, however, that the passage of time allowed the landowners to rely upon the defence that they were good faith purchasers of the land for value.

The Court of Appeal agrees that there was no surrender, and accepts the proposition that aboriginal title over land can only be lost through a surrender of the lands to the Crown.  The Court thus considers that the issue of importance in this case is whether, in the absence of a surrender, the Chippewas have a right to the relief sought, or whether their claims are barred, either by statutory limitations or equitable doctrines.

The present landowners and the Government of Ontario argued that the Crown Liability and Proceedings Act, the Nullum Tempus Act of 1769, and a series of pre-confederation limitation statutes operated to bar the Chippewas claim.  The Court dismisses their arguments on the first two statutes because their application is restricted to proceedings by the Crown, and are thus inapplicable to the Chippewas’ claim.  Regarding the pre-Confederation statutes, the Court holds that the legislation did not evince a “clear and plain” intention to extinguish aboriginal title, and thus could not legally bar the Chippewas from seeking a remedy.

With respect to the application of equitable doctrines, the Court holds that the unique nature of aboriginal title does not preclude analogizing from, or adapting, the rules of the common law.  The reasons why public and private law defences must be available are particularly evident in a case such as this, where the remedies claimed by the Chippewas invoke both the principles of public law, to challenge the legality of governmental action, and those applicable to private property, to assert a claim against the private citizens now occupying the disputed lands.

The Court states that despite the absence of a surrender in this case, the Cameron patent was valid on its face.  Accordingly, since apparently valid acts of public officials are relied upon by members of the public at large in planning their affairs, the rights of the Chippewas as the party aggrieved by the error must be balanced with the interests of the subsequent purchasers and the interests of orderly administration.  As a principle of law, the Court holds that a remedy may be refused where delay by the aggrieved party in asserting the claim would result in prejudice or hardship to the public interest or to third parties who have acted in good faith upon the censured act.

Considering the Chippewas’ acceptance of the Cameron transaction, the Court holds that, while this does not and cannot amount to a formal surrender, it is a fact that may be considered in a decision to grant or to withhold a remedy.  Another factor of importance is that, from a purposive perspective, the transaction occurred in such a way that the concerns that the surrender requirement was meant to address were in fact dealt with. For example, the Crown did interpose between the parties to ensure that the Chippewas’ interests were protected and the sale proceeds, which reflected the market value of the land, were paid to the Crown, and held in trust for the Chippewas.  The Court finds that there is every indication that the Crown officials had every intention of following the usual practice and obtaining a formal surrender, but that for some reason they neglected to do so.

Therefore, while the Cameron patent is subject to judicial review because of the failure to obtain a formal surrender, it does not appear to have been the source of prejudice to the Chippewas at the time they sold the disputed lands.  The third party purchasers relied upon the patent for over 150 years, and the Court finds that the good faith purchaser for value defence should be applied in favour of the present landowners to dismiss the Chippewas’ claim against them.

As a result, the Court allows the appeals by the landowners, along with the Governments of Canada and Ontario, to dismiss the Chippewa claim as related to the land in question on the basis that the 1853 Cameron patent was valid.  The Court dismisses the Chippewas’ appeal on their motion for summary judgment in respect of the invalidity of the patent.  The Chippewas are left with their claim in damages against the Crown.
