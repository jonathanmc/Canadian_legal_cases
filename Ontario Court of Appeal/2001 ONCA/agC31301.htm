On appeal from the conviction entered by Justice Edward F. Wren, sitting with a jury, dated November 20, 1998 and the sentence imposed on December 18, 1998.*

ENDORSEMENT

[1]               The appellants were convicted of sexual assault.  It was alleged that they and two others gang raped a 15 year old girl who had previously dated A.  A. was also convicted of threatening the complainant in a telephone conversation a short time after the rapes and with breaching a recognizance.  This endorsement addresses the issues raised on both appeals.

1.         The Crown’s closing argument

[2]               B. testified that he engaged in consensual sexual activity with the complainant after a person named “Allan” had done so.  According to B.’s testimony, comments made by Allan immediately after Allan had engaged in sexual activity with the complainant led to B.’s consensual sexual activity with the complainant.  B. said that he did not know Allan, but that Allan was a friend of A.  In cross-examination, B. agreed that once he became aware that the victim was alleging that she did not consent to the sexual activity, he made no attempt to locate or contact Allan and did not even so much as ask A. about Allan.  In closing argument, Crown counsel contended that B.’s indifference to Allan’s whereabouts, or even his existence, was inconsistent with B.’s testimony as to how the consensual sexual activity had occurred, but was consistent with an attempt to shield a co-perpetrator from exposure to the justice system.

[3]               Whatever force this argument may have had, it was open to the Crown on the evidence.  The Crown did not suggest that B. had an obligation to assist the police or an obligation to produce witnesses.  The Crown simply argued that B.’s evidence was not credible given his own conduct after the relevant events. 

2.         B.'s evidence and the verdict against A.

[4]               A. did not testify, but did rely on B.’s evidence to the extent that it contradicted the evidence of the complainant concerning sexual activity with A.  Counsel for the appellant argues that the trial judge should have told the jury that A. was entitled to be acquitted if the evidence of B. left them with a reasonable doubt as to A.’s culpability. 

[5]               The trial judge repeatedly told the jury that A. must be acquitted if based on all of the evidence they had a reasonable doubt as to his culpability.  The trial judge also told the jury that the onus was on the Crown to prove the absence of consent.  Finally, with respect to the two conflicting versions of events advanced by the complainant and B., the trial judge told the jury that they were not to choose between the two, but must acquit the accused, if after considering all of the evidence they had a reasonable doubt as to guilt.  He did not limit this instruction to B.  These instructions made it clear that A. was entitled to be acquitted if the jury had a reasonable doubt as to whether his sexual activity was consensual, whatever the evidentiary source of that doubt.

[6]               Even if the trial judge should have instructed the jury in the manner described by the appellant, a failure to do so caused no prejudice to A.  The jury was told that it must acquit B. if his evidence left them with a reasonable doubt as to his guilt.  The jury convicted B.  It is impossible to imagine how the jury could have totally rejected B.’s evidence as it applied to B., but somehow had a reasonable doubt based on that very same evidence as it applied to A.

3.        The "Miller" error

[7]               The trial judge repeatedly told the jury that its verdict must be based on all of the evidence.  Unlike R. v. Miller (1991), 68 C.C.C. (3d) 517 (Ont. C.A.), the trial judge did not tell the jury to proceed through their deliberations with a two-step approach, first determining the facts and then the verdict based on the facts as found.  Nor, did the trial judge tell the jury that an acquittal could be based only on evidence that was accepted. 

[8]               The trial judge’s clear indication to the jury that the Crown could prove its case based only on evidence that the jury accepted as true was a correct instruction in law.  Similarly, his instruction that the jury should look for evidence confirming the complainant’s evidence only in evidence it found to be credible was a proper instruction.

4.         The re-reading of the complainant’s evidence

[9]               The jury asked to have a part of the complainant’s testimony read to it during its deliberations.  With the support of at least counsel for one of the appellants (the other said nothing), the trial judge asked the jury to clarify its request.  In doing so, the trial judge distinguished between examination-in-chief and cross-examination.  He told the jury that the cross-examination was more difficult to locate and read back than was examination-in-chief.  The trial judge made it clear, however, that both the examination-in-chief and cross-examination could be made available to the jury. 

[10]          The jury clarified its areas of concern and told the trial judge that it needed to hear only the examination-in-chief.  Without objection by counsel for either appellant, the trial judge proceeded to have the reporter read back a large part of the complainant’s examination-in-chief.  This exchange between the judge and the foreperson of the jury followed:

The Court:  I had mentioned to you, but you appear from your answers to be satisfied with having this and not having the cross-examination? 

The Juror:  That’s correct, sir.

The Court:  If there is anything else then we will remain at your disposal. …

[11]          Immediately after the jury retired, the trial judge became concerned that he should have required that the jury hear the parts of the complainant’s cross-examination dealing with the topics raised by the jury.  After consultation with counsel, the trial judge recalled the jury to tell them that the cross-examination could be made available to them.  He reminded them that the purpose of cross-examination was to test the reliability and the truth of the evidence given in examination-in-chief.  The trial judge told the jury that there was a danger in looking only at the victim’s evidence in-chief.  He admonished the jury that concerns about lengthening the proceedings should not inhibit them from listening to the cross-examination.  He concluded by telling the jury that if it was their firm decision not to hear the cross-examination, he would abide by that decision.   The jury responded with the following note:

… The members of the jury did not request more than the testimony of the Crown in order to conserve time.  The reasons for the request were the sequence of events according to P.H. [the complainant] alone during a brief period and the wording according to Ms. H. of the alleged threat.  The jury has been polled on the above and are unanimous in their decision.

[12]          The jury announced that it had a verdict in the same note.

[13]          The trial judge was obviously trying to respond to the jury’s question in a way that would provide the most assistance to the jury.  He received little help from counsel in that endeavour.  In our view, the trial judge was wrong to distinguish between examination-in-chief and cross-examination when seeking clarification from the jury as to its areas of concern.  The trial judge’s remarks could have discouraged the jury from including the cross-examination in its request to rehear certain evidence.

[14]          It is not uncommon for a jury to request to have evidence made available to it during the course of its deliberations.  Where their request is unclear or so broad as to make compliance impractical in the context of an ongoing jury trial, it is entirely appropriate for the trial judge to seek clarification of that request.  If the trial judge seeks clarification, he should attempt to have the jury identify the areas of the evidence that are of interest to them.  They may do so by reference to the witnesses, by reference to topics, or in some other way.  The trial judge should not say anything that might invite the jury to pick and choose from among the various parts of the evidence it has identified as relevant to its request.  After the trial judge has clarified the jury’s request to the extent possible, the jury should be excused and counsel invited to make submissions as to what parts of the evidence should be read to the jury.  The trial judge is entitled to the assistance of counsel and will no doubt give considerable weight to counsel’s submissions as to which parts of their examination or cross-examination should be read to the jury. 

[15]          The aim must be to provide the jury with all of the evidence that is responsive to the question regardless of whether that evidence comes by way of examination-in-chief, cross-examination or reply.  While the trial judge no doubt has some leeway not to read evidence which is simply repetitive of evidence already read, he or she must proceed cautiously.  Repetition is not necessarily harmful.  Where evidence in-chief has been read to the jury, the trial judge must include any cross-examination which weakens or qualifies the part of the examination-in-chief which has been read to the jury:  R. v. Olbey (1979), 50 C.C.C. (2d) 257 at 273 (S.C.C.). 

[16]          Counsel for both appellants cross-examined the victim on matters referred to in the part of the victim’s evidence read to the jury.  Neither cross-examination was extensive.  As a result of that cross-examination, three relatively minor inconsistencies or qualifications were revealed in the complainant’s testimony.  At least those parts of the cross-examination should have been re-read to the jury. 

[17]          We are satisfied, however, that the failure to read those parts of the cross-examination did not prejudice the appellants.  Of the minor inconsistencies, only one related to the actual events at which the jury’s question was directed.  That inconsistency did not go to a crucial element of the allegation against either appellant.  In addition, that inconsistency had been mentioned by the trial judge in the course of his review of the position taken on behalf of the appellants. 

[18]          There was no suggestion by counsel for the appellants at trial that the relevant parts of the cross-examination should be read to the jury until the trial judge raised the issue.  No doubt, counsel realized that a reading of the cross-examination pertaining to the topics to which the jury’s question was directed would result in a repetition of a great deal of the evidence given by the complainant during her examination-in-chief.

[19]          Finally, and perhaps most importantly, we know exactly what this jury was concerned about.  As indicated in their final note to the trial judge, all twelve jurors were interested in “the sequence of events according to P.H.” and “the wording of the alleged threat”.  Nothing said in cross-examination qualified the victim’s evidence given in-chief on either of these matters.

5.         The sentence appeals

[20]          The sentences were substantial and deservedly so.  This was a vicious gang rape of a young girl by four men.  The appellants are young, come from good families and have enjoyed academic success.  Despite these mitigating factors, nothing short of a significant penitentiary term could adequately reflect the seriousness of the assault on the complainant.  The appellants also have criminal records involving crimes of violence. 

[21]          The trial judge’s reasons for sentence demonstrate no error in principle and the length of the sentence imposed cannot be said to be manifestly unreasonable. 

[22]          The conviction appeals are dismissed.  Leave to appeal the sentences is granted and the sentence appeals are dismissed.
