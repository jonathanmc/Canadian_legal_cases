HEARD: July 4, 2001

 

On appeal from the judgment of Justice Donald R. Cameron dated November 5, 1999, reported at (1999), 181 D.L.R. (4th) 545.

ABELLA J.A.:

BACKGROUND

[1]               This is an appeal by the City of Toronto[1] from a decision declaring parts of a City by-law unconstitutional. The challenged provisions prohibit anyone on the premises of a Collision Reporting Centre from recommending an automotive repair or body shop to motor vehicle owners involved in an accident. They also require the conspicuous posting at these premises of three signs stipulating that insurance company representatives and employees of the Collision Reporting Centre are forbidden from making any such recommendations. The basis for the declaration of invalidity was the unjustified infringement of the insurance companies guarantees of freedom of expression contrary to s. 2(b) of the Charter of Rights and Freedoms, which states:

      2. Everyone has the following fundamental freedoms:

(b) freedom of thought, belief, opinion and expression, including freedom of the press and other media of communication

[2]               The history of the establishment of Collision Reporting Centres (CRCs) in Toronto is set out with efficient clarity in the reasons of Cameron J. and there is therefore no need to reformulate them here. The following portions of his reasons dealing with this history set out the factual background to the legal issues:

Collision Reporting Centres

CRCs are offices with adjacent parking lots where people who have been involved in motor vehicle accidents involving combined damages exceeding a specific amount (now $1,000) must attend within 24 hours to report the accidents to the police, pursuant to the Highway Traffic Act, 1990, c.H.8, s.199 and O. Reg. 537/97. In Toronto all accidents must be reported at a CRC. CRCs also provide the opportunity for insureds to contact their insurers through claims representatives either on site or by telephone at the CRC.

CRCs were initiated by the Toronto police force with the assistance of the insurance industry in the early 1990s in order to reduce the time and cost associated with investigating and administrating motor vehicle accidents. The first full service CRC pilot project opened in North York in January, 1994. There are now [over 16] CRCs in Ontario, three of which operate in the City of Toronto, in Scarborough, Etobicoke and North York.

The three [then existing] Toronto CRCs are owned and operated for profit by private companies all of which are controlled by a company which also owns and operates a towing business.

The Toronto CRCs have on the lawns of the CRC, visible to the public, signs showing the location of the CRC and the name and symbol of Toronto Police and the Ontario Provincial Police. There is also, equally prominent on the entrance door and on a wall of the CRC building, the name and symbol of the police and the name and corporate logo of the company which operates the CRC.

There is no cost to the police or the public for the use of the CRC. The owners of CRCs receive their revenue from rental of space to insurance companies and from fees charged to insurance companies for copies of accident reports to the police and self reports to insurers prepared by insureds.

All CRCs operate in a similar fashion. After a motorist has been involved in an accident, the vehicle is driven by the insured owner or towed to a CRC. On arrival at the CRC, the insured completes a self-report form. The insured then meets with a member of the police force on site who inspects the vehicle and completes a police report of the accident. Both forms are then forwarded to the insured's insurer.

After completing this process, the insured is directed to an area of the CRC that is dedicated to insurance companies. Some motor vehicle insurers, such as General Accident and Allstate, have claims representatives on site at the CRC. Where there is no claims representative, the insured is provided with access to a telephone to call his or her insurance company. The insured can discuss with his insurer issues respecting coverage, rental vehicles and repairs. Arrangements will also be made to inspect the vehicle either at the CRC, the insured's home or a Repair Shop.

Prior to leaving the CRC the vehicle owner is given a notice of: (a) the maximum towing rates permitted by the By-law; (b) the prohibition in the By-law against tow truck drivers recommending a Repair Shop or recommending that a vehicle be taken to a Repair Shop before being taken to the CRC, and (c) the requirement in the By-law that the tow truck must drop the vehicle at the CRC and leave immediately. The owner is also given a brochure, prepared by the insurers, which describes the repair options, the advantages of selecting a Repair Shop selected by the owner's insurer and recommends that the owner call the insurer to discuss repair options further. While at the CRC the owner can also obtain a list of the names and addresses of approximately 220 Repair Shops in Toronto which meet the Repair Shop accreditation standards established by the By-law. Finally, the owner may also receive a brochure as to how the CRC operates, describing it as a partnership between the police and the insurance industry. [Emphasis added]

Repair Shops

One of the key concerns of insured vehicle owners at the time of an accident is how and where to get their vehicles repaired. To meet this need, it is common industry practice in Canada for insurance companies to recommend Repair Shops to their customers, when asked to do so. The insurer only recommends a Repair Shop. It does not require that the insured use the recommended Shop. However the Standard Conditions of Policies in s.6 of Schedule I of O. Reg. 777/93 Statutory Conditions - Automobile Insurance and s.5.4 of Form OAP 1 - Ontario Automobile Policy (OAP) permit the insurer a reasonable opportunity to inspect the damage and to elect, within 7 days after the accident is reported, to effect the repairs itself. The use of preferred Repair Shops by insurers is also a common industry practice in the United States and Europe.

An insured is free to take the car to a Repair Shop of the insured's choice which is not recommended by the insurer, subject to the insurer's rights under the policy to effect the repairs itself.

Insurers perceive that the use of preferred Repair Shops offer a number of advantages to both insurers and insureds. Where an insurer refers a number of repair jobs on a continuing basis to a Repair Shop it is often able to negotiate discounts on parts and labour, thus reducing the costs of repair. In addition, because repairs are often done on a priority basis for the insurer's customers, the insurer enjoys savings in the cost of replacement rental vehicles and storage. Finally, operational savings are realized from the fact that more claims can be dealt with or adjusted at fewer locations, thus reducing the number of field adjusters required to appraise damaged vehicles.

Towing Rates and Services

In the early years of this decade the public showed concern about the cost of towing services in Toronto. In or about September 1993, the council of the former Municipality of Metropolitan Toronto (Metro Council) recommended that the Metropolitan Toronto Licensing Commission (the Commission) review the rates and fees charged for services provided by tow truck operators. In 1995, the Commission struck a Special Purpose Committee on Towing Rates to investigate this matter (the Special Purpose Committee) and to investigate possible overcharges and other improper business practices in connection with towing damaged vehicles to CRCs.

Over time, the mandate of the Special Purpose Committee was expanded to include a review of other matters, including the regulation of CRCs and the establishment of an accreditation program for Repair Shops in Metropolitan Toronto.

The Human Rights [Services] of Metro Council was involved in considering proposals to amend the By-law and periodically reported to Metro Council. Concerns were expressed by many constituencies, including insurers, tow truck operators, repair shops, CRCs and individuals who had used their facilities. The concerns dealt with unregulated towing rates from private property and from accidents. There was a practice of tow truck drivers towing vehicles from accident sites to preferred Repair Shops in return for a commission from the repairer of 15 - 20% of the repair costs. This cost would ultimately be paid by the vehicle owners or their insurers by way of higher repair bills or fraudulent charges.

The Human Rights [Services] stated in its Report to Council No. 6, Attachment C, May 6, 1997 that the essential aspects of its recommendations for amending the By-law, which must be taken as a package, were:

(a)   Mandatory drop off of damaged vehicles by tow trucks at CRCs following an accident;

(b)  Development of a mechanism that would allow all Repair Shops to have fair access to the collision repair business within Metropolitan Toronto;

(c)  Capping of tow truck rates; and

(d)  CRCs must be accountable to elected representatives.

Following the amalgamation of Metropolitan Toronto and its constituent municipalities into the City of Toronto in 1998, the Municipal Licensing and Standards Department of the Municipal Licensing Standards Division of Urban Planning assumed responsibility for administering and enforcing the By-law.

At about the same time as the Provisions were being considered in May 1997, Schedule 37 of the By-law was amended to provide for maximum towing charges. Prior to that time, tow truck operators were required only to file their rates with the Licensing Commission. There was no restriction on the amounts they could charge.

Licensed Repair Shops

Following the enactment of the Amending By-law there were additions to Part 6 of Schedule 24 providing for making available to the public a list of all licensed Repair Shops which become accredited under the By-law by meeting the standards in Appendix A to Part 6. A list showing all of the Repair Shops which meet the accreditation standards of the By-law is available to the public at each CRC. This list contains the names of over 200 Repair Shops, listed by geographical region. The list is substantially longer than any list any one insurer might recommend to its insured.

 

Relevant By-Law

[3]               The challenged by-law provisions, passed on December 18, 1997, are sections 14(2) and 15 of Part 6 to Schedule 24 of By-law No. 20-85 of the City of Toronto. Sections 14 and 15 state:

14. (1) No owner or operator, or employee of such owner or operator, shall recommend a body shop or vehicle repair facility to any motor vehicle owner.

 

(2) No owner or operator, or employee of such owner or operator, shall permit any other person in any part of the licensed premises to recommend a body shop or vehicle repair facility to any motor vehicle owner.

 

15. (1) Every owner and operator shall display and maintain at least three signs in conspicuous places at the licensed premises stating the following: It is expressly forbidden for any employee or official of Collision Reporting Centre or a representative of an insurance company on the premises to recommend a body shop or automotive repair facility.

 

(2) The signs referred to in subsection (1) of this section shall be made of durable material capable of withstanding outdoor climatic conditions and the letters thereon shall be at least three inches high with a stroke of at least half an inch wide. The letters on the signs shall be of a contrasting colour to the background of such letters.

.

[4]               The City of Toronto conceded that these provisions infringe the insurers freedom of expression contrary to s. 2(b) of the Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms, but argued they were a justified limitation under s. 1.

[5]               The basis of the Citys concession was Irwin Toy Ltd. v. Qubec (Attorney General), [1989] 1 S.C.R. 927. In particular, the City conceded that the recommendation of repair shops constitutes expression within the scope of s. 2(b) since it conveys or attempts to convey a meaning. However, it asserts that the provisions under scrutiny address a pressing and substantial concern, namely, to ensure that members of the public who are required by law to attend at a Collision Reporting Centre, are not subject to external pressure about where to have their car repaired.

[6]               The City argued that the provisions have two objectives:

(a)              to ensure that members of the public who have been involved in motor vehicle accidents have an opportunity to make decisions regarding the disposition of their vehicles without external pressure from persons seeking their business or from insurers and CRC operators within a CRC; and

(b)              to preserve the impartiality of CRCs, given that members of the public are required by law to attend at CRCs after certain collisions and CRCs are presented as police facilities.

Decision of the Application Judge

[7]               In rejecting the Citys argument, the application judge ascribed to the City a different motive for the provisions. He found that the true purposes of the provisions were to assuage the towing industry and to prevent the concentration of business in those shops recommended by insurers:

In my opinion, the purpose of the Provisions is to quell the objections of the Repair Shop industry and the towing industry to the insurers on site at CRCs recommending Repair Shops before [] the vehicle owner can consider the list of municipally accredited Repair Shops. The Provisions were not directed to protecting vehicle owners at a vulnerable time or protecting the image of objectivity and integrity of the police at the CRC site.

[8]               The application judge found that these concerns were not sufficiently pressing and substantial to override the insurers s. 2(b) Charter rights. First, there was no evidence of a complaint from an insured about insurers recommending repair shops. Second, in his view, the purpose of the provisions was not to protect insureds from pressure but to deprive insurers of the first opportunity to make a recommendation to their insureds in order to broaden the distribution of repair work among more Repair Shops. Third, a 1999 report prepared by the Competition Bureau of Industry Canada had found, in his words, that the practice of insurers recommending preferred repair or body shops has not resulted in, nor is it likely to result in, a substantial lessening of competition.

[9]               The application judge concluded that permitting the insurers to recommend repair shops could result in less expensive insurance, quality repair services, lower costs and greater profits to the insurers, and better business for the recommended repair shops. He found that these interests were equally deserving of protection in a fairly operating free market economy as the rights of other repair shops and tow truck drivers to continued sources of business. In his view, these latter rights of other repair shops and tow truck drivers could still be furthered, since, in his words:

The Repair Shops not recommended by insurers are free to advertise, either individually or under some common umbrella to the extent permitted by law, and to charge labour rates and provide a quality of repairs, levels of service and other elements of their business which would make them more attractive to vehicle owners and competitive with the Repair Shops recommended by insurers. As a group they could seek to participate in the marketing of the municipal accreditation program and distribution of the list of accredited Repair Shops, indicating the benefits of dealing with Repair Shops on that list as opposed to others. The Repair Shops not on the insurers' preferred lists could affiliate themselves with other business organizations serving the driving public such as automobile clubs, gasoline and service stations or automobile dealers.

 

[10]          The application judge credited the City with wanting, in good faith, to prevent discontent in the towing industry and concentration of the repair business into those shops recommended by insurers. Having ascribed this new objective to the City, he then found that the protection of the commercial interests of the towing industry and repair shops was not sufficiently pressing to outweigh the expressive rights encompassed in the insurers commercial interests in being able to communicate freely with their insureds. He found that both sets of commercial interests were in any event equally far from the core s. 2(b) values referred to by La Forest J. in Ross v. New Brunswick School District No. 15, [1996] 1 S.C.R. 825 and RJR-MacDonald Inc. v. Canada (Attorney General), [1995] 3 S.C.R. 199.

[11]          The application judge noted that those core values, as articulated by LaForest J. in Ross at pp. 876-77 and RJR-MacDonald at pp. 280-81, are those involved in the search for political, artistic and scientific truth, the protection of individual autonomy and self development, and the promotion of public participation in the democratic process. The further the impugned expression lies from these core values protected by s. 2(b), the lower the standard of justification required by s. 1 will be.

[12]          Since the commercial interest sought to be advanced by the City was no more pressing than the one the insurers sought to promote, the application judge found the objectives of the provisions insufficiently significant to override the Charter right. He therefore found it unnecessary to embark on the second stage of the s. 1 analysis, namely, the proportionality test outlined in R. v. Oakes, [1986] 1 S.C.R. 103.

ANALYSIS

[13]          In a s. 1 analysis, a trial judges findings involving legislative, social, scientific or policy evidence are reviewable with less deference than is normally accorded to factual findings. As McLachlin J. stated in RJR-MacDonald at p. 334:

. . . in the context of the s. 1 analysis, more deference may be required to findings based on evidence of a purely factual nature whereas a lesser degree of deference may be required where the trial judge has considered social science and other policy oriented evidence. As a general matter, appellate courts are not as constrained by the trial judges findings in the context of the s. 1 analysis as they are in the course of non-constitutional litigation, since the impact of the infringement on constitutional rights must often be assessed by reference to a broad review of social, economic and political factors in addition to scientific facts. At the same time, while appellate courts are not bound by the trial judges findings in respect of social science evidence, they should remain sensitive to the fact that the trial judge has had the advantage of hearing competing expert testimony firsthand.

[14]          Applying this threshold to a review of the findings of the application judge, and considering that this case was argued on the basis of affidavit and documentary evidence, I do not, with respect, agree with his conclusion that the objectives of the provisions were different from those stated by the City. In my view, based on the evidence, the City has discharged its burden and proven that the limitation of the insurers expressive rights is reasonable and demonstrably justified.

[15]          There is reliable evidence supporting the Citys submissions that the objectives of the provisions were, as previously stated, to protect the public involved in car accidents from business pressures at a vulnerable moment and location, and to preserve the impartiality of Collision Reporting Centres.

[16]          The evidence supports the conclusion of the application judge that the by-law was also enacted to respond to lobbying from non-preferred repair shops about their competitive disadvantage under the former regime. However, the overall structure and content of the Citys legislative package dealing with Collision Reporting Centres relegate this purpose substantially below the two relied on by the City, as the background to the amendments reveals.

[17]          Prior to the enactment of the amendments, there were complaints about excessive towing rates charged by tow truck operators, who towed vehicles to the Centres (which were owned by towing companies), waited while the drivers reported the accident, charged them waiting fees, then directed them to repair shops from which they allegedly received kickbacks.

[18]          Insurance companies on the premises referred motorists to a small list of preferred repair shops, which were alleged to be discount shops using recycled parts to reduce repair costs. According to an April 1997 submission to the Human Services Committee from the Independent Auto Repairs Association, this:

reduced all repair standards to a level causing budget oriented and make shift repairs and finishings to be the acceptable standard.

Documented research supports the fact that most consumers are not aware that preferred/discount auto body shops (and preferred/discount subcontractors) recommended and supported by the insurance industry must give preference to imitation or used replacement parts, provide a discounted labour rate, as well as parts discounts and other incentives to the benefit of the insurers. There are also limited conditional warranties.

[19]          These practices were particularly troubling because they were taking place in connection with a facility that motorists were required by law to attend and with which the police were affiliated.

[20]          In response to these and other public concerns, Metro Council directed the Metropolitan Licensing Commission (MLC) ,[2] the body responsible for the administration and enforcement of the Metro Council Licensing By-law, to review the rates charged by tow trucks in September, 1993.

[21]          The MLC, in turn, established the Special Purpose Committee on Towing Rates early in 1995. This Committee met until March, 1996, hearing concerns about the towing rates charged by tow truck operators at the only then-existing pilot Collision Reporting Center in North York and about the practice of vehicles being towed directly from the accident sites to preferred repair and body shops. The Committee heard about pressures being applied to vulnerable accident victims by tow truck drivers, Collision Centre employees, and on-site insurers about where to direct their repair business.

[22]          As a result of the recommendations of the Special Purpose Committee, Metro Council decided on June 19, 1996 that:

The Metropolitan Licensing Commission Special Purpose Committee on Towing Rates be reconstituted to examine the impact of the Collision Reporting Centres (CRCs) on the towing industry and continue to investigate the operations of CRCs and continue to work towards an acceptable set of rates and report further in this regard

[23]          A year later, in May, 1997, Metro Council enacted a by-law placing a cap on the rates that could be charged by tow truck drivers and owners for taking vehicles involved in accidents to designated locations, including Collision Reporting Centres.

[24]          James Ridge, the former Executive Director of the department that has administrative responsibilities for the Toronto Licensing Commission, confirmed that the purpose of these provisions is to ensure that:

members of the public who have been involved in motor vehicle accidents have an opportunity to make decisions regarding the disposition of their vehicles without external pressures from persons seeking their business

[and that]

members of the public are not subjected to pressures from tow truck owners and drivers and from sources within the CRCs with respect to vehicle repairs and not for reasons involving concerns with respect to competition between auto body repair shops. This is especially significant in view of the physical presentation of the CRCs and the representation of the CRCs as police facilities in the material which is distributed at the CRCs by the owners, operators and employees of the CRCs and by the representatives of the insurance companies affiliated with the CRCs.

[25]          In its February 1996 report to the Metropolitan Toronto Licensing Commission, The Tow Truck Advisory Committee addressed perception and concerns related to the Reporting Centres about highly aggressive solicitation of accident victims for their claims processing and preferred body shop repairs agreement by the private sector operators of these centres. In the Committees view, these perceptions jeopardized the objectives of the Centres as neutral public facilities and facilitated monopolistic repair practices. It also attributed most of the excessive processing delays to the insurance industrys private sector representatives, in directing/steering of accident victims to preferred body shops.

[26]          Other concerns and perceptions included:

        General towing and repair body shop industry slandering tactics on accident victims, during persuasive preferred shop referral attempts by [CRCs] private sector owners/representatives.

 

        Concerns with [CRCs] private sector staff leveraging [CRCs] repair work referral commitments to independent repair facilities, in exchange for securing the independent repair facilities towing business for tow companies owned and operated by [CRCs] private sector ownership.

 

        Unfair perception of Police Departments support towards private enterprise monopolization of Accident towing and Automotive related repair industries.

 

        Current non-competitive monopolization of [CRCs] operations (all three centers owned and operated by same ownership), while all other police contract awarding policies have always strictly imposed One bidder rule policy.

 

        Depriving already under stress and vulnerable accident victims from being accompanied by anyone to the private sector operated area of the centre, and from exercising their freedom of choice rights in regards to repairing their vehicles.

 

        Motorists becoming victims of competitive struggle between [CRCs] private sector sections staff and the customer chosen repair facility.

[27]          In his March 1996 submissions to the Human Services Committee and Metropolitan Toronto Licensing Commission, Robert M. Curry, General Manager for the Police Reporting Centres, addressed the abuse of accident victims from outrageous costs and very strong solicitation by tow truck operators. He stated:

The best protection for the public who are accident victims, is to have a fair, all inclusive tow rate to the closest Police Reporting Centre where their cars will be dropped. This cool down period is required for customers to calm down, have the necessary reports finished and contact their work or next of kin to advise them about the accident.[3] . . .

The customer can then be advised of all their options from the police, insurance company, and any other persons who they can call for advice. The customer can then call the shop of their choice to have the vehicle picked up for repair. There is no storage cost at any of the Police Reporting Centres for 24 hours to give the customer ample time to make their choice. There is no cost to the customer for our services. Participating insurance companies pay flat fees for the services provided to their customers who utilize the reporting centre, and this funds the cost of these facilities for the police and public. Motorists who have their car towed to the reporting centre and dropped, yet have no insurance, would only be responsible for the set tow fee to pick up their car within 24 hours.

[28]          The Citys response was to enact a legislative package, including these challenged provisions, on December 18, 1997, expressly to protect both the public and the integrity of these mandatory reporting premises. These objectives, it seems to me, were pressing at the time and remain substantial. They responded not only to the concerns and perceptions of affected consumers, but also to the recommendations of the Special Purpose Committee and the Human Services Committee of Metro Council, both of which recommended that a statement of rights be distributed to motorists setting out, among other things, their right to choose a repair facility. The mere fact that the recommendation of a statement of rights was made confirms that consumer protection was the objective of the reports that, in turn, inspired the legislative package.

[29]          The recommendation in the March 1996 Report of the Special Purpose Committee that the preferred repair and body shop system be reviewed to ensure more even-handed business distribution to the repair industry was similarly motivated by consumer protection. The recommendation itself demonstrates this motivation:

The Metro Police Services Board should be requested to review the preferred body shop system with a view to replacing it with a system that distributes business evenhandedly to the repair industry. This could be made a condition of a collision reporting centre contract. The present system has enormous potential for payoff from preferred bodyshops to insurance companies, their agents or employees. [Emphasis in original]

[30]          There was, in other words, significant evidence emanating from the hearings, submissions, and reports that justified the Citys concerns about the operation of Collision Reporting Centres, authorized its legislative response, and authenticated its submission that the purpose of the limitation was the protection of the public and the preservation of the integrity of Collision Reporting Centres. These, in my view, are pressing and substantial objectives.

[31]          The remaining inquiry focuses on whether the means used to limit the right are proportional. I begin by observing that we are not dealing with core expression rights, those rights fundamental to the fulfilment of democratic values. We are, instead, dealing with the right of insurers to recommend preferred repair and body shops.

[32]          This right is subject to the reduced justificatory burden applied when the infringement is directed at expression asserted for commercial purposes. As McLachlin J. stated in Rocket v. Royal College of Dental Surgeons of Ontario, [1990] 2 S.C.R. 232, at 241-42 and 246-47:

Although it has been clearly held that commercial expression does not fall outside of the ambit of s. 2(b), the fact that expression is commercial is not necessarily without constitutional significance. Regulation of advertising may offend the guarantee of free expression in s. 2(b) of the Charter, but this does not end the inquiry. The further question of whether the infringement can be justified under s. 1 of the Charter must be considered. It is at this stage that the competing values -- the value of the limitation and the value of free expression -- are weighed in the context of the case. Part of the context, in the case of regulation of advertising, is the fact that the expression at issue is wholly within the commercial sphere.

Placing the conflicting values in their factual and social context when performing the s. 1 analysis permits the courts to have regard to special features of the expression in question. As Wilson J. notes in Edmonton Journal v. Alberta (Attorney General), [1989] 2 S.C.R. 1326, not all expression is equally worthy of protection. Nor are all infringements of free expression equally serious.

 

 

(See also Irwin Toy, supra).

[33]          As for the rational connection test, it seems to me to be axiomatic that eliminating the ability of insurers at Collision Reporting Centres to pressure vulnerable drivers about where to have their cars repaired is rationally connected to the Citys objectives of protecting autonomous consumer decision-making.

[34]          The limitation so minimally impairs the right as to be de minimus, especially when compared with its public interest objectives, namely, to enhance consumers abilities to make an economic decision free from external pressure, and to preserve their confidence in the integrity and impartiality of the facility. All that is being restricted is the location and time of the insurers ability to express their preferred choice of repair shops. The insurers are not entirely prohibited from expressing their thoughts, only from expressing them when the insured is at the Collision Reporting Centre. The insurers can make their recommendations anywhere else and at any other time. They are also free to distribute brochures at the Centres promoting the advantages of choosing a repair shop selected by the insurers and urging the insured to contact the insurer quickly to discuss repair options. Moreover, they are free to make their recommendations if the insured contacts them by telephone from the Centres.

[35]          The City has discharged its onus of showing that the limitation of the insurers expressive rights in these impugned provisions is justified. This case is essentially a competition between the right of insurers to promote their commercial interests without restriction, and the right of the City to protect the publics confidence in the integrity of facilities that the law requires the public to attend at a vulnerable time. These competitive values yield consumer protection as the clear victor.

[36]          There was a cross-appeal by the insurers from a finding by the application judge that the by-law provisions were not ultra vires. We did not call on the City to respond to the cross-appellants submissions. In my view, the Supreme Court of Canada decision in 114957 Canada Lte (Spraytech, Socit darrosage) v. Hudson (Town), [2001] S.C.J. No. 42 is determinative on this issue and I agree with the conclusion of Cameron J.

[37]          I would allow the appeal with costs, set aside the order of Cameron J., and dismiss the insurers application with costs. The cross-appeal is dismissed with costs.
