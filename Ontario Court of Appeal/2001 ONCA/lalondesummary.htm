December 7, 2001

Summary of Lalonde v. Commission de restructuration des services de santé

Summary of the decision

From May 14th to 17th, 2001,  a panel of the Court of Appeal for Ontario, composed of Court of Appeal Justices Karen Weiler and Robert Sharpe, and Superior Court Justice Paul Rivard (sitting as an ad hoc member of the Court of Appeal) heard appeals from a decision of Ontario's Divisional Court dated November 29, 1999.  In that decision, Superior Court Justices James Carnwath, Robert Blair, and Michel Charbonneau quashed directions of the Health Services Restructuring Commission (the "Commission") ordering Ottawa's Hôpital Montfort ("Montfort") to substantially reduce its services. 

Montfort is the only hospital in Ontario in which the working language is French and where services in French are available on a full-time basis.  The Divisional Court found that the Commission's directions would reduce the availability in the Ottawa-Carleton region of health care services in French, jeopardize the training of French-speaking health care professionals, and cripple Montfort as an important francophone institution.  The directions were therefore quashed because they failed to consider the unwritten constitutional principle of respect for and protection of minorities.  The Divisional Court also held that the Commission's directions did not violate the equality rights of francophones under s. 15 of the Canadian Charter of Rights and Freedoms.

The Ontario Minister of Health ("Ontario") appealed from the decision of the Divisional Court.  Montfort cross-appealed from the Court's ruling on s. 15 of the Charter.

The Court of Appeal unanimously upheld the decision of the Divisional Court.  The Court ruled, first, that the Commission did not comply with s. 7 of the French Language Services Act because it failed to establish that it was "reasonable and necessary" to reduce health care services in French and also because it failed to take "all reasonable measures" to comply with the Act.   Second, in failing to give serious weight and consideration to the importance of Montfort to the survival of the Franco-Ontarian minority, the Commission failed to exercise its public interest mandate as required by the fundamental principles of the Constitution.  Third, the Court affirmed the Divisional Court's ruling that the directions of the Commission did not violate s. 15 of the Charter.  The Court therefore dismissed the appeals, affirmed the order quashing the Commission's directions, and ordered the Minister of Health to reconsider the matter. 

Background

Montfort was founded in 1953 by leaders of the Franco-Ontarian community under the direction of a religious order of nuns.  Unlike other hospitals in the Ottawa area that were either English or bilingual, Montfort was originally a homogeneous francophone hospital. Although today it also provides bilingual services in English, Montfort's medical services and training remain essentially francophone.  It is the only hospital in Ontario to provide a wide range of medical services and training in a francophone setting.     [Paragraph 4]

In 1996, the Health Services Restructuring Commission was established to restructure the delivery of health care services in Ontario.  The Public Hospitals Act, section 6, authorized the Commission to issue directions to public hospitals to: close, amalgamate operations with other hospitals, or change the level and nature of health services.  In issuing its directions, the Commission was required to consider what was "in the public interest" and to "[have] regard to district health council reports" for the communities concerned.      [Paragraphs 8 - 13]

The Commission released its first report in February 1997 and issued a notice of intention to close Montfort.  The decision was met with a storm of protest.  In particular, the District Health Council of Eastern Ontario and the Ottawa-Carleton Regional District Health Council stressed that the decision would imperil the training of health care professionals in French and make access to health services in French more difficult.   [Paragraphs 33 - 36]

In August 1997, the Commission released its final report and issued directions reversing its initial proposal to close Montfort.  Montfort's services, however, would be substantially reduced.  Montfort would go from receiving funding as a 196-bed general community hospital to receiving funding for 51 mental health beds and 15 low-risk obstetrical beds.  Montfort would no longer provide emergency, intensive care, and general surgery services associated with short-term hospital admission.  Montfort would also no longer offer short-term admission and treatment for a variety of ailments in family medicine or internal medicine.  Cardiology, Montfort's second largest program, would be transferred to the General campus of the amalgamated Ottawa Hospital.  Montfort would only offer "urgent care" (a form of walk-in clinic and some day surgery), low risk obstetrical beds, and psychiatric services.  [Paragraph 44]

Montfort in short would cease to function as a community hospital.  These directions were issued despite the recommendations of both the Ottawa-Carleton and Eastern Ontario Health Councils that Montfort should continue to operate as a community hospital to meet the needs of the francophone community.  [Paragraph 45]

In July 1998, the Commission further directed that 22 sub-acute beds be allocated to Montfort, for a total of 88 beds. [Paragraph 47]

On February 22, 1999, the Commission responded to submissions that it had not considered Montfort's larger institutional role as an agent for the preservation of the language and culture of Franco-Ontarians.  A letter was written by the Commission's president, Dr. Duncan Sinclair, to Ms. Michelle de Courville Nicol, the president of Montfort's board of directors.  The letter stated that "[d]ebate of this belief is not within the purview of the Health Services Restructuring Commission".  [Paragraph 49]

Montfort and the respondents then brought an application to the Divisional Court to set aside the directions of the Commission.  The Court granted the application and quashed the directions.  Ontario appealed and Montfort cross-appealed to the Court of Appeal.  [Paragraphs 50 - 56]

Decision of the Court of Appeal

The Court affirmed the decision of the Divisional Court, quashed the Commission's directions, and dismissed Ontario's appeal.  The Court also dismissed Montfort's cross-appeal. 

The Court considered the following issues [paragraph 57]:

1.  Did the Divisional Court err in its findings of fact?

2.  Does s. 16(3) of the Charter protect the status of Montfort as a francophone institution?

3.  Do the Commission's directions infringe s. 15 of the Charter?

4.  What is the relevance to Montfort of the unwritten constitutional principle of respect for and protection of minorities?

5.  Do the Commission's directions violate the French Language Services Act?

6.  Are the Commission's directions reviewable pursuant to the unwritten constitutional principle of respect for and protection of minorities?

The Court first ruled that the Divisional Court made no errors in its findings of fact.  The Court affirmed the Divisional Court's findings that the Commission's directions to Montfort would reduce the availability of health care services in French, jeopardize the training of French language health care professionals, and impair Montfort's broader role as an important linguistic, cultural, and educational institution that is vital to the minority francophone population of Ontario [paragraphs 58 - 76].

The Court next turned to the legal issues.  The Court ruled that neither s. 16(3) of the Charter (advancement of status or use of English and French) nor s. 15 (equality rights) applied to protect the status of Montfort as a francophone institution.  The Court was not persuaded that s. 16(3) provided constitutional protection for government measures taken to advance linguistic equality.   The Court held that section 16(3) was not a rights-conferring provision but rather a shield against government action that would otherwise contravene s. 15 or exceed legislative authority.  The Court also affirmed the Divisional Court's ruling that the Commission's directions did not violate s. 15 of the Charter, holding that s. 15 could not be invoked to supplement language rights that the Charter had not expressly conferred.  [Paragraphs 92 - 102]

The Court then considered the Constitution's unwritten principles as recognized by the Supreme Court of Canada in the 1998 Quebec Secession Reference decision.  These principles are federalism, democracy, constitutionalism and the rule of law, and respect for and protection of minorities.  The latter principle is a fundamental structural feature of the Canadian Constitution that both explains and transcends the minority rights that are specifically guaranteed in the constitutional text.  These rights include s. 16(1) of the Charter which proclaims English and French to be the official languages of Canada, and s. 23 of the Charter which guarantees the general right of primary or secondary school instruction in the language of the English or French linguistic minority population of a province.  The structural principle of respect for and protection of minorities is a bedrock principle of the Constitution that bears directly on the interpretation of the French Languages Services Act of Ontario and on the legality of the Commission's directions affecting Montfort.   [Paragraphs 103 to 125.]

The Court noted that the French Language Services Act builds on the language rights contained in Canada's Constitution to advance the equality of status or use of the French language as provided in s. 16(3) of the Charter.  One of the underlying purposes of the Act is to protect the minority francophone population in Ontario.  Other underlying purposes include the advancement of the French language and the promotion of its equality with English.  These purposes coincide with underlying unwritten principles of the Constitution of Canada.  Underlying constitutional principles may in certain circumstances give rise to substantive legal obligations because of their powerful normative force.  [Paragraphs 127 - 143]

The Court held that the Commission's directions to Montfort would reduce the availability and accessibility of healthcare services in French in the Ottawa-Carleton region and eastern Ontario.  The directions would also imperil the training of health care professionals in French.  This would in turn increase the assimilation of Franco-Ontarians.  Montfort's designation as a French language service provider included not only the healthcare services available in French at the time of designation but also whatever structures were necessary to ensure the delivery of those services, including the training of healthcare professionals in French.  To give the legislation any other interpretation is to prefer a narrow, literal, compartmentalized interpretation to one that recognizes and reflects the intent of the legislation.  Language and culture are not separate watertight compartments.  [Paragraphs 144 - 162]

The Court found that the serious adverse effects of the Commission's directions were inconsistent with the purpose and objectives of the French Language Services Act.  In designating Montfort under the Act, Ontario had indicated that the health care services at Montfort, offered at the time of designation, were intended to be permanently offered and readily accessible in French.  Ontario was therefore required under s. 7 of the Act to continue to provide those services in French subject to "such limits as are reasonable and necessary".  Ontario was also required show that "all reasonable measures" for compliance with the Act had been taken before limiting Montfort's services as a community hospital.  This does not simply mean directing another hospital to attain designation under the Act and then transferring some of the health care services offered at Montfort to that hospital before designation has been attained.  Nor does "all reasonable measures" mean creating a seemingly insurmountable problem for the training of healthcare professionals in French and essentially leaving the affected community to solve the problem itself.  [Paragraphs 163 - 165]

The Court therefore concluded that the Commission's directions did not comply with s. 7 of the French Languages Services Act.  The Court said that it was impossible to specify precisely what is encompassed by the words "reasonable and necessary", but at a minimum some justification or explanation was required for directions limiting the rights of francophones to benefit from Montfort as a community hospital.  [Paragraph 166]

The Court stated that although the Commission, now Ontario, may change and limit the services offered in French by Montfort, it cannot simply invoke administrative convenience and vague funding concerns as the reasons for so doing.  The Commission's mandate has to be reconciled with the statutory requirements of the French Language Services Act.  The Commission may not remove from Montfort medical services provided in French without showing that it is "reasonable and necessary" to do so, particularly when those services are not available in French on a full-time basis elsewhere in the Ottawa-Carleton region.  [Paragraph 168]

The Court also concluded that the Commission's directions failed to reflect the fundamental constitutional principle of respect for and protection of minorities.  The Commission was required by statute to exercise its powers in accordance with the public interest.  In determining the public interest, the Commission was required to have regard to this fundamental constitutional principle.  The Commission was also required to consider the views of regional health councils which made explicit recommendations based on Montfort's importance to the francophone community.  The Commission, however, regarded consideration of Montfort's larger institutional role as beyond its mandate.  [Paragraph 180]

The Court agreed with the Divisional Court that the language and culture of the francophone minority in Ontario "hold a special place in the Canadian fabric as one of the founding communities of Canada and as one of the two official language groups whose rights are entrenched in the Constitution".  If implemented, the Commission's directions would greatly impair Montfort's role as an important linguistic, cultural, and educational institution, vital to the minority francophone population of Ontario.  The directions are thus contrary to the fundamental constitutional principle of respect for and protection of minorities.  [Paragraph 181]

The Court noted that the Commission offered no justification for diminishing Montfort's important linguistic, cultural, and educational role in the Franco-Ontarian community.  The Commission failed to heed the relevant constitutional values.  It also failed to justify departure from those values on the grounds that it was necessary to do so to achieve some other important objective.  Although the Commission was entitled to deference, deference does not protect directions taken "in the public interest" that impinge without justification on fundamental Canadian constitutional values.  Because the Commission failed to give serious weight and consideration to the linguistic and cultural significance of Montfort to the survival of the Franco-Ontarian minority, contrary to the unwritten constitutional principle of respect for and protection of minorities,  the Commission's directions must be quashed.  [Paragraphs 184 - 187]

The Court therefore dismissed the appeals, affirmed the order quashing the Commission's directions, and ordered the Minister of Health to reconsider the matter.
