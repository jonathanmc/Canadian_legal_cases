On appeal from his conviction by Justice S. Glithero, sitting with a jury, on April 26, 1996

 

ROSENBERG J.A.:

[1]               On September 8, 1994, the appellant took his five and one-half month old stepdaughter to the hospital. The following day, the infant was pronounced dead and sometime later the appellant was charged with first degree murder. It was the theory of the Crown that the infant was the victim of Shaken Baby Syndrome. The appellant admitted shaking the infant, but he claimed that he did so in a fruitless attempt to revive her from an accidental drowning. The appellants intent was therefore a central issue in the case. To rebut the appellants defence and support the Crowns theory that the appellant had the requisite intent for murder, the Crown relied upon similar fact evidence in the form of expert evidence of a prior shaking incident. I have concluded that the trial judge did not adequately direct the jury concerning the use to be made of the evidence of this earlier incident. In view of the importance of this evidence, I would allow the appeal and order a new trial.

[2]               The appellant raised a number of other grounds of appeal, including incompetency of trial counsel, and sought to adduce fresh evidence from an expert in neo-natal care. Because of my conclusion on the similar fact evidence instruction, it is not necessary to deal with these grounds of appeal.

[3]               The charge of first degree murder was based upon the theory that the killing was committed in the course of a sexual assault. The basis for the sexual assault was a bruise found at autopsy around the infants anus. The appellant submits that the verdict of murder and in particular first degree murder is unreasonable. If the appellant is right, the new trial should be limited to manslaughter or second degree murder. I will therefore deal with this ground of appeal. In summary, I have concluded that it cannot be said that the verdict is unreasonable. Accordingly, I would order a new trial on the charge of first degree murder.

THE FACTS

[4]               Since I have concluded that there must be a new trial, I will limit my summary of the facts to what is necessary to appreciate the grounds of appeal that I intend to deal with. The appellant met Tamara Kincaid in October 1993 and they began to live together in November of that year. They lived with Tamaras sister, Laurie Kincaid, and her two young children. Tamara became pregnant in November before she began to live with the appellant. The appellant was not the father of the child. The child, Mariah, was born March 23, 1994. She was ten weeks premature and was kept in the hospital for several weeks after she was born.

[5]               The appellant had relatively little experience caring for infants except that he had seen his mother Bonnie Shepherd take care of his younger brothers. Mrs. Shepherd would usually bathe his younger brothers in the kitchen sink. The appellant took part in the care of Mariah. He would play with her, change her and feed her on some occasions. Mrs. Shepherd, Tamara and Laurie Kincaid testified that the appellant acted kindly and gently towards Mariah as well as towards Lauries children. He appeared to love Mariah and treated her as his daughter.

[6]               The morning of September 8, 1994, Tamara bathed Mariah in the bassinet, before the appellant returned from work. Sometime during the day Tamara and Laurie traded babies. This simply meant that Tamara took care of Lauries baby and Laurie took care of Mariah. The appellant arrived home around 5:30 p.m. and took a shower. Then, as was his habit, he played his guitar and sang songs to Mariah. Laurie left the home about 6:00 p.m. Between 6:30 and 7:00 the appellants parents picked up Tamara and they went out, leaving the appellant with Mariah. Tamara told the appellant that she would be back in an hour. The appellant seemed normal and relaxed. Mariah was fine.

[7]               After the appellants parents left with Tamara, the appellant sat on the couch with Mariah on his lap. It was hot and humid in the apartment and Mariah was dressed only in a diaper. The appellant was now alone with Mariah and he was the only witness to the events that followed. There was conflicting evidence about the appellants brothers during this period. The appellant testified that at some point, his brother telephoned and said that he was coming over. However, Mrs. Shepherd testified that she had instructed her sons not to visit the appellant while he was babysitting Mariah. This strange piece of evidence was not further explored at the trial.

[8]               The appellant testified that he decided to give Mariah a bath because she was sweating. He filled the kitchen sink with water, took off Mariahs diaper and placed her in a corner of the sink so that she was supported by the two sides. The water came up to Mariahs navel. At some point, the appellant went into the living room to turn down the stereo and get a cigarette. He turned down the stereo, lit a cigarette and retrieved Mariahs sleepers. On the way back to the kitchen, he put the cigarette in an ashtray. As he reached the entrance to the kitchen, he realized that he could not see Mariahs head. He ran to the sink and found Mariah lying with her face in the water. He picked her up and put her on his shoulder. She seemed limp and was making a gurgling sound. The appellant testified that he began to shake Mariah and she went limp. The appellant held Mariah around the chest as he was shaking her. He could not say how many times or for how long he shook her. When the shaking did not revive Mariah, the appellant began pushing on her chest. He then took her to the couch and began mouth-to-mouth resuscitation. Mariah brought up water and milk while he was doing so. After wiping the vomit from around Mariahs mouth, the appellant put on her diaper and called the hospital. He told the person that he needed help because his baby was not breathing and he needed an ambulance. The person on the telephone told him to give the baby C.P.R. The appellant continued to do so and then wrapped Mariah in a blanket and went out to the driveway to wait for the ambulance. After waiting some time, the appellant went inside and called his brother and told him he needed help because Mariah was not breathing. One of the appellants brothers testified that the appellant called at about 7:20 p.m. Two of the appellants brothers drove over, picked up the appellant and Mariah and drove to Norfolk Hospital. They arrived around 7:30 p.m.

[9]               The physician who spoke to the appellant on the telephone testified that she received the call about 7:15 p.m. The appellant told her his baby was not breathing. She told him to perform mouth-to-mouth and bring the baby in immediately. The appellant then hung up.

[10]          A nurse met the appellant at the door to the emergency room. The appellant was very distraught. He was crying and hyperventilating and said that the baby had drowned. When the baby was first examined she was wearing a dry diaper and neither her body nor hair were wet. She was not breathing, was cold to the touch and unresponsive to any painful stimuli. In the course of treating the child, a nurse inserted a rectal probe. Neither the nurse nor any of the other medical personnel who testified noticed anything unusual about the anal area.

[11]          Mariah was transferred to McMaster Hospital. The treating physician noticed the baby had bleeding in her retina and the outer surface of the eye. He suspected she was a victim of Shaken Baby Syndrome, not drowning. The baby was pronounced dead the following day.

[12]          Dr. Rao, the pathologist who conducted the autopsy of Mariah, concluded that she died from an episode of Shaken Baby Syndrome. The infant had bleeding on the surface of the brain, surrounding the optic nerve, and along the spinal canal all of which was consistent with Shaken Baby Syndrome. Dr. Rao was of the view that the incident that caused the injuries occurred from zero to four hours before the child became comatose. On the basis of the autopsy findings alone, Dr. Rao could not rule out drowning as a possible cause of death. In her opinion, however, the injuries were not consistent with the possibility that Mariah became unconscious because of near drowning and then was shaken in an attempt to resuscitate. The childs lungs were heavy and congested and contained fluid. It was Dr. Raos opinion that the fluid was produced as a result of the injury to the childs brain.

[13]          The Crown also called Dr. Huyer as an expert in Shaken Baby Syndrome. He agreed with Dr. Rao that the child died from Shaken Baby Syndrome. In court, he demonstrated the force necessary to cause the injuries observed in the child. He held his hands extended fully in front of him and moved them back and forth rapidly more than four times. Dr. Huyer testified that a layman would recognize problems with a small child during a shaking incident. In his testimony, the appellant agreed that the kind of shaking demonstrated by Dr. Huyer was severe and that he would have intervened if he had seen someone else treating Mariah in that fashion. However, he also stated that in the state he was in when he found Mariah, it was possible he had shaken her in that way.

[14]          Dr. Huyer testified that the childs injuries were not typical of those found in attempts to resuscitate following a near drowning. Dr. Huyer was of the view that Mariah would not have been able to sit upright and support herself without some type of assistance. Tamara, however, testified that Mariah could sit upright when propped up by the couch.

[15]          As indicated, none of the treating medical staff had noticed anything unusual about the area around the anus. At the autopsy, however, Dr. Rao found a one centimeter area of bruising around the anus. In her opinion, the bruising was caused by rubbing action from a blunt object such as a finger or penis. While she could not say if there had been penetration by the object, there were no tears or fissures on the lining of the inner anal canal, indicating an absence of penetration. She estimated that the trauma that led to the bruising could have occurred up to six hours prior to Mariah becoming comatose. However, since the comatose state would retard the healing process, Dr. Rao thought it likely that the trauma occurred closer to the six-hour mark. Neither the rectal probe used at the hospital nor the plug in the kitchen sink could have caused the bruising observed at the autopsy. Dr. Huyer believed that the infliction of the injury that led to this bruise would have caused pain to Mariah and that she likely would have reacted by crying out and trying to pull away. The appellant testified that he had no explanation for the bruise. He denied that he did anything that could have caused it.

The similar fact evidence

[16]          Dr. Rao testified that she found evidence of a previous injury to the brain that may have been caused by shaking the infant. This injury occurred three to ten days prior to the death. She testified that the injury from the prior shaking was not that great. Dr. Huyer was of the view that the injury was at least seven days old and more likely fourteen days prior to the death. He testified that the old injury resulted from extreme force that would be recognized as potentially injurious to the child. There was also an area of old bruising on Mariahs face behind her left ear.

[17]          On September 4, 1994 (four days before the fatal incident) the appellants mother was babysitting Mariah. She took Mariah to an after-hours clinic because the infant was vomiting and had a slight fever. The physician did not detect a fever and found no signs of vomiting. He diagnosed the infant as having a viral bowel infection. On September 6th, the infant was taken to the family physician for a follow-up visit. The physician found nothing abnormal.

[18]          Dr. Huyer testified that the symptoms displayed by Mariah on September 4th were consistent with a previous episode of shaking. The milder symptoms of baby shaking are irritability, poor feeding, some vomiting, and some increased lethargy. Such symptoms are not uncommon in infants and physicians will diagnose the baby as having feeding problems, stomach flu or a viral infection. Dr. Huyer also testified that there would likely be some abnormality in the child soon after the shaking in that the child would likely not be quite as alert. However, that is sometimes hard to judge in an infant. Dr. Huyer agreed that one would have to be fairly intimate with the childs behaviour to appreciate the change in these milder symptoms.

[19]          The evidence as to who had care of Mariah on September 4th, before Mrs. Shepherd began babysitting, was unclear. It would seem that Tamara, Laurie Kincaid, and the appellant would have had contact with the child. It is also unclear where Mrs. Shepherd was babysitting Mariah on September 4th. If she was at her own house, her husband and her three other sons could have had contact with the child. Mrs. Shepherd and Laurie Kincaid denied that they shook the baby.

[20]          If the prior shaking did not occur on September 4th, a real possibility in view of the expert evidence as to the timing of the injury, the number of people who had contact with the child is probably greater. There was little evidence in this regard. My reading of the evidence is that the persons most likely to have been left in charge of the child are Tamara, the appellant, the appellants mother and Laurie Kincaid. This would not exclude the possibility, however, of some brief interaction between the infant and other members of the two families.

ANALYSIS

Unreasonable verdict

[21]          The appellant makes two submissions concerning the reasonableness of the verdict. He submits that there was insufficient evidence of the requisite intent for murder and no evidence that the killing was committed in the course of a sexual assault so as to constitute first degree murder. The trial judge dismissed an application by the defence for a directed verdict of acquittal of first degree murder.

[22]          In my view, the verdict of first degree murder was not unreasonable. In R. v. Biniaris (2000), 143 C.C.C. (3d) 1 (S.C.C.) at 24, Arbour J. set out the process this court must undertake in determining whether the verdict of guilty is unreasonable:

It requires not merely asking whether twelve properly instructed jurors, acting judicially, could reasonably have come to the same result, but doing so through the lens of judicial experience which serves as an additional protection against an unwarranted conviction.

[23]          The cause of death was not in doubt, nor was there any doubt as to the appellants responsibility for the death. He admitted shaking the baby. The jury could find on the basis of the expert evidence that the appellant had shaken the baby so hard that any reasonable person would recognize the risk of serious injury and intervene. The appellants only defence was that he was in such a state of confusion and upset that he failed to recognize the consequences of his actions. Assuming that the jury rejected the appellants explanation, they could conclude that he had the intent under s. 229(a)(ii), namely that he meant to cause the infant bodily harm that he knew was likely to cause her death, and was reckless whether death ensued or not.

[24]          It was open to the jury to reject the appellants story that the shaking was done when he was in a state of panic and confusion provoked by the near drowning. There was not very much support for the appellants story of a near drowning. The story itself was somewhat improbable. Although the appellant had little experience with infants, a jury could reasonably take the view that he would know better than to leave a very young infant alone in a sink of water. If the jury rejected the appellants evidence in this regard, they were left with a severe shaking done either out of anger or, perhaps, to try to quiet the child who was screaming in pain from the infliction of the injury to the anus. In either case, it would be open to a jury acting reasonably to conclude that the appellant had the requisite intent for murder under s. 229(a)(ii) of the Criminal Code.

[25]          I have also concluded that it was open to the jury to find that the killing was committed in the course of a sexual assault. There was really no innocent explanation for the very serious injury to the anal opening. The possibilities of an accidental injury from the plug in the sink or insertion of the rectal probe at the hospital were virtually eliminated. This left deliberate application of blunt force to the childs anus. On the record at trial, the only serious question was whether the appellant was the author of that injury. Admittedly, the expert evidence concerning the timing of the injury, standing alone, tended to favour the appellant not being the perpetrator. However, that was not the only evidence. Mrs. Shepherd and Laura Kincaid denied inflicting the injury. Tamara was not asked directly whether she inflicted the injury but it is a fair inference from her evidence that she did not. Further, the appellant and the other witnesses testified that the child was fine and not in any distress when Tamara left. Accordingly, I would not give effect to this ground of appeal.

Evidence of the Earlier Injury

[26]          The appellant submits that the evidence of the prior shaking should not have been admitted and, in the alternative, that the trial judge did not adequately direct the jury as to the use to be made of that evidence. No objection was taken to the admission of this evidence and thus the trial judge was not called upon to consider its admissibility and in particular not required to weigh the probative value of the evidence against its prejudicial effect.

[27]          The appellants principal submission in this court is that the evidence was inadmissible because the Crown failed to link that evidence to the appellant. Crown counsel counters that the appellants admission that he shook the infant at the time of the fatal injury supplies the necessary nexus. She submits that it is highly unlikely that there would be two people in the same household both subjecting the child to the same kind of trauma over a relatively short period of time.

[28]          In R. v. Millar (1989), 49 C.C.C. (3d) 193 (Ont. C.A.), Morden J.A. dealt with the admissibility of this kind of evidence in a child abuse case. Millar was charged with manslaughter as the result of the death of his nine-week-old son. The cause of death, as here, was injuries from shaking the baby. As well, as in this case, the accused admitted that he had shaken the baby and the only issue was the accuseds state of mind. The accused claimed that the child had stopped breathing and he had shaken him in an attempt to revive him. The accused was convicted and on appeal argued that the trial judge had erred in admitting evidence from the post-mortem examination that showed the child had three sets of fractured ribs, a fractured tibia, and a fractured ulna. The accused argued that this evidence was inadmissible because the Crown had not proved that the injuries were inflicted by the accused. The Crown argued that since the accused had admitted shaking the baby on the occasion leading to his death, the jury could infer that he had inflicted the earlier injuries. At pp. 214-15, Morden J.A. dealt with the circular reasoning and held that it could not afford the basis of a tenable argument. I would apply his analysis to this case. However, that does not mean that the evidence should be excluded.

[29]          In Millar, as in this case, the Crown could not prove that the accused had the exclusive opportunity to inflict the earlier injuries, since for most of his short life the infant was in the care of his mother, the accuseds wife. She testified at the trial, and it was a fair inference from her evidence, that she did not inflict the earlier injuries. Morden J.A. held that, if the jury accepted her evidence and rejected the accuseds evidence, there was an evidentiary basis that could reasonably support a finding that the accused caused the earlier injuries.

[30]          While the situation in this case is somewhat more complex because of the larger number of caregivers, I would reach a similar conclusion. If the jury accepted the evidence of Tamara, Mrs. Shepherd and Laura Kincaid and rejected the appellants denials then there was an evidentiary basis for admission of the evidence. The Crown did not have to prove beyond a reasonable doubt that the appellant inflicted the earlier injury. The test for admission was set out in R. v. Sweitzer, [1982] 1 S.C.R. 949 at p. 954:

Before evidence may be admitted as evidence of similar facts, there must be a link between the allegedly similar facts and the accused. In other words there must be some evidence upon which the trier of fact can make a proper finding that the similar facts to be relied upon were in fact the acts of the accused for it is clear that if they were not his own but those of another they have no relevance to the matters at issue under the indictment. [Emphasis added.]

[31]          That standard was most recently approved by the Supreme Court of Canada in R. v. Arp (1998), 129 C.C.C. (3d) 321 at 346-47. In my view, the Crown led sufficient evidence to establish this nexus.

[32]          The real questions are what inferences are legitimately open from this evidence, whether the probative value of the evidence outweighed its prejudicial effect, and whether the jury was properly instructed on the use to be made of that evidence. It would seem that the evidence was potentially admissible at the instance of the Crown for three purposes. First, since it was conceded that the appellant had put his character in issue, the evidence was admissible to rebut the evidence of good character and thus to some extent was also relevant to credibility. Second, the evidence was admissible to show an animus or hostility to the child and thus a motive. Third, the evidence could be used to rebut his innocent explanation for shaking the infant. As I understand it, it was this third purpose upon which the Crown placed most of the case for admission of the evidence.

[33]          The trial judge referred to this evidence in his charge to the jury on three occasions as follows:

(1) I want to speak to you for a moment, ladies and gentlemen, about the matter of prior injuries. You may recall, if you remember the evidence as I do, but again it is for you to say not me, but I understood the evidence of Doctor Rao the Pathologist to indicate that during the post-mortem examination, she found hemorrhaging of the brain and of the retina which, in her opinion, consisted of both fresh or recent injury and some which was not fresh injury. I understood her to give the opinion that the old hemorrhaging was likely 7 to 10 days old.

If you accept that evidence as given by Doctor Rao, I want you to clearly understand the limited use you can make of it. Mr. Shepherd is not charged with any past misconduct towards Mariah, but only what is alleged to have occurred on September 8th, 1994. Before you can use the evidence of past injuries at all, you must be satisfied that those injuries were inflicted by Mr. Shepherd and that they were intentionally inflicted rather than being caused accidentally. If you are so satisfied, then you may only consider the evidence of prior injuries on the issue of the intention of Mr. Shepherd, and the issue of his knowledge of what he was doing, his state of mind. The issue of whether he was aware of the risk of injury resulting from his actions.

What I want you to perfectly understand is that you are not, not permitted to conclude that as a result of such evidence, Mr. Shepherd is the type of person who is more likely to have committed the offence charged or any included offence, and to find him Guilty on that basis. If you are satisfied that there were old injuries, and that Mr. Shepherd caused them, and that he did so intentionally, then such evidence may be considered by you as being relevant to his state of mind, his intention, his knowledge of the risk inherent in shaking the baby. But you are not permitted to conclude that because he injured her before, if indeed thats your view of the evidence, that he is therefore criminally responsible for the death on the date in question.

Bonnie Shepherd and Laurie Kincaid frequently had contact with Mariah. Both were asked and both denied ever shaking her. I dont believe Tammy Kincaid to have been expressly asked that question, but I think it was obviously not asked because of her evident distress while she was testifying. Mr. Shepherd has denied shaking Mariah on any previous occasion.

I repeat, you must not consider this evidence of prior injury unless you are satisfied the previous injuries were caused by Mr. Shepherd, and that they were intentionally caused. If you are not so satisfied, then the evidence should be disregarded. If you are so satisfied, its only possible relevance would be in relation to the accuseds state of mind to his state of knowledge to his intention on the date in question.

(2) [Dr. Huyer] was asked about the old hemorrhages detected in a cat scan, and offered the opinion that old in this context means 7 to 10 days prior. That such old injuries could not be from birth because they would have been detected or picked up in the ultrasound tests which had been done according to the records as he reviewed them.

In his opinion, the old bruising observed in the optic nerve sheath means that there was a previous shaking incident. He offered the opinion that Mariah may have just had the milder symptoms from the earlier shaking; that is, would appear tired, irritable, vomit. He expressed the view that a doctor or a layman may not observe these milder symptoms because they are not unusual for a youngster, in any event, and are consistent with a lot of other conditions. They are fairly normal. Mild symptoms are subtle and hard to see. For example, if a baby is more irritable it could be from all sorts of things so its hard to ascribe the cause of the irritability to any one particular matter. [Emphasis added.]

(3) You know Doctor Rao gave you her opinion with respect to the ageing of the injuries, that is the old injuries that she felt were not fresh, and that she indicated that, in her opinion, they were injuries caused some 7 to 10 days previously. It is right and proper that I remind you that Doctor Huyer also spoke to this issue and gave you the opinion that the old injuries were, in his view, likely 7 to 14 days old and probably towards the 14 days or even in excess of that.

[34]          In these instructions, the trial judge tracked some of the language used by Morden J.A. in R. v. Millar, supra at pp. 226-27:

In order for the jury to perform their duty as the sole finders of the facts I think that, at a minimum, instructions to the following effect should have been given.

As I have said, the jury would first be required to determine how and, if relevant, by whom, the other injuries were caused. The opinion evidence would be of assistance in determining whether they were accidentally or intentionally caused.

If the jury determined that the injuries were intentionally inflicted then they would have to determine who inflicted them. The opinion evidence would not be relevant to this issue and would be of no assistance to the jury with respect to it.

Only if the jury found that the appellant had intentionally inflicted the other injuries could they be considered evidence in the case against him. If the jury did not make the finding that the appellant had intentionally inflicted the injuries, then the evidence of them should be disregarded.

If the jury found that the appellant had intentionally inflicted the other injuries then they could take this finding into account, together with all of the other evidence, including the expert evidence, on the issue of his state of mind at the time he shook the baby on August 1st: generally, on whether his intention was an innocent or guilty one; more specifically, on whether the consequence of the shaking, the injuries, was accidental or intended. If the other injuries were intentionally inflicted then this evidence could be considered on the question whether or not those of August 1st were intentionally inflicted.

It should be emphasized that the evidence of the other injuries was before the jury solely on the issue of the accused's state of mind at the time he shook the baby; it could not be used to support a general inference that the appellant was the sort of person likely to have committed the crime in question: Makin v. A.-G.N.S.W., [1894] A.C. 57 at p. 65 (P.C.). [Emphasis added.]

[35]          There is some similarity between the evidence in this case and the evidence in Millar. In both cases, the child died from excessive shaking and in both cases, there was evidence of prior abuse. However, there was an important distinction. The prior injuries in Millar were multiple fractures. It is apparent that this evidence was primarily admitted to show that the accused intentionally inflicted the fatal injury just as he had intentionally fractured the childs bones on prior occasions.

[36]          The prior injury in this case was different; it was evidence of a prior shaking. While that evidence might have been admissible in favour of the Crown in the same sense as in Millar, to rebut the appellants innocent explanation, it seems to me that the evidence was also of considerable importance in understanding the appellants state of mind under s. 229(a)(ii). That is, the evidence was important in determining whether the appellant intended to cause bodily harm and, if so, whether the appellant knew that it was likely to cause death, and was reckless whether death ensued or not.

[37]          In the first reference to this evidence, the trial judge indicated to the jury that if the earlier injuries were intentionally inflicted by the appellant, that evidence could be considered on the issue of the intention of Mr. Shepherd, and the issue of his knowledge of what he was doing, his state of mind. The issue of whether he was aware of the risk of injury resulting from his actions. In the second excerpt, which was later in the charge, the trial judge reminded the jury of Dr. Huyers evidence. I am not satisfied, however, that the trial judge adequately related the evidence to the legal issue of the intent under s. 229(a)(ii) of the Criminal Code. Had he done so, it would have been apparent that the evidence failed to support the Crowns position and to the contrary was evidence in the appellants favour. It will be recalled that it was Dr. Huyers evidence that a severe shaking would have been required to cause the old injury, yet no one, including two physicians, noticed anything untoward about the child, except possibly flu-like symptoms. Moreover, the evidence was relevant on this issue, even if the appellant had not shaken the baby and whether or not the prior shaking was intentional, provided he was aware of the incident and the aftermath.

[38]          In R. v. Cooper (1993), 78 C.C.C. (3d) 289 (S.C.C.) at 301, Cory J. explained the duty on the trial judge in the following terms:

It has been said before but it bears repeating that it would be difficult if not impossible to find a perfect charge. Directions to the jury need not, as a general rule, be endlessly dissected and subjected to minute scrutiny and criticism. Rather the charge must be read as a whole. The directions to the jury must, of course, set out the position of the Crown and defence, the legal issues involved and the evidence that may be applied in resolving the legal issues and ultimately in determining the guilt or innocence of the accused. At the end of the day, the question must be whether an appellate court is satisfied that the jurors would adequately understand the issues involved, the law relating to the charge the accused is facing, and the evidence they should consider in resolving the issues. [Emphasis added.]

[39]          The evidence of the earlier shaking was important. If not properly related to the issues in the case and presented solely to rebut the innocent explanation, it could be highly prejudicial to the appellant. If placed in its proper context and related to the legal issue of the appellants intent to cause bodily harm and his knowledge and foresight, it could be of great assistance in resolving the case in favour of the appellant on the issue of intent under s. 229(a)(ii) of the Criminal Code. If the appellant had shaken the baby on the prior occasion, or simply witnessed it, and the child suffered no ill effect, or only the subtle effects noticed by the physicians, the evidence of the prior shaking did not support the Crown theory that the appellant intended to cause bodily harm or knew that the fatal shaking incident was likely to cause death, and was reckless whether death ensued or not. I cannot avoid the conclusion that the overall impression left with the jury was that this evidence could be useful to the prosecution in proving the intent for murder when it could very well have had the opposite effect.

[40]          Admittedly, the charge to the jury was unduly favourable to the appellant in some respects. For example, the trial judge did not direct the jury that the similar fact evidence could be used to rebut the evidence of good character. Nevertheless, having regard to the importance of this evidence and the significant prejudicial impact on the appellant's defence if it was not properly related to the elements of murder under s. 229(a)(ii), I cannot be satisfied that the jury was adequately directed. This is not a case for application of the proviso in s. 686(1)(b)(iii) of the Criminal Code. I am not satisfied that had the jury been properly directed the verdict would necessarily have been the same. The evidence bore directly on the central issue of the appellants state of mind and if properly related to the issues in the case could have raised a reasonable doubt whether the appellant had the requisite intent for murder.

[41]          As I have indicated, since no objection to this evidence was taken at trial, the trial judge was not called upon to weigh the probative value of the evidence. Since I have concluded that there must be a new trial, it is not necessary to resolve the question of admission of the evidence. That will be for the trial judge at the new trial should the Crown seek to lead this evidence.

DISPOSITION

[42]          Accordingly, I would allow the appeal, set aside the conviction and order a new trial on a charge of first degree murder.
