Heard:  May 14, 2002

On appeal from the order of Justice Mary Lou Benotto dated May 22, 2001.

ROSENBERG J.A.:

[1]               This is one of two appeals heard by this court arising out of an alleged relationship between the appellants’ mother and the respondent.  On motions under Rule 21, Benotto J. struck out the mother’s claim and most of the claims of these appellants seeking damages from the respondent for, in effect, failing to provide adequate support.  I agree with the result reached by the motions judge and would dismiss this appeal.  I have reached a similar conclusion in respect of the mother’s claim in separate reasons being released with these reasons.

THE FACTS

[2]               As this was a motion under Rule 21, the facts contained in the statement of claim are assumed to be true.  Those facts may be summarized as follows.  The appellants Kim and Todd Louie were born in 1958 and 1962 and are now 43 and 40 years of age respectively.  They are both employed and obviously are no longer children.  They say that their mother, Grace Louie, entered into a clandestine relationship with the respondent, Lastman, in 1957 and that he is their biological father.  The respondent’s relationship with the appellants’ mother ended in 1971.  During the relationship, the appellants knew the respondent only as a friend of their mother.  From time to time the respondent took the appellants on various outings and purchased small gifts for them.

[3]               Both Mrs. Louie and the respondent were married to other persons.  Mrs. Louie has two other children.  The appellants lived with Mr. and Mrs. Louie and their two children as part of the Louie family.  Mrs. Louie and her husband separated in 1969.  After the separation, the appellants and their mother were dependent on social assistance and small monthly payments from the respondent.  Those payments were always inadequate for their basic needs.  The monthly payments ceased in 1974 when Mrs. Louie and the respondent reached an agreement.  The respondent paid Mrs. Louie a lump sum of $25,000 and $2,500 for legal fees.  Mrs. Louie executed a release, the terms of which are more fully discussed in the companion reasons for judgment, and withdrew any allegation or suggestion that the respondent was the father of the appellants.

[4]               The respondent is the mayor of Toronto.  The appellants say that Kim Louie suspected that the respondent might be his father when he saw him on television and was struck by the resemblance in appearance and mannerisms between the respondent and himself.  Kim Louie confronted his mother and she admitted that the respondent is the father of both appellants.

[5]               The appellants allege that, as their father, the respondent had a fiduciary obligation to make adequate provision for their support to the extent of his ability to do so.  There is no question of his financial ability because the respondent was a successful merchant before becoming mayor.  In their claim, the appellants compare their impoverished lifestyle with the lavish lifestyle of the respondent and his family.  They say that they did not have adequate housing, food, clothing, dental care and schooling.  They left school early to seek full-time employment because of the necessity to assist in providing for their own support.

REASONS OF THE MOTIONS JUDGE

[6]               The appellants claim compensation for breach of fiduciary duty and unjust enrichment and damages in tort for intentional or negligent infliction of mental suffering and intentional or negligent infliction of impoverishment.  The motions judge found that it was plain and obvious that none of those claims could succeed.  The appellants appeal from her findings only as they relate to breach of fiduciary duty.  I will therefore focus my summary of the motions judge’s reasons on that part of the claim.

[7]               The motions judge found that, on the facts as pleaded, there was no fiduciary relationship between the appellants and the respondent.  The respondent never acted in a position of trust or control over the appellants’ lives.  The appellants were part of another family unit and, within that family unit, it was their mother who was in charge of their lives during their dependency.  Even if there was a fiduciary relationship, the motions judge found that the facts pleaded did not show that the respondent breached any duty to them.  The appellants claimed that the respondent had a duty to reveal that he was their father and to provide support for them.  As to the former, the motions judge held that unless clearly authorized by statute or motivated by the parens patriae jurisdiction, the courts cannot be called upon to interfere with parents’ decision making regarding their children.  There was no basis for the court to interfere in this case with the mother’s decision to keep secret the identity of the appellants’ father.  As to the obligation to pay support, the motions judge pointed out that the respondent paid a lump sum of support and thus the case was really about the adequacy of the child support agreement.  It was not open to the courts to review the adequacy of the child support agreement after the appellants had attained their majority.  Such a review would be inconsistent with the statutory scheme in place for determination of child support and for resolution of family law disputes generally.  The motions judge relied upon Frame v. Smith, [1987] 2 S.C.R. 99.

[8]               As to the appellants’ claim for unjust enrichment, this was first based upon breach of a fiduciary duty.  Since the appellants had not made out any such breach, it was plain and obvious that the claim for unjust enrichment could not succeed.  The motions judge also analyzed the claim for unjust enrichment as an independent cause of action.  She held that it was plain and obvious that it could not succeed because the alleged enrichment of the respondent was not based on any contribution by the appellants.  In any event, the element of reasonable expectation was not made out.  The motions judge summarized her holding as follows:

Any analysis of unjust enrichment also involves the reasonable expectation of the parties.  Where one person is prejudiced in the reasonable expectation of receiving compensation and the recipient “freely accepts” the benefits conferred in circumstances where he knows or ought to know of that reasonable expectation, it will be unjust to allow the recipient to retain the benefit.  The facts pleaded do not support the reasonable expectation or free acceptance requirements.

[9]               In the result, the motions judge dismissed the fiduciary duty and unjust enrichment claims, as well as the various tort claims.  The respondent had not sought to strike out the claim for a declaration of paternity.  The motions judge held that the appellants could amend the claim to plead the facts necessary to that claim.

THE POSITIONS OF THE PARTIES

[10]          The appellants submit that the motions judge erred in finding that it was plain and obvious that there was no fiduciary relationship.  Relying upon cases such as Lac Minerals Ltd. v. International Corona Resources Ltd., [1989] 2 S.C.R. 574 and M.(K) v. M. (H.), [1992] 3 S.C.R. 6, they submit that the parent-child relationship is one of the traditional relationships in which the characteristics or criteria for a fiduciary relationship are presumed to exist. It is not plain and obvious that there was no fiduciary relationship in this case.  That determination can only be made after a trial.  Moreover, there were any number of ways that the respondent could have fulfilled his obligation to his children without interfering with the mother’s decision to conceal the appellants’ true paternity.  For example, the respondent could have set up a trust for the children.  The appellants do not submit that the respondent had a duty to declare he was their father against the wishes of their mother.

[11]          In a broad and wide-ranging submission, the intervener submits that biological parents always have a fiduciary relationship with their children and that children have a right to know the identity of their biological parents.  Accordingly, biological parents have a fiduciary obligation to disclose their identity, irrespective of the wishes of the custodial parent.  That submission runs directly counter to the position of the appellants and I do not intend to make any further reference to the submissions of the intervener.

[12]          The respondent submits that the appellants’ claim is nothing more than a claim for retroactive child support.  The Ontario legislature has established a complete code dealing with child support.  As adults, the appellants have no claim for child support.  In any event, the respondent submits that he owed no fiduciary obligations to the appellants.  He never stood in a relationship of father to the appellants; the mother’s husband filled that role.  The mother made the decision to raise the appellants as part of the Louie family and it would violate and dishonour the decisions she made for the courts now to intervene.

ANALYSIS

[13]          No matter how the appellants attempt to frame their action, in the end it is nothing more than a claim for retroactive child support and, as such, it cannot succeed.  I would not foreclose the possibility of a fiduciary claim for child support outside the legislative scheme where the child is in need and there is a gap in the legislative scheme.  That, however, is not this case.  The appellants concede that had their mother made a claim for child support under the legislation in force while they were children, the court could have made an order for support.  It is not open to the appellants to come forward and make a support claim decades after they are no longer dependent.  In my view, the decision of the Supreme Court of Canada in Frame v. Smith is determinative against the appellants.

[14]          The plaintiff in Frame v. Smith was the father of three children.  After he and his wife separated, a court awarded custody of the children to the wife with generous access to the plaintiff.  Shortly after the separation, the wife began to live with the co-defendant and according to the pleadings they embarked on a course of conduct designed to frustrate the plaintiff’s access to the children.  The plaintiff obtained a number of court orders in various Canadian courts and spent considerable sums of money in an attempt to gain access to his children.  He was unsuccessful and claimed that the defendants caused him severe emotional and psychological distress.  He claimed damages for wrongful interference with the legal relationship he had with his children and sought recovery of his out-of-pocket expenses in addition to general and punitive damages.  The defendants moved to strike out the statement of claim on the ground that it disclosed no reasonable cause of action.  They were successful before the motions judge, in this court and in the Supreme Court of Canada.  Speaking for the majority of the Supreme Court, La Forest J. held that the plaintiff’s claim could not succeed either as a tort claim or as a claim for breach of fiduciary obligation.

[15]          The core of La Forest J.’s reasoning was that the comprehensive scheme for custody and access at both the federal and provincial levels left no room for judicial initiative.  His reasoning is summarized at p. 112 in the following passage:

It seems obvious to me that the Legislature intended to devise a comprehensive scheme for dealing with these issues.  If it had contemplated additional support by civil action, it would have made provision for this, especially given the rudimentary state of the common law.  Indeed, as we saw, the Legislature in a separate statute (the Family Law Reform Act) went out of its way to abolish all the relevant, if inadequate, remedies then existing at common law.

[16]          Then, in words that have direct application to this case, La Forest J. said at p. 113:

More generally, what the present action appears to contemplate is the enforcement of a statutory duty, or what amounts to the same thing, an order made by virtue of a statutory discretion, by means of a civil action rather than by means of the remedies provided by the Act.

[17]          The appellants in this case were born in 1958 and 1962.  They attained the age of 18 years in 1976 and 1980.  Throughout this period, provincial legislation imposed obligations on parents to maintain their children and provided mechanisms for the appellants’ mother to apply to a court to require the respondent to pay child support.  Those obligations were set out, for example, in the Children’s Maintenance Act, R.S.O. 1950, c. 52, the Deserted Wives’ and Children’s Maintenance Act, R.S.O. 1950, c. 102 and the Child Welfare Act, R.S.O. 1960, c. 53.  Towards the end of the period, the Family Law Reform Act, 1978, S.O. 1978, c. 2 was in force.  The common feature of all of these statutes is that they impose obligations upon parents, including the putative father, to provide for their children and, if necessary, to pay for the support or maintenance of their children.  As counsel for the appellants conceded in argument, had Mrs. Louie applied to a court while the appellants were children, she could have obtained an order requiring the respondent to make payments for the maintenance or support of the appellants.  None of these statutes contemplates claims like those made here for support of persons who no longer come within the definition of children and who are no longer in a position of dependency. 

[18]          This court has not categorically rejected all claims for retroactive child support.  In Brett v. Brett (1999), 44 O.R. (3d) 61, the mother brought an application in 1980 under the Family Law Reform Act, 1978 for various orders including child support.  At the time, the children were all under the age of 18 years.  The issue of interim child support was adjourned sine die.  By the time of the trial, all but one of the children were no longer dependants and that child was living with the father.  The mother calculated that she had spent $760,000 supporting the children during their dependency and sought retroactive child support of $500,000.  The trial judge rejected the claim in reasons reported at (1996), 24 R.F.L. (4th) 224 (Ont. Gen. Div.).  On appeal, Osborne J.A., speaking for this court, held that claims for retroactive child support must be considered case by case.  A successful claim, however, must bear some relation to the needs of the child and to his or her support.  Osborne J.A. stated, at p. 67:

The purpose of child support is to assist the custodial parent in meeting the day-to-day expenses of raising children.  It is often said that children should have the right to support by the non-custodial parent.  However, where the custodial parent with full knowledge of that right chooses not to pursue a claim for interim child support, particularly where the custodial parent and the children do not need child support in the pre-trial period, it is open to a trial judge to reject the custodial parent’s claim for retroactive child support … There is no room for a judge-made rule, for example, that after a particular number of years a custodial parent’s claim for child support would be extinguished.  This would not be sound policy.  Claims for retroactive child support should be considered on a case-by-case basis.

There is no evidence that any of the children suffered from a lack of financial support during the period of their respective dependencies.  Throughout that period Mrs. Brett was financially able to provide ample support for the children.  If a retroactive child support order were to be made now, as a purely practical matter, the support paid would have nothing to do with the needs of the children, or their support.

[19]          Admittedly, this case is unlike Brett insofar as these appellants claim that their mother was not able to provide ample support for them.  Nevertheless, any order that could now be made would have nothing to do with the appellants’ needs as children.  They ceased being children, and in positions where they were potentially dependent upon the respondent, over twenty years ago.  Any such order would be inconsistent with the purpose of child support, which is to assist the custodial parent in meeting the day-to-day expenses of raising children.

[20]          If the legislative scheme is no longer available to the appellants [1] because they long ago ceased being children, I do not think that they can assert a right to some equitable remedy to obtain, in effect, what might have been obtained had the legal remedies been pursued in a timely fashion.  La Forest J. dealt with that issue in Frame v. Smith at p. 114-15:

The Legislature created the rights of custody and access and, as we saw, provided a whole array of remedies for enforcing them, from directions for supervising access, to restraining orders against interference, to apprehending the child, if necessary by permitting entries into premises and searches by the police or the sheriff, to fines and imprisonment.  Why the legislature should be thought to have intended enforcement by an action for breach of a fiduciary obligation when there is a failure to comply with an access order, when an intention to permit a tortious action will not be implied, I fail to understand.  All the more so when the Legislature has taken pains to abolish all non-statutory actions that had any obvious relevance to the matter.  Indeed there are in my view stronger reasons to doubt that the Legislature would have contemplated recourse to this action.  It is extremely ill-defined and it would scarcely be one that would immediately leap to mind.

[21]          In this respect, I agree with the motions judge when she said the following:

Parents have an obligation to support their dependent children.  There are serious consequences for those who default.  They can lose their drivers’ license.  They can lose their passport.  They can go to jail.  The stringent enforcement provisions reflect the moral outrage of a society that labels them “deadbeats”.  But the laws are meant to provide for children.  They are meant to ensure that children receive support for their day-to-day needs while they are dependent.  Child support legislation was not intended to operate as a weapon in the hands of grown-ups who sue their parents for perceived deficiencies in their upbringing.  That is why the Supreme Court of Canada [in Frame v. Smith] refused to allow persons to sue for damages for breach of a family law statutory obligation.  The statutory scheme provides the entire remedy.  If the legislature had intended to extend the remedy to damages, it would have said so.

[22]          In view of that conclusion, it is not strictly necessary to deal with the appellants’ submission that the respondent owed them a fiduciary obligation that he breached by failing to provide adequate support.  I therefore will make only a few further comments. 

[23]          In most circumstances, the relationship between parent and child is fiduciary in nature.  The Supreme Court of Canada made this clear in M. (K.) v. M. (H.), at pp. 61-62:

It is intuitively apparent that the relationship between parent and child is fiduciary in nature, and that the sexual assault of one’s child is a grievous breach of the obligations arising from that relationship. Indeed, I can think of few cases that are clearer than this. For obvious reasons society has imposed upon parents the obligation to care for, protect and rear their children. The act of incest is a heinous violation of that obligation. Equity has imposed fiduciary obligations on parents in contexts other than incest, and I see no barrier to the extension of a father’s fiduciary obligation to include a duty to refrain from incestuous assaults on his daughter [emphasis added].

[24]          La Forest J. then referred to Guerin v. The Queen, [1984] 2 S.C.R. 335 where, at p. 384, Dickson J. described a fiduciary relationship as one “where by statute, agreement, or perhaps by unilateral undertaking, one party has an obligation to act for the benefit of another, and that obligation carries with it a discretionary power”.  In M.(K) v. M.(H.), La Forest J. held at p. 63 that in some circumstances a fiduciary obligation may be imposed even in the absence of any unilateral undertaking by the fiduciary:

I would go one step further, and suggest that fiduciary obligations are imposed in some situations even in the absence of any unilateral undertaking by the fiduciary. In the present case, however, it is sufficient to say that being a parent comprises a unilateral undertaking that is fiduciary in nature. Equity then imposes a range of obligations coordinate with that undertaking.

[25]          Finally, La Forest J.’s judgment in M.(K.) v. M.(H.) refers with approval to Wilson J.’s dissenting opinion in Frame v. Smith, where she described at p. 136 the three general characteristics of relationships in which fiduciary obligations have been imposed, namely:

(1) The fiduciary has scope for the exercise of some discretion or power.

(2) The fiduciary can unilaterally exercise that power or discretion so as to affect the beneficiary’s legal or practical interests.

(3) The beneficiary is peculiarly vulnerable to or at the mercy of the fiduciary holding the discretion or power.

[26]          La Forest J. in M.(K.) v. M.(H.) at p. 64 found that the parent-child relationship met this test:

Even a cursory examination of these indicia establishes that a parent must owe fiduciary obligations to his or her child. Parents exercise great power over their children’s lives, and make daily decisions that affect their welfare. In this regard, the child is without doubt at the mercy of her parents.

[27]          These excerpts from M. (K.) v. M. (H.) might suggest that the parent-child relationship will impose fiduciary obligations in all circumstances.  However, La Forest J. also referred to his own reasons in Lac Minerals and McInerney v. MacDonald, [1992] 2 S.C.R. 138.  Those decisions indicate that the nature of the obligations will depend on the particular factual context and that the parent-child relationship raises only a presumption of fiduciary obligations.  As La Forest J. observed in M.(K.) v. M. (H.) at pp. 65-66:

The inherent purpose of the family relationship imposes certain obligations on a parent to act in his or her child’s best interests, and a presumption of fiduciary obligation arises.

In Lac Minerals I stressed the point, which also emerges from Frame v. Smith, that the substance of the fiduciary obligation in any given case is not derived from some immutable list of duties attached to a category of relationships. In other words, the duty is not determined by analogy with the “established” heads of fiduciary duty. Rather, the nature of the obligation will vary depending on the factual context of the relationship in which it arises  [emphasis added].

[28]          Sopinka J. made a similar point in his reasons in Lac Minerals at p. 598:

When the Court is dealing with one of the traditional relationships, the characteristics or criteria for a fiduciary relationship are assumed to exist.  In special circumstances, if they are shown to be absent, the relationship itself will not suffice.

[29]          I agree with the motions judge that on the facts pleaded it is plain and obvious that no fiduciary obligations were imposed upon the respondent in relation to the appellants.  The appellants’ mother chose to raise them as part of the Louie family.  In 1974, she released all claims against the respondent.  He at no time acted as, or was called upon to act as, a parent to the appellants.  Throughout, his role was that of a friend of the appellants’ mother.  The respondent did not assume any power or control over the lives of the appellants, nor did he dominate or influence their up-bringing  Because of the choices made by the appellants’ mother, the respondent had no scope for the exercise of discretion or power over the day-to-day lives of the appellants.  Whatever fiduciary obligations may be imposed upon a biological parent, it is plain and obvious that on the facts pleaded in this case, the respondent did not breach any obligation to support these appellants.

DISPOSITION

[30]          Accordingly, I would dismiss the appeal.  In his factum, the respondent did not seek costs and I would award none.
