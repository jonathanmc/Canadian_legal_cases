Heard:  May 22, 2002

On appeal from the judgment of Justice Gloria R. Klowak of the Superior Court of Justice dated June 6, 2000, reported at [2000] O.J. No. 2683.

MACPHERSON J.A.:

A.                 INTRODUCTION

[1]               The appellant Toronto-Dominion Bank (“the bank”) appeals from the judgment of Justice Gloria Klowak dated June 6, 2000 declaring that all debts under the line of credit extended by the bank to the respondents Margaret Valentine and the Estate of Peter Valentine are discharged.  As a result of this declaration, Klowak J. further ordered the bank to discharge a collateral mortgage on the respondents’ property.  The principal issues on the appeal are whether Margaret Valentine is bound by the line of credit which she signed and the relationship between the bank’s demand for repayment of the line of credit and the life insurance on the line of credit arranged by the bank for the Valentines.

B.                FACTS

(1)              The parties and events

[2]               Margaret Valentine (“Margaret”) and Peter Valentine (“Peter”) married in 1957.  They bought a property on Moore Lake in Haliburton County in 1957.  Together they built a home on the property and moved into it in 1961.  Both Valentines were school teachers at the time.  Soon they started a family.  Three children (Scott, Mark and Laurie) were born in the early and mid-1960s.  Margaret retired from her teaching position when the children arrived:

Q.          So you became a stay at home mom?

A.     You bet, June Cleaver.

[3]               In 1976, Margaret resumed her teaching career in Minden which she continued until 1996.  From 1962 to 1972, Peter, who was trained as a civil engineer, worked as a school teacher in Minden.  From 1972 to 1985, he worked in various engineering and construction businesses.  From 1985 to 1989, he was a school teacher again.  In 1989, Peter resigned his teaching position and decided to try to re-establish his construction business.

[4]               In August 1990, Margaret and Peter entered into a line of credit agreement for $75,000 with the bank.  The loan was initially sought by Peter who was a customer of the bank.  The purpose of the loan was to enable Peter to pay off his business and personal debts and to provide working capital for his construction business.  As security for the loan, the Valentines granted to the bank a collateral second mortgage on their family property in the amount of $75,000.  In conjunction with the loan, the Valentines obtained life insurance through the bank to cover the loan in the event that either of them died.

[5]               The August 1990 application for a line of credit was the third such application by Peter.  In January 1990, Margaret became aware of Peter’s first application for a loan when an appraiser visited their family property.  She met with Paul Norry (“Norry”), the manager of the bank branch in Minden, to express her concerns: “I told him he was crazy to lend my husband that much money”.  She was worried about the possibility of a second mortgage on the property (there was a first mortgage of about $40,000 with National Trust on the property which had a value of about $400,000) at a time when she was making all the payments on the first mortgage and Peter’s construction business “wasn’t even off the ground”.

[6]               Margaret decided to consult a solicitor, Jennifer Sillberg (“Sillberg”), who recommended that she enter into a marriage contract to protect her interests.  Margaret told Norry that she was having a marriage contract prepared and she obtained a copy of the bank’s line of credit application from him.  The marriage contract was finalized on June 15, 1990.  It provided that the Valentines would cooperate in obtaining a line of credit of no more than $75,000 to be secured by a second mortgage on the family home.  At the time the agreement was signed, Margaret thought that Peter would be using the line of credit to pay about $33,000 in business and personal debts.

[7]               On June 25, 1990, Peter signed the bank’s Equitymanager Line of Credit agreement.  Margaret signed the agreement on July 3, 1990, in the presence of her lawyer, and at the same time she executed a mortgage on the property in favour of the bank.  Sillberg signed the bank’s certificate of independent legal advice.  The next day, July 4, Sillberg wrote a letter to the bank explaining that the provisions of the Valentines’ marriage contract required a joint account to be set up through which all line of credit funds would flow, and that two signatures would be required on all cheques.  Sillberg also asked to be advised if Peter did not meet these requirements.  There is a dispute about what happened next.  Norry testified that he telephoned Sillberg and told her that the bank could not accommodate the request.  Sillberg was not called as a witness at the trial.  The trial judge found as a fact that no one from the bank responded to Sillberg’s letter, based on the absence of any written communication or internal notes or memoranda.

[8]               The bank had an arrangement with the insurance company Mutual Life which enabled the bank to offer life insurance relating to loans to the bank’s customers.  The Valentines filled out an application for life insurance on August 13, 1990.  It purported to protect both of them from making payments on the line of credit if either died.  A crucial provision of the agreement they signed, as it turned out, was paragraph 7:

7.            TERMINATION OF INSURANCE

The insurance will terminate on the earliest of: . . .

g.      the date the Bank has demanded payment of the outstanding balance including any unpaid premium, . . . .

[9]               In August 1990, the bank was in the process of changing its line of credit forms.  Accordingly, on August 20 the Valentines went to the bank again and signed the Credit Line Agreement which was substantially similar to the second agreement they had signed in June (Peter) and July (Margaret).  Margaret did not seek independent legal advice in August.  After signing the August agreement, Margaret was given copies of four cheques totalling about $34,000, representing funds that would be withdrawn from the line of credit.  Most of the money was being paid directly to the bank to pay off Peter’s unsecured loans and business debts.

[10]          Peter was hospitalized for a nervous breakdown in November 1990 and was released from hospital in early January 1991.

[11]          By the end of December 1990, the line of credit was almost exhausted.  Over $73,000 had been advanced.  Under the terms of the agreement, the bank could take “minimum monthly payments” from the line of credit.  The agreement provided that the bank take any “accrued and unpaid interest”.  Instead, the bank took 3 per cent of the outstanding balance at the end of each month.  If the bank had complied with its own agreement, it would have been entitled to take $3593.27 from the line of credit.  Instead, by taking 3 per cent of the outstanding balance between August and December 1990, it depleted the line of credit by $10,120.72.  This difference of $6527.45 would become crucial in March 1991 when the bank decided to make its demand.

[12]          By the end of 1990, the line of credit was in arrears.  Shortly after Peter was released from hospital in January 1991, the Valentines met with bank manager Norry to discuss the delinquent status of the line of credit.  Although Peter agreed to put the line of credit in good standing by selling his truck, the bank received no further payments.

[13]          By March 1991, the line of credit exceeded its $75,000 ceiling.  Norry wrote a letter to the Valentines on March 5 in which he stated:

As you have not responded to our request for payment we are obliged to declare your total debt due and payable as of the date of this letter.

Therefore, this letter serves as a formal demand on you for repayment in full of your Select Line indebtedness in the amount of Seventy-five thousand, four hundred, twenty-four dollars and sixty-five cents ($75,424.65) plus a per diem rate of $25.79 from the date of this letter to date of repayment.

[14]          The bank subsequently allowed the Valentines time to deal with their personal issues (their marriage was shaky) and to resolve the amount owing to the bank.  In the absence of any payment, the bank commenced power of sale proceedings on June 5, 1991.

[15]          In early July, Margaret attended at the bank and deposited a cheque payable to Peter from Revenue Canada for $4635.18.  The bank reinstated the line of credit.

[16]          On August 31, 1991, Peter Valentine died of natural causes.

[17]          With the bank’s assistance, Margaret applied for the life insurance benefit under the insurance policy relating to the line of credit.  Mutual Life denied coverage on the basis of paragraph 7(g) of the policy.  Its position was that the bank’s demand for payment of the line of credit on March 5, 1990 had terminated the policy.

[18]          In the next few months, no payments were made on the line of credit.  In the summer of 1992, the bank recommenced power of sale proceedings.  Margaret commenced an action and sought an injunction with respect to these proceedings.  The parties agreed that the power of sale proceedings would be suspended and that the bank would proceed by way of counterclaim in the action.

(2)              The litigation

[19]          The trial proceeded before Klowak J. on nine days from April 12 to July 9, 1999.  On April 27, 2000, the trial judge released her reasons for judgment.

[20]          The trial judge held that Margaret was not bound by the letter of credit agreement.  She found that the bank had treated Margaret in a secondary manner, ancillary to its arrangement with Peter and otherwise to be ignored.  The trial judge determined that solicitor Sillberg’s letter to the bank on July 4, 1990 set out conditions precedent to Margaret’s agreement to participate in the line of credit, despite the fact that the second line of credit and the certificate of independent legal advice had been signed the day before.  Accordingly, both the July 3 and the “substantially similar” August 20 agreements were of no force and effect in relation to Margaret.

[21]          The trial judge also held that the bank had a duty to give the Valentines notice of its intention to make a demand for payment of the line of credit and notice of the effect of the demand on their insurance.  The trial judge held that the transactions with respect to the line of credit did not establish a fiduciary relationship, but, if necessary, a court might find a fiduciary relationship existed with respect to the insurance and that the bank breached the relationship “by failing to keep the Valentines advised, particularly of its intention to trigger termination of the policy”.  Accordingly, the bank was responsible for the Valentines’ losses up to the limits of the insurance policy.

[22]          On the question of remedy, the trial judge held:

The measure of the plaintiffs’ loss is the insurance they would have had if it had not been terminated.  Since that amount would have been paid to the Bank to discharge the loan, the appropriate disposition in my view is to declare that any and all debts under the line of credit have been discharged with respect to both Mrs. Valentine and her husband’s estate and order the bank to discharge its collateral mortgage on their property.

C.        ISSUES

[23]          I would frame the issues in the appeal in this fashion:

(1)              Did the trial judge err in holding that Margaret Valentine was not bound by the line of credit agreement?

(2)              Did the trial judge err in holding that the bank was legally responsible for the termination of the life insurance contract and could not, therefore, enforce the line of credit agreement against the respondents?

(3)              If the answer to question (1) is ‘No’, did the trial judge nevertheless err by holding that the Estate of Peter Valentine had no liability to repay the funds received under the line of credit to the bank?

D.                ANALYSIS

(1)              Margaret’s liability under the line of credit

[24]          There appear to be three strands in the trial judge’s reasons in support of her conclusion that “Mrs. Valentine is not bound by the line of credit and it is unenforceable against her”.

[25]          First, the trial judge regarded the bank as being in a conflict of interest position with Margaret. This tenuous position required the bank to make full disclosure of its interest in the proposed line of credit, namely, that a substantial portion of the funds advanced under the joint line of credit, which would be secured by a mortgage on the family property, would be used to pay off some of Peter’s unsecured debts to the bank.  The trial judge found that the bank breached this duty of full disclosure.

[26]          Second, and crucially, the trial judge held that solicitor Sillberg’s letter of July 4, 1990 to the bank was effective in law to link the Valentines’ marriage contract to the line of credit agreement that Margaret signed on July 3.  The trial judge reasoned:

The letter from Ms. Sillberg clearly states that the line of credit was agreed to by Mrs. Valentine “as a result of certain provisions in the marriage contract”. . . The provision with respect to a joint account requiring two signatures immediately follows.

Although the wording might perhaps have been better, I nevertheless find that Ms. Sillberg’s letter clearly advises Mr. Norry that Mrs. Valentine only agreed to the line of credit on the provision that funds would be drawn as outlined.

[Emphasis in original.]

According to the trial judge, since all of the draws on the line of credit from August to December 1990 were authorized only by Peter, it followed that Margaret was not liable for repayment of the accumulated debt in the line of credit.

[27]          Third, the trial judge held that the only way the bank could have overcome the linkage between the marriage contract and the line of credit agreement established by Sillberg’s letter of July 4, 1990 was either by replying to it and stating its disagreement or by ensuring that Margaret received independent legal advice again before she signed the third line of credit agreement on August 20, 1990.

[28]          With respect, I do not agree with the trial judge's reasons or conclusion on the issue of Margaret’s liability under the line of credit.

[29]          The bank was not in a conflict of interest position with Margaret.  It was dealing with a joint application for a line of credit.  Margaret knew and accepted that some of the line of credit would be used to pay off Peter’s personal and business debts; indeed, the marriage contract she entered into with Peter specifically contemplated this usage.  On August 20, 1990, when she signed the third agreement which eventually activated the line of credit, Margaret was shown copies of four unsigned cheques, three of which totalling about $30,000 represented funds which would be withdrawn from the line of credit to pay off Peter’s outstanding debts to the bank.  She testified that she was upset when she saw these cheques and called her solicitor the same day.  Notwithstanding this, neither Margaret nor the solicitor took any steps to prevent the bank from activating the line of credit and using the funds in the manner it proposed.  In short, on the very day Margaret signed the line of credit agreement she knew, and accepted, that a significant portion of the line of credit would be used to repay Peter’s bank loans.

[30]          Turning to Sillberg’s July 4, 1990 letter to the bank, in my view it does not assist Margaret.  On July 3, she signed the line of credit agreement in the presence of her solicitor who had been assisting her with regard to marital and financial matters for several months.  The agreement Margaret signed on July 3 did not contain the conditions Sillberg attempted to impose through her letter to the bank the next day.  Moreover, when Margaret signed the third agreement on August 20, 1990 which activated the line of credit, again the conditions which were contained in the July 4 letter were not included in the agreement and Margaret knew that the bank did not accept them.  Nevertheless, she signed and neither she nor anyone on her behalf raised the matter with the bank again after August 20, 1990.

[31]          Finally, I do not accept that the bank did not reply to Sillberg’s letter or that the bank had a duty to ensure that Margaret received independent legal advice before signing the third line of credit agreement on August 20.  Although the trial judge found as a fact that Norry did not specifically respond, either orally or in writing, to Sillberg’s letter, the reality is that on August 20, when Margaret signed the third agreement, she knew that the bank did not accept the conditions set out in the Sillberg letter.  Moreover, Margaret received legal advice throughout the spring and summer of 1990.  In particular, she signed the second line of credit agreement on July 3 in the presence of Sillberg who in turned signed the bank’s certificate of independent legal advice on the same day.   Since the third agreement which Margaret signed on August 20 was, as found by the trial judge, “substantially similar” to the second agreement, and since Margaret, an educated school teacher, had involved Sillberg regularly in her financial affairs for several months, I think the trial judge set the bar too high by concluding that the bank had a duty to ensure that Margaret received independent legal advice again before signing the third agreement.

[32]          In summary, I agree with the appellant that the trial judge erred in holding that Margaret was not bound by the line of credit agreement. 

(2)              The life insurance issue

[33]          The trial judge concluded that the Valentines should succeed on this issue for two reasons: first, the failure of the bank to provide notice prior to making its demand, including notice of the effect of the demand on the life insurance; and, second, a finding, “were it necessary to do so”, of a fiduciary relationship between the bank and the Valentines which the bank breached.

[34]          In my view, it is not necessary to consider the potential question of fiduciary relationship to which the trial judge briefly alluded. That is because I agree with the ultimate conclusion reached by the trial judge on the life insurance issue.  I do so for two reasons - one being contractual, the other being, as the trial judge held, an improper failure by the bank to provide appropriate notice to the Valentines about the cancellation of the life insurance.

[35]          The line of credit agreement between the bank and the Valentines was a contract.  One of its provisions was that the minimum monthly payment was “any accrued and unpaid interest”.  In spite of this provision, from August to December 1990 the bank collected 3 per cent of the balance on the entire line of credit each month.  The following table reflects what the bank was entitled to take and what it actually took by way of minimum monthly payments in that time frame:

[36]          Importantly, the effect of the bank’s error was described quite vividly by both of the bank’s witnesses at the trial, Norry, the manager, and Tracy Pilat (“Pilat”), the loans officer.

[37]          Norry agreed that part of the relationship between the bank and the Valentines was that Valentine Engineering, Peter’s company, would draw money from the line of credit.  Then the bank would directly debit Valentine Engineering for the next monthly minimum payment which, contrary to the contract, it set at 3 per cent of the balance of the line of credit.  The consequences of this for the Valentines were described in this exchange during the cross-examination of Norry:

[39]          It is clear, then, that the bank’s breach of the line of credit agreement by taking $6527.45 to which it was not entitled resulted in the line of credit being in arrears in January 1991.  It is also clear that the bank’s breach led to the line of credit being three months in arrears by March 1991.  Finally, it is clear that the bank’s breach led to the line of credit being over its $75,000 ceiling by March 1991 (it was at $75,424,65).  Thus, whether the bank decided to make a demand on March 5, 1991 because the line of credit was in arrears for three months, or because it exceeded the maximum that could be drawn, or both, is of no consequence.  But for the bank’s breach of contract throughout the autumn of 1990, there would have been no basis for making the demand on March 5, 1991.  Moreover, but for the demand, the life insurance would have continued in effect.

[40]          The bank contends that a demand probably would have been made in any event because if the bank had not improperly added $6527.95 to the line of credit, Peter’s use of the line of credit during the same period indicates that he would have withdrawn these available funds to use in his business.

[41]          I regard this submission as pure speculation: see Twardy v. Humboldt Credit Union Ltd. (1985), 34 C.C.L.T. 140 at 141 (Sask. Q.B.).  I prefer to rely on the facts as established in the record.  Fact 1 - The bank took $6527.45 to which it was not entitled.  Fact 2 - But for the bank’s conduct, the line of credit would not have been in arrears in January 1991.  Fact 3 - But for the bank’s conduct, the line of credit would probably not have been three months in arrears on March 5, 1991.  Fact 4 - But for the bank’s conduct, the line of credit would not have exceeded its ceiling on March 5.  Fact 5 - There would have been no basis for making a demand on March 5.  Fact 6 - The life insurance would have continued in force.  Fact 7 - Without either the bank or the Valentines doing anything, there would have been just under $6000 ($75,424.65 minus $6527.45) in the line of credit on March 5. Fact 8 - Without either the bank or the Valentines doing anything, and using Pilat’s estimate of a minimum monthly payment of “interest only” of $875, the line of credit would have continued in force for six or seven months, until September or October 1991.  Fact 9 - Peter Valentine died on August 31, 1991.  Accordingly, the conclusion I reach is that the bank’s breach of the line of credit agreement led directly to the termination of the life insurance contract.  The damages that flow from this breach is the benefit of the life insurance policy - namely, removal of the requirement to make payments under the line of credit once Peter died.

[42]          The conclusion I have reached grounded in breach of contract is sufficient to dispose of this issue (and indeed the appeal).  However, for the sake of completeness, I observe that, in the circumstances of this case, I agree with the trial judge that the bank had a duty to inform the Valentines that one of the consequences of the demand on March 5, 1991 was the termination of the life insurance on the line of credit.

[43]          The bank’s conduct or, more accurately, its silence on this matter is, in a word, troubling.  Norry testified that he did not know that a demand would result in cancellation of the insurance. This is surprising in light of the fact that he was a manager of a bank branch with many years of experience.  Moreover, there was a Master Policy Agreement (“MPA”) between the bank and Mutual Life which set out clearly the contents of the insurance programs these two corporate entities were jointly operating.  The MPA clearly stated the situations in which insurance coverage would cease, including if the bank made a demand.

[44]          Pilat testified that she did know that the effect of a demand was the termination of the insurance.  She notified the bank’s head office on May 6, 1991 that the insurance should be cancelled.  She did nothing to notify the Valentines of this change, even though the bank and the Valentines were still discussing ways of putting the line of credit back in good standing.

[45]          In July 1991, when the payment of $4635.18 by the Valentines was accepted by the bank as sufficient to reinstate the line of credit, no one at the bank told the Valentines that one of its components - the life insurance - no longer existed.

[46]          Finally, I note that the bank’s silence about the termination of the insurance occurred at a time when Peter, to the knowledge of Norry and Pilat, had been hospitalized from November 1990 to January 1991 and again in June 1991.

[47]          The bank submits that Margaret signed the life insurance contract and must be deemed to be aware of its contents, including the termination that would be caused by a demand on the line of credit.  In my view, this is not an attractive submission in light of the fact that Norry, a bank manager responsible for administering and explaining all aspects of the line of credit, did not know of this consequence; indeed, after Peter’s death, he told Margaret that there would be “no problem” with the insurance and he submitted the relevant claim forms on her behalf.

[48]          I conclude that the bank had a duty to inform the Valentines that their life insurance coverage relating to the line of credit had been terminated when the bank made its demand for repayment on March 5, 1991.  It could have fulfilled this obligation in any of three ways: first, by referring to it in the March 5 demand letter which, incidentally, was founded on a continuation, not a termination, of the line of credit agreement; second, by notifying the Valentines on May 6, 1991 that the insurance had been cancelled pursuant to Pilat’s instructions to head office on that date; or, third, by telling the Valentines in July 1991 that the reinstatement of the line of credit agreement following their payment of $4635.18 did not revive their life insurance.  Unfortunately and, in my view, improperly, the bank took none of these steps. [1]

[49]          In summary, I agree with crucial features of the trial judge’s reasoning and with her conclusion on this issue.  I do not accept the appellant’s submissions and would not give effect to this ground of appeal.

(3)              Separate liability of the Estate of Peter Valentine

[50]          In light of my conclusion on the first issue, this issue does not arise.

E.                 DISPOSITION

[51]          I would dismiss the appeal with costs to the respondents on a partial indemnity scale.  If the parties are unable to agree on costs, brief written submission may be filed with the court by the respondents within 10 days of the release of these reasons and by the appellant within 10 days thereafter.

FINLAYSON J.A. (dissenting):

I.  Background

[52]          I have read the majority reasons in this appeal written by MacPherson J.A. and I am unable to agree with them.  Our differences are fundamental and are both factual and legal.  First of all, I do not agree that the conduct of the employees of the appellant Toronto-Dominion Bank in Minden, Ontario (“the Bank”) was responsible for the breach by the respondents Peter and Margaret Valentine of their line of credit agreement with the Bank. Secondly, I do not accept that a finding should or could be made in this court and on this record that had Mrs. Valentine known that the life insurance protecting the joint indebtedness of herself and her husband would terminate if the loan was declared in default, she would have ensured that the joint account was always in good standing.  Thirdly, the majority acted on its own initiative in propounding a remedy for Mrs. Valentine that she neither asked for nor adopted when it was suggested from the bench.  This initiative, in my respectful opinion, gave this court's seal of approval to the conversion of Mrs. Valentine’s action for damages into an equitable defence to a counter-claim by the Bank for the recovery of the debt of Mrs. Valentine and her late husband’s estate.

[53]          As I understand the holdings of the majority, there is now no issue that Margaret Valentine and her late husband Peter Valentine made a joint application to the Bank for a line of credit with a limit of $75,000 to enable the husband to pay off some $33,000 in existing debts and to finance an engineering consulting business.  The line of credit was secured by a second mortgage on the couple’s matrimonial home.  Both husband and wife were teachers but the husband had retired.  He held an engineering degree and had supplemented his teacher’s income in the past by doing engineering consulting work on the side.  Mrs. Valentine received independent legal advice throughout.

[54]          Unfortunately, the Valentines, while living together, were having matrimonial difficulties, which steadily worsened.  To my mind, this deteriorating marriage was the overriding influence on the conduct of Mrs. Valentine, and this factor was not sufficiently understood by the court, here and below or if it was, it was not given sufficient weight. From a reading of the record, it is apparent that the state of accounts between her husband and the Bank was always a secondary consideration with Mrs. Valentine.  Her primary objective was to use his need for fresh money from the Bank to secure from him a favourable division of family assets and in particular to acquire full ownership of the matrimonial home. 

[55]          Mr. Valentine made the initial application for the line of credit without consulting his wife.   She found out about it when an assessor came around to value the matrimonial home for mortgage purposes.  After confronting her husband about the surprise loan, she went to the Bank and stated to Paul Norry, the Bank Manager that he was “crazy to lend her husband that much money”.  However, instead of blocking the loan to her husband by declining to become personally liable for the loan and exercising her rights under s. 21 of the Family Law Act, R.S.O. 1990, c. F.3 to prevent her husband from encumbering the matrimonial home, Mrs. Valentine decided to become involved in the credit application with a view to exercising some control over her husband and their joint assets.  But first she went to a lawyer for advice.  That lawyer, Jennifer Sillberg, acted as Mrs. Valentine’s personal solicitor throughout.  She first advised Mrs. Valentine that she should enter into a marriage contract to protect her interests.  Mrs. Valentine obtained a copy of the Bank’s line of credit application and delivered it to Sillberg who drafted the marriage contract and submitted it to the husband’s lawyer. 

[56]          The marriage contract provided that the Valentines would cooperate in obtaining a line of credit of no more than $75,000 to be secured by a second mortgage on the family home.  It specifically contemplated that some of the line of credit would be used to pay off the husband’s outstanding personal and business debts and listed them.  A separate paragraph provided that the listed debts were to be paid immediately through the line of credit from the Bank. As to the matrimonial home, it was to be conveyed by the husband and wife as joint tenants to the husband and wife as tenants in common.  The husband was to be solely responsible for payments on the collateral second mortgage to the Bank.  The marriage contract also provided that the husband would indemnify and save harmless the wife from any claims made by the Bank arising from any default and payment of moneys as they fell due under the collateral mortgage registered against the family residence.  While the Bank was aware that such an agreement as this existed, a copy was not provided to it.

[57]          On June 25, 1990, Mr. Valentine executed the first of three of the Bank’s line of credit agreements, all of which were the same for our purposes.  Mrs. Valentine signed the second agreement on July 3, 1990, in the presence of her lawyer, and at the same time she executed a mortgage on the property in favour of the Bank.  Sillberg signed the Bank’s certificate of independent legal advice. It stated that Mrs. Valentine was executing the second credit agreement and the mortgage “freely and voluntarily and as her own act and deed without any fear, threat, influence or compulsion of, from or by her said husband”.  Mrs. Valentine also signed the form acknowledging that the statements in the certificate were correct.

[58]          The next day, July 4, Sillberg wrote a letter to the bank explaining that the provisions of the Valentines’ marriage contract required a joint account to be set up through which all line of credit funds would flow, and that two signatures would be required on all cheques.  In point of fact the marriage contract stipulated no such condition. Sillberg also asked to be advised if Peter Valentine did not meet these requirements. 

[59]          There is a dispute about what happened next.  The Bank Manager testified that he telephoned Sillberg and told her that the bank could not accommodate the request.  Sillberg was not called as a witness at the trial.  However, the majority of this court accepted that neither Mrs. Valentine nor her solicitor followed up on Sillbeg’s letter and that Mrs. Valentine knew that the Bank was not going to honour Sillberg’s “conditions” when she subsequently signed the third agreement on August 20, 1990. The third agreement contained nothing more than a change to the Bank’s name for the type of line of credit.  From my point of view, what is significant is that despite the insistence by the wife that the Bank become involved in monitoring the marriage contract between herself and her husband, the Bank refused to do so. 

[60]          I am setting out below a paraphrase of the significant terms of the line of credit agreement.

·          The line of credit was for $75,000;

·          Monthly payments were interest only;

·          Until the Bank demands payment in full, the Valentines promise to pay the minimum monthly payment due (cl. 2);

·          The Bank may require the Valentines to repay their outstanding balance, plus interest, plus costs, plus charges, on demand at any time (cl. 5);

·          The Bank may change the provisions of the agreement from time to time. These changes include changes to the credit limit or interest rates payable.  Written notice of any change will be sent and if the amount of indebtedness is not repaid in full within ten days of receiving the change the Valentines will be deemed to have accepted the change (cl. 11);

·          Communication will be sent by ordinary uninsured mail (cl. 13);

·          If more than one person signs the agreement, communication with any one of the two will serve as notice to all (cl. 13);

·          Each person who signs the agreement is jointly and severally liable with the others for payment of the outstanding balance and costs (cl. 16); and

·          If the Bank determines that it has improperly credited an amount to the Valentines account, it can adjust the balance at any time, regardless of when it discovers the error (cl. 8(d)).

[61]          Since a great deal turns on the right of the Bank to demand payment, I will set out the relevant portion of clause 5 in full.

5.  Demand

We may require you to repay your outstanding balance, plus interest, plus the costs specified below, plus charges, on demand at any time.

[62]          It appears that Mrs. Valentine was correct in her assessment of the wisdom of the Bank lending money to her husband.  His credit record at the Bank was terrible.  He had three accounts, none of them in good standing.  His personal account was overdrawn from December 15, 1988 until this line of credit was approved on August 17, 1990.  It was overdrawn again by December 3, 1990.  His company, Valentine Engineering, had an account but it was inactive until the line of credit was approved.  It appears that this account used the line of credit to finance its activities.  It did not have a credit balance for any significant period during the relevant times.  It was largely through Valentine Engineering that Peter Valentine drew steadily against the line of credit from August 1990 to the end of December of that year by which time he had exhausted the $75,000 line of credit regardless of whose accounting one accepts.  However, in the last analysis, little turns on the precise figures.  Of the four monthly cheques he deposited to keep the line of credit in good standing by paying any accrued and unpaid interest, two of them bounced. Peter Valentine was not making regular monthly payments of interest in any amount and contrary to the approach taken to this appeal by the majority, the Bank did not take action to have the Valentines bring the credit line into good standing because the credit limits were reached, but because Peter Valentine was not making the regular monthly payments that were his clear obligation under the credit agreement.  I cannot accept the suggestion, implicit in the reasoning of the majority, that he was excused from making payments at all because on an ex post facto analysis it is determined that the interest payments were improperly calculated by the Bank.

[63]          It should be further noted that there is no three-month requirement of default before a Demand can be served.  The fact that the Bank waited three months after their informal demand that the line of credit be brought into good standing did not amend the credit line agreement to make such notice a requirement.

[64]          According to the Bank, the line of credit had been in arrears since November 1990 because Peter Valentine was not making the monthly payments of accrued and unpaid interest required by the Credit Line Agreement. The Bank met with the Valentines  on January 18, 1991 to discuss the delinquent status of the line of credit. The Valentines were advised that they should take steps to put it into good standing.  Peter Valentine agreed and promised to sell his truck and pay the proceeds to the Bank but he never did.  On March 5, 1991, some three months after the default in November 1990, the Bank wrote a letter to the Valentines demanding payment of the outstanding loans.

[65]          The marriage of the Valentines continued to deteriorate and following the meeting with the Bank on January 18, 1991, Mrs. Valentine had again consulted her lawyer.  The trial judge described her purpose:

      Subsequent to the meeting in late January 1991, the wife went back to her lawyer to develop a strategy for buying out her husband’s interest in the home, which was valued about $400,000 to $500,000.  Other than the collateral mortgage securing the line of credit, there was only a small first mortgage which she anticipated being able to re-finance in order to take over the line of credit.  [Her] lawyer’s letter to that effect was timed so it would arrive during March break of 1991, when Mrs. Valentine planned to be away.

[66]           The letter Sillberg wrote to her husband stated that Mrs. Valentine wished to separate from him and desired to reach a settlement with him based on the terms of the marriage contract.  As part of the proposed settlement, Margaret Valentine offered to buy her husband’s one half interest in the matrimonial home and take over the responsibility for the collateral mortgage. 

[67]          On March 8, 1991, the Bank noted in internal correspondence that Tracy Pilat, a personal loans officer and the Bank Manager had attempted to contact Margaret and Peter Valentine on more than one occasion without success.  Margaret Valentine testified that notwithstanding that they were still living together, she never asked her husband if he had made any payments respecting the line of credit.

[68]          Following receipt of the letter demanding payment of the line of credit, Mrs. Valentine attended at the Bank on March 22, 1991 and was told the matter was in the hands of the Bank’s lawyer.  Subsequently, the Bank agreed to allow the Valentines to deal with the issue of the Bank indebtedness between themselves and determine the outstanding amount owing to the Bank.  However, nothing concrete appeared from the Valentines’ camp and, in the absence of any payments towards the indebtedness, on June 5, 1991 the Bank initiated power of sale proceedings under its collateral mortgage.  There was no defence.  In early July 1991, Mrs. Valentine brought a cheque payable to and endorsed by her husband from Revenue Canada. She applied the cheque to the outstanding debt as a partial stop gap to the continuance of the power of sale but other than that no attempts were made to retire the indebtedness or do the refinancing that is now suggested in this court as being feasible.

[69]          Then on August 31, 1990, eight months after the line of credit was exhausted, the husband unexpectedly died of natural causes that were unrelated to the problems he had been having with depression.  The Coroner’s report states that his death was caused by a fainting spell of unknown cause, presumably a slip and fall in the bathroom.  The report indicates that there was no pre-existing condition. 

[70]          Almost one year after the death of Peter Valentine, and in the absence of any payments from the wife or the estate of her husband, the Bank reinstituted power of sale proceedings in the summer of 1992.  But now there was a defence.  It was alleged that the Bank should not have allowed the life insurance protecting the line of credit account to have lapsed.  Mrs. Valentine sued the Bank for $150,000 in damages.  In the alternative, she asked for a declaration that there was no longer a debt and sought an injunction restraining the Bank from proceeding with its power of sale proceedings.  The Bank counterclaimed against the wife and her husband for payment of the debt to the Bank but to no avail.  I will deal with this action in more detail later on.

II.  Analysis

[71]          The first step in analysing the legal problems in this case is to recognize that nothing now turns on the fact of the indebtedness itself.  The Valentines signed the necessary documentation, they both had their own lawyers advising them, they received the money and the husband spent it.   The suggestion that the Bank is the fiduciary of Mrs. Valentine has no substance. The relationship remained one of debtor/creditor throughout.  In point of fact, when Mrs. Valentine tried to impose additional responsibilities on the Bank to oversee and police her marriage contract with her husband, the Bank refused to alter its usual practice to accommodate her. She was aware from the outset that the loan was a bad one from the point of view of the borrower and consequently the borrower’s wife and she tried to talk the Bank out of making it.  When the Bank refused to listen to her, she agreed with her husband to support him on the line of credit in return for his signing a marriage contract. There was a suggestion by counsel for the Valentines that the Bank, for reasons that make no possible sense, talked Peter Valentine into overreaching his credit potential and then deliberately encouraged him to draw down his credit to exceed the $75,000 limit so that it could realize on the security of the matrimonial home.  However, the trial judge did not accept this scenario.  Accordingly, both Mrs. Valentine and the estate of her late husband are liable to repay the moneys they borrowed with interest.  Judgment should issue on the counter-claim of the Bank.  We turn now to the separate issue of the insurance policy.

            Termination of the Insurance Policy

[72]           According to Mrs. Valentine, this subsidiary issue caused the obligation to the Bank to disappear. She argues that as a result of the Bank’s failure to notify her of the impact a demand would have on her insurance coverage, the documents executed with the Bank and referred to above were no longer of legal effect nor binding on either Mrs. Valentine or her late husband.  It appears that the Bank had an arrangement with an insurance company, Mutual Life Assurance Company of Canada (“Mutual Life”) that enabled the Bank to offer life insurance protection for outstanding loans to the Bank’s customers.  The Valentines asked for such coverage and they attended at the Bank and filled out an application for life insurance on August 13, 1990.  It provided that on the death or accidental dismemberment of the insureds or one of them, Mutual Life would pay a benefit to the Bank of an amount equal to the outstanding balance and accrued interest under the line of credit on the day prior to the triggering event.  One provision of the agreement was paragraph 7(g), which stipulated that the insurance would terminate on the date the Bank demanded payment of the outstanding balance.

[73]          There was considerable discussion in this court on the failure of the Bank to advise both Valentines of the fact of paragraph 7(g) and there was criticism arising from the revelation that the Bank Manager, Paul Norry did not know of the existence of the termination clause.  However, Tracy Pilat, the personal loans officer who looked after the detail of the loan arrangements knew about it.  With her memory refreshed by the documentation with her name on it as a witness to the signatures of the Valentines, she testified that she met with Mr. and Mrs. Valentine together on August 13, 1990 to go over the insurance application with them and she witnessed their signatures on the application.  Pilat had no independent recollection of the meeting but testified that her normal practice was to go over the form with the customer and highlight the terms and conditions of the insurance coverage and provide a copy of the signed form to the customer.

[74]          In summary, the evidence of Mrs. Valentine was that she, together with her husband, decided to get insurance on the line of credit. They made an appointment with Paul Norry and Tracy Pilat. The Valentines both signed the insurance form on August 13, 1990. Mrs. Valentine understood that there would be payments of premiums for the insurance. Paul Norry told her that they would be paid automatically by deductions from the line of credit. She did not recall anything else discussed at that meeting.  Her evidence was that she did not read all of the terms of the agreement and indicated that she did not take a copy home with her although she did admit in cross-examination that it is possible that she did just that. She did not see Mr. Valentine read the agreement and does not know whether he took a copy of the insurance agreement home with him. Since Mr. Valentine could give no evidence, Mrs. Valentine’s testimony is the only evidence of a lack of knowledge of either of them.  In the circumstances, reference to the transcript is useful.

[75]          After entering the insurance form as an exhibit; white copy, yellow copy, pink copy – counsel for the Bank asked Mrs. Valentine whether she remembered taking the document home with her and she said that she did not. She cannot say for sure whether Mr. Valentine did or did not get a copy. Referring to examinations in 1997, counsel put to Margaret that at examinations he asked:

Q.  Okay, do you recall getting a copy of [the insurance application]? Of what you signed for insurance?

A.  I don’t recall, but I may have.

In continuing at trial he asked:

Q.  I’m going to suggest to you as a result of those questions that it is possible, possible that you received a copy of that document and that Peter received a copy of that document, too. I am going to suggest to you that it is possible, is that fair?

A.  It’s possible.

[76]          What appears obvious to me from the troubled matrimonial relationship between the two Valentines is that the Bank is being blamed for a number of things that it had little knowledge of and no control over.  The error in charging interest is a red herring.  Banks frequently make similar errors and they are corrected in due course.  The notion that this error should disentitle the Bank to recover any of the moneys advanced under the credit agreement from either of the husband and wife debtors boggles the mind.  If it were necessary for me to do so, I would agree with counsel for the appellant Bank that even had the $6, 527.45 over charge been corrected when it was first noted by the Bank it would not have delayed the action of the Bank by more than a month.  However, the flaw in the majority's reasoning is not that it failed to recognize the inevitability that Mr. Valentine would eventually have exhausted his credit by continuing to make withdrawals.  Rather, it is that the loan was already in default and absent the Bank's error it still had sufficient reason to call in the loan.  The default in maintaining the monthly interest charges was not purged by any overcharge of interest because the Valentines were making no effort to make interest payments in any amount.

[77]          The majority of the court has presented some calculations that purport to show that, if certain overcharges of interest had not been improperly made, the line of credit would have not been overdrawn until after the death of Peter Valentine on August 30, 1991, in which event the insurance benefits would have retired his indebtedness and we would not be here.  I do not accept that this approach is even valid but I have made my own calculations as to the state of the husband’s line of credit and I find that this assumption by the majority is tenuous at best.  This is not surprising because the Valentines did not advance this rather startling proposition at any time here or below.  Indeed, when it was suggested from the bench during the course of argument, counsel for the Valentines did not adopt it.  In the result, my calculations have not been tested in argument either.

[78]          I have constructed an Exhibit A to these reasons from Tab 33 of the compendium of the appellant and Tab 92 of the Exhibit Book, Volume II.  It illustrates that the line of credit began operating on August 20, 1990.  Exhibit A demonstrates that excluding all interest charges and insurance premiums, the husband drew upon his line of credit from 20 August 1990 to 17 December 1990 for a total of $77,559.02 and deposited against that sum $7,893.24 for a net draw down of $69,665.78.  If we add the interest charges that the majority accepts as accurate ($3,593.27) and the insurance premiums that are undisputed ($169.22), the net draw down is $73,428.27.

[79]          The statement put forward by the appellant Bank for the line of credit (appellant’s compendium – Tab 33) indicates a similar pattern but erroneously excludes a credit of $2,236.20 on November 1, 2002.  I have set it out as Exhibit B.  Assuming that the interest charge of $3,593.27 adopted by the majority is correct, and adding the missed credit, Peter Valentine had still drawn a net $73, 428.27 on the line of credit in the five months from opening of the account in August until it was exhausted in December:

·          $73,869.29 was drawn from the line of credit (excluding insurance premiums, and interest);

·          $7,893.24 was credited to the line of credit as monthly payments (at p. 8 of the respondent’s factum, the respondent’s include a December payment in their comparison of monthly charges, however, this was not paid into the account in December). Only $4203.51 was actually charged to the account as two of the four monthly payments were reversed;

·          $3,593.27 is charged in interest; and

·          $169.22 is charged in insurance premiums.

[80]          While I have used the majority’s interest figure, there is some doubt that even that is accurate.  Roughly (not adjusting for interest charges on the overpaid amounts), it would appear that had the Bank been charging interest only, the payments to the Bank to cover monthly payments would have been $4299.97 less than was charged using 3% of balance as the monthly payment.  However, when one considers that the indebtedness of the line of credit was only increased by the successful, as opposed to the reversed, minimum monthly payments charged to the account, the 3% of balance payments were only $610.24 more than the interest only payments would have been.

[81]          The evidence in Exhibit Book Volume I of copies of cheques drawn on the Valentine Engineering Account, which was receiving a transfer of funds from the line of credit, demonstrates Peter Valentine’s spending habits in terms of frequency and amount. After having drawn $73,869.29 from the line of credit over the course of less than five full months, it seems eminently reasonable that Peter Valentine, without the intervention of the Bank, would have drawn the comparatively small amount in one month necessary to bring the credit line up to $75,000. However, it is not necessary to resort to even this modest speculation.  January’s interest only charge would have been $874.76 (or some lesser amount, adjusted for the reduced monthly payments) and in January would have pushed the indebtedness on the line of credit back to where it was in December.

[82]          While I concede the unsatisfactory nature of this exercise conducted after the conclusion of the hearing of the appeal without the benefit of submissions of counsel, it is at least sufficient to raise serious concerns as to the soundness of the position of the majority that the Bank placed the Valentines in an overdraft position by extracting illegal interest charges.  These illegal interest charges, it is said, were the sole justification for the Demand that terminated the life insurance policy and left Mrs. Valentine without the means to pay off their joint indebtedness when Mr. Valentine unexpectedly died on August 30, 1991.  Mr. Valentine’s credit record does not support the basic premise that he would have maintained this line of credit in good standing if the Bank had not exhausted the credit line by illegal interest charges and certainly Mrs. Valentine would not have used her money to do so.  The Bank was more than justified in calling in the Valentine’s loan when Mr. Valentine failed to honour their joint obligation to pay current and accrued interest charges, and it was  more than generous in accepting Mr. Valentine’s  undertaking to make good the default. In the circumstances, and following Mr. Valentine’s  failure to keep his promise, the Bank can scarcely be faulted for exercising its contractual right on March 5, 1991 to serve its Demand.

            The Nature of the Claim and the Evidentiary Burden 

[83]          While paying lip service to the association of the Valentines and the Bank as debtor and creditor, the trial judge and this court have treated the role of the Bank as paternal and required of it a degree of accountability more appropriate to a fiduciary relationship.  Consequently, the court has ignored the obvious shortcomings in the performance of the Valentines under the line of credit agreement in favour of a critical analysis of the role of the Bank.  In the result, these proceedings have been conducted as if the Valentines were the defendants in proceedings instituted by the Bank to recover the debt incurred by its customers.  In point of fact, the action commenced on November 16, 1992 was by Mrs. Valentine alone for damages of $150,000 arising out of the failure of the Bank to inform her of clause 7(g) of the insurance agreement restricting Mutual Life’s exposure to loans that were not in default.  The rest of the claims are a series of non sequiturs.  She asked alternatively for a declaration that the debt owed by her as joint obligee pursuant to a line of credit agreement has been discharged, an interlocutory and permanent injunction restraining the Bank from enforcing the terms of the said agreement, a declaration that the collateral mortgage is unenforceable, an interlocutory and permanent injunction restraining the Bank from proceeding with power of sale proceedings, and an order directing the Bank to discharge the said mortgage and interest and costs.

[84]          The much-amended Statement of Claim alleges liability for damages on the part of the Bank for failure to notify the Valentines that it “intended to demand that the Line of Credit be repaid in full”.  The balance of paragraph 23A alleges:

As a consequence of the Bank’s demand by its letter dated March 5, 1991, the benefits under the Insurance Policy were automatically terminated without giving the plaintiff and Peter Valentine an adequate opportunity to prevent such a termination.  As a result of the Bank’s breach, the plaintiff suffered damages, including the benefits under the Insurance Policy which would have extinguished any amounts owing to the Bank under the Line of Credit.

[85]          Under the rules of pleading, it is the responsibility of the plaintiff to prove what she alleges.  Mrs. Valentine has failed to meet this burden.  In her factum, she points out that she is a working teacher and could have brought the loan into good standing if she had known that default entailed, beyond the usual consequences of suit, foreclosure and sale, that the life insurance on her husband would terminate.  In fact, nobody testified as to this possible scenario at trial, certainly not Margaret Valentine. Had she done so it would be diametrically opposed to what she was trying to accomplish with the marriage contract.  She had made it clear in that document that the loan was her husband’s responsibility and she at no time put up any of her own money to keep the loan in good standing since its inception.  The notion that had she known the insurance policy was in jeopardy would hardly seem sufficient to have her change her conduct when she had every reason to believe that her husband would live indefinitely.  Put another way, her rescue plan for the matrimonial home certainly did not rely on her husband’s sudden death.

[86]          The action in negligence for damages must fail.  An essential element of the tort of negligence is that there must be damages flowing from the act or acts of negligence.  As it is often said, there is no such thing as “negligence in the air”.  In this case nothing flows from the failure, if failure there was, of the Bank to notify the Valentines that they had to keep the loan in good standing, a thing they had promised to do anyhow.  There is no direct evidence that either of the Valentines would have ensured that the loans remained in good standing and indeed, as I think I have conclusively demonstrated, the circumstantial evidence is all to the contrary.  As to the secondary position put forward by the majority of this court that the Bank had a duty to inform the Valentines that one of the consequences of the Demand of March was the termination of the life insurance on the line of credit, there was no evidence from Mutual Life or anybody that once terminated the insurance could be restored by any unilateral act of the Valentines. Again, nothing flows from the alleged negligence.

[87]          When looked at, as it should be, in isolation from the counter-claim made by the Bank, this action has to be dismissed.  And then, looking at the counter-claim in isolation, it is clear that the Bank is entitled to recover its debt with interest.  There is nothing to set off against it.

[88]          There really is only one issue in this appeal.  The rest are window dressing.  Did Mrs. Valentine prove on a balance of probabilities that she and her husband were unaware that this insurance policy covered their joint lives to the extent of their outstanding loans that were not in default and that had they known otherwise they would have ensured that their line of credit remained in good standing? The suggestion that save for the improper interest charges the line of credit would not have been in default and would have remained in good standing for the months remaining of Peter Valentine’s life holds no water.  The suggestion that the Bank deliberately overcharged interest to place the account into default has not been accepted by anybody. The submission that the bank, which had shown great patience in dealing with a bad account, was required in addition to its oral advice to remedy the default to serve a notice of intention to Demand as opposed to a Demand has no support in law or bank practice.

[89]          While the trial judge did not make express findings that Mr. and Mrs. Valentine did not know about the termination provisions of the insurance they had bought, it is implicit in her reasons and is central to her conclusion.  There is in fact no evidence whatsoever that Peter Valentine was not aware of this limitation and the trial judge’s reasons with respect to Mrs. Valentine are fatally flawed by her unsupportable finding that Mrs. Valentine did not have independent legal advice. She clearly did and in the absence of the testimony of Sillberg as to the extent of her involvement in these entire proceedings, the trial judge was not entitled to cast Mrs. Valentine in the role of the stereotypical housewife dependent on the good faith of the Bank officials for guidance and advice. 

[90]          Mrs. Valentine had access to legal advice, she and her husband sought the insurance, it was not a requirement of the Bank, on her testimony she did not choose to read the document, she is not clear on whether she took it home with her or not, she knew nothing of what her husband knew of or did with the document and she gave no evidence whatsoever of what significance such knowledge would have been to her had she acquired it. 

            The Nature of the Relief Granted

[91]          However, the fundamental error that besets this judgment and the reasons of the majority of this court is the nature of the relief that is granted to the wife and, without elaboration, to the husband’s estate.  I have already pointed out that on the plaintiff’s statement of claim, she must establish the tort of negligence. An award in negligence is stated in the form of damages causally connected to the negligence found by the court.  No damages are awarded here.  No attempt is made to quantify the loss and consider discounts for the likelihood that the line of credit would not be maintained in good standing until the death of Peter Valentine.  Instead the remedy is to dismiss the counterclaim of the Bank against both the plaintiff Margaret Valentine and the estate of her late husband which was added to the proceedings by the Bank as a defendant by counterclaim, to declare that all indebtedness of Mrs. Valentine and her husband’s estate under the line of credit is discharged and to make an order that the Bank discharge the collateral mortgage.  

[92]          The majority in this court avoid this problem in their reasoning (but not the result) by ignoring the insurance issue and finding that properly viewed the Demand constituted a breach of the line of credit agreement between the parties and therefore the Bank improperly terminated the insurance.  Accordingly it is not necessary to determine if the Bank was negligent with respect to the issue of the insurance and the whole basis of Margaret Valentine’s action for damages is irrelevant. 

[93]          The approach of the majority may have some appeal because of its simplicity but the basic premise that but for the illegal Demand the line of credit would have remained in good standing for many months ignores the reality that Peter Valentine, and in consequence his wife as obligor, were already in breach of the loan agreement. Further, it is premised upon an optimistic view of Peter Valentine’s fiscal responsibility that the record does not support and on some arithmetical calculations that in my opinion simply do not bear scrutiny.  Raised as the approach was by the court on its own motion and without satisfactory notice to counsel for the Bank, this disposition of the appeal gives rise to concerns of lack of procedural fairness in our court.  Moreover, the legal concept that a Bank can be denied recovery of all of its customer’s debt because of an error in the calculation of its interest rate by a few dollars (my figures) without any concern about the conduct of the customer under the loan agreement is truly startling and should not be decided at this level in this manner.

[94]          My own approach to this case is to allow the appeal.  The Bank was fully justified under the credit line agreement in serving the Demand.  The agreement clearly stated that it could be served at any time and the Valentines were in default under the agreement virtually from its inception.  The notion that the error in calculating the interest had any influence on the conduct of events is imaginative but unsupportable. I would have found as a trial judge that if Mrs. Valentine chose not to read the insurance policy that she asked for herself, she cannot now be heard to complain.  But I am not a trial judge and simply observe that no attempt was made in her action to prove any damages flowed from her lack of knowledge of the document’s contents.  There was no defence in law established to the counter-claim and it should be allowed in full.

III.  Disposition

[95]          For the reasons set out above, I would allow the appeal, set aside the judgment of the trial judge and order:

(a)   that the plaintiff’s action be dismissed with costs;

(b) that the counter-claim of the Bank be allowed and that judgment be entered on behalf of the Bank against the plaintiff and the estate of Peter Valentine providing for payment of the amount of the line of credit plus accrued interest to the date of the order and possession of the property described; and

(c)   that the Bank be awarded its costs of the appeal on a partial indemnity basis.
