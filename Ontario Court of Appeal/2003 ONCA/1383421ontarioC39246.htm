On appeal from the judgment of Justice Douglas Coo of the Superior Court of Justice, dated December 2, 2002, reported at [2002] O.J. No. 4722 (S.C.J.).

GILLESE J.A.:

[1] Ole Miss Place Inc. owns a building in Mississauga in which the respondent numbered company is a commercial tenant. The tenant wanted to renew the lease but when it sought to exercise the option to renew, Ole Miss Place Inc. refused. The tenant then applied for, and obtained, a declaration that it had validly exercised its right of renewal.

[2] Ole Miss Place Inc. appeals. It argues that the lease expressly provides that the tenant could renew only if, among other things, it was not in default at the time it sought to exercise its right of renewal. Ole Miss Place Inc. says that because the tenant was in default at the time it sought to renew, Ole Miss Place Inc. had the right to refuse the purported renewal.

[3] For the reasons that follow, I agree and would, therefore, allow the appeal.

BACKGROUND

[4] The appellant, Ole Miss Place Inc. ("the Landlord"), is the owner of 3050 Confederation Parkway in Mississauga ("the building"). The building is a mixed-use property with retail on the first floor, five floors of office space, and a residential unit on the top floor that is occupied by the Landlord's property manager, Anna Prokop. The building has approximately 70,000 square feet of rentable area and between twenty and twenty-five commercial tenants.

[5] In July 1993, the previous owner of the building leased unit 104 to Da Silva Contractors Inc. for a term of ten years from March 1, 1993 to February 28, 2003. The Landlord acquired the property in 1999. At that time, Da Silva was operating a bar on the premises and there were problems with drunken patrons. As a result, the Landlord gave notice of its intention to terminate Da Silva's lease.

[6] On November 26, 1999, the Landlord consented to Da Silva assigning its lease to the respondent, 1383421 Ontario Inc. ("the Tenant").

[7] Section 8:01 of the lease stipulates that the premises are to be used solely as a café/restaurant. Section 18:01 is an option to renew clause that permits the Tenant to renew the lease for an additional five years, provided that three conditions are met: (1) the Tenant is not in default under the lease; (2) the Tenant is in actual possession of the leased premises; and (3) the Tenant gives written notice of its intention to exercise the option to renew at least 180 days prior to the expiration of the lease.

[8] On August 1, 2002, the Tenant notified the Landlord by letter that it intended to exercise the option to renew. On August 15, 2002, the Landlord's solicitor wrote to the Tenant, informing it that the Landlord was not prepared to renew the lease because the Tenant was in breach of several of the lease provisions.

[9] The alleged breaches related to the Tenant operating its business as a bar and serving alcohol to its patrons until they became so intoxicated that they would vomit and urinate around the building, harass other occupants of the building, and create disturbances. The Landlord took the position that, as a result of the continued over-serving, the Tenant was in breach of sections 8:01, 8:02, 8:03 and 8:04 of the lease agreement and that the alleged breaches constituted acts of default. The Landlord purported to terminate the lease immediately. It gave the Tenant until September 3, 2002 to remove its belongings.

[10] The Landlord continued to accept rent after delivering the notice of intention to terminate the lease.

[11] The lease expired on February 28, 2003.

[12] The Tenant brought an application before the Superior Court for a declaration that it was entitled to exercise, and had validly exercised, its lease renewal. On December 2, 2002, the applications judge granted the Tenant's application and declared that it had validly renewed the lease on August 1, 2002.

THE ISSUES

[38] The central issue on this appeal is whether the applications judge erred in finding that the Tenant validly renewed the lease.

[39] A second issue is whether the Landlord's acceptance of rent payments, after delivery of the Notice of Termination, operates to bar the Landlord from refusing to renew the lease.

[40] Third, if it is determined that the Tenant did not validly renew the lease and that the Landlord was entitled to refuse to renew, the court must determine whether to exercise its jurisdiction and grant equitable relief in favour of the Tenant.

ISSUE #1 - Did the Tenant validly renew the lease?

[41] Section 18:01 of the Lease gives the Tenant the option to renew provided that the Tenant: (1) is not in default under the lease; (2) is in actual possession of the premises; and (3) has given written notice to the Landlord, at least 180 days prior to the expiration of the lease term, of its intention to exercise the option.

[42] The Landlord concedes that the Tenant met the latter two conditions but argues that the Tenant was in default on August 1, 2002, when it purported to renew the lease, and therefore could not validly exercise the option to renew.

[43] After the applications judge set out the Landlord's argument that continued over-serving of patrons by the Tenant and the resulting problems constituted default by the Tenant, he concluded:

I have decided that the tenant is entitled to the declaration which it seeks, in effect and in essence for the reasons set forth in the revised factum of the applicant. It seems to me that the way in which the landlord ought to have and may indeed in the immediate future ought to exercise its rights under the lease to remove the tenant is to take those steps which are envisaged by the lease itself and to give appropriate notices of continuity of breach and then to take steps to seek removal. I don't think that's what happened here, on the basis of the material which I have, which I find at least in part, more than modestly puzzling; and in this regard I make special reference to the cross-examination of Anna Prokop.

I have great sympathy for what may be the circumstances at least as they are perceived by the landlord. I don't think any final determination of the extent of "misconduct" on the part of the tenant's representatives can be made on the material before me. I am not however prepared to conclude that there has, on the balance of probabilities, been that outrageous reach of conduct upon which the landlord seeks to rely in this proceeding.

[44] In the Tenant's revised factum, to which the applications judge refers, the Tenant denied that it was in breach of the lease provisions and argued that (1) pursuant to section 8:02(b) of the Lease, the Landlord was obliged to give the Tenant notice of the alleged breach and sufficient notice had not been given and (2) the Landlord had waived its right to forfeiture of the lease by accepting rent after the notice of termination was delivered. Alternatively, the Tenant asked for relief from forfeiture.

[45] Setting aside the matters of forfeiture and relief from forfeiture, which are dealt with below as Issues 2 and 3, it appears that the applications judge found the Tenant not to be in breach because:

(1) the Landlord ought to have given notice of the breaches and taken steps to remove the Tenant;

(2) he could not make a final determination of the extent of the Tenant's misconduct on the material that was before him; and

(3) he could not conclude, on a balance of probabilities that the Tenant was guilty of that "outrageous reach of conduct" alleged by the Landlord.

[46] I will deal with each of these reasons in turn.

The Landlord's Giving of Notice

[47] The question for the applications judge was whether the Tenant was in default under the Lease at the time of renewal. In answering that question, it appears that the applications judge concluded that the Landlord had to give notice of default and/or take steps for removal before it could rely on the Tenant's default as a basis to refuse to permit the renewal.

[48] I begin by noting that nothing in the renewal clause requires the Landlord to give notice of default. This is by way of contrast to other lease provisions that expressly require that notice be given by the Landlord. For example, section 15:01(b) provides that the Landlord must give notice of certain types of default and provide the Tenant with an opportunity to rectify before exercising the right of re-entry.

[49] The Landlord's right, upon notice, to take steps to remove the Tenant or re-enter is quite distinct from the Tenant's right to exercise the option to renew. In the circumstances, the absence of a notice requirement in the renewal clause strongly suggests that notice is not required.

[50] While there appears to be no Canadian jurisprudence on point, there is strong English authority for the proposition that a landlord need not give notice of the breach in order to refuse renewal. In West Country Cleaners (Falmouth) Ltd. V. Saly [1966] 3 All E.R. 210 (C.A.), a tenant was found to be in breach of its obligations to paint the inside of leased premises once every three years or more often, as needed. The court held that as the tenant was in breach at the relevant date, it was not entitled to renew the lease. [1]

[51] I see no reason in law to impose such a notice requirement upon the Landlord particularly where, as in this case, the Landlord has repeatedly made the Tenant aware that it was in breach of the lease.

[52] Thus, with respect, the Landlord's actions (or lack thereof) in respect of notice and removal are not relevant to the question of whether the Tenant was in breach. The applications judge erred in relying on the Landlord's failure to give notice when determining whether the Tenant had validly renewed the lease.

Determination of Misconduct - (i) Burden of Proof

[53] When the applications judge says that he is unable to make "any final determination of the extent of [the Tenant's] misconduct", I understand him to have concluded that he could not decide whether the Tenant was in default. To so conclude, in the circumstances, is an error in law.

[54] Where, as in the instant case, performance of the lease covenants is a precondition to renewal, the onus rests on the tenant to show due performance. That is, the Tenant has the burden of proof to show that it was not in default. See Fingold v. Hunter, [1944] 3 D.L.R. 43 at 46 (Ont. C.A.), in which this court held that where "[d]ue performance of the lessee's covenants is a condition precedent....The burden is upon the lessee in such a case to show that he had performed the terms upon which his right depends."

[55] Since the onus to establish that there was no default rested on the Tenant, if the evidence was not sufficient to determine that matter, the Tenant failed to meet its onus and the application ought to have been dismissed on that basis alone.

Determination of Misconduct - (ii) Is the Tenant in Default?

[56] In addition, in my view, the application ought to have been dismissed on the basis that the Tenant was in default at the time of renewal.

[57] In determining whether the Tenant was in default, one must begin from the proposition that it is not necessary that the Tenant perform all lease terms perfectly throughout the course of the tenancy. As explained by Lord Kerr in Bass Holdings Ltd. v. Morton Music Ltd., [1987] 2 All E.R. 1001 at 1005-1006 (C.A.), what is required is that there be no subsisting breach on the operative date:

This brings me to the classification of breaches of covenants by reference to the time of their occurrence and subsistence, on which the present issues turn. As explained below, there is clearly a long-standing conveyancing practice, going back more than two centuries, whereby tenants' options in leases are made subject to provisos dealing with the observance of the tenants' covenants....In all such cases the provisos link the required observance of the covenants to a point in time in the nature of a terminus ad quem. This may be either the date of the exercise of the option as here, or the date of the expiry of the option, or, in cases of options for renewals or for the purchase of the freehold, the date of the expiry of the lease. For present purposes the particular date referred to in the proviso (the operative date) does not matter. What matters is whether the breach (or breaches) of covenant on which the landlord relies as precluding the exercise of the option has only occurred in the past, so that its effect is spent by the operative date in question, or whether there is still a breach (or at any rate a cause of action based on a breach, whether for forfeiture or damages or both) which subsists on the operative date. I will refer to the breaches of covenant in these two situations respectively as 'spent breaches' and 'subsisting breaches'.

…

The upshot of [the] authorities is that spent breaches will not destroy the tenant's right to exercise the option, but subsisting breaches will....[T]he reasoning is in effect as follows. Firstly, it must be accepted that absolute and precise compliance by the tenant with every single covenant throughout the period of the lease prior to the operative date is virtually impossible of attainment. If this were required as a condition precedent, then the option would in practice be worthless or merely at the mercy of the landlord. Therefore the parties cannot have intended that the absence of spent breaches should be a condition precedent. Secondly, however, it is natural and sensible that the landlord should require the tenant not to be in breach of any covenant on the operative date and that all outstanding claims for breach of covenant should have been previously satisfied, so that the lease is then effectively clear. The proviso is therefore to be construed as intended to apply to subsisting breaches, with the result that the relevant condition precedent is the absence of any subsisting breach.

…

But it must also be borne in mind that the condition requiring the absence of any subsisting breach on the operative date involves not only that any breach should have ceased, but also that there should be no subsisting cause of action in respect of any breach.

[58] In considering the question of whether there was a subsisting breach at the operative date, one must have regard to the doctrine of spent breach recognized by the Supreme Court of Canada in Sail Labrador Ltd. v. Challenge One (The), [1999] 1 S.C.R. 265. At paras. 68 - 69, Bastarache J. stated:

One example of the flexibility courts have applied to performance in option contexts is the doctrine of spent breach. This doctrine mitigates against the severity of the application of the strict performance rule to options by preventing deficiently performed conditions precedent which have been remedied by the time the option is to be exercised from rendering the option void. For example, in Bass Holdings Ltd. v. Morton Music Ltd., [1987] 2 W.L.R. 397 (Ch. D.), a lease contract included an option to renew provided the tenant was not in breach of any of the tenant's covenants contained in the lease. The tenant was at one time in breach of this condition because his rent was overdue. However, he had remedied that breach by the time he sought to exercise the option. In its interpretation of the contract, the court found that such "spent" breaches do not prevent the exercise of the option. The language here may be somewhat confusing. In reality, this doctrine simply provides that the option clause is interpreted to mean that conditions precedent are met providing that the positive covenants of the underlying contract have been performed at the time of the exercise of the option. There is, therefore, no breach, even though some payments were made late. Late payment can be remedied because this possibility is implied in the interpretation of the option clause.

The doctrine of spent breach has also been recognized by Canadian appeal courts. In Birchmont Furniture Ltd. v. Loewen (1978), 84 D.L.R. (3d) 599 (Man. C.A.), a lease contained an option to purchase "provided there be no default by the Tenant in any of the terms and conditions expressed or implied in the within Lease" (p. 599). The tenant had defaulted under the lease, but there were no outstanding defaults when the time came to exercise the option. The Manitoba Court of Appeal, per Hall J.A., confirmed the trial judge's interpretation of the option clause and allowed the tenant to exercise the option because the deficient performance of the lease had been remedied prior to the exercise of the option. See also Petrillo v. Nelson (1980), 114 D.L.R. (3d) 273 (Ont. C.A.).

[59] As well, in Jardine v. Attorney General for Newfoundland, [1932] A.C. 275 (P.C.) at 288, the court noted the importance of determining whether the relevant conditions were "broken once and for all, or were conditions of a continuing nature so that the licensee was from the moment of the original breach of a condition continuously committing a breach thereof."

[60] Moreover, if a tenant is in breach after the operative date but before the lease expires, the landlord is entitled to refuse to renew the lease. In Loveless v. Fitzgerald (1909), 42 S.C.R. 254, lessees gave proper written notice of their intention to extend the lease. Subsequently, the lessees breached a covenant in the lease not to assign the lease without leave. At the end of the original lease term, the lessor refused to grant the extension and brought an action for possession. The lessees submitted that since the breach occurred after notice had been given, it could not disentitle them to the lease extension. They submitted that the lessor could only rely on forfeiture and that forfeiture had been waived by the acceptance of two rent payments. The Supreme Court of Canada rejected the lessees' argument, holding that the lessees' right to extend was dependent upon the lessees fulfilling the covenants until the expiration of the original lease term. Since a covenant had been breached, the lessor was justified in refusing the extension.

[61] Accordingly, in order to determine whether the Tenant was in default, the following questions must be answered. (1) What is the "operative date" for renewal? And (2) Was there a subsisting breach as of the operative date? In answering this question, regard must be had to whether the breach was of a continuing nature. A final question must be asked in order to determine whether the Landlord can refuse to renew, namely, whether the Tenant was in breach after the operative date but before the lease expired.

The Operative Date

[62] The operative date is a matter of interpretation of the option to renew clause.

[63] In Advanced Car Specialties Ltd. v. Jakobsons (1982), 34 O.R. (2d) 630 (H.C.J.) the court was faced with determining the operative date for an option to renew. The lessee had a right to renew its lease if it had "well and truly performed its covenants and obligations under the lease" (at p. 631). At p. 635, Cromarty J. noted that it was common ground that the operative date must be one of: (a) the date the lessee must give notice to exercise its right to renew; (b) the date of the lessor's refusal to renew; or (c) the expiry date of the lease. On the facts of that case, it was unnecessary for the court to determine the operative date because the alleged breaches had been remedied prior to the earliest of the three dates.

[64] Applying the same logic to this case, the operative date would be one of: (a) August 1, 2002 - the date the Tenant was required to (and did give) notice that it was exercising its right to renew; (b) August 15, 2002 - the date the Landlord informed the Tenant that it was refusing renewal; or (c) February 28, 2003 - the expiry date of the current lease.

[65] In my view, although not spelled out in the option to renew clause, it is implicit that August 1, 2002 is the operative date. The renewal clause requires the Tenant to notify the Landlord 180 days prior to the expiry of the lease term (i.e. August 1, 2002) of its intention to renew the lease otherwise the right to renew is lost. In addition, the renewal clause provides for arbitration three months in advance of the lease expiry date if the parties are unable to agree on minimum rent. It would make little sense for the parties to arbitrate prior to the operative date. I note that, to the extent the parties turned their minds to this matter, it appears that they proceeded on the basis that the notice date of August 1, 2002, was the operative date.

Subsisting Breach

[66] The evidence on the record overwhelmingly supports the conclusion that the Tenant is regularly serving patrons after they are drunk. The Tenant continues to run its business in that fashion despite numerous warnings, in writing and orally.

[67] Where, as in this case, the breach is continued over-serving, it is no answer for the Tenant to say that it cleans up the vomit or urine or replaces windows broken by its patrons. The breach is the continued over-serving rather than the situations that result from over-serving. In that sense, the Tenant is continuously committing a breach.

[68] The Landlord may reasonably deem the continued over-serving of patrons to be a nuisance or annoyance within the meaning of section 8:03(i) as it interferes with the enjoyment of the building by the other tenants and their customers and harms the business and reputation of the Landlord.

[69] The continued over-serving of patrons and the resulting activities of those patrons in terms of urinating, vomiting and fighting in and around the Building is also a breach of the Tenant's covenant in section 8:02(a) to conduct its business in a manner that is consistent with the best interests of the Building as a whole.

[70] It may well be that the Tenant is also in breach of the covenants in sections 8:01 and 8:04(a) but that need not be decided in light of finding the Tenant to be in breach of two other covenants in the lease.

[71] I have no difficulty in concluding, on the record, that there was a subsisting breach on the operative date of August 1, 2002 due to the manner in which the Tenant was running its business, that the Tenant was in default as a consequence and that the Landlord was justified in refusing to renew the lease.

[72] The record also supports a finding that the breach continued after the operative date but before the lease expired. On the basis of Loveless v. Fitzgerald, supra, the Landlord is entitled to refuse to renew the lease on that ground as well.

Outrageous Conduct

[73] The applications judge stated, as a further reason for granting the Tenant's application, that he was unwilling to find "that outrageous reach of conduct upon which the landlord seeks to rely". This statement appears to be a response to the Landlord's argument that very offensive conduct can amount to a breach at common law even if it is not a breach of the lease. In light of my conclusion that the Tenant was in default under the terms of the Lease, there is no need to decide whether such a proposition is sound in law.

ISSUE #2 - Is the Landlord barred from refusing to renew the lease?

[74] The Tenant argues that the Landlord's acceptance of rent after delivery of a notice of termination extinguishes the Landlord's right to forfeiture and serves to bar the Landlord from refusing to renew the lease on the basis of breaches by the Tenant. With respect, it seems to me that such arguments are misguided.

[75] A right of forfeiture may arise when a tenant breaches a condition or covenant in a lease. The breach will give rise to a right of forfeiture if the lease grants the landlord a right of re-entry upon breach of that condition or covenant. In such a case, if the landlord, with full knowledge of the breach, subsequently accepts rent payments, the landlord will be deemed to have waived its right to forfeiture. See Jardine, supra, and Lippman v. Lee Yick, [1953] O.R. 514 (H.C.J.).

[76] Acceptance of rent by the Landlord after the known breaches is relevant to the Landlord's right of forfeiture. However, forfeiture relates to the right to terminate an existing lease term, not the option to create a new lease term. The matter in issue on this appeal is not whether the Landlord waived its right of forfeiture but whether the Tenant validly exercised the option to renew the lease.

[77] The argument that acceptance of rent amounts to a waiver of the Tenant's breaches, such that the Landlord is barred from raising the breach in the context of lease renewal, is not legally sound. In Jardine, the Privy Council stated that acceptance of rent payments with knowledge of a breach waives the right to forfeiture; it does not waive the covenant or condition itself. The court stated, at p. 289: "In the case of a forfeiture clause, acceptance of rent after a known breach of a covenant or condition is not waiver of the covenant or condition or some part thereof, but waiver of the right to forfeit which has arisen from the breach".

[78] In any event, the Landlord served a notice of immediate termination of the lease as a result of defaults by the Tenant. As this court held in ARC v. Delia Catering Inc. (1997), 14 R.P.R. (3d) 275 (C.A.), where notice of termination is immediate, the subsequent acceptance of rent does not constitute a waiver by the landlord.

[79] Finally, when, as in the instant case, the breach is properly characterized as a continuing breach, acceptance of rent with knowledge of the breach does not constitute waiver of the right to forfeiture. See Jardine, supra, at pp. 287-288.

ISSUE #3 - Should the court grant relief for failure to renew the Lease?

[80] The court's equitable jurisdiction to grant the Tenant relief from a failure to renew the lease properly is limited. One condition necessary for the jurisdiction to be exercised is that "the tenant has made diligent efforts to comply with the terms of the lease which are unavailing through no default of his or her own". See Ross v. T. Eaton Co. (1992), 11 O.R. (3d) 115 at 125 (C.A.) and 120 Adelaide Lease Holds Inc. v. Oxford Properties Canada Ltd. [1993] O.J. No. 2801 at para. 9 (C.A.).

[81] The Tenant was in default under the terms of the lease as a result of the Tenant's deliberate acts. It does not satisfy the condition. In the circumstances, the Tenant has no right to equitable relief.

DISPOSITION

[82] Accordingly, the appeal is allowed with costs of the application and the appeal to the Landlord. Costs of the appeal are fixed in the amount of $6,000 plus disbursements and GST.
