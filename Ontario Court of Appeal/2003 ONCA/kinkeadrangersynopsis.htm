DATE: September 12, 2003
COA FILE NO.: C31117 and C34162

Synopsis of R. v. Rohan Ranger and R. v. Adrian Kinkead

Overview

Two sisters, Marsha and Tamara Ottey, were stabbed to death in their home in Scarborough in the morning of August 16, 1995. Marsha Ottey's former boyfriend, Rohan Ranger ("Ranger") and Adrian Kinkead ("Kinkead"), who is Ranger's cousin, were charged with the murders of Marsha and Tamara Ottey.

Following a jury trial, Ranger was convicted of first-degree murder in relation to Marsha Ottey and of manslaughter in relation to Tamara Ottey. Kinkead was tried by a separate judge and jury and was convicted of the first-degree murders of Marsha and Tamara Ottey. Both men appealed their convictions.

A panel of the Court of Appeal for Ontario, composed of Justices Charron, Feldman and Simmons, heard these two appeals sequentially from March 31 to April 2, 2003. In a judgment released today, the panel unanimously allowed Ranger's appeal on the basis that the cumulative effect of the errors committed during Ranger's trial by the trial judge rendered the trial unfair and requires that Ranger be retried. In a separate judgment released today, the panel unanimously dismissed Kinkead's appeal.

R. v. Rohan Ranger

Ranger was charged with two counts of first-degree murder. He was tried by a jury, with Justice Ewaschuk presiding. In June 1998, the jury found Ranger guilty of the first-degree murder of Marsha Ottey and of manslaughter of Tamara Ottey. The trial judge sentenced Ranger to life imprisonment on the murder conviction and 15 years concurrent imprisonment on the manslaughter conviction.

Ranger appealed his convictions to the Court of Appeal. In his grounds of appeal, Ranger alleged that the trial against him was unfair as a result of various errors committed by the trial judge regarding the admission of evidence as well as in the trial judge's final charge to the jury. Justice Charron wrote the Court's reasons for judgment. The grounds of appeal that form the basis for ordering the new trial and the reasons for judgment are briefly summarized below. The court concluded that the cumulative effect of the errors requires a new trial.

Admission of evidence at trial

(i)

Ranger argued that the trial judge erred in permitting a Crown witness to give expert opinion evidence that the crime scene appeared to have been staged to look as if there had been a burglary, and to testify about the type of person likely to have committed the crime (namely, that the perpetrator had staged the crime scene to divert attention from himself and had a particular interest in Marsha Ottey).

The court found no reason to interfere with the trial judge's decision to allow the expert opinion testimony that the crime scene was staged. The court concluded, however, that the trial judge erred in allowing the police expert to testify about the perpetrator's likely motivation for staging the crime scene and his characteristics as a person associated with the victims and having a particular interest in Marsha Ottey. The court characterized this latter evidence as criminal profiling evidence. Criminal profiling is a novel field of scientific evidence, the reliability of which was not demonstrated at trial. To the contrary, it would appear from the witness' limited testimony about the available verification of opinions in her field of work that her expert opinion amounted to no more than educated guesses. Further, the criminal profiling evidence approached the ultimate issue that the jury had to decide, and, hence, was highly prejudicial. This prejudice was further heightened by the limits placed on defence counsel's cross-examination of the expert witness and by the prominence that the trial judge gave to her evidence in his charge
   
(ii)

Ranger argued that the trial judge erred in admitting evidence of his trip to Jamaica in January 1996 and the circumstances of his return to Canada.

The trial judge permitted the Crown to introduce evidence about Ranger's trip to Jamaica and his overstay as evidence of flight from which the jury could infer consciousness of guilt. The trial judge also permitted the Crown to introduce evidence about Ranger's lawyer's discussions with the police and about the hostile climate of the populace in Jamaica to Ranger. The trial judge later reversed his ruling and ultimately told the jury to disregard the Jamaica evidence on the issue of consciousness of guilt.

The court concluded that the Jamaica evidence was of no probative value and highly prejudicial to Ranger. Based on the trial record, and as the trial judge later ruled, the evidence could not reasonably support an inference that Ranger, months after the event, had fled the jurisdiction. It therefore should not have been admitted for that purpose. The court also concluded that the jury might have inferred guilt from the highly speculative evidence of Ranger's lawyer's discussions with the police, particularly the suggestion that Ranger's father had contacted the lawyer immediately after Kinkead's arrest because, as the Crown put it, "everybody seems to know of a connection between [Ranger] and Adrian Kinkead in terms of the murders of these two young women." The court also concluded that the evidence of the public animosity towards Ranger in Jamaica was highly inflammatory and suggested that anyone who knew anything about Ranger knew that he was responsible for the deaths of the Ottey sisters.

The trial judge erred by permitting the Jamaica evidence to be called in the first place, and having admitted it in, he erred in his instructions to the jury on what use they could make of it. Although he did instruct the jury to disregard the Jamaica evidence in regard to consciousness of guilt at one point, he left it for their consideration in other parts of his instructions and he failed to instruct the jury that they should not consider this evidence for any purpose.

Trial judge's instructions to the jury

(i)

Ranger argued that the trial judge erred in his charge to the jury regarding the Crown's mischaracterization of the evidence that he was at the Scarborough Town Centre very shortly after the killings as a "false alibi".

Ranger and a friend were captured on a security video camera at the Scarborough Town Centre at 8:08 a.m. on August 16, 1995. Taken at its highest, this would have left Ranger with between 16 and 26 minutes to enter the Ottey house, stab each sister numerous times, ransack the house, clean himself up, drive to the Scarborough Town Centre, park, and go to the area where the camera was located. Ranger relied on this evidence as the centrepiece of his defence, taking the position at trial that it established an exceptionally small, if not non-existent window of opportunity for him to have killed or helped to kill Marsha and Tamara Ottey. In his closing address to the jury, the Crown relied extensively on the Scarborough Town Centre evidence as evidence of a false alibi from which the jury could infer Ranger's guilt.

The Crown on appeal did not contend that there was any evidence to support the suggestion that Ranger had deliberately tried to get himself filmed in order to create a false alibi. The trial judge told the jury to disregard the Crown's reference to a false alibi because Ranger was not relying on the defence of alibi. However, the trial judge did not tell the jury that the reference should be disregarded because there was no evidence to support it. Further, in reading the instructions as a whole, the court concluded that the jury could well have understood that they were entitled to consider the alleged fabrication of an alibi in their deliberations. The court agreed with Ranger's submissions that the Scarborough Town Centre evidence provided a basis on which the jury could conclude that Ranger did not have a reasonable opportunity to kill the Ottey sisters. The court therefore concluded that the exculpatory value of this evidence was significantly undermined by the Crown's unfounded speculation of false alibi and that the trial judge's instructions failed to remove the prejudice created by the Crown's false alibi submission to the jury.

   
(ii)

Ranger argued that the trial judge erred by charging the jury that he could be found guilty even he was not present at the scene when Marsha and Tamara Ottey were killed.

The Crown's theory at trial was that Ranger was present when Marsha and Tamara Ottey were killed. The trial judge's instructions to the jury left it open to them to find Ranger guilty, even if they did not accept that he was present at the time of the killings, if they were satisfied that he somehow aided or abetted Kinkead in killing Marsha and Tamara Ottey.

The court concluded that, in the particular circumstances of this trial, it was reasonable for defence counsel to have understood from the various discussions that preceded his closing jury address that the trial judge would not charge the jury on this basis. Independent of any conclusions as to whether the trial judge's instructions were correct in law and whether they were supported by the evidence, in the context of this case, these instructions effectively undermined the essence of the defence's position, that is, that Ranger should be acquitted because the Crown failed to prove that he was at the Ottey home in the early morning of August 16, 1995. In these circumstances, it could not be said that Ranger was not materially prejudiced by the trial judge's failure to notify counsel of his intention to charge the jury on this additional theory of liability before counsel's final submissions to the jury.

   
(iii)

Ranger argued that the trial judge erred in his charge to the jury regarding the identification evidence.

The Crown called five witnesses who testified about having seen a man in a walkway across the street from the Ottey home around 7:30 a.m. on the days immediately preceding and on the morning that Marsha and Tamara Ottey were killed. Two of those witnesses gave evidence directly implicating Ranger as the person seen in the walkway.

The court observed that it would be a critical finding against Ranger if the jury was satisfied beyond a reasonable doubt that he was in the walkway across from the Ottey home, particularly on the morning of August 16. In these circumstances, it was essential that the trial judge instruct the jury properly in connection with the identification evidence, most particularly in connection with the evidence of the two witnesses who directly implicated Ranger. However, the trial judge's instructions, when considered as a whole and in the light of the Crown's closing submissions, did not effectively convey to the jury the dangers of relying on the evidence of the walkway witnesses. Rather, these instructions may have left the jury with the impression that they could place greater weight on this evidence than it reasonably deserved. First, the evidence of the two witnesses who purported to identify Ranger had little, or no, probative value. One witness had not only been unable to pick Ranger from a photo line-up shortly after the events, but had picked out another person as the person he had seen in the walkway. The other witness' identification of Ranger in a photo line-up was equivocal and at trial, non-existent. Second, the trial judge failed to instruct the jury that a witness' failure to identify Ranger at trial was entitled to be given some weight. Third, the trial judge failed to correct a misstatement of the evidence by the Crown regarding a critical part of a witness' description of the person in the walkway. Rather, the trial judge repeated this error in his charge.

R. v. Adrian Kinkead

Kinkead was charged with two counts of first-degree murder. He was tried by a jury, with Justice LaForme presiding. In June 1999, the jury found Kinkead guilty of both counts of first-degree murder. Justice LaForme sentenced Kinkead to life imprisonment, without parole eligibility for 25 years.

Kinkead appealed his murder convictions to the Court of Appeal, claiming that he was denied the right to a fair trial because of the errors made by the trial judge. Justice Simmons wrote the Court's reasons for judgment dismissing Kinkead's appeal. Kinkead's grounds of appeal, and the reasons for dismissing these grounds are briefly summarized below.

(i)

Kinkead argued that the trial judge failed to cure the prejudicial effect of the mid-trial publicity relating to his previous murder conviction.

A few days after the start of Kinkead's trial, there were media reports about the issuance of an arrest warrant for Kinkead's former lawyer in connection with an unrelated matter. Some of the reports mentioned that the lawyer had acted for Kinkead in connection with his prior murder conviction for the slaying of a TTC employee. As a result of these media reports, Kinkead asked the trial judge to declare a mistrial. After hearing submissions from counsel and questioning the jury, the trial judge concluded that the jury would be able to set aside anything that they may have heard and denied Kinkead's request for a mistrial.

The courtconcluded that the trial judge properly dealt with the mid-trial publicity. The court rejected Kinkead's submissions that the trial judge had prejudged the mistrial application, had failed to address the proper issue in assessing whether to grant a mistrial, and had failed to properly instruct the jury in regard to the media reports. To the contrary, the court found no basis for finding that the trial judge had prejudged the issue. Further, the court noted that the trial judge's conclusion as set out in his ruling on the mistrial application was that he was satisfied that the jurors could set aside anything they may have heard, which was the precise issue that he was required to determine. Last, the court held that the instruction that the trial judge gave to the jury was appropriate in the circumstances, where the trial judge had to walk a fine line between cautioning those who had heard the reports, and not drawing unnecessary attention to the problem, for those who had not.

   
(ii)

Kinkead argued that the trial judge erred by admitting evidence that Kinkead had, in the past, carried a knife, and that, on one occasion, in Ranger's presence, Kinkead had threatened an acquaintance with a knife. He also alleged that the trial judge exacerbated this error by failing to give the jury proper instructions concerning the use of this evidence.

The court held that the trial judge properly admitted this evidence and gave the jury appropriate instructions regarding the use that the jury could make of this evidence. The court agreed with the trial judge that this evidence was relevant to two live issues at trial: possession of the murder weapon and possible motive for Kinkead's presence at the Ottey home. In the event the jury found that Kinkead was present in the Ottey home at the time of the murders, evidence that he had a habit of carrying a knife similar to the murder weapon had some probative value on the issue of whether he possessed or supplied the murder weapon. Similarly, the fact that Ranger was present when Kinkead used a knife to threaten another person adds some credence to the likelihood that Ranger recruited Kinkead to assist in the killings and, therefore, to the likelihood that, if Kinkead was present at the murder scene, he was not there for an innocent purpose.

The court rejected Kinkead's argument that this evidence carried significant potential prejudice. The risk of "reasoning prejudice" was minimal: the evidence was straightforward and unlikely to confuse the jury or consume an inordinate amount of time. The risk of moral prejudice ("the potential stigma of bad personhood") was also limited. As noted by the trial judge, none of the impugned evidence was "highly discreditable" in the context of a murder trial. There was little risk that the jury would convict Kinkead to punish him for having carried a knife or having once threatened someone. Nor is it likely that the jury would have used the evidence that Kinkead carried a knife or once threatened someone to conclude that he was the type of person who would commit a planned and deliberate murder.

   
(iii)

Kinkead argued that the trial judge erred by ruling that the Crown could use Kinkead's compelled testimony at Ranger's trial for purposes of cross-examination.

The court dismissed this ground of appeal. The court concluded that Kinkead's prior testimony did not incriminate him at the time it was given and therefore, this evidence was not subject to the restrictions that would apply if it had been incriminating at the time it was given. The court also observed that a decision about whether an accused person can be cross-examined on prior compelled testimony that was not incriminating at the time it was given must be based on the testimony of the accused person at the subsequent trial, or, at the very least, on his anticipated testimony. In his case, Kinkead sought the ruling before deciding whether to testify and he did not provide the trial judge or the Court of Appeal with any indication about what his anticipated testimony might be.

   
(iv)

Kinkead argued that the trial judge erred by failing to instruct the jury that certain evidence relating to post-offence conduct had no probative value as evidence of consciousness of guilt and by failing to instruct the jury how this evidence was relevant to the issue of planning and deliberation.

Three pieces of trial evidence were relevant to this ground of appeal: first, evidence that Kinkead's association with Ranger came to a complete halt after the killings until October 25, 1995 when the two men were observed meeting at a Scarborough park where they spent time in a forested area and were later seen reading a road atlas; second, evidence that Kinkead's former girlfriend had driven Kinkead to the park, going past Ranger's house without going in; and third, evidence that Kinkead and his former girlfriend were in Florida together at the end of 1995, where Kinkead and Ranger met on one occasion.

The court concluded that the trial judge's failure to give the jury a "no probative value" instruction was at least neutral, and may have inured to Kinkead's benefit, given that the evidence of the hiatus in contact between Ranger and Kinkead after the day of the killings and the evidence that Kinkead and his former girlfriend drove by Ranger's house before the two men met in the park (suggesting a clandestine element to the meeting) could have used by the jury to draw an inference of consciousness of guilt. Further, the court was of the view that it was unlikely that the jury would have used the evidence of Kinkead going to Florida, standing alone, to draw an inference of consciousness of guilt based on post-offence flight. There was no evidence or suggestion that Kinkead left the jurisdiction until at least two months after the Ottey killings. Similarly, there was no evidence that the police were investigating Kinkead for the Ottey murders at the time he left for Florida or that there was any basis for him to be concerned about that possibility. Finally, neither the Crown nor the trial judge said anything to suggest that the jury could rely on the evidence that Kinkead went to Florida as evidence of flight. To the contrary, the more likely use of that evidence was that suggested by the trial judge, namely, that the hiatus in contact, the October 25 meeting in the park, and the subsequent rendezvous in Florida were part of an original plan and constituted evidence of planning and deliberation.

In the reasons for judgment in Kinkead's appeal, the court noted that expert opinion evidence regarding the staging of the crime scene and the potential perpetrator was given at Kinkead's trial. The admission of this evidence at Ranger's trial is one of the bases on which this Court ordered a new trial for Ranger. The court determined that it was unnecessary to address this expert opinion evidence in the context of Kinkead's appeal for three reasons. First, this expert opinion evidence incriminated Ranger, rather than Kinkead. Second, Kinkead admitted at his trial that Ranger had been convicted of killing Marsha and Tamara Ottey, and therefore, evidence that incriminated Ranger (as the criminal staging and profiling evidence did) was not inconsistent with Kinkead's position at his trial. Third, Kinkead did not object to this expert opinion evidence at trial nor rely on it as a ground of appeal.
