Synopsis of The Ontario Judges' Association, The Ontario Family Law Judges' Association and the Ontario Provincial Court (Civil Division) Judges' Association v. The Majesty the Queen in Right of the Province of Ontario, as represented by the Chair of the Management Board

On September 16 th and 17 th , 2003, a panel of the Court of Appeal for Ontario, composed of Associate Chief Justice Dennis O'Connor and Justices Stephen Borins and James MacPherson, heard appeals from a decision of Ontario's Divisional Court dated February 15, 2002. In that decision, Superior Court Justices John O'Driscoll, Edward Then and Tamarin Dunnet dismissed an application for judicial review by The Ontario Judges' Association, The Ontario Family Law Judges' Association and the Ontario Provincial Court (Civil Division) Judges' Association (“the judges' associations”). The judges' associations sought judicial review of a decision by Her Majesty the Queen in Right of the Province of Ontario, as represented by the Chair of the Management Board (“the Government”) not to accept the recommendations of the Fourth Triennial Provincial Judges' Remuneration Commission (“the Commission”) relating to pensions for provincial judges.

Background

The judges' associations represented the justices of the Ontario Court of Justice before tthe Commission. The Commission was convened to make recommendations relating to judicial compensation for the 1998-2000 period. The Commission's jurisdiction and terms of reference were governed by the Courts of Justice Act and the Framework Agreement attached as a schedule to that Act. The Commission's recommendations relating to judicial salaries, benefits and allowances were binding on the Government. Its recommendations relating to judicial pensions were not binding.

The Commission conducted hearings and received several written submissions from the judges' associations and the Government in late 1998 and early 1999. On May 20, 1999, the Commission released its report. In regard to salary, the Commission recommended a staged increase from the then base salary of $130,810 to $150,000 in 1998, $160,000 in 1999 and $170,000 in 2000. This represented a 28 per cent salary increase. Since provincial judges' pensions are linked with the current salaries of provincial judges, this salary increase also increased provincial judges' pensions.

The Commission also recommended significant improvements to the provincial judges' pension scheme. At the time, provincial judges were entitled to a base pension of 45 per cent of salary at 65 years of age and after 15 years of service. The Commission offered three different options for improving the provincial judges' pension scheme, and recommended that the Government implement one of the three options. The Commission also recommended that the Government reform its treatment of provincial judges who retired before age 65.

The Government implemented the Commission's recommendations relating to salary, which were binding on the Government. The Government engaged PricewaterhouseCoopers LLP (“PwC”) to review the Commission's pension recommendations. In particular, PwC was engaged to determine the cost of the recommended pension options in light of the salary increases implemented by the Government.

On February 1, 2000, the Government indicated in a letter to the Chair of the Commission that it had decided not to implement any of the recommendations of the Commission concerning pensions. The letter included seven reasons for this decision.

On August 10, 2000, t he judges' associations sought judicial review of the Government's decision not to accept the Commission's recommendations relating to pensions. They sought an order quashing and setting aside the Government's decision and an order directing the Government to implement the Commission's recommendations relating to judges' pensions. In response to this application for judicial review, the Government filed two affidavits by Owen M. O'Neil, the current senior team leader at PwC on the judges' pension file, detailing PwC's work for the Government.

The Divisional Court dismissed the application for judicial review. The Divisional Court found that (1) the Government's engagement of the actuarial firm following receipt of the Commission's recommendations did not render the commission process ineffective, nor did it run contrary to the Framework Agreement governing the commission process; (2) the O'Neil affidavits were admissible; and (3) the Government's reasons for rejecting the Commission's pension recommendations complied with the 'simple rationality' test established by the Supreme Court in the P.E.I. Reference , [1997] 3 S.C.R. 3. The judges' associations appealed.

Decision of the Court of Appeal

The Court of Appeal unanimously affirmed the decision of the Divisional Court and dismissed the judges' associations' appeal.

The Court considered the following issues:

  1. Did the Divisional Court err by concluding that the Government's engagement of PwC after the Commission delivered its report was constitutional and complied with the Framework Agreement?
  2. Did the Divisional Court err by admitting the two O'Neil affidavits tendered by the Government in the judicial review proceedings?
  3. Did the Divisional Court err by concluding that the Government's reasons for not accepting the Commission's recommendations relating to judges' pensions met the 'simple rationality' test from the P.E.I. Reference ?

The Court began its reasons by noting that judicial independence is a constitutional principle. This principle imposes constitutional requirements on the process by which judicial compensation is set. In the P.E.I. Reference , the Supreme Court held that in order to ensure judicial independence, judicial compensation must be determined through an independent commission process. Once a commission has made its report, the government must consider that report. The government is not bound to accept the commission's recommendations; however, the recommendations “should not be set aside lightly”.

On the first issue, the judges' associations argued that the constitutional imperative that the commission process be effective required the Government to put all of the information and arguments which supported its position before the Commission. The Court found that (1) a government considering commission recommendations must determine the costs of those recommendations (if the costs are unknown) and (2) a government may seek external advice from experts to determine those costs. The Commission did not determine the costs of the three pension models that it recommended when those models were linked with the judges' new salaries. The judges' associations also argued that if the Government sought additional information regarding the costs of the pension recommendations, it was obligated to return to the commission process so that the Commission could determine those costs. The Court found that while the Framework Agreement permitted the Government to return to the commission process for this reason, the Government was not obligated to do so.

On the second issue, the Court explained that pursuant to the P.E.I. Reference , if a government rejects the recommendations of a commission, it must be prepared to justify that decision, “if need be, in a court of law”. Once the Government's decision was challenged in a court of law, it was entirely appropriate for the Government to place before the reviewing court both the fact of its engagement of PwC and the role PwC's advice played in its decision-making process. Hence the Divisional Court did not err by admitting the two O'Neil affidavits.

On the third issue, the Court found that any judicial review of a government's final decision with respect to judicial compensation should be a cautious and modest review. In the P.E.I. Reference , the Supreme Court stated that the standard of review in this context was one of 'simple rationality'. The Supreme Court cautioned that a reviewing court applying this standard “does not engage in a searching analysis of the relationship between ends and means”. The purpose of the 'simple rationality' test is to screen out government decisions that are based on purely political or discriminatory reasons and to review the reasonableness of the factual foundation underlying a government's decision. The Court agreed with the Divisional Court that the Government's reasons were “clear, logical, relevant and consistent with the Government's position taken before the Commission.” It was not irrational for the Government to respond to the Commission, in effect, that a 28 per cent increase in judges' salary and pension was enough at that time in light of economic conditions in Ontario and the Government's treatment of other people who received their salaries and wages from the public purse. The Court therefore dismissed the appeal.
