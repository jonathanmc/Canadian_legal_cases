On appeal from conviction for second degree murder by Justice William Morrison of the Superior Court of Justice, and a jury, on December 17, 1997 and from the sentence of life imprisonment with no eligibility for parole for 14 years imposed on December 19, 1997.

MOLDAVER J.A.:

[1] On December 17, 1997, after a 56-day trial before Justice W. A. Morrison and a jury, the appellant was convicted of second degree murder. On December 19, 1997, he was sentenced to life imprisonment and his period of parole ineligibility was increased from ten to fourteen years.

[2] The appellant appeals from both conviction and sentence. If successful on his conviction appeal, he seeks a new trial; if unsuccessful, he seeks a reduction in the period of his parole ineligibility. For reasons that follow, I would dismiss the appeal in its entirety.

OVERVIEW

[3] The appellant was charged on July 10, 1995 with the murder of his wife, Kamlesh. Three days earlier, on July 7, Kamlesh was strangled to death. Her lifeless body was discovered on the night of the 7th at around 11:30 p.m. by a passing motorist. She had been dumped at the side of a rural road north of Milton. It was about a half-hour drive from that location to her home in Brampton.

[4] Four hours earlier, at 7:30 p.m., Kamlesh and the appellant were at home alone. After that, until the discovery of her body, Kamlesh was not heard from or seen by anyone other than the appellant.

[5] The sole issue at trial was the identity of Kamlesh's killer. The appellant did not testify, but in a pre-arrest videotaped statement introduced by the Crown, he denied killing his wife. In support of his denial, he relied on the evidence of family members and others who attested to the soundness of his marriage and the lack of any apparent motive on his part to kill Kamlesh. He also relied on the evidence of an independent witness who claimed that at 8:30 p.m. on the night of July 7, when the appellant was definitely at home in Brampton, she saw the Sodhi family Chrysler Concorde being driven by someone on a street in Milton. If reliable, her evidence created a serious problem for the Crown since it was the Crown's position that the appellant killed Kamlesh in the family home sometime between 7:30 p.m. and 8:30 p.m. and then used the Chrysler Concorde to dispose of her body.

[6] Against the evidence favouring the appellant, the Crown adduced a massive body of circumstantial evidence designed to show that the appellant was indeed the killer, that he killed Kamlesh in the family home, and that after disposing of her body, he took steps to cover up his crime by making it appear as if she had been robbed and murdered by a stranger.

[7] As will become apparent when I review the salient features of the evidence, the circumstantial case implicating the appellant as the killer was virtually insurmountable. That fact was not lost on the appellant. It explains why, in the last analysis, he was forced to rely on the fanciful theory that after Kamlesh was murdered, the real killer broke into his home and his white Ford van and planted incriminating evidence in an effort to frame him for a crime he did not commit.

[8] My characterization of that theory as fanciful foreshadows the outcome of this appeal. As will be seen, it allows for the safe application of the curative proviso in s. 686(1)(b)(iii) of the Criminal Code, R.S.C. 1985, c. C-46 to overcome the one error of law that, in my view, the appellant has made out on this record. I will address that error and the facts surrounding it in due course. It involves the improper admission of statements made by the appellant to the police on July 18, eight days after his arrest. For now, I propose to review the facts that are germane to the central issue in this case, namely, whether the appellant killed Kamlesh in the family home or whether she was killed by a stranger outside the home.

THE FACTS

[9] The appellant's wife was strangled to death some time after 7:30 p.m. on Friday, July 7, 1995. That night, at around 11:30 p.m., a passing motorist discovered her lifeless body in a ditch alongside a rural road north of Milton. Forensic evidence established that she had not been killed at the place where her body was found. The vegetation surrounding her body was undisturbed and her feet, though bare, were clean. Forensic testing also revealed that Kamlesh had not been sexually assaulted and apart from a ligature mark that encircled her neck, no other injuries were detected. A pinkish-orange substance, presumably vomit, was dripping from her nose and mouth. Most of the jewellery that she had been wearing was missing. All that remained was a silver bracelet of religious significance referred to as a "Kara".

[10] Kamlesh's body was located about a half-hour drive from her home in Brampton. Earlier that evening, at 7:30 p.m., she and the appellant were at home alone. At that time, they placed a long distance call to Cornwall to extend birthday greetings to their brother-in-law. When no one picked up the phone, they left a voice message. Until her body was discovered at 11:30 p.m., Kamlesh was not heard from or seen by anyone other than the appellant.

[11] According to the appellant, after completing the phone call to Cornwall, he and Kamlesh ate a quick dinner consisting of pop and nachos. Kamlesh then left in the red Chrysler Concorde to do some shopping for a family outing to Canada's Wonderland the next day.

[12] In three statements made to three different people within twelve hours of the time that Kamlesh was supposed to have left the house, the appellant variously described her departure time as 8:30 p.m., 7:00 to 7:30 p.m. and 7:30 to 7:45 p.m. The appellant also gave inconsistent statements regarding the shoes that Kamlesh was wearing when she left. In a statement to the police, he claimed that she was wearing a jogging suit and black shoes. To a family member who confronted him with the fact that none of the shoes that Kamlesh normally wore when driving the car were missing, he denied knowing what shoes she was wearing because he had been in the washroom when she left. That statement was inconsistent with a third statement to another family member in which the appellant stated that he had kissed Kamlesh goodbye as she was leaving. In a similar vein, the appellant told the police that he had given Kamlesh $100.00 in cash to do the shopping and that he had kept $50.00 for himself. Later, however, when the police searched the blue jeans that he had been wearing at the time of her alleged departure, they discovered $150.00 in cash, precisely the amount of money he had withdrawn earlier that day from the bank. The bank teller who served him testified that the denominations of the bills found in his pocket were the same as those she had given to him.

[13] At about 8:30 p.m., the appellant received a phone call from his tenant, Mr. Lalsingh. Mr. Lalsingh and his wife occupied a self-contained basement apartment in the appellant's home. Mr. Lalsingh was calling to inquire about some appliances that he believed the appellant had for sale. The first call, placed at around 8:30 p.m., went unanswered. A second call, placed a few minutes later, was answered by the appellant. The two men spoke briefly. According to Mr. Lalsingh, the appellant sounded less friendly than usual.

[14] At 9:00 p.m., the appellant telephoned his sister-in-law's home to speak to his two children who were spending the night there. After that, he was not heard from or seen by anyone until 11:15 p.m.

[15] According to the appellant, between 10:00 p.m. and 10:30 p.m., he started to worry about Kamlesh because she had not returned home. As a result, he got into his white Ford van and drove around to various stores but could not find her. At 11:00 p.m., he returned home and placed another call to his sister-in-law's home. His brother-in-law, Vijay, answered the phone and told him that Kamlesh was not there. Sometime between 11:15 p.m. and 11:30 p.m., while he was still speaking to Vijay, the appellant's nephew, Sanjeev (who was staying with the appellant and Kamlesh for the summer), returned home from work. A short time later, Vijay and various members of his family arrived at the appellant's home. Some of them went to look for Kamlesh, while Sanjeev remained at home for the remainder of the night. The appellant and another family member drove to a police station to report Kamlesh missing. Before doing so, the appellant changed his clothing.

[16] Upon returning home from the police station, the appellant retrieved some photographs of Kamlesh. After dropping the photos off at the police station, he, Vijay and another relative began to search for Kamlesh. As they drove up Highway 10, the appellant told Vijay to drive to various stores and plazas. Eventually, at around 1:00 a.m., they arrived at a plaza on the south-east corner of Kennedy Road and Clarence Street in Brampton. It was there that the appellant spotted the family Chrysler Concorde. The distance between the plaza and the appellant's home was 5.7 kilometres.

[17] Kamlesh, of course, was nowhere to be found. At that point, no one in the appellant's search party knew that her body had already been discovered. Upon looking inside the car, it was obvious to all that the interior had been vandalized. The upholstery was slashed and the inside of the car was covered with yellow spray paint.

[18] Kamlesh's purse was found on top of the gear shift. That is where she normally kept it. It was speckled with yellow spray paint. The contents of her purse were missing. Forensic evidence established that the purse had been placed over the gearshift after the car had been spray-painted. A pair of black dress shoes was found on the front passenger floor. They too were speckled with yellow paint.

[19] The trunk of the car was completely empty. Even the window washer fluid that Kamlesh normally kept there was missing. Noteably, on Thursday night, July 6, the family had gone to a drive-in movie and they had stored blankets, chairs and a cooler in the trunk. Upon returning home from the drive-in, it was decided that those items would be left in the trunk until the following day. On Friday afternoon, July 7, when Sanjeev left for work at 1:45 p.m., they were nowhere in sight. When he returned home from work at around 11:25 p.m., he found them stored in the front hall by the front door. At no time did the appellant explain how or when those items came to be in the front hall.

[20] On Saturday morning, July 8, the appellant provided the police with a lengthy video-taped statement in which he described his activities on July 7, both before and after Kamlesh's disappearance. While waiting to be interviewed, he remarked to Vijay, "how one minute can change one's whole life". Asked later what he meant by that, he stated that one minute everything was fine and the next, all this happened.

[21] In his statement to the police, the appellant described, among other things, how Kamlesh was dressed when she left to go shopping and what jewellery she was wearing. Towards the end of the interview, he was told that a body, believed to be that of Kamlesh, had been found. When asked if he had any idea who might have wanted to hurt her or why she had been killed, he stated that he had "no idea". To his knowledge, she had no enemies and everyone loved her. He denied killing her or being responsible for her death.

[22] After finishing at the police station, the appellant was escorted home by the police. While at home, he changed his clothes and turned over to the police the clothing he was wearing. Unbeknownst to the police, the clothing that he turned over was not the clothing he had been wearing when his nephew, Sanjeev, returned home from work on Friday night. Rather, it was the clothing into which he had changed before going to the police station to report Kamlesh missing.

[23] After turning over his clothes, the appellant was driven by the police to the home of his sister-in-law where his two children were staying. The time was now 12:25 p.m. Shortly after his arrival, the appellant locked himself in the bathroom and refused to come out. After twenty minutes, he opened the door and went to his children. It was then that he told them that their mother was dead. He was very emotional and tearful at the time.

[24] At about 2:00 p.m., Kamlesh's eldest sister, Asha, arrived. She had come from Cornwall. As the eldest sibling, she was viewed as a mother figure by the appellant and Kamlesh. When the appellant realized that she was at the front door, he locked the door and said to Vijay, "I can't face her". He then asked for the keys to Vijay's car and left the house through the garage. Some time later, when asked by Asha why he had bolted, he said that "he could not look her straight in the eye" and that "he would have been compelled to tell her everything". When she asked him what he meant by "everything", he said "he couldn't say, he had been asked by his lawyer not to say anything to anyone". He also stated that "he needed some time to think things over".

[25] After leaving in Vijay's jeep, the appellant (who by now was under police surveillance), drove to a hardware store where he purchased two large exacto knives and a roll of duct tape. He then drove to a park on Lakeshore Road near Oakville, got out of the car, and walked towards the shore of Lake Ontario. A short time later, the police officer who had been following him found him sitting on a rock, clutching an exacto knife. He had slashed his neck and left wrist. He was immediately rushed to the hospital.

[26] In Vijay's jeep, the police located a number of documents prepared by the appellant, including a hand-written will and a note to his two children. In the note to the children, he told them how much he and Kamlesh loved them and that he had to leave them to be with her.

[27] While he was recuperating in the Oakville Trafalgar Hospital from his suicide attempt, the appellant was arrested for Kamlesh's murder. Prior to arresting him, the police conducted a thorough search of his home, his white Ford van and the clothes that he was wearing at the time of his attempted suicide. Those searches revealed a host of items connecting the appellant to Kamlesh's disappearance and death. Many of them are listed below. Their discovery created a huge dilemma for the appellant. As indicated, his only explanation was one that required the jury to speculate that the real killer had planted them in an effort to frame him.

Items located in the laundry room

[28] In the laundry room of the appellant's home, the police found:

  • a light shell jacket on the washing machine; it contained a pinkish-orange substance on the sleeve similar to the substance seen leaking from Kamlesh's nose and mouth; and
  • a plastic bag in a laundry basket on top of the dryer; it contained the contents of Kamlesh's purse, including her wallet, identification, credit cards, papers, letters and $12 in cash; also found in the bag were two blue and red bandanas, which were normally kept in the Chrysler Concorde and used by the appellant and his son to cover their heads when entering a Sikh temple.

Items found in the appellant's master bedroom and blue jeans

[29] In the master bedroom, the police located the jeans that the appellant had been wearing when Sanjeev returned home from work on Friday night at around 11:25 p.m. The following items were located in the pockets of his jeans:

  • a set of protective glasses sprinkled with yellow-paint;
  • $150 in cash in the same denominations that the appellant had received from the bank on the afternoon of July 7;
  • a wallet containing a photograph of the appellant on his honeymoon and a special note to Kamlesh from her son that Kamlesh normally kept in her purse;
  • a bobby pin; and
  • a set of keys to the appellant's white Ford van (this set was in addition to the set found on the appellant at the time of his attempted suicide).

Items located in the white Ford van

[30] Inside the appellant's van, the police located a plastic bag behind the driver's seat. It was spotted with yellow paint and contained:

  • a blue Brooks jacket (which the appellant's daughter had seen before, probably at her house); it had yellow paint on it and two pinkish-orange stains, one located on the right shoulder and the other on the lower right back.

[31] On the inside pockets of the Brooks jacket, the police found:

  • a black cloth belt likely used to strangle Kamlesh; among other things, it contained specks of yellow paint and skin fragments matching Kamlesh's DNA profile;
  • five rings (all cut in two or three places), two cut gold bracelets and a chain bracelet which belonged to Kamlesh; the rings had been cut from her fingers;
  • two audiocassettes from the Chrysler Concorde, each sprinkled with yellow paint; and
  • a set of Chrysler keys with yellow paint on them (the van and house keys that should have been on the same key ring were missing).

[32] Other plastic bags were found in the van. They too had yellow paint on them and contained:

  • a welder's hammer and an exacto knife; each was spotted with yellow paint and each had been used to cause damage to the upholstery in the Concorde;
  • a set of wire cutters spotted with yellow paint, which had been used to cut the rings off of Kamlesh's fingers;
  • a pair of latex gloves with yellow paint on them;
  • a cap from a spray-paint can with a bar code identifying it as "sunshine yellow", a brand of paint sold at Canadian Tire stores; and
  • a terry cloth towel, stiff with yellow paint, recognized by the appellant's daughter as having come from the kitchen of their home.

Items located in the clothing worn by the appellant at the time of his attempted suicide

[33] Inside the clothing worn by the appellant at the time of his attempted suicide, the police found:

  • a pair of pearl-clustered earrings that Kamlesh had worn to work on July 7, the day she was murdered;
  • a pair of gold coloured studs, which according to her sisters, Rajni and Asha, Kamlesh "always" or "almost always" wore;
  • Kamlesh's watch, which according to Rajni, Kamlesh "always" wore;
  • a Kara bracelet with yellow paint on it belonging to the appellant;
  • two photos of the appellant and family members; and
  • the appellant's wallet with his identification, credit cards and over $100 in Canadian currency.

[34] According to Rajni, within several weeks of Kamlesh's murder, she asked the appellant how he had come to have various items of Kamlesh's jewellery in his pockets at the time of his attempted suicide. He responded by saying that he had seen the items in the bedroom when the police brought him home to change his clothes on the morning of the 8th. He said it was then that he had put them in the pants he was wearing at the time of his attempted suicide. When asked by Rajni why he had not reported his finding to the police officers, he said that if he had told them, "they wouldn't have let him go and see his kids".

[35] As indicated, the appellant did not testify. In an effort to counter the case against him, he relied upon his July 8th statement to the police, evidence obtained through cross-examination of various Crown witnesses, and evidence called by the defence.

[36] Within the Crown's case, a number of family members and others attested to the soundness of the appellant's marriage and the lack of any apparent motive on his part to kill Kamlesh. Certainly, no one knew of any prior violence in the marriage.

[37] With respect to the adequacy of the police investigation, cross-examination revealed that the police had failed to conduct DNA testing on certain items. As well, it was suggested that the police had failed to adequately investigate a dispute between the appellant and some members of the Nanaksar Temple dating back to 1994. The dispute related to electrical work the appellant had done for the temple and the money he claimed was owing to him. In his "day book" for February 1994, the appellant recorded some of the highlights of the dispute. The entries indicated that he had been threatened but that someone from the temple apologized to him the next day. Later that month, according to the entries, he was paid most of the monies owing to him.

[38] The police were made aware of the temple incident. They interviewed the president of the temple but no one else. They also reviewed a journal from the temple that identified the meetings referred to in the appellant's daybook. The detective in charge of the investigation testified that the incident at the temple "was not a high priority" and that no further steps were taken to investigate the matter.

[39] Within the defence case, evidence was led to provide an innocent explanation for the yellow paint found on the appellant's safety glasses and his Kara. In particular, it was established that between May 11 and June 12, 1995, in the course of his work, the appellant had been using yellow and blue spray paint on electrical conduit pipes and the yellow paint may have been the same type as that found in the Chrysler Concorde and on the items retrieved from his van. (Of note, however, no blue paint specks were found on either the safety glasses or the appellant's Kara).

[40] The defence also relied on the evidence of Christine Myers, an independent witness, who claimed that on Friday, July 7 at about 8:30 p.m., she saw a red Chrysler Concorde with the licence plate "SODHI 1" being driven by someone on a street in Milton. She took no notice of the occupant or occupants. If her evidence was accurate, the appellant could not have been in the car. At 8:30 p.m., he was at home speaking on the telephone to his tenant, Mr. Lalsingh.

[41] Ms. Myers stated that she remembered the car and the time and date because she was with her eleven-year old son and her son asked her about the licence plate. She also noticed the car because it was bright red and it reminded her of a car that she and her husband had been looking at.

[42] Five days later, on July 12, Ms. Myers read an article in a local newspaper that referred to the Sodhi vehicle and the murder of Kamlesh. She could not remember at first where it was that she had seen the car. According to her evidence, she tossed and turned that night trying to piece things together and she finally came up with Friday, July 7, because she "didn't think [she] would have remembered it as well if it had been the week before that". Ms. Myers provided the police with a statement on July 14, one week after the murder.

[43] Against that backdrop, I turn to the grounds of appeal.

GROUNDS OF APPEAL

Introduction

[44] The grounds of appeal raised by the appellant can be conveniently divided into three categories: issues relating to the charge to the jury, the admissibility of the appellant's July 18th statements to the police, and improprieties in the Crown's closing address. I propose to deal with the jury charge matters first because they are straightforward and can be disposed of summarily.

I. ISSUES RELATING TO THE CHARGE

[45] The appellant submits that the trial judge committed three errors in his charge to the jury. Before addressing them, I note that none drew the attention of experienced defence counsel at trial [not Mr. Lockyer nor Mr. Cowan]. Moreover, in assessing the alleged errors, I have considered them, as I must, in the context of the charge as a whole. On that score, the appellant has no cause for complaint. Viewed as a whole, the charge was clear, thorough and even-handed. Indeed, in my view, it was a model of fairness.

(1) Instructions on circumstantial evidence

[46] The appellant submits that although the trial judge properly instructed the jury that the prosecution could rely on circumstantial evidence to prove its case, he failed to tell them that from the defence perspective, they could look to the circumstantial evidence to raise a reasonable doubt.

[47] I would not give effect to that submission. Although there are isolated passages in the charge that support it, when those passages are read in context and against the charge as a whole, I am satisfied that the jury would have appreciated that they could look to the circumstantial evidence as a basis for convicting the appellant and for acquitting him.

(2) Instructions on motive

[48] The appellant submits that the trial judge misdirected the jury on the issue of motive when he instructed them as follows:

Give the evidence of motive such weight as you think it deserves, based on the facts as you find them to be. Motive alone is insufficient to prove guilt beyond a reasonable doubt. Rather, it is a factor which you may consider.

[49] The impugned instruction forms part of the trial judge's general instructions on the issue of motive. The appellant's concern with the passage in question stems from the fact that the Crown did not prove that the appellant had a motive to kill Kamlesh. Accordingly, he submits that it was wrong for the trial judge to tell the jury that motive was a factor which they could consider in deciding whether the Crown had proved its case beyond a reasonable doubt. In doing so, he invited the jury to speculate. The appellant further submits that the error was compounded by Crown counsel's invitation to the jury in her closing address to speculate about possible motives the appellant may have had for killing Kamlesh.

[50] I would not give effect to this ground of appeal. In his general instructions, the trial judge made it clear to the jury that they could consider absence of motive as a "circumstance … tending to support the presumption of innocence" and that they should give it such weight as they deemed proper. In the impugned passage, he reinforced to the jury that they were to give the evidence of motive such weight as it deserved having regard to the facts as found by them. Significantly, the trial judge did not tell the jury that there was evidence from which they could find that the appellant had a motive to kill Kamlesh. On the contrary, he specifically reminded the jury of the defence position that the appellant loved Kamlesh and that there was "no motive whatsoever for him having wanted to kill his wife".

[51] In my view, while it might have been preferable in the circumstances of this case if the trial judge had refrained from instructing the jury in general terms about the use they could make of a proved presence of motive, it is speculative to think that the jury would have latched on to that instruction and used it as an invitation to conjure up evidence of motive where none existed. Nor in my view, did Crown counsel [not Ms. Roberts] extend such an invitation to the jury in her closing address. She did nothing more than remind the jury that in any relationship, appearances can be deceiving, and she highlighted certain stresses and tensions existing within the Sodhi household at the time of the murder. Her remarks were not improper. Accordingly, I would not give effect to this ground of appeal.

(3) Instruction on after-the-fact conduct evidence

[52] The appellant submits that the trial judge erred in leaving his attempted suicide to the jury as after-the-fact conduct capable of supporting an inference of guilt. In essence, it is his position that the instruction should not have been given because the suicide attempt was as consistent with grief as it was with guilt.

[53] With respect, I do not agree. Unlike the situation in R. v. Arcangioli (1994), 87 C.C.C. (3d) 289 (S.C.C.), this is not a case in which the appellant admitted to killing Kamlesh but denied a specific level of culpability in doing so. Nor is it a case in which he denied committing some related offence arising from the same operative set of facts. Here the appellant denied any involvement in Kamlesh's death and his identity as the perpetrator was the only live issue for the jury. In that sense, the facts are more closely aligned to those in R. v. White (1998), 125 C.C.C. (3d) 385 (S.C.C.), where the two accused denied committing the murder for which they were charged and sought to attribute their flight and attempt to dispose of evidence to the commission of other offences. In those circumstances, the Supreme Court held that the jury was entitled to consider the evidence of flight and the attempt to dispose of evidence as circumstantial evidence capable of linking the accused to the murder.

[54] In my view, the appellant's suicide attempt merits the same treatment. Grief was certainly one possible explanation for it and the trial judge put that explanation to the jury. Suicide, however, is a very extreme expression of grief. It is not, in my view, the typical reaction that one would expect from someone whose spouse has been murdered by a third party. Therein lies the fallacy in the appellant's argument that his suicide attempt was as consistent with grief as guilt and therefore incapable of supporting an inference of guilt. On this record, it was open to the jury to view the suicide attempt as the final act of a desperate man consumed with guilt for having killed his wife. The trial judge did not err in so instructing the jury. This ground of appeal accordingly fails.

II. THE APPELLANT'S JULY 18th STATEMENTS TO THE POLICE

[55] It will be recalled that the appellant was arrested at the Oakville Trafalgar Hospital on July 10, three days after Kamlesh had been murdered and two days after his attempted suicide. At the time of his arrest, the appellant handed a letter to the investigating officers, Detectives Boychuk and Woudstra. The letter was from his lawyer. It advised the police in clear language that the appellant had retained counsel and that he wished to exercise his right to remain silent.

[56] The contents of that letter did not deter the detectives from following through with a plan they had devised to stimulate conversation with the appellant. To that end, they presented the appellant with an up-to-date narrative of the investigation and they asked him various questions. In doing so, they appealed to his emotions and conscience. The appellant nonetheless remained steadfast and he either refused to respond to their questions or told the officers to speak to his lawyer.

[57] In his ruling on the voir dire the trial judge characterized the conduct of the officers as "a constant and repetitive effort … to have Mr. Sodhi talk about the case". He described their approach as one based on a belief that they could "put pressure on [Mr. Sodhi] to speak through an emotional appeal to negate the advice of his lawyer and to influence him so that he would tell them what happened".

[58] I mention the July 10th interview only because it forms part of the backdrop leading to the impugned July 18th statements. At trial, the Crown sought to have the July 10th interview admitted into evidence as part of the narrative leading up to the July 18th statements. The trial judge refused. In his view, apart from the questionable conduct on the part of the police, the July 10th conversation had "no probative value" and for that reason alone, it was inadmissible. That brings me to the events of July 18.

[59] On July 18, pursuant to a prearranged plan, Detectives Boychuk and Woudstra picked up the appellant from the detention centre where he was being held and drove him to court for a scheduled remand. They then drove him to the police station for photographs and fingerprints, following which they returned him to the detention centre. Later that afternoon, the two detectives returned to the detention centre and spoke to the appellant once again. Detective Boychuk readily admitted that although he and his partner did not have a formal plan, it was their intention to build a rapport with the appellant during the drive in the hope that he might change his mind and make a statement. To that end, Detective Boychuk sat beside the appellant in the back seat of the car as they travelled to and from the various destinations and he did most of the talking.

(a) Conversation 1: from the detention centre to the courthouse (9:25 a.m. – 9:35 a.m.)

[60] On this leg of the trip, Detective Boychuk asked the appellant about his treatment in the detention centre and the welfare of his children. He told the appellant that he should be proud of his children. The appellant replied that his children had told him that there was money missing from the house and that the house had been left in a poor condition after the police had searched it. The remainder of the conversation on the way to the courthouse essentially revolved around those two matters.

(b) Conversation 2: from the courthouse to police headquarters (10:15 a.m. – 10:30 a.m.)

[61] On the way to police headquarters, Detective Boychuk complimented the appellant on the choice of his new lawyer, Edward Greenspan (Mr. Greenspan did not act for the appellant at the trial). He told the appellant that Mr. Greenspan was "one of the best" and that it was important that he tell Mr. Greenspan the truth if he wanted proper representation.

[62] The appellant raised the question of Kamlesh's funeral. He wanted to know who had prevented him from attending it. In the ensuing discussion, among other things, the appellant reportedly said, "Irregardless of what happened, [I] should have been able to see [my] wife". Detective Boychuk's response included the following:

At that point, I explained to Mr. Sodhi that there were things I could do for him and things I couldn't. Like, when we had first met, I had told him that we would do everything that we could to find the person responsible for his wife's murder. Mr. Sodhi replied, "I respect you. I want you to do a good job". I told Mr. Sodhi that I thought he knew we had done a good job and that was why we were here, and that he was in the position he was in. I told him that the investigation was nothing personal, but both Detective Woudstra and I respected him. He was a man a lot like us, a hardworking family man. There was no question he loved his wife and children.

[63] Detective Boychuk then told the appellant that he and his partner were "good judges of character", to which the appellant replied, "there is so much I want to answer but my lawyer says not to". Detective Boychuk continued, telling the appellant that the only question they wanted answered was "why?" The appellant replied "so do I", to which Detective Boychuk responded, "there [is] only one person in the car who [has] that answer". Detective Boychuk went on to explain that the police had followed "all of the leads" and that "in this case, it all [came] back to [the appellant] as being the person responsible". He reminded the appellant of the physical evidence that had been recovered, all of which pointed to him. The appellant responded, "I have answers to all that but my lawyer told me not to say". Detective Boychuk replied that "it was his decision whether he wanted to say anything or not".

[64] Detective Boychuk continued, telling the appellant that the police did not "consider him to be a stupid man and he shouldn't take [them] to be stupid either". He then outlined some of the physical evidence and asked the appellant to explain how he had managed to get yellow paint on his Kara bracelet. The appellant hinted that it was work-related. The detective then asked the appellant when he had last used paint of that nature, to which the appellant did not respond. Detective Woudstra then interrupted, accusing the appellant of "trying to treat [them] like [they] were [stupid]". Again, the appellant replied, "he [had] answers for everything but [couldn't] say".

[65] Detective Boychuk next broached the subject of the appellant's attempted suicide and proceeded to provide his own lengthy interpretation of it as follows:

At this point, I outlined a scenario as to what I felt had happened. I told Mr. Sodhi that as soon as he realized that we were going to search his residence, that it was all over, because he knew what we'd find there. So, in his mind, he started thinking about what he was going to do, almost like he had a "trial" within himself. I told him that I understood his religion, that it was like many, an eye for an eye and so forth, and he interrupted me and said that he does not practice just one religion. At that point, I interrupted him and said that all major religions have similar themes and I continued by saying that this would explain why he was so quiet both at this house and at Vijay's. That he was going through all this in his mind and concluded that the only way he could rectify the terrible wrong he had done was to take his own life. I said to him that the death of his wife alone was not enough to explain his actions. It was more than grief. He had his two children who were a part of him and a part of her, and that he was, in effect, abandoning them. It had to be something terrible to make him do that, and the only thing it could be was that he was responsible for her death and that taking his life was the only way to make things rights.

I told him that I've spoken to friends of my own and all had said that, under similar circumstances, they would not have acted the way he had and left the children. Mr. Sodhi said, "You have no idea until it happens to you. They're only saying that because it never happened to them."

(c) Conversation 3: from police headquarters to the detention centre (11:00 a.m. – 11:20 a.m.)

[66] After leaving police headquarters, Detective Boychuk related the following conversation between himself and the appellant:

… Mr. Sodhi said, "I have two questions to ask. Where was my wife found and how did she die?" I told Mr. Sodhi that I didn't think I had to answer that because he knew the answers. He replied that, "if you don't want to answer me, that's okay". I told him that I have no problems answering the questions, just that I believed he knew the answers already.

[67] Detective Boychuk told the appellant where his wife's body had been found and the appellant indicated that he did not know the area. Several moments later, Detective Boychuk told the appellant that Kamlesh had been strangled. According to the detective, the appellant showed "no visible reaction or sign of emotion" upon hearing that information. The appellant then asked some questions about the keys to his van and a gift box that had been placed in his van. Detective Boychuk said the police would look into the matter of the box. After a brief period of silence, the appellant said, "questions I have, I wish I could ask. I wish". The appellant then asked about his car and a discussion ensued about to whom it should be returned. As they drove into Milton, the appellant asked the name of the town. He was told that it was Milton. When asked if he had ever been there before, he said, "only once to fix a lighting fixture" at a relative's drug store.

(d) Conversation four: at the detention centre (2:58 p.m. – 3:30 p.m.)

[68] When the detectives returned to the detention centre in the afternoon, they provided the appellant with an itemized list of jewellery they had seized and they talked to him about the money allegedly missing from his home. At one point, the appellant told them that his children were finding it hard to express their feelings. Detective Boychuk said, "like you?". After hesitating the appellant replied, "yes". The appellant then explained why his children were so upset about the missing money and Detective Boychuk continued as follows:

I explained that we take things like this very seriously and that we would investigate fully if he requested it. I then talked to him about trust and how he had some judgments about us and our sincerity and that our integrity was very important to us. … He confirmed with us that we had treated him fairly.

Trial Judge's Ruling

[69] The defence sought to exclude the July 18th statements on the basis that they had been obtained in a manner that violated the appellant's right to counsel and his right to remain silent under ss. 10(b) and 7 of the Charter. In particular, defence counsel maintained that in the circumstances, at a very minimum, the police had a duty to reinstruct the appellant on his s. 10(b) rights before attempting to speak to him on July 18. The essence of his argument is captured in the following submission:

My argument is that where the police have contact with someone who is under arrest who has previously indicated to them in clear and unambiguous terms he wishes to exercise his right to silence, and at which point their intention in advance is to elicit statements from him, they must at that point re-advise him of his right to counsel in order that he can make an informed decision as to whether or not to speak to them.

[70] The trial judge disagreed. In his view, there was nothing in the circumstances that required the police to re-instruct the appellant on his s. 10(b) rights. Those rights, he found, had been afforded to the appellant a week earlier and nothing had changed in the meantime. Based on the evidence before him, the trial judge was satisfied that on July 18, the appellant understood "the seriousness of the charge against him" and he also understood "his rights to counsel and his right to remain silent". Accordingly, the trial judge concluded that the July 18th statements were admissible.

The Appellant's Position on Appeal

[71] The appellant submits that the trial judge erred in concluding that his s. 10(b) rights were not violated by the police conduct on July 18. He further submits that the trial judge erred in not considering the issues from a broader perspective than simply that of a possible violation of s. 10(b). In that regard, he maintains that in the context of what occurred on July 18, s. 10(b) and s. 7 of the Charter, as well as the common law discretionary power of exclusion regarding the admissibility of confessions, now enshrined in s. 11(d) of the Charter, are all relevant to the assessment of the propriety of the July 18th questioning.

[72] Concerning s. 10(b), the appellant invites us to formulate a rule that would preclude the police from confronting an accused "whom they know has retained counsel, regarding a crime for which he has already been charged and for which the court process has commenced, except through counsel or as a result of conversations initiated by the accused". In support of this position, he relies primarily on the case of R. v. McCorkell, which is referred to by M. H. Harris, "Notes and Comments: Concerning Statements to Police Officers" (1962), 7 Crim.L.Q. 395 at 396-7 and various American authorities including Maine v. Moulton, 474 U.S. 159 (1985).

[73] In the alternative, the appellant submits that in the face of his repeated assertions of his right to silence, it was wrong for the police to engage in conduct designed to weaken his resolve and lull him into giving up that right. In conducting themselves as they did, he argues that the police violated his right to silence under s. 7 of the Charter.

[74] In the further alternative, the appellant submits that the July 18th statements should have been excluded under s. 11(d) of the Charter as their admission rendered his trial unfair. He relies on a number of factors, including the purposive conduct of the police designed to circumvent his asserted right to silence, the nature and manner of their questioning, the lack of probative value in any of the conversations and the prejudice resulting from, among other things, the jury hearing "strongly worded police opinions of the appellant's guilt".

[75] Finally, the appellant makes the overriding submission that at common law, the July 18th statements should have been excluded because their prejudicial effect exceeded their probative value.

Analysis

[76] I agree with the appellant's final submission that the July 18th statements should not have been admitted into evidence because their prejudicial effect exceeded their probative value. In view of that conclusion, while I do not wish to minimize the importance of the appellant's "Charter-based" arguments, I find it unnecessary to deal with them.

[77] Regarding the probative value of the July 18th statements, I am of the view that the first and fourth conversations had no probative value whatsoever. Those are the conversations that occurred on the way to the courthouse from the detention centre and at the detention centre in the afternoon. On that basis alone, those conversations should have been excluded. [1]

[78] As for the second and third conversations, to the extent that either one had any probative value, in my view, it was slight.

[79] In the second conversation on the way to police headquarters from the courthouse, the only discussion of any possible relevance related to the appellant's Kara bracelet and how it had come to have yellow paint on it. In the third conversation on the way to the detention centre from police headquarters, the appellant's request for information about how and where his wife died, and his professed lack of familiarity with the town of Milton were the only possible matters of relevance.

[80] To the extent that the second and third conversations may have contained snippets of relevant information, the probative value of such information was in my view negligible. Indeed, in the overall scheme of things, I believe that if the jury had been deprived of that information, it would not have made the slightest bit of difference to the case.

[81] As for the prejudicial effect of the statements, for reasons that will be provided when I consider the application of the curative proviso, I believe that it too was slight [see paras. 104-116, infra]. On balance, however, I am satisfied that it overshadowed whatever limited probative value the statements may have had.

[82] Accordingly, I am respectfully of the view that the July 18th statements should not have been admitted into evidence. I would therefore give effect to this ground of appeal.

III. CROWN IMPROPRIETIES

(1) Crown counsel's improper use of demeanour evidence

[83] The appellant takes issue with certain aspects of the Crown's closing address. In particular, he submits that it was wrong for the Crown to invite the jury to consider the appellant's demeanour at various stages of the investigation and use it as after-the-fact evidence capable of supporting an inference of guilt.

[84] In support of his position, the appellant listed eleven examples from the closing address that he submits are offensive. I note that, once again, none drew the attention or objection of defence counsel at trial.

[85] To resolve this ground of appeal, I find it unnecessary to list the eleven examples. Many of them do not warrant criticism. Several for example relate to the appellant's demeanour during his videotaped interview on July 8, the morning after the murder. To the extent that the appellant was relying on the content of that interview to further his defence of denial, the jury was entitled to consider his demeanour in assessing the truthfulness of his answers and the weight to be given to them.

[86] In other instances, Crown counsel merely referred the jury to evidence that formed part of the narrative, the admissibility of which was not contested. On those occasions, she did not invite the jury to draw impermissible inferences of guilt from that evidence and those aspects of her address were accordingly also proper.

[87] There were, however, several instances in which Crown counsel crossed the line and invited the jury, at least implicitly, to infer guilt from equivocal reactions and behaviour on the part of the appellant. For example, I think it was improper to invite the jury to infer guilt from the appellant's failure to ask certain questions of the police during the July 8th videotaped interview and his failure to express outrage upon being asked if he had killed Kamlesh. In a similar vein, it was improper to invite the jury to infer guilt from the appellant's failure to show an appropriate level of concern about Kamlesh's disappearance in his 11:00 p.m. telephone conversation with Vijay.

[88] There can be no getting around the fact that a few inappropriate comments of this nature were made. I note, however, that they formed a small part of a lengthy and otherwise proper closing address. In addition, as the respondent points out, it is at least arguable that in making the impugned comments, Crown counsel was merely responding to similar comments made by defence counsel in his closing address. The following passage from the defence closing serves to illustrate the point:

Now my friend may invite you to draw some inferences from Mr. Sodhi's demeanour on the videotape. I don't know what she's going to suggest to you. I don't know if she's going to say he doesn't look sufficiently upset, or that somehow he looks guilty. What I will say to you is there is nothing in Sodhi's demeanor during that videotape interview that should cause you any concern. But, more importantly, if you're going to draw inferences from demeanour, resist the invitation to tunnel vision. Draw your inferences from all of the demeanour evidence that you have before you.

Remember that Sodhi sounded genuinely concerned when he called Vijay during the second phone call; that Mr. Sodhi seemed genuinely concerned when the relatives arrived at 18 Waterloo Court. Remember, he wept in the presence of Rajni and Jyoti at 50 Kennedy Road. Remember that, at 50 Kennedy Road, when they hear the woman scream, they all start running around, calling out Kamlesh's name and Mr. Sodhi did that, as well. Remember that he was described as numb at Vijay's house.

And, of course, the real problem with drawing any inference from somebody's demeanour is that we're all different. We all express ourselves in different ways. Before you can draw an inference, you need an understanding of how a person usually acts, how they usually express their emotions. And, in this case, we have that base line. All of the relatives, all of Kamlesh's relatives, agree that Mr. Sodhi is not an emotional man or a man who outwardly displayed his emotions. He's not an emotionally demonstrative person.

[89] To be sure, this was not a one-way street. Both sides invited the jury to consider equivocal demeanour evidence in support of their respective positions. Counsel were involved in a hard-fought murder trial and they pressed their positions with vigour. Just as perfection is not required in a jury charge[2], so too, in my view, it is not required in a closing address.

[90] What is significant about this case and what distinguishes it from R. v. Baltrusaitis (2002), 162 C.C.C. (3d) 539 (Ont. C.A.), upon which the appellant relies, is the fact that the trial judge did not instruct the jury that they could use the appellant's equivocal conduct or reactions as after-the-fact conduct capable of supporting an inference of guilt. The only instruction given by the trial judge on the after-the-fact conduct was the one given in relation to the appellant's attempted suicide. As I concluded earlier, that instruction was entirely proper.

[91] The jury was bound to follow the trial judge's instructions on the use they could make of the evidence and particular aspects of it. While it would have been preferable if the trial judge had instructed the jury that they could not use the appellant's equivocal conduct in the manner urged by the Crown, in my view, it is speculative to think that they would have latched on to the few improper remarks made by Crown counsel and used them to draw impermissible inferences of guilt against the appellant. Accordingly, I would reject this ground of appeal.

[92] Before moving to the next issue, I would suggest that in future cases, counsel would be well advised to seek direction from the trial judge before addressing the jury on the use they can make of evidence of after-the-fact conduct by an accused.

(2) Crown counsel's improper remarks about paint on the appellant's blue jeans

[93] The police did not seize the appellant's blue jeans because on visual inspection they saw no yellow paint specks on them. The officer who looked at the jeans admitted that he did not inspect the inside pockets of the jeans. He also stated that on his initial inspection of the safety glasses found inside the jeans, he did not see any yellow paint specks on them.

[94] In her closing address, Crown counsel reminded the jury that the paint on the glasses had been missed initially and she suggested that "it's quite possible that there were sprays of yellow paint on the jeans that were missed by the police as well".

[95] The appellant takes issue with that remark. He characterizes it as speculative and improper and an attempt by the Crown "to take advantage of the police failure to preserve evidence by suggesting that their failure had deprived the Crown of incriminating evidence". He further submits that the trial judge should have "commented severely" on the Crown's comment and "told the jury that the absence of evidence, forensic or otherwise, to contradict Sgt. Greenhill's evidence that there was no paint on the jeans supported the defence position that there was no paint on the jeans".

[96] I would not give effect to this ground of appeal for several reasons. Before outlining them, I think it is important to set out the facts surrounding the finding of the jeans.

[97] It will be recalled that the appellant was not entirely candid with the police when he told them that the clothing he was wearing at the time of his July 8th videotaped interview was the clothing that he was wearing on the evening of July 7, 1995. The clothing that the police recovered from the appellant on July 8 was the clothing that he had changed into before going to the police station to report Kamlesh missing. As such, it is understandable that when the police looked at the jeans hanging on the back of the closet in the appellant's bedroom, they had no reason to believe that the jeans themselves would be of much evidentiary value. That is so despite the finding of the safety glasses in them. As indicated, the police did not notice yellow specks of paint on the safety glasses at that time. It was much later when the glasses were looked at microscopically that the yellow paint was discovered.

[98] Had the appellant been completely candid and told the police that he was wearing the jeans at the time of Kamlesh's alleged disappearance, there can be little doubt that the police would have seized and carefully inspected them. Because of a pre-trial ruling,[3] however, the jury was not made aware of the appellant's arguable duplicity regarding the clothes he was wearing at the time of Kamlesh's disappearance.

[99] That is the backdrop against which I measure the appellant's submission.

[100] The impugned comment was by and large innocuous. As the respondent points out, viewed in context, it was more in the nature of an aside. The Crown's main point in the impugned passage was to highlight the finding of the safety glasses in the jeans as a basis for rebutting the defence suggestion that the killer had not worn these jeans because no yellow paint was observed on them. In that sense, the Crown's submission was entirely fair and proper.

[101] Viewed against the case as a whole, the impugned remark is really quite insignificant. Bearing that in mind, as well as defence counsel's failure to object to it, I would not give effect to this ground of appeal.

CURATIVE PROVISO

[102] The appellant's statements to the police on July 18 should not have been admitted into evidence. That is the one error of law that the appellant has made out on this record.

[103] For reasons that follow, I am of the view that the error occasioned little harm to the appellant and certainly none that would warrant a new trial. In all likelihood, the slight prejudice that may have been occasioned by the admission of the July 18th statements had no impact whatsoever on the verdict. But even if the error was not harmless, I am satisfied that the verdict would necessarily have been the same had it not occurred. The case against the appellant was overwhelming. There is no realistic possibility that a new trial would produce a different result.

[104] Commencing with the first branch of the proviso, harmless error, just as the July 18th statements had very little probative value, so too, in my view, their prejudicial effect was minimal. In the end, they did little more than convey to the jury that the officers in charge did not consider themselves to be stupid, that they believed that the appellant was guilty, that they believed they had treated him fairly and that they had a theory as to why he had attempted to commit suicide.

[105] In my view, none of this amounted to very much. It added little if anything to what the jury would have already assumed or eventually been told by Crown counsel in her closing address. Thus, while I accept that the jury should not have been exposed to the opinions and theories of the investigating officers, the resulting harm viewed realistically was likely minimal.

[106] As I understand it, however, the appellant's chief complaint lies not so much with the prejudice occasioned by the police rhetoric, but with the prejudice resulting from the references to his dealings and communications with counsel. In particular, he points out that on two occasions, he told the police that although he wanted to answer their questions, he could not do so on the advice of counsel. He submits that because he failed to answer other questions put to him by Detective Boychuk in like fashion, there was "a considerable danger" that the jury would infer that this was "because he only had self-incriminating answers to them".

[107] Alternatively, the appellant submits that in light of his response on two occasions that he did have answers to the questions posed by police, the jury was likely to wonder why it is that he had not taken the witness stand. His concern in that regard was heightened by the fact that on three occasions following his arrest in conversations with his wife's eldest sister, Asha, he responded to questions posed by her in like fashion. In other words, he told her that he had answers to her questions but on counsel's advice, he could not respond. According to the appellant, in these circumstances, it was a virtual certainty that the jury would speculate that his failure to testify indicated that he really did not have answers and that he must therefore be guilty.

[108] On a more subtle level, the appellant also complains about the "less obvious but real prejudice" flowing from Detective Boychuk's comments about Mr. Greenspan and his admonition to the appellant that he should tell Mr. Greenspan the truth if he wanted proper representation. The appellant contends that because Mr. Greenspan did not act for him at trial, the jury was likely to infer that this was because the appellant had lied to him.

[109] With respect, I believe that the concerns raised by the appellant are overstated and largely speculative.

[110] Commencing with the alleged prejudice occasioned by the appellant's stated inability to answer some of the questions posed by the detectives and Asha due to the advice of counsel, the trial judge instructed the jury on no less than three occasions (twice during the trial and once in his charge) that the appellant was entitled to retain counsel and that his decision to follow counsel's advice was irrelevant to the issue of guilt or innocence and was to play no part in the jury's discussions or deliberations. One such instruction came at the end of Asha's evidence. A portion of it is reproduced below:

You've heard evidence that Mr. Sodhi said that he had been advised by his lawyer not to discuss the circumstances of his case with anyone. You just heard that evidence from the last witness.

Now, this is standard advice that a defence counsel would give to any client accused of a criminal offence. You must not draw any inferences from the fact that Mr. Sodhi's lawyer gave him this advice or from the fact that Mr. Sodhi indicated to others that he intended to follow that advice.

These facts are not relevant to the issue of guilt or innocence and must not be considered by you during your discussions or deliberations in reaching your verdict.

[111] In his charge, the trial judge said this:

Also, I tell you as a matter of law, it is not -- I repeat -- not relevant to your determination of guilt or innocence that, different parts of those conversations with Asha or Detective Boychuk that Mr. Sodhi said his lawyer had told him not to talk to anybody about the case and he, therefore, was following his lawyer's advice. Those parts of the various conversations to those persons by Mr. Sodhi, when he referred to what his lawyer told him to do and what he was going to do in not speaking to Asha and to Detective Boychuk in order to follow that advice, is not to be taken into account by you as part of the evidence in this case as to guilt or innocence.

It is not relevant that his lawyer gave him that advice and it is not relevant that Mr. Sodhi said he was going to follow it. [emphasis in original.]

[112] Defence counsel was satisfied with those instructions. He asked for nothing more. In light of those instructions, I think there is little risk that the jury would have engaged in the impermissible reasoning envisaged by the appellant. On the contrary, it is far more likely that they followed the trial judge's instructions and removed from their consideration any and all references to counsel's advice in the appellant's statements.

[113] In so concluding, I do not gainsay the possibility that the jury may have wondered why they had not heard from the appellant. But if they did, as was their right,[4] I doubt that it had much to do with the appellant's failure to make good on his stated ability to "explain everything". Rather, in my view, it is much more likely due to the fact that the case against the appellant was overwhelming and it cried out for a response.

[114] As for Detective Boychuk's remarks about Mr. Greenspan, the concern raised by the appellant is, in my view, highly speculative. I reject out of hand the notion that in arriving at its verdict, the jury may have somehow been influenced by the fact that Mr. Greenspan was not the appellant's trial counsel.

[115] From this, it follows that although the concerns raised by the appellant cannot be completely discounted, I believe that the prejudice flowing from the improper admission of the July 18th statements was slight. The same holds true for those portions of Asha's evidence about which the appellant takes issue. Accordingly, even if the error was not totally harmless, I believe that the verdict would necessarily have been the same had it not occurred.

[116] Before concluding this aspect of my analysis, I should point out that on appeal, the appellant argues that the trial judge should have edited the conversations with Asha and removed all references to counsel. He makes the same submission with respect to the appellant's July 18th statements to the police, assuming those statements were otherwise admissible.

[117] I do not doubt that editing was a viable option in this case. Indeed, when it comes to references to counsel such as those in question, I believe that as a general rule, the better practice would be to excise them rather than to make them the subject of a limiting instruction. Had that approach been followed here, it certainly would have eliminated the risks identified by the appellant.

[118] Defence counsel at trial did not ask the trial judge to edit out the references to counsel. Had he done so, the trial judge might well have acceded to his request. As it is, the trial judge provided the jury with strong limiting instructions. For reasons already stated, I believe that those instructions went a long way towards removing the prejudice about which the appellant now complains for the first time on appeal.

[119] Turning to the second branch of the proviso, the strength of the Crown's case, even if I have understated the risk of prejudice flowing from the improper admission of the July 18th statements, I would nonetheless apply the proviso. In short, I am satisfied that the case against the appellant was so overwhelming that the verdict would necessarily have been the same had the error not occurred.

Circumstantial evidence establishing that Kamlesh was killed at home by the appellant

[120] Out of the mass of circumstantial evidence linking the appellant to Kamlesh's disappearance and death, there are a number of discernible items of evidence that belie the appellant's "I was framed" theory and prove instead that he killed Kamlesh at home some time after 7:30 p.m. The following non-exhaustive list consists of hard evidence. It does not include questionable comments or inconsistent statements made by the appellant, nor does it incorporate questionable after-the-fact conduct engaged in by him:

  • Kamlesh was found barefoot. All of the shoes that she would have worn to go shopping were located at her home. She would not have been wearing the black dress shoes found on the front passenger floor of the Concorde because she never wore shoes of that nature when driving the car. Moreover, she would not have worn such shoes when dressed in a jogging suit.
  • The trunk of the car of the Concorde was completely empty. On the night of July 6, it contained a blanket, chairs and a cooler. Those items were nowhere in sight when the appellant's nephew, Sanjeev, left for work on the afternoon of July 7 at 1:45 p.m. When he returned home from work at about 11:25 p.m., they were piled up by the front door. There was no explanation as to how those items came to be in that location; nor was there any explanation as to why the window washer fluid was also missing from the trunk. In other words, there was no evidence to counter the Crown's theory that the appellant used the trunk of the Concorde to remove Kamlesh's body from the scene of the crime.
  • The money found in the appellant's jeans matched the bills he received from the bank on the afternoon of July 7. That called into question his statement to the police that he had given Kamlesh $100.00 to go shopping. Contents of Kamlesh's purse, located in the laundry room, contained $12.00. No groceries were found in the Concorde. If she was robbed, the thief likely would have taken the $12.00.
  • The earrings and watch that Kamlesh was wearing on the night of July 7 were easily removable. They were found in the pants worn by the appellant at the time of his attempted suicide. They did not have yellow paint on them. In all likelihood, the appellant removed those items from Kamlesh at the house before placing her body in the trunk of the car.
  • The sleeve of the light shell jacket found in the laundry room contained the same orange-pinkish substance that was seen leaking form Kamlesh's nose and mouth. Although a similar substance was found on the Brook's jacket located in the appellant's van, the placement of the staining on that jacket (the right shoulder and lower right back) rendered it unlikely that Kamlesh was wearing the Brook's jacket when she was killed. It is far more likely that she was wearing the jacket found in the laundry room which, unlike the Brooks jacket, was free of yellow paint.
  • Kamlesh's purse was found over the gear shift of her car. It had been placed there after the car had been vandalized and spray-painted. The fact that she normally kept her purse in that location is something that would only have been known by someone very familiar with her habits.
  • The only piece of jewellery found on Kamlesh's body was her Kara, an item that she and the appellant always wore. The appellant was well aware of the religious significance of that item.
  • Items removed from Kamlesh's purse such as the photograph of the appellant on his honeymoon and the special note from her son would only have been meaningful to the appellant and his family. The same can be said for the bandanas that the appellant and his son used when entering a Sikh temple.
  • Kamlesh's keys to the Concorde were found together with her jewellery in the appellant's Ford van. The keys to the van had been removed from that ring. A set of van keys was found in the pocket of the blue jeans the appellant was wearing on the night of the murder. Another set of van keys was recovered from the appellant after his suicide attempt.
  • The appellant's safety glasses were found in the pocket of the blue jeans he was wearing on the night of the murder. They had yellow paint on them. Although the appellant attempted to explain the yellow paint spots as having been deposited a month or so earlier when he was using yellow and blue spray paint at work, his explanation failed to account for the fact that no specks of blue paint were found on the glasses. As well, no explanation was given as to why the glasses would have been in his jeans to begin with.
  • The appellant's Kara had yellow paint on it. His explanation that this too had occurred at work a month or so earlier rang hollow. The appellant always wore his Kara and if it had been stained with yellow paint for several weeks, he surely would have noticed it. According to expert testimony, the paint from the Kara could easily have been removed. As well, no blue paint specks were found on the Kara.
  • A towel from the appellant's kitchen was found in the appellant's van together with the cut-up jewellery. It was stiff with yellow paint. The suggestion that the real killer surreptitiously broke into the appellant's home and van and planted evidence in both locations is difficult enough to accept. The further suggestion that while in the home, the killer went to the kitchen, retrieved the towel, used it to wipe off and then deposited it in the appellant's van borders on the ridiculous.
  • On the appellant's version of the events, if the real killer did plant evidence in his home and van, this could only have occurred after 11:30 p.m. on Friday night. Between 10:00 p.m. and 11:00 p.m., if the appellant had been looking for Kamlesh as he claims, he would have been in his van. Hence, the killer could not have planted the items found in the van during that timeframe. After the appellant returned home at 11:00 p.m., someone was always present at the home until 10:15 a.m. the next morning when the police secured the premises. Specifically, after the appellant's nephew returned home from work at around 11:25 p.m. on Friday night, he did not leave the house. The chances of someone entering into the laundry room without him knowing were slim. The chances of someone entering the kitchen and taking a towel without him knowing were even more remote.

[121] As indicated, this substantial body of evidence proves to a virtual certainty that the appellant killed Kamlesh at home some time after 7:30 p.m. on the night of July 7. The evidence upon which the appellant relies to resist that finding falls far short of the mark. His attempt to explain the yellow paint on the safety glasses located in the pocket of his jeans rings hollow. It fails to account for the lack of blue paint on the glasses and it also fails to explain why the glasses were in his jeans to begin with. They were not required for the work that he was doing on July 7 and there is no suggestion from anyone that he was wearing the glasses that day.

[122] Similarly, the fact that he lacked an apparent motive to kill Kamlesh is of no great consequence when one considers the overwhelming volume of circumstantial evidence implicating him as the killer. My best sense of it is that he probably killed Kamlesh in a moment of rage. That would explain why he commented to Vijay at the police station "how one minute can change one's life". It would also account for his attempted suicide and his inability to come to grips with and acknowledge what he had done.

[123] That leaves Ms. Myers' evidence. Admittedly, her alleged sighting of the Chrysler Concorde in Milton on July 7 at 8:30 p.m. is, on its face, problematic. Nonetheless, when her evidence is stacked up against the evidence implicating the appellant as the killer, it is apparent to me that she must have been mistaken. That comes as no surprise, considering that she had no reason to think about the appellant's car until July 12, five days after the murder. On her own testimony, she tossed and turned that night trying to piece things together and she finally came up with July 7 because "she didn't think [she] would have remembered it as well if it had been the week before that".

[124] Putting all of this together, I am firmly of the view that on a new trial, if the appellant's July 18th statements and portions of Asha's evidence were excluded, there is no realistic possibility that the verdict would be different. In so concluding, I have not ignored the fact that at one point in their deliberations, the jury asked for "further direction on what constitutes ‘reasonable doubt'". Not surprisingly, the appellant relies on that as a basis for arguing that the curative provision cannot safely be applied. Manifestly, I do not share the appellant's view. It is always a risky business to attempt to divine what one or more jurors may have been thinking from a question posed by the jury. That holds true in this case.

[125] The appellant would have it that "the jury may have been hesitating … over where the reasonable doubt line should be drawn" in his case. I cannot say with certainty that he is wrong. But in my view, bearing in mind the appellant's "I was framed" theory, it is far more likely that the question was asked because the jury wanted to be sure of the basis upon which they could legitimately find a reasonable doubt. In particular, they needed to know that a reasonable doubt was not a fanciful or imaginary doubt but a real doubt based on reason and common sense. In the end, it is clear that the jury rejected the appellant's "I was framed" theory as fanciful. In my view, that conclusion is unassailable.

[126] For these reasons, I am satisfied that this is a proper case to apply the curative proviso. Accordingly, I would dismiss the appeal from conviction.

SENTENCE APPEAL

[127] The trial judge increased the appellant's period of parole ineligibility from ten to fourteen years. The appellant accepts that that sentence was within the range of sentences (twelve to fifteen years) approved by this court in R. v. McKnight (1999), 135 C.C.C. (3d) 41. He maintains, however, that the trial judge fell into error when he described the scene as "terrible" and "one of the worst fact situations". He also submits that the trial judge was wrong to find that there were "no mitigating factors" in the case. Absent those errors, the appellant submits that the trial judge would have set the period of parole ineligibility at twelve years. He asks that we reduce it accordingly.

[128] The trial judge gave detailed and cogent reasons for sentence. He thoroughly reviewed the appellant's background and history and was well aware of the fact that the offence was out of character for the appellant. It is in that context that the trial judge's remark about the lack of mitigating factors must be viewed. Clearly, he was aware of the mitigating factors relating to the offender.

[129] The trial judge's perception of the scene as "one of the worst fact situations" also must be viewed in context. The trial judge quite properly rejected the defence submission that this was not a brutal killing. In his opinion:

Ms. Sodhi was brutally assaulted. She was throttled at close range by the accused who had a ligature nearby and used it to make sure of her death.

[130] I see no basis for interfering with that finding. That said, it is at least arguable that the trial judge overstated the case when he described the scene as one of the worst fact situations. In my view, however, what separates this case from others and takes it out of the twelve-year range is the appellant's conduct after the event.

[131] The appellant's attempt to cover-up his crime and his persistent efforts to create suspicion that someone else had committed it were despicable and cowardly. This conduct amounted to a serious aggravating feature and, in and of itself, warranted a significant increase in the period of parole ineligibility. Combining that with the domestic nature of the crime and the brutality associated with it, I cannot say that the trial judge erred in imposing the sentence he did.

[132] Accordingly, although I would grant leave to appeal sentence, I would dismiss the appeal from sentence.
