On appeal from the conviction entered by Justice Gerald R. Morin of the Superior Court of Justice, sitting with a jury, dated November 19, 1998 and the sentence imposed by Justice Morin dated February 12, 1999.

ENDORSEMENT

[1]               The appellant appeals his conviction and sentence on charges of sexual assault with a weapon, forcible confinement, and attempted anal intercourse.  The sentence appeal is withdrawn.  With respect to the offence of attempted anal intercourse, the Crown properly concedes that the appellant’s conviction on that charge is null and void and the appellant should not have been convicted of it.  We turn now to the appeal on the charges of sexual assault with a weapon and forcible confinement.  The appellant raises two issues: 1) the admissibility of part of the similar fact evidence and 2) whether part of the Crown’s cross-examination was improper.

1. Did the trial judge err in admitting the similar fact evidence?

[2]               The factual background to this issue is the following.  The complainant is the appellant’s stepdaughter.  She was 30 years old at the time of the incident.  The charges against the appellant arose from an incident on August 2, 1997.  The complainant was visiting her mother and the appellant at their home near Lanark, and was leaving for Montreal the next day after a two-week stay.  At about 5:00p.m., the appellant and the complainant drove the complainant’s mother to the restaurant where she worked.  The three of them planned to have dinner and watch movies when the mother’s shift ended at 9:00p.m., so the appellant and the complainant rented some videos and then returned to the appellant’s home.

[3]               The complainant testified that when she and the appellant got home, she sat on the couch in the living room and began reading a book.  The appellant confronted her with a kitchen knife in his hand, and told her to go to the bedroom and get undressed.  The complainant asked repeatedly if the appellant was going to kill her, and begged him to put down the knife.  She went with him into the guest bedroom, where the appellant demanded that she get undressed.  She pleaded with him that she would get undressed if he put the knife down, and he eventually agreed.  The complainant took off her clothes, but the appellant did not put down the knife. 

[4]               Over the course of the next number of hours, the appellant sexually assaulted the complainant in the guest bedroom, the shower, and the living room.  The complainant testified that the appellant told her that he was in love with her, that he had been dreaming of this for a long time, and that it was worth going to prison over.  The appellant testified that he and the complainant had expressed their mutual attraction when the complainant was sitting on the living room couch, and that the sexual activity that followed was consensual. 

[5]               The complainant went to pick up her mother at around 8:30p.m.  She told her mother what had happened, and then contacted the police.  When the police came to arrest the appellant, the appellant waved a knife back and forth at the officers.  He slashed his wrist and throat with the knife and tried to run away, but was caught and taken into custody.

[6]               The appellant had committed a prior assault on the complainant on January 2, 1984.  At that time, the complainant was 16 years old and was visiting her mother and stepfather over the Christmas holiday.  She was asleep in the guest bedroom and awoke to find the appellant sitting on the edge of the bed, fondling her breasts.  When the complainant resisted, the appellant put one hand over her mouth and one on her throat.  He told her not to scream, and threatened to kill her and her mother.  When the complainant began to scream, the appellant started strangling her.  The complainant continued to struggle, and managed to run into her mother’s room.  The appellant was originally charged with sexual assault, but he pleaded guilty to common assault and received a suspended sentence and three years probation, which included a term of psychiatric counselling.  He was also asked to submit to a penile tumescence test, but he refused.

[7]               At trial, the trial judge admitted the prior assault as similar fact evidence and, over defence counsel’s objections, permitted the complainant to testify about the sexual aspects of the 1984 incident.  The trial judge held that the probative value of the evidence outweighed its prejudicial effect, and that it would be unfair to the jury not to have evidence before it that the appellant had made prior unwanted sexual advances toward the complainant.  He further held that an appropriate instruction could address any concerns that the jury might be misled or confused as to how to use the evidence in their deliberations. 

[8]               The appellant submits that the prejudicial effect of the evidence of the prior sexual assault was so great that the jury should only have been told that fourteen years previously the appellant had attempted to choke the complainant and, as a result, had been convicted of common assault.  Defence counsel at trial did not suggest the form of proposed editing now suggested on appeal.

[9]               In our view the Crown was entitled to introduce the sexual aspect of the prior conviction.  The appellant’s argument ignores the fact that much of the probative value of the evidence concerning the prior incident goes to the issue of consent and significantly, the credibility of the parties in relation to that critical issue.  The jury would not have had a true context in which to understand why the complainant told the appellant she was consenting to sex with him when he waved the knife at her without understanding that previously when the complainant rejected the appellant’s sexual advances he had resorted to choking her.  The evidence was also admissible to show that the appellant had a long-standing passion for the complainant, that he had a motive to commit the crime and to rebut the defence of innocent association.  The trial judge was alive to the question of probative value versus prejudicial effect and he did not err in principle in deciding to admit the evidence.  We would dismiss this ground of appeal.

2. Was the cross-examination of the Appellant on his psychiatric counselling and his refusal to consent to penile testing improper?

[10]          The appellant submits that the Crown’s cross-examination on these matters was only designed to leave the jury with a negative impression of the appellant and that this was a prejudicial area of inquiry unconnected to any fact in issue at trial. 

[11]          In cross-examining the complainant, the defence elicited from her that her understanding was that after the appellant was convicted of common assault he had to have “...prods on his penis with pictures of little girls to see how he reacts” and that the basis for this understanding was that her mother had told her this.  After eliciting this evidence the defence did not ask the trial judge to direct the jury to ignore the evidence on the basis of hearsay nor did he seek a mistrial.  The appellant also testified in response to a question by the defence that he had complied with his probation.

[12]          The evidence elicited by the Crown in cross-examining the appellant supported the complainant’s credibility in that it confirmed that the conviction for common assault had a sexual aspect to it.  The Crown was also entitled to test the appellant’s credibility concerning whether he had breached his probation.  In addition to having some probative value, the Crown’s cross-examination did not make matters significantly worse for the appellant.

[13]          Accordingly, save in respect of the charge of attempted anal intercourse, the appeal is dismissed.  The appeal in respect of attempted anal intercourse is allowed and the conviction is quashed.  The appellant acknowledges that the quashing of the conviction for attempted anal intercourse has no effect on the global sentence originally imposed by the trial judge.
