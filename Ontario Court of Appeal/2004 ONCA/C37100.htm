On appeal from the convictions entered by Justice Peter Grossi of the Superior Court of Justice, sitting with a jury, on February 25, 1999.

ARMSTRONG J.A.:

[1]               After trial before Justice Grossi of the Superior Court of Justice and a jury, the appellant was convicted of sexual assault, unlawful confinement and uttering a threat to cause death.  He appeals the convictions on the basis that the trial judge erred in admitting similar fact evidence.  This is the sole issue in this appeal. 

[2]               For the reasons that follow, it is my view that the similar fact evidence was properly admissible and the appeal should be dismissed.

(i)            Background

[3]               The victim in this case, A.G., was employed as a consultant in an office in the City of Toronto.  The appellant, a computer technician, worked in the same building as A.G. and from time-to-time serviced the computers in A.G.’s office.  According to A.G., the relationship between the appellant and her was “strictly business”.

[4]               On August 29, 1996, the appellant attended at A.G.’s office on a service call.  During this call, the appellant asked A.G. if she was a mother to which she replied that she was not.  The appellant also offered to install a Windows operating system onto her home computer.  This was the renewal of an offer made on a number of previous occasions.  The appellant said he would provide this service for $20 an hour and that it would take 3 to 4 hours to complete the installation.  Arrangements were then made for the appellant to attend at A.G.’s residence that night to install the Windows system.

(ii)      The Assault

[5]               The appellant arrived at A.G.’s residence at about 7:00 p.m.  He worked on the computer until he finished at about 10:45 p.m.  According to A.G., she went to her closet to take $80 out of her purse to pay the appellant.  The appellant then embraced A.G. from behind and covered her mouth with his hand.  She testified that when she had tried to pull the appellant’s hand away, he told her that he had a knife and would cut her if she struggled.  A.G. did not resist further and the appellant dragged her to her bed and raped her. 

[6]               After the appellant left the apartment, A.G. telephoned a girlfriend who then went to A.G.’s apartment and called the police.  A.G. was taken to Women’s College Hospital for examination.  Sperm was recovered from A.G. which, upon DNA analysis, was found to be consistent with that of the appellant. 

(iii)     The Similar Fact Evidence

[7]               In 1984, the appellant pleaded guilty to four counts of sexual assault with a weapon and one count of attempted sexual assault with a weapon.  Three of the women, who had been victims in the prior offences, testified at the trial of this matter. 

(a)            S.D.

[8]               On June 12, 1984, S.D. was babysitting at a house in Toronto when the appellant came to the door, allegedly to see a car that had been advertised for sale by the owner of the house.  S.D. invited the appellant into the house and once inside, he attacked S.D. and put his hand over her mouth and tried to drag her upstairs.  The appellant had a knife and threatened to kill her.  When a child of the house returned for lunch, the appellant fled. 

(b)            D.S.

[9]               On June 18, 1984, the appellant attended at the house of D.S. in Toronto.  He told D.S. that he was responding to a newspaper advertisement concerning the sale of furniture.  He asked to see a double bed and was taken into a bedroom by D.S.  While there, he immediately slammed the door and grabbed D.S. around the chest from behind and held a knife to her throat.  He threatened to slash her throat if she screamed.  He ordered her to undo her pants and she obeyed but requested that he not do anything until she could get her child out of the house.  The appellant agreed.  She was thus able to free herself and ran upstairs screaming for the landlady.  The appellant fled. 

(c)            G.S.

[10]          On June 21, 1984, the appellant attended the house of G.S. in Toronto – again allegedly in response to an advertisement offering an automobile for sale.  He inquired about the car’s registration and was invited into the house.  While in the house, the appellant grabbed G.S. around the neck.  As G.S. attempted to loosen his grip, she was badly cut by a knife which the appellant was holding.  A struggle ensued which concluded with the appellant raping G.S., following which he tied her up with a telephone wire and gagged her with a towel.

(iv)      The Defence

[11]          The appellant denied the charges against him.  He testified that he struck up a friendship with A.G. in the summer of 1996.  He said that they engaged in consensual sexual intercourse on five or six occasions.

[12]          The appellant acknowledged that he went to A.G.’s apartment to install a Windows system in her computer on August 29, 1996.  He said that after he finished the job, he engaged in sex with A.G.  After they had sex, he told A.G. that he was terminating the relationship.  He also told her that he was living with another woman.  At that point, according to the appellant, A.G. became angry and he left the apartment. 

(v)       The Ruling on the Similar Fact Evidence

[13]          The ruling on the similar fact evidence was made by the trial judge in an earlier trial and was adopted by the trial judge in the second trial.  I will therefore refer to the trial judge in the first trial as the motions judge.  The motions judge’s consideration was informed by the judgment of Charron J.A. in R. v. B. (L.) [1997], 35 O.R. (3d) 35 (C.A.).  In particular, the trial judge cited the following passage from the judgment in R. v. B. (L.) at p. 43:

   Because of the inherently prejudicial nature of evidence of discreditable conduct, it is subject to a general exclusionary rule unless the “scales tip in favour of probative value”.  The trial judge who is charged with the delicate process of balancing the probative value of the proposed evidence against its prejudicial effect should inquire into the following matters.

1. Is the conduct, which forms the subject-matter of the proposed evidence, that of the accused?

2. If so, is the proposed evidence relevant and material?

3. If relevant and material, is the proposed evidence dis-creditable to the accused?

4. If discreditable, does its probative value outweigh its prejudicial effect?

The motions judge answered each of the aforesaid questions in the affirmative.

[14]          The motions judge found that the facts in the three earlier cases displayed a striking similarity to the allegations before the court.  In this regard, he said:

In all four cases the accused had arranged a legitimate pretense of gaining entry to the house.

In all cases the women were alone when the attacks occurred.  all were of the similar age, 24 to 30 years.

In three of the cases the attacks occurred, or are alleged to have occurred in the bedroom.  In the first case the accused was attempting to drag the victim to the bedroom.

In each case, the accused came to the home armed with a knife.

In each case the accused approached the victim from behind and threatened her with his knife.  He forced one of the previous victims to remove her pants while he had vaginal intercourse with her with ejaculation and without a condom, exactly in the same manner as in the present allegation.

Two of the earlier victims were fortunately able to escape prior to the accused having an opportunity to complete forced intercourse.

In no case did the accused attempt to hide his identity.

[15]          The motions judge concluded his analysis as follows:

   In my view, the similarity of the acts are compelling.  It establishes a pattern of behaviour, or a system of conduct by the accused, and is relevant and material to rebut a defence of false allegation or consent or belief in consent.

   The evidence is discreditable, but the probative value outweighs its prejudicial effect in the circumstance of this case.

(vi)      The Appeal

[16]          The appellant submits that the motions judge overstated the common features between the offence charged and the proposed similar fact evidence.  He argues that the motions judge failed to appreciate that the circumstances surrounding the appellant’s attendance at the complainant’s residence were significantly dissimilar from the similar fact evidence.  He points out that the appellant was known to the complainant and attended the residence at her invitation.  He also submits that the trial judge failed to consider the absence of a temporal connection between the earlier sexual assaults and the offence charged.  There is a gap of some 12 years.

[17]          The appellant further submits that, apart from the features of the assault, the identified similarities were limited to generalities consistent with consensual sexual activity which could not support a finding that the proposed evidence was so strikingly similar. 

[18]          In this case, the similarities arise by reason of the modus operandi of the appellant:

(i) a legitimate pretext to gain entry to the victim’s residence;

(ii) the sneak attack from behind;

(iii) the use of a knife; and

(iv) the use of a threat of physical harm with the knife.

[19]          I also observe that while the motions judge did find that the prior events were strikingly similar to the alleged offence, it was unnecessary for him to do so.  Recent judgments of the Supreme Court of Canada suggest that a finding of striking similarity is reserved for cases where identity is the issue.  See R. v. Arp, [1998] 3 S.C.R. 339 at para. 43; and R. v. Handy, [2002] 2 S.C.R. 908 at para. 79.

[20]          The appellant also submits that the similar fact evidence was of limited probative value on the issue of whether the admitted sexual activity occurred without the complainant’s consent.  I disagree.  In my view, there was no error on the part of the motions judge in admitting the similar fact evidence for the purpose of rebutting the defence of consent.

[21]          The appellant submitted that the motions judge erred in failing to appreciate the prejudice resulting from the admission of the similar fact evidence.  He argues that such evidence presumes “an irresistible invitation to infer guilt from bad character”.   In my view, the evidence was relevant and compelling.  I agree with the motions judge that its probative value outweighed its prejudice.  Further, the suggestion that the evidence would be used by the jury to infer guilt from bad character was a matter to be dealt with by the judge in his charge to the jury.  In this respect, the trial judge told the jury:

You must not use this evidence to conclude that Mr. Whitehead is a bad man deserving of punishment or a man who has a propensity to engage in this type of conduct.

 I note that this appeal involves no objection to the jury charge. 

[22]          Finally I note that as judges sitting in appeal we “must show a high degree of deference to a trial judge’s decision on the application of the similar fact evidence rule.”  See R. v. B. (L.), supra at p. 59.  I see no basis to interfere with the motions judge’s ruling in this case.

[23]          I would therefore dismiss the appeal.
