On appeal from the conviction by Justice Rose T. Boyko of the Superior Court of Justice dated February 21, 2002 and sentence imposed by Justice Boyko dated March 4, 2002.

ROSENBERG J.A.:

[1]         The appellant appeals his convictions by Boyko J. for sexual assault and sexual interference and from the sentence of fifty-four months imprisonment.  The complainants in this case were the appellant’s son and stepdaughter.  The appellant’s son alleged a single act of anal penetration when he was four or five years of age.  The trial judge convicted the appellant of sexual interference in relation to this act and stayed the charge of sexual assault.  The appellant’s stepdaughter alleged that the appellant sexually assaulted her over a period of several years.  The trial judge convicted the appellant of sexual interference and sexual assault in relation to this continuing abuse.  An important part of the stepdaughter’s story revolved around the appellant’s use of child pornography.  The police were able to determine that the appellant’s computer was used to access pornography including child pornography of a type described by the complainant.

[2]         The appellant submits that the trial judge erred in preventing his counsel from cross-examining one of the complainants on a report from a counsellor and in refusing to admit the evidence of a defence expert in child behaviour.  The appellant also submits that the verdicts are unreasonable and in the alternative that the trial judge’s reasons are inadequate, disclose a misapprehension of the evidence and demonstrate that she more strictly scrutinized the defence evidence than Crown evidence. 

[3]         In my view, the appeal from conviction in respect to the appellant’s son should be allowed and an acquittal entered.  I would dismiss the appeal from conviction as it relates to the appellant’s stepdaughter.  To reflect the result of allowing the conviction appeal in part, I would reduce the total sentence in relation to the stepdaughter (counts three and four in the indictment) to forty months imprisonment.

FACTS

[4]         The complainants in this case were C.C., the appellant’s son, and K.C., the daughter of the appellant’s second wife.  The appellant had two children from his first marriage, C.C. and another son, B.C.  The allegations of sexual abuse came to light on the Thanksgiving weekend in 1999.  At that time, the appellant’s sons were visiting with the appellant, his wife and her daughter, K.C.  C.C. and K.C. were ten years old at this time.  On Sunday October 11th, the three children went to a park where they played a game of “truth or dare” in which they told each other false stories.  They then exchanged their “deepest and darkest secrets”.  At this time, K.C. said that years ago the appellant had pulled down her pants and touched her “bum”.  After hearing this, C.C. told the other children that years ago the appellant had put chocolate syrup on his “bum” and then licked it off.  C.C. said that until he heard K.C.’s story he believed that he had dreamt this incident. 

[5]         The following day, B.C. told his mother about the disclosures by the other two children.  She contacted police and C.C. was interviewed by the police on October 18, 1999.  The following day the police contacted K.C. and she gave the first of three video taped statements.  The police then contacted the appellant’s wife and she in turn contacted the appellant and told him that K.C. said he had touched her.  The police charged the appellant later that day.

            The allegation by C.C.

[6]         The story told by C.C. at trial was quite different from the secret he disclosed to his brother and K.C. and as disclosed in a videotaped statement C.C. gave to the police on October 18, 1999.  He testified that when he was four or five years old, the appellant told him to go to his, the appellant’s, bedroom.  In the bedroom, the appellant told him to get on the bed and bend over.  The appellant stood behind him and pulled C.C.’s pants down and C.C. felt something go into his “butt”.  The incident lasted a couple of minutes.  A few days later, the appellant and C.C.’s mother had a fight and the appellant left the home.  C.C. and his brother continued to see the appellant every second weekend.

[7]         As indicated, when he talked to his brother and K.C., C.C. described one incident involving chocolate sauce.  In his videotaped statement, C.C. said that he was sodomized twice within a week and on one occasion the appellant used a lubricant, which C.C. thought was chocolate sauce.  He also told the police that the events had become blurred since they were so long ago and he thought it was a dream until he heard what K.C. said the appellant had done to her.  At trial, C.C. testified that he had been nervous when he spoke to the police.  He also testified that he was trying to block out the events from his memory.

            K.C.’s allegations

[8]         K.C. was seven years old when the appellant moved in with her and her mother.  During most of this time, the appellant was not working and he would babysit K.C. while her mother was at school or working.  For most of 1999, her mother was working from 3:00 p.m. to 10:00 p.m.  In July 1999, the appellant began to attend school, taking a computer course.

[9]         The first incident of sexual abuse by the appellant occurred when K.C., her mother, and the appellant were visiting a friend of the appellant’s for the weekend.  It appears this visit occurred around Christmas 1997.  On Saturday morning, C.C. came out of the bedroom she was sharing with the daughter of the appellant’s friend and found the appellant lying on the couch watching television.  The appellant told her to lie beside him and said “if you take off your pants and show me your bum, I’ll show you mine”.  K.C. pulled down her underwear and then lay with the appellant under the blanket.  The appellant put his hand on her “bum”.  He later stood in front of the television and pulled down his pants.  The appellant denied committing this act.  He testified that he stayed up all night, talking and drinking.  He also adduced evidence that there was no television in his friend’s basement.

[10]          K.C. testified that after this first incident there were many occasions when the appellant masturbated by rubbing his penis between her thighs or against her buttocks.  Usually they would be alone in the basement of their home.  She would be lying down on the couch in front of him with her back against his stomach.  She would be naked or her clothes would be down by her ankles.  On some occasions the appellant had K.C. dress seductively while he masturbated.  She described one incident when she wore a very small denim skirt without underwear and the appellant had her walk on her hands and knees like a cat.  On another occasion the appellant had her wear the skirt and take up “weird” poses while he masturbated.  K.C.’s mother (the appellant’s second wife) testified for the defence that this denim skirt was too small for K.C. to have been able to wear it and she actually used it as clothes for her doll. 

[11]          On another occasion when the complainant and the appellant were going to the store, she wanted to explore a nearby forest.  They went into the forest and the appellant had her pull down her pants and underwear and he rubbed his penis against her buttocks.  On another occasion around the 1999 Thanksgiving weekend she was playing hide and seek with the appellant’s sons.  She went down to the computer room as part of the game and found the appellant in front of his computer masturbating while viewing pornography.  He had the complainant come in, pull her panties down and sit on his lap.  He then rubbed his penis between her thighs.

[12]          In another incident, her mother was cooking in the kitchen and she and the appellant were lying on the couch in the living room.  There was an opening between the kitchen and the living room.  The appellant pulled down the complainant’s pants and his pants and rubbed his penis between her legs.  The complainant’s mother testified that she did not see any such incident.

[13]          The complainant also testified to several incidents of fellatio and cunnilingus and to two incidents when the appellant penetrated her anus with his penis.  She was very sore after the first incident of anal penetration.

            The pornography

[14]          K.C, testified that the appellant had her look at adult and child pornography on the “Web TV” and on his computer.  He told her that he wanted her to do things like that to him.  On one occasion, however, the complainant found the appellant watching pornography on the Web TV and the appellant told her to leave.  In his testimony, the appellant remembered this incident.  He claimed that he had been “surfing the net” and accidentally came across pornography.  When he noticed that the complainant was in the room he was embarrassed.  He did not believe that the complainant actually saw anything.

[15]          The appellant and his wife testified that they monitored the complainant’s use of the Web TV and the computer.  The appellant installed a parent block on the complainant’s user name on the Web TV so that she was restricted to children’s sites.

[16]          The appellant purchased a computer in August 1991 and installed Internet service on that computer in mid-September.  The computer was seized by the police when the appellant was arrested.  A computer forensic analyst was able to retrieve images of pornography from the computer’s cache files that had been stored and later deleted in the days prior to the appellant’s arrest.  Over 800 images of child and adult pornography had been accessed on the computer in October.  The images of child nudity and child pornography constituted over 80% of the pornographic images.  The bulk of the images had been stored under a profile user name that the appellant eventually admitted under cross-examination he had created.  The appellant’s testimony with respect to the pornography was generally unsatisfactory.  He initially denied knowing anything about pornography on the Internet.  In cross-examination he admitted to intentionally going to adult and “teen” pornography sites but denied accessing any of the child pornography found on his computer. 

[17]          The complainant and her mother denied that they accessed child pornography sites. 

[18]          K.C. testified that when her mother was out, the appellant would sit on the couch and look at pornography in the basement rec room.  She described in graphic detail some  scenes she had been shown involving adults, teens and children.  Several of the images retrieved from the appellant’s computer were similar to images described by K.C.

[19]          The computer expert testified that someone attempted to delete the pornography files from the computer in the time between the disclosure of the sexual abuse by the children and the appellant’s arrest.  The appellant adduced evidence to show that on at least one occasion he was not home when child pornography images were accessed and an attempt made to delete them.  The appellant’s computer teacher testified that the appellant would know how to permanently delete files from the computer.

            The defence

[20]          The appellant testified and, as indicated, denied committing any of the sexual acts alleged by his son and by K.C.  He also adduced evidence from other witnesses including his wife, K.C.’s mother, to demonstrate the improbability of certain of the acts alleged by the complainant.  When she testified, the appellant’s wife was involved in litigation in the family court to obtain custody of K.C. and her newborn child who had both been removed by the Children’s Aid Society when the charges were laid. 

[21]          The appellant also attempted to introduce the evidence of a psychologist, Dr. Moore, to testify about techniques for interviewing children.  The complainants had given several statements to the police prior to trial.  Their versions of events changed from one interview to the next and again at the preliminary inquiry and the trial.  In C.C.’s case, his memory of the events had faded by the time of the trial.  In K.C.’s case, the number of incidents and the detail increased over time.

[22]          Dr. Moore testified on a voir dire about proper techniques for interviewing children.  He had viewed the videotaped interviews, read the preliminary inquiry transcript and some of the trial transcripts.  He found a number of errors in the interviewing techniques.  The officer did not establish a base line narrative that attempts to make an appraisal of the child’s communicative abilities by asking the child to provide an account about some event such as a birthday party.  The danger of not getting the baseline narrative is that it becomes difficult to interpret subsequent testimony if the testimony is disjointed or disorganized or self-contradictory.  The court will not be certain whether the nature of the testimony is a consequence of poor narrative skills or a reflection of unreliable information being reported.  The interviewer also did not establish with the children the importance of telling the truth at the outset of the interview.  This was particularly important in this case because of the context in which the disclosure came out.

[23]          Dr. Moore was also concerned that on some occasions the interviewing officers used leading or suggestive questions.  For example, the interviewer was the first one to use the term “touching of private parts” in the interview with K.C.  As well, with C.C. in the October 18th, 1999 statement, the officer was the first one to ask whether the appellant had touched his “bum”.  The problem with using such questions is that one cannot be certain whether the information is reliable but once the child has said it, he or she will tend to be consistent.  He was also concerned about the use of reinforcement, meaning comments by the interviewer that the child could construe as praise for their testimony.  For example, the interviewer told K.C. that she was doing a great job when she produced incriminating statements.  She was also told to “keep up the good work”.  Such comments are ill-advised because they could be misconstrued by the child.  Another problem was the interviewer’s failure to follow up on inconsistencies.  Dr. Moore was also critical of the witnesses reviewing their preliminary inquiry transcripts before testifying. 

THE GROUNDS OF APPEAL

[24]          The appellant raised the following grounds of appeal:

(1)            The trial judge erred in refusing to permit defence counsel to cross-examine K.C. on counselling reports that were in the appellant’s possession.

(2)            The trial judge erred in refusing to admit Dr. Moore’s evidence.

(3)            The verdicts are unreasonable.

(4)            The trial judge’s reasons are inadequate.

(5)            In her reasons, the trial judge erroneously placed an onus on the appellant, applied a stricter standard of proof to the appellant, misapprehended the evidence, and considered an irrelevant factor.

(6)            The sentence is outside the appropriate range.

ANALYSIS

            Cross-examination on counselling records

[25]          Near the conclusion of his cross-examination of K.C., counsel for the appellant sought to cross-examine her on a report prepared by a social worker, Mr. Fair.  K.C. had seen Mr. Fair between December 15, 1999 and February 16, 2000 to receive counselling as a result of her complaint of sexual abuse by the appellant.  After the charges were first laid, K.C. had been apprehended by the Children’s Aid Society and had been referred to Mr. Fair by the C.A.S.  The report was prepared by Mr. Fair for use in the child protection proceedings.  The appellant was given a copy of this report and there is no suggestion that the appellant did not have lawful possession of the report.

[26]          Crown counsel at trial objected to the use of the report to cross-examine the complainant.  The trial judge held that the report was a personal information record and its use was governed by the personal information record provisions of the Criminal Code in ss. 278.1ff.  She held that these sections had supplanted the common law test for admission of defence evidence set out in R. v. Seaboyer (1991), 66 C.C.C. (3d) 321 (S.C.C.).  At common law, the court may exclude otherwise admissible defence evidence only where the prejudicial effect of the evidence substantially outweighs its probative value.  However, under the personal information record provisions of the Criminal Code, the court must balance the privacy and equality rights of the complainant with the accused’s right to make full answer and defence.  The trial judge reviewed at length the purpose for which the appellant sought to use the Fair report.  She concluded that its use was not necessary for the appellant to make full answer and defence.  The report had very little probative value and there was not much new in the report that had not already been disclosed to the appellant and that he would have used in preparing for trial.  Weighed against the slight probative value of the report was the societal interest in encouraging young complainants to report sexual abuse and seek counselling.  Finally, the trial judge concluded that “even if the common law test was used, there is substantial prejudice to the complainant’s rights outweighing the value of the evidence to the defence”.

[27]          When she made her decision, the trial judge did not have the benefit of the decision of the Supreme Court of Canada in R. v. Shearing (2002), 165 C.C.C. (3d) 225 (S.C.C.).  In Shearing, the Court held that ss. 278.1 to 278.9 concern the production of personal information records and therefore do not apply to the admissibility of records already in the possession of the accused.  In this court, counsel for the respondent Crown submitted that Shearing was not determinative of the issue in this case.  She pointed out that Shearing involved a diary kept by the complainant, not therapy records, and referred us to a portion of para. 95 of the reasons of Binnie J. as follows:

Here the state is not using its coercive power to compel production in ways which invoke s. 8 of the Charter ("unreasonable search or seizure"). Nor does the present case raise Parliament's specific concerns in ss. 278.1 to 278.9 about protecting the confidential relationship of a patient-therapist, nor the encouragement of victims of sexual assault to seek professional treatment, and in turn not to discourage reporting of sexual offences. (See s. 278.5(2)(f) and (g).)  [Emphasis added.]

[28]          In view of the wording of ss. 278.1 to 278.9 and the holding in Shearing, I have little doubt that those sections do not apply to records already lawfully in the possession of the accused.  As Binnie J. points out in Shearing, the procedure in those sections concerns production of records, not their use or admissibility.  That is not to say that in measuring the prejudicial effect of the use of the records under the Seaboyer test for the purpose of determining their admissibility, a trial judge cannot at least take into account the complainant’s very high privacy interests in records relating to a therapeutic relationship. See R. v. Mills (1999), 139 C.C.C. (3d) 321 (S.C.C.) at para. 94.

[29]          In any event, it is not necessary to finally decide this issue since I am satisfied that the trial judge was right to exclude the evidence on the basis that its prejudicial effect substantially outweighed its probative value.  As the trial judge found, the Fair report had only slight probative value.  Most of the matters upon which the appellant sought to cross-examine the complainant, such as her animus towards the appellant, had already been fully disclosed through other means, such as K.C.’s statements to the police and cross-examination of K.C. at trial.  The appellant also sought to cross-examine K.C. on the manner in which she described the abuse as related in the Fair report.  For example, she is reported to have said that the appellant never “hurt” her.  However, in context, it is apparent that the “hurt” refers to physical injury, not sexual abuse.  The appellant also wanted to cross-examine the complainant on the fact that as reported by Mr. Fair, when asked what was the “grossest thing” that the appellant did, K.C. said it was watching child pornography on the Internet involving sexual activity with children.  Again, in context, this comment attributed to the complainant does not detract from her complaints of sexual abuse by the appellant.  It would have slight, if any, probative value in relation to the complainant’s credibility.

[30]          On the other hand, the use of the report in these proceedings did have a substantial prejudicial effect in distorting fact finding and interfering with an important therapeutic relationship. 

[31]          Accordingly, I would not give effect to this ground of appeal.

            Exclusion of Dr. Moore’s evidence

[32]          The trial judge gave lengthy and detailed reasons for holding that Dr. Moore’s evidence was inadmissible.  In summary, she found that the evidence did not meet the necessity element of the test as set out in R. v. Mohan (1994), 89 C.C.C. (3d) 402 (S.C.C.).  She held that the observations Dr. Moore made on the voir dire were apparent from the video taped interview and a lay person could make the same observations and take them into account in assessing the probative value of the complainants’ evidence. 

[33]          We did not call on Crown counsel to respond to this ground of appeal.  Most of Dr. Moore’s testimony concerned matters that were self-evident, such as the effect of leading questions, refreshing a witness’s memory, the need for the child to understand the duty to tell the truth and failing to explore inconsistencies.  This is the stock and trade of what trial judges do day in and day out.  Thus, most of Dr. Moore’s proposed evidence would have been of no additional assistance to the trial judge.  Even if the trial judge erred in excluding the evidence, that ruling could not have prejudiced the appellant.  Through the voir dire, the trial judge was made aware of the Dr. Moore’s concerns about the manner in which the interviews were conducted and she was able to and did expressly take them into account in assessing the complainants’ credibility. 

            Reasonableness of the verdicts

[34]          In my view, the verdict respecting C.C. was unreasonable and must be set aside.  There was direct evidence from C.C. about the sexual act committed upon him by the appellant.  The issue therefore is whether the trial judge’s finding in favour of C.C.’s credibility was reasonable. This court’s power to set aside a conviction on the basis that the trial judge’s assessment of credibility was unreasonable is limited.  The test was described by McLachlin J. in R. v. W. (R.), (1992), 74 C.C.C. (3d) 134 (S.C.C.) at 141-42:

It is thus clear that a court of appeal, in determining whether the trier of fact could reasonably have reached the conclusion that the accused is guilty beyond a reasonable doubt, must re-examine, and to some extent at least, reweigh and consider the effect of the evidence. The only question remaining is whether this rule applies to verdicts based on findings of credibility. In my opinion, it does. The test remains the same: could a jury or judge properly instructed and acting reasonably have convicted? That said, in applying the test the Court of Appeal should show great deference to findings of credibility made at trial. This court has repeatedly affirmed the importance of taking into account the special position of the trier of fact on matters of credibility: … The trial judge has the advantage, denied to the appellate court, of seeing and hearing the evidence of witnesses. However, as a matter of law it remains open to an appellate court to overturn a verdict based on findings of credibility where, after considering all the evidence and having due regard to the advantages afforded to the trial judge, it concludes that the verdict is unreasonable.

[35]          There are serious problems with the reliability of C.C.’s evidence. First, there were two significant inconsistencies that went to the core of C.C.’s allegations. By the time of trial, he had no recollection of the use of chocolate syrup and he now alleged only one incident. He provided no explanation for these significant discrepancies and, significantly, the trial judge did not find any explanation. These discrepancies were not in respect of minor or peripheral matters. Second, the allegations date back to when C.C. was only five years of age and the manner in which his memory was revived, through the “truth or dare” game, tends to undermine the reliability of the allegations. Finally, unlike the case with K.C., there is not a single piece of evidence to support the allegations. The trial judge did not explain why C.C.’s evidence would be found reliable in the light of these significant problems. 

[36]          In R. v. Biniaris (2000), 143 C.C.C. (3d) 1 (S.C.C.) at para. 37, Arbour J. explained the manner in which an appellate court should exercise the unreasonable verdict jurisdiction:

The review for unreasonableness on appeal is different, however, and somewhat easier when the judgment under attack is that of a single judge, at least when reasons for judgment of some substance are provided. In those cases, the reviewing appellate court may be able to identify a flaw in the evaluation of the evidence, or in the analysis, that will serve to explain the unreasonable conclusion reached, and justify the reversal.  [Emphasis added.]

[37]          The fact that the trial judge did not explain why C.C.’s evidence should be accepted despite its apparent unreliability is demonstrative of the flaw in the evaluation of the evidence and the analysis.  In my view, the evidence of C.C. was so fraught with problems that a verdict of guilty was unreasonable. 

[38]          I reach a different conclusion with respect to K.C.  The expert evidence establishing that the appellant’s computer had been used to access child pornography of the type described by K.C. stood as compelling confirmation of her evidence. Her account did not suffer from the same type of inconsistencies that undermined C.C.’s account.

            Adequacy of the reasons

[39]          The appellant submits that the trial judge’s reasons were inadequate because she failed to address a number of serious frailties and inconsistencies in the evidence of both of the complainants.  In view of my conclusion regarding C.C., I only need to deal with this ground of appeal as it affects K.C.

[40]          The trial judge dealt expressly with the four major inconsistencies raised by the appellant.  She explained how she resolved the inconsistencies.  I do not need to explore all four of these concerns raised by the appellant.  One example of the trial judge’s approach will suffice.  The appellant submits that the trial judge failed to deal with the fact that the complainant alleged the appellant had her wear a particular skirt, which according to her mother, would have been too small for her to have worn.  But the trial judge did consider this apparent inconsistency.  She noted that the complainant herself acknowledged that the skirt no longer fit her.  However, as the trial judge noted, the issue was whether K.C. could physically get into the skirt for the appellant’s purposes of having her expose herself, not whether she would have worn it in normal circumstances.  The trial judge had the advantage of observing the complainant’s size against the various descriptions of the skirt.  She was satisfied that it was entirely possible that she was able to wear it in the manner described.  The trial judge was also very sceptical of K.C.’s mother’s evidence in view of her own agenda with the pending child protection proceedings.

[41]          I am satisfied that the trial judge also satisfactorily resolved the other concerns with K.C.’s account.  She dealt at length with these apparent inconsistencies and, unlike the case with C.C., satisfactorily explained why she still accepted K.C.’s evidence.  I would not give effect to this ground of appeal.

            Errors in the reasons

[42]          The appellant submits that the trial judge made several legal errors in her assessment of the evidence.  I will deal with those alleged errors as they relate to K.C.’s allegations.

            (a)            The burden of proof

[43]          The appellant submits that the trial judge erroneously placed the burden on him to explain some of the Crown evidence and placed a stricter standard of proof on him than on the complainant.  The appellant particularly relies upon a passage from the trial judge’s reasons dealing with the computer evidence where the trial judge stated that the appellant did not “make any real effort to contradict [his wife’s denial that she created and deleted the child pornography files], he simply says he denies any knowledge about the pornography files just as his wife has done.  This rings rather hollow.”  Taken in isolation this passage could be considered problematic suggesting that there was some duty on the appellant to explain his wife’s evidence.  However, this passage is part of a lengthy discussion of the computer evidence where the trial judge gave detailed reasons for finding that it was the appellant who accessed the child pornography.  In particular, she noted that the appellant was the person in the household with the greater computer skills, he was admittedly a regular user of the computer, many of the files were created late at night when he was using the computer and he admitted viewing pornography sites, including “teen” pornography sites.  I am satisfied that the trial judge did not place any burden of proof on the appellant.  In context, the impugned passage is little more than a description of the evidence.

[44]          The appellant also submits that the trial judge subjected his evidence to a stricter standard of proof than the evidence of the complainant.  The appellant’s principal concern is that the trial judge was prepared to speculate in order to explain some of the inconsistencies in the complainant’s evidence but made no allowances for the appellant.  For example, the appellant submits that the trial judge erred in finding that the appellant was evasive when he testified about the use of the computer and the pornography.  In my view, these complaints about the trial judge’s reasons are not borne out when those reasons are considered in context.  What the appellant describes as speculation, as with respect to the skirt incident, are inferences the trial judge could draw from the evidence.  The trial judge’s assessment of the appellant’s evidence respecting the computer and pornography is fully supported by the record.  Initially, the appellant denied knowing anything about pornography on the Internet but then testified that 95 percent of the Internet is pornography and that the sites are all the same.  In cross-examination he eventually admitted coming across pornography banners and had “run across” adult and teen pornography.  At first, he said that he came across this pornography by accident and then conceded that he deliberately went to those sites.  He also admitted to being familiar with the sex search engines that the computer expert found had been used by a user of the computer.  This provided an adequate basis for finding that the appellant’s evidence with respect of pornography was evasive.

[45]          The appellant also submits that the trial judge was overly critical of the defence witnesses, including the appellant’s wife and a witness from the computer school the appellant attended for part of the relevant time.  In my view, the trial judge’s findings in relation to these witnesses were supported by the evidence.  There was evidence from which the trial judge could reasonably conclude that the witnesses were biased, not simply because of their relationship with the appellant.

            (b)            Reliance on an irrelevant factor

[46]          In her reasons, the trial judge took into account that K.C. was able to provide details of sexual acts.  She concluded that it was “unlikely that she could provide such detail if these things did not actually happen to her”.  The appellant submits that this is an improper inference because of the Criminal Code prohibition on questioning a complainant in a sexual case on sexual activity he or she may have engaged in.  He submits that there was a possibility that K.C. gained her knowledge of sexual matters from other sources.  There is no merit to this submission.  It was proper for the trial judge to take into account the fact that the young complainant was able to recount explicit sexual acts and that she was also able to describe in graphic detail the child pornography the appellant showed her from the Internet.  If there was some other source of this knowledge, other than from the appellant, it would have been open to the appellant’s counsel to explore the matter at trial in accordance with the provisions of s. 276 of the Criminal Code.

            (c)            Misapprehension of evidence

[47]          The appellant submits that the trial judge misapprehended crucial evidence concerning the pornography files found on the computer.  The appellant submits that the evidence is overwhelming that the appellant did not have the opportunity to delete certain of these files because independent evidence shows that he was at school when some of the files were deleted.  This evidence relates to the period between the complaint to C.C.’s mother and the appellant’s arrest about a week later. 

[48]          However, the trial judge’s reasons demonstrate that she understood this evidence.  She found that it was possible that the appellant’s wife had attempted to delete these files, while the appellant was attending classes.  The evidence showed that the appellant, with his expertise, would have been able to delete the files in a way that they could not have been retrieved by the police expert.  This supported the trial judge’s finding that some of the files were deleted by the appellant’s wife, perhaps because she knew that an investigation was underway.  This evidence did not undermine the more important finding that the appellant had accessed the child pornography sites.  It was still open to the trial judge to find that the appellant was the person in the household viewing the child pornography and that he had shown it to K.C.

[49]          The appellant also complains about several other alleged instances where the trial judge misapprehended the evidence.  These were minor matters and could not have affected the trial judge’s overall findings.

[50]          Accordingly, I would dismiss the appeal from the convictions in relation to K.C.

THE SENTENCE APPEAL

[51]          The appellant submits that the trial judge made several errors in principle in her reasons for sentence.  The most substantial submission is that the trial judge picked the wrong range of sentence.  The appellant submits that the appropriate range was three to four years imprisonment and that the appellant should then have been given credit for the equivalent of over twenty months imprisonment.  The appellant submits that in the result he was eligible for a conditional sentence of imprisonment.

[52]          The trial judge imposed sentences as follows:

Count four -  sexual assault K.C. six years

Count three - sexual interference K.C. three years concurrent

Count one - sexual interference C.C. three years concurrent

Count two - sexual assault C.C. stayed

[53]          The trial judge then deducted eighteen months taking into account seven and one-half months pre-sentence custody and three months when the appellant was on severe (house arrest) bail conditions.  In the result, the sentence was reduced to fifty-four months (four and one half years).

[54]          In my view, the global sentence was within the appropriate range for sexual abuse of two children with whom the appellant was in a position of trust.  The abuse of K.C. was particularly serious involving acts of increasingly greater intrusion over a lengthy period of time.  However, as I would set aside the conviction relating to C.C., it is necessary to consider the sentence anew.  In my view, taking into account the seriousness of the abuse of K.C. and its duration, an appropriate sentence would be in the range of four to five years less time spent in pre-trial custody.  I would reduce the sentence on count four to forty months, taking into account credit for pre-sentence custody.  The sentence on count three will stand.

DISPOSITION

[55]          Accordingly, I would allow the appeal in relation to count one (sexual interference on C.C.) set aside the conviction and enter an acquittal.  I would dismiss the appeal from conviction on counts three and four relating to K.C.  I would grant leave to appeal sentence, allow the appeal and reduce the sentence on count four to forty months imprisonment
