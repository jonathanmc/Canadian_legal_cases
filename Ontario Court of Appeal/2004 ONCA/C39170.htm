On appeal from the conviction by Justice John Macdonald of the Superior Court of Justice dated October 25, 2002 and sentence imposed by Justice Macdonald on December 16, 2002.

ROSENBERG J.A.:

[1]               The appellant appeals from his conviction by a court composed of Macdonald J. and a jury on charges of attempted rape and indecent assault.  He also appeals from the sentence totalling thirty-six months imprisonment.  At the conclusion of the oral arguments we indicated that the appeal from conviction was dismissed for reasons to follow.  We reserved our decision on the sentence appeal.  For the following reasons the appeal from sentence is allowed and the sentence is reduced to two years imprisonment.

[2]               The complainant is the appellant’s niece.  She was 45 years of age when she testified about events that occurred some thirty years before.  The complainant was brought up in a highly dysfunctional family marked by serious physical abuse by the father upon the complainant, her siblings and her mother.  The complainant would seek refuge in the basement of her family’s home.  She was a lonely child who was also mercilessly bullied at school.  But, in 1969 when she was twelve years of age, her uncle, the appellant, suddenly began to take an interest in the complainant.  He became friendly and attentive. 

[3]               This “friendship”, however, was simply a precursor to sexual abuse.  The complainant gave somewhat vague evidence of abuse alleged to have occurred prior to the charged incidents.  The abuse began with attempts by the appellant to show the complainant how to kiss.  This activity progressed to the appellant fondling the complainant’s breasts, sucking on them and hurting them.  He became more aggressive, with conduct including digital penetration and having the complainant masturbate him to ejaculation. 

[4]               The charges of indecent assault and attempted rape related to two specific incidents.  With respect to the indecent assault, the appellant repeatedly attempted to force his penis into the complainant’s mouth.  The complainant struggled very hard and kept her mouth closed so that the attempt failed.  With respect to the attempted rape, the appellant began to fondle the complainant’s breasts and vagina.  He then forcibly removed her clothing and attempted to force his penis into her vagina.  At the same time, he covered her mouth with his hand or tried to kiss her to keep her quiet.  The complainant described a fierce struggle in which she attempted to fight the appellant off while he repeatedly attempted to penetrate her.  Eventually, the appellant gave up and put his erect penis between the complainant’s thighs so he could simulate intercourse.  This incident lasted about thirty minutes.

[5]               All of the sexual abuse, including the charged incidents, took place in the basement when the complainant was twelve or thirteen years of age.  She made no complaint at the time and the incidents came to light many years later.  Although the appellant did not testify, it was the theory of the defence that it was implausible that the complainant would continue to seek refuge in the basement despite what she claimed was escalating abuse by the appellant.  The defence also sought to cast doubt on the reliability of the complainant’s evidence about the charged incidents given her uncertain memory of the other incidents.  The complainant conceded that she had only “snippets” of memory of these other events.

THE CONVICTION APPEAL

[6]               The appellant raises two grounds of appeal respecting conviction.  He contends that the trial judge erred in failing to give a limiting instruction concerning the similar fact evidence of other discreditable conduct described by the complainant.  He also submits that the trial judge erred in putting to the jury an unfounded Crown theory that the appellant was “grooming” the complainant.

[7]               The appellant concedes that the evidence of the other acts by the appellant was properly admitted as part of the narrative of events and to support the credibility of the complainant.  He submits however that the trial judge should have instructed the jury as to the limited use to which this evidence could be put. In particular, the appellant argues that the jury should have been instructed that it could not use this evidence simply to find that the appellant was the kind of person likely to have committed the charged offences.  No complaint is made about the trial judge’s charge to the jury concerning the use of the evidence of each of the charged counts on the other.

[8]               This submission must be put in its proper context.  During her examination in chief the complainant made only very brief and vague reference to the other sexual abuse.  It was only in cross-examination that there was any lengthy and detailed examination of the uncharged incidents to support the theory of the defence.  Experienced defence counsel did not object to the charge to the jury.  In those circumstances, no special instruction was required concerning the use of the other evidence.  This case is very much like R. v. Batte (2000), 145 C.C.C. (3d) 449 (Ont. C.A.), where this court held that no such instructions were required.  As in that case, neither the Crown nor the defence asked the trial judge to instruct the jury on the use of the evidence at issue.  While the evidence was capable of supporting the credibility of the complainant it was principally used by the defence to attempt to undermine the complainant’s other evidence.  Any direction by the trial judge that explained both the proper and improper uses of the propensity reasoning would have operated against the appellant’s interests.  There was, therefore, no error and no prejudice to the appellant.

[9]               The appellant’s submission concerning “grooming” also requires some explanation.  In his jury address, Crown counsel said the following:

He started treating her as special.  He is engaging, interested, friendly.  She had no friends.  She was lonely.  She thought he wanted to be her friend.  One might, one might suggest that at this point, you could look to the evidence and see that he is grooming her for sexual abuse.  Obviously, it’s up to you to decide if that’s in the evidence or not or it’s up to you to consider the issue.  [Emphasis added.]

[10]          In his charge to the jury, the trial judge also made use of the term grooming in explaining the theory of the Crown.

[11]          Counsel for the appellant makes two submissions on this issue.  First, he says that grooming is a recent phenomenon which did not exist thirty years ago.  He contends that it is unfair to take a recent concept and apply it to these dated events.  Second, relying upon a definition of grooming from Childnet International, he contends that the conduct of the appellant as described by the complainant did not amount to grooming.  There is no merit to these submissions.  The term “grooming” is merely a description of conduct where the perpetrator attempts to prepare the child victim for increasingly more intrusive sexual abuse.  It is not a new concept; it is simply a compendious description of a course of conduct.  The appellant’s conduct towards the complainant easily fits within the notion of grooming.  He befriended the lonely and isolated complainant.  He began with less intrusive conduct such as kissing and when he saw that she would not complain he resorted to increasingly more serious abuse. 

THE SENTENCE APPEAL

[12]          Sentencing of the appellant represented a special challenge in this case.  In 1983, the appellant pleaded guilty to one count of incest in respect of acts he committed against one of his daughters in 1981.  He was given a suspended sentence and three years probation.

[13]          The appellant is now 67 years of age.  He committed these offences around 1970 when he was approximately 34 years of age, before his 1983 conviction for incest.  Thus, he had no criminal record when the offences involving this complainant were committed. 

[14]          There is a further unusual feature of this case.  This was the second trial of the appellant on these offences.  In April 2001, the appellant was tried by a judge and jury on these two charges and two counts of gross indecency involving the same complainant but concerning events when the complainant was only nine years of age.  The jury convicted the appellant on the two counts of gross indecency but were unable to reach a verdict on the charges of indecent assault and attempted rape.  The appellant appealed his convictions and this court quashed the conviction on one of the counts of gross indecency but affirmed the conviction on the other.  The appellant was sentenced to a nine-month conditional sentence on that charge.  He served that sentence without incident.  Given the chronology, the trial judge treated the appellant as a first offender since he had not been convicted of any offences when he committed the offences of indecent assault and attempted rape.

[15]          On his appeal from sentence, the appellant makes four basic submissions.  He repeats his complaint about the use of the term “grooming” and says that there was insufficient evidence upon which the trial judge could treat the alleged grooming as an aggravating factor.  Second, he submits that the trial judge erred in finding that the appellant was in a fiduciary relationship with the complainant.  Third, he submits that the sentence was outside the appropriate range.  Counsel for the appellant has assembled a significant body of case law to demonstrate that the total sentence of three years imposed in this case was unfit.  Finally, counsel for the appellant has presented as fresh evidence a series of reports from various health care officials to demonstrate the impact a sentence of imprisonment would have on the physical and psychological health of the appellant.  The appellant has also filed a letter from his probation officer affirming his suitability for a non-custodial disposition.

            Grooming and fiduciary relationship

[16]          I find it convenient to deal with the issues of grooming and fiduciary relationship together.  The submission in both cases is the same.  The appellant says that there was insufficient evidence to find that either was an aggravating factor in this case.  There is no question that the trial judge found that the appellant’s grooming of his niece was an aggravating factor.  He also tended to link the concept of fiduciary relationship to what he found to be grooming behaviour.  He made several references to grooming and fiduciary relationship in his reasons, most importantly in the following:

I find that [the appellant] concocted the plan of grooming her for the purpose of achieving his sexual gratification.  He befriended her, for which, initially, she was very grateful, due to her own neediness.  He moved from teaching her how to kiss, to putting his tongue into her mouth, to fondling her breasts, to fondling her vagina, to inserting his fingers into her vagina and to having her masturbate him.

It kept getting more and more aggressive, in part, I find, because [the appellant] became more emboldened by [the complainant’s] lack of complaint or disclosure to others about his more preliminary violations of her.

I find that [the appellant] was in a position of authority towards [the complainant] as an adult member of this family, and that he made use of that position of authority to groom her as his sex object.

He began a year’s-long process of subjecting her gradually to his sexual desires.  In that grooming process, by making use of the blandishment of friendship towards a lonely child, by making use of his position of authority and her youthful subservience, he created a relationship of a fiduciary nature, given his power and her vulnerability in the context of the family relationship.  He created that fiduciary relationship for the purpose of violating it by violating her.

There is a technical distinction between a fiduciary relationship and a relationship of trust, but that is of little or no significance in the circumstances here.  This was clearly a trust-like relationship created by [the appellant] as a means of achieving his criminal objectives.

[17]          In my view, it was open to the trial judge to find that there was a pattern of behaviour and conduct that could properly be described as grooming, which was an aggravating factor the trial judge could take into account.  The trial judge could not, of course, sentence the appellant for the uncharged incidents, many of which could have been separately prosecuted as assaults or indecent assaults.  The grooming aspect of the conduct was relevant because that conduct showed an element of planning and premeditation that made the offences more serious.  In the passage from the trial judge’s reasons set out above, the trial judge explained both his approach to and the nature of the evidence he relied upon to find this planning and premeditation.  There was evidence to support this finding.  The trial judge was also careful to state that he could only sentence the appellant for the two offences upon which he was tried and convicted.

[18]          The finding of a fiduciary relationship is somewhat different.  As he recognized, the term “fiduciary relationship” has a technical meaning.  I am not convinced that there was such a relationship in the technical sense.  But, I am also convinced that the trial judge’s use of this term had no impact on the sentence.  The point was that the appellant was in a position of trust in relation to his niece.  He was a guest in the house by virtue of his status as the complainant’s mother’s brother.  It is apparent that because of this relationship he had easy access to the complainant.  The family was described as a very traditional Italian family in which female children would not have been left alone with older males, yet the appellant was trusted to be alone with the complainant.  He took advantage of his position of trust within the family in the manner described by the trial judge.  In his reasons, the trial judge tended to use the terms fiduciary relationship and relationship of trust and authority somewhat interchangeably but this did not give rise to a reversible error with respect to the sentence he imposed. What mattered to the trial judge was the trust-like relationship.

            The appropriate range

[19]          In determining the range for sentence in this case, the trial judge applied the reasons of this court in R. v. D. (D.) (2002), 163 C.C.C. (3d) 471 at para. 44.  In that case, this court held that adult offenders in a position of trust who sexually abuse children “on a regular and persistent basis over substantial periods of time, … can expect to receive mid to upper single digit penitentiary terms”.  The trial judge went on to state that he was actually imposing a sentence below the range suggested by the court in R. v. D. (D.) because of the evidence of the appellant’s reformation, especially since his conviction for incest, and because of the psychological and psychiatric evidence.  The trial judge also took into account that the maximum sentences for attempted rape and indecent assault were ten and five years respectively.  The trial judge held that but for these factors he would have imposed a sentence of five years in the penitentiary, in light of R. v. D. (D.).  He held that a penitentiary sentence was required principally as a matter of denunciation.  He rejected a conditional sentence.

[20]          In my view, the trial judge erred in principle in basing his sentence on the range suggested in R. v. D. (D.).  That case concerned a different type of offence and offender.  It dealt with an offender who had abused a number of children over a long period of time.  In R. v. D. (D.), the offender was convicted of eleven sexual offences involving four boys as a result of conduct spanning seven years.  In that case, the trial judge imposed an effective sentence of nine years.  One of the principal issues in R. v. D. (D.) concerned the impact of this court’s earlier decision in R. v. Stuckless (1998), 127 C.C.C. (3d) 225. In that case, the respondent, Stuckless, pleaded guilty to 24 counts of indecent assault and sexual assault committed over a 20-year period involving many young boys ranging in age from 10 to 15 years.  This court imposed a sentence of six years on a Crown appeal. While it is unnecessary to set out at length the nature of the conduct in the two cases, suffice it to say that Moldaver J.A. speaking for the court in R. v. D. (D.), found that R. v. Stuckless did not represent the high-water mark for sentencing for offenders who commit multiple offences involving many victims over a long period of time.  He also found that in any event the offences committed by D.D. were more serious than those committed by Stuckless.

[21]          It is apparent that both R. v. Stuckless and R. v. D. (D.) involve qualitatively different types of conduct and a different type of offender than in this case.  Without in any way minimizing the seriousness of the conduct involved in this case, and I will return to that below, the fact remains that the offences committed by the appellant here involved one victim and compared to R. v. Stuckless and R. v. D. (D.), were committed over a relatively short time.  The trial judge was wrong to consider that the range of “mid to upper single digit penitentiary terms” was the starting-point for sentencing in this case.

[22]          In light of this error in principle, it remains for this court to determine a fit sentence.  In my view, these offences were so serious that a penitentiary sentence was required, principally to denounce the conduct.  I have briefly described the circumstances of the offences in dealing with the conviction appeal.  In his reasons, the trial judge described the offences at considerably greater length.  I accept that description.  He described the attempted rape as a terrible, violent struggle in which the complainant fought with all her might to fend off the appellant.  He characterized the appellant’s conduct as a “frenzy of determined effort to rape her.  He did not succeed in entering her, despite repeated attempts.”  The incident lasted twenty to thirty minutes.  The trial judge described the indecent assault in these terms:

[The appellant] attempted to force his erect penis into her mouth.  He pushed her down and held her head.  She tried to get up.  He pushed her head down and held her chin up.  She struggled very hard.  She had her mouth clenched.  His penis was on her lips, and he struggled to insert it into her mouth.  She succeeded in preventing that, even though he held her head.  It was a terrible struggle and a terrible experience.

[23]          The offences have had a devastating impact on the complainant that is described in graphic and poignant terms in the victim impact statement.  The trial judge summarized some of the impact of the offences in these terms:

[The complainant], I find, has difficulty sharing intimacy with her husband and her children.  She continues to have flashbacks during moments of physical contact between husband and wife such that what is in her mind is the sexual abuse perpetrated by [the appellant].

In referring to the attempted rape, she mentions how vivid, still, is the terror she felt while he was attempting to perform his crime.  That is a terror with which she has lived now for over thirty years.  She still has nightmares about the attempted rape.

One of the global impacts as a result of his sexual abuse is that [the complainant] is overly protective of her children.  That is, in my view, an indication of how deep are the scars caused by [the appellant’s] crimes.

[The complainant] describes herself as “sentenced to a lifetime of shame, humiliation, anger, rage, distrust, deep sadness, turmoil and pain.” She struggles daily with the burden of [the appellant’s] actions. Only after therapy, in recent years, has she been able to see that what [the appellant] did was not her fault, and some of her deep feelings of despair are being alleviated.

[24]          The cases in this area have considered whether actual intercourse occurred as an important aggravating factor.  At the time this offence was committed, Parliament also made that distinction.  While the maximum sentence for rape was life imprisonment, the maximum sentence for attempted rape was ten years.  In R. v. B. (J.) (1990), 36 O.A.C. 307 (C.A.), Blair J.A. said the following:

The decisions of provincial appellate courts establish that, except in unusual circumstances, a penitentiary sentence is called for in all cases of sexual abuse of children to whom the convicted person stands in loco parentis if the abuse involves sexual intercourse. Such sentences reflect society's denunciation of this abhorrent conduct and the breach of trust reposed on parents or guardians of children. Both counsel agreed that the usual range of sentences for this type of offence is from three to five years. The length of sentence within the range of three to five years depends on a number of factors. These include the age of the victim, the duration and frequency of the sexual assaults, the criminal record of the offender, the effects on the victim and the presence or absence of collateral violence or remorse.

[25]          This case, while a serious example of attempted rape did not involve actual intercourse. Further, the appellant, while certainly in a position of trust, did not stand in loco parentis to the complainant.  The indecent assault was also a most serious example of that offence and warranted a severe sentence. 

            The fresh evidence

[26]          Counsel for the appellant presented this court with a very complete picture of the appellant’s present physical and mental health.  This material essentially updates the material that was before the trial judge.  I will briefly summarize that material.  Psychological testing confirms that the appellant does not have personality traits that predispose him to criminal activity.  He was very remorseful with respect to the incest committed against one of his daughters and has made every effort to rectify his actions by reconciling with his daughter.  He has attended individual and family counselling and has been free of any criminal activity since the incest conviction.  Other material confirms that the appellant and his daughter have reconciled and that his family is generally supportive of him.  At this time, the appellant is divorced and lives with his son. 

[27]          The appellant suffers from depression, high blood pressure and was being examined by physicians for possible coronary artery diseases.  He also suffers from irritable bowel syndrome, which is responsible for persistent and chronic abdominal complaints.  He is on medication to control heart palpitations and high blood pressure and for his abdominal complaints.

[28]          A report from a probation officer confirmed that the appellant completed his nine-month conditional sentence without incident.  He was described as a responsible individual who is deeply involved in his work.  He has also been caring for his ailing mother on a daily basis for the past decade.  His mother had only recently entered into long-term care when he was no longer to provide the care that she requires.  The probation officer was of the view that the appellant would benefit from a sentence in the community.

            A fit sentence

[29]          Although neither of these offences involved actual intercourse, in my view they required a penitentiary sentence.  The age of the complainant, the position of trust, the use of violence, the persistence with which the appellant pursued the commission of the offences, and the pattern of grooming the complainant, make these serious examples of attempted rape and indecent assault.  I agree with the trial judge that denunciation and general deterrence are the most important objectives of sentencing and that a conditional sentence would not meet these objectives.  There are, however, a number of factors that, in my view, require that the sentence be the minimum penitentiary term:

·        While the appellant’s subsequent conviction for incest cannot be wholly ignored, as the trial judge recognized, at the time these offences were committed the appellant had no prior criminal record.

·        Since the incest conviction over twenty years ago, the appellant has taken substantial steps to rehabilitate himself and he no longer represents a danger to the community or to children.

·        The appellant is 67 years of age and in frail health.

·        The appellant has already served a nine-month conditional sentence in relation to this same complainant for similar but less serious conduct.

·        These offences were committed over thirty years ago.

[30]          In my view, an appropriate sentence for these offences is two years imprisonment for the charge of attempted rape and one year concurrent for the charge of indecent assault.  The trial judge imposed consecutive sentences.  While this was not an error in principle, in my view, concurrent sentences are more appropriate given the fact that these two offences were part of a course of conduct involving the same complainant some thirty-three years ago.

DISPOSITION

[31]          Accordingly, I would dismiss the appeal from conviction.  I would grant leave to appeal sentence, allow the appeal and vary the sentence to two years imprisonment for attempted rape and one year imprisonment concurrent for indecent assault.
