LASKIN J.A.:

A.     Introduction

[1]               The appellant, David Baldwin, was charged with sexually assaulting J.M.; threatening to cause bodily harm to J.M.’s thirteen-year-old babysitter, C.K., during the sexual assault; and breaching a term of his probation prohibiting the possession and consumption of alcoholic beverages.  The appellant and the complainant met one evening at a hotel bar in Brockville.  The incidents giving rise to the first two charges occurred several hours later in the complainant’s apartment.

[2]               The appellant was tried before Lalonde J. and a jury.  On the sexual assault charge, the main issue was whether J.M. consented to the sexual activity that took place.  On the threatening charge, the main issue was whether the appellant had made the threat.  On the breach of probation charge, the evidence that the appellant possessed and drank beer was uncontradicted.  The appellant did not testify. 

[3]               The jury found the appellant guilty of sexual assault and breach of probation, but not guilty of threatening.  The trial judge found him to be a long-term offender and sentenced him to five years imprisonment and ten years supervision. 

[4]               The appellant appeals his conviction for sexual assault.  He makes three submissions:

1.   The trial judge erred by permitting the Crown to lead evidence that the appellant had declined to make a formal statement to the police;

2.   In his charge to the jury, the trial judge contravened s. 4(6) of the Canada Evidence Act, R.S.C. 1985, c. C-5 by referring to the appellant’s failure to testify; and

3.   The trial judge’s instructions to the jury on the issue of consent were so confusing that they deprived the appellant of a fair trial.

B.    Relevant Facts

(a) Facts leading up to the alleged sexual assault

[5]               J.M. was a single mother with three children.  She lived in the upstairs unit of an apartment building.  On the evening of January 13, 2001, she went drinking with some friends.  She left her three children and a friend’s two children with her babysitter, C.K.  J.M. left her apartment at about 11:00 p.m.  She and her friends went to the bar at the Commercial Hotel, went to another bar for a while, and then returned to the hotel bar, where they stayed until it closed at 2:00 a.m. 

[6]               At the hotel, J.M. was drinking with a group that included the appellant.  Although they had not met before, she and the appellant appeared to be good friends.  She sat on his knee while they talked.  He gave her a necklace.

[7]               After the bar closed, J.M., the appellant, and two others went back to J.M.’s apartment.  Their arrival awakened the children.  But soon the children fell asleep again.  The group began drinking beer that the appellant purchased at the hotel.  At one point, J.M. kissed the appellant. 

[8]               The group stayed at J.M.’s apartment for about half an hour.  Then the two guests went home. The appellant and J.M. went to her neighbour V.M.’s apartment on the main floor.  V.M. soon “kicked” the appellant out of her apartment because he put his hands on her shoulders and startled her.  The complainant stayed for a while.  She left around 4:30 a.m. and returned to her apartment, where, unexpectedly, she found the appellant asleep on her couch. 

(b) The alleged sexual assault

[9]               J.M. testified that she decided to let the appellant sleep at her place until morning.  However, she thought he looked uncomfortable so she woke him and suggested that he lie down.  He stood up and laid her on the couch.  She said nothing.  After lying on the couch for a while they “somehow” ended up lying on the floor. 

[10]          The appellant began rubbing J.M.’s arms, legs, and clothing.  Then he got on top of her.  She tried to push him off, unsuccessfully.  He unbuttoned her shirt, pulled up her tank top and bra, and removed her jeans and underwear.  She said, “No.  Don’t do this. Let’s talk.”  But the appellant did not stop.  He kissed her vagina and performed cunnilingus.  Again, she asked him to talk.  He asked what there was to talk about as she had been leading him on all night.

[11]          The appellant pinned J.M. to the floor.  She screamed.  He said to be quiet or he would “do the girl”.  When she screamed again, he threatened to tie her up, and make her watch him rape C.K.  He then penetrated her, but did not ejaculate.  She kept asking him to stop but he would not do so.  The appellant finally let J.M. up when she said she had to go to the washroom.  Then she said she had to go to V.M.’s place to get toilet paper.  As she left her apartment the appellant tried to stop her, but she managed to leave.  According to J.M., the appellant’s last words were, “Are you really going to [V.M.]’s to get toilet paper or are you going to call the cops?”

[12]          J.M. ran into V.M.’s apartment and claimed she had been raped.  She was hysterical.  She was wearing a tank top, a bra and a shirt, but nothing below the waist.  She asked V.M.’s boarder, G.T., to go to her apartment and tell the appellant to leave.  The appellant told G.T. that he and J.M. were “fooling around and she started freaking.”

[13]          C.K., the young babysitter, confirmed some but not all of J.M.’s account.  She gave evidence that after the group left the apartment she fell asleep on the living room floor.  She awoke to panicky screaming from a woman she later realized was J.M.  The screams were followed by moaning.  The moaning lasted a long time – perhaps several hours – until she heard J.M. say “get off me, you’re hurting me, I need to use the washroom”.  Later she heard J.M. leave the apartment, slamming the door behind her.  C.K. testified that she did not hear any threats directed towards her.  The jury likely accepted this testimony or at least concluded that it raised a reasonable doubt, because it acquitted the appellant on the threatening charge. 

C.    The Issues

First Issue: Did the trial judge err by permitting the Crown to lead evidence that the appellant had declined to make a formal statement to the police?

[14]          After his arrest, on the advice of his counsel, the appellant declined to make a formal statement to the police.  The investigating officer, nonetheless, engaged him in conversation.  During their conversation, the appellant said that the night before he had been on a binge.  In answer to the officer’s suggestion, he agreed that “[d]rinking plays a part in these things.” 

[15]          The Crown led the evidence of the investigating officer.  The officer testified that the appellant had been advised of his right to counsel and cautioned about making a statement.  On further questioning, Crown counsel elicited both the appellant’s refusal to make a statement and the appellant’s subsequent conversation with the officer:

MS. HEINE: Q: Officer, you had spoken to his lawyer and you asked him, I presume, about a statement?

A. Yes, I did.

Q. What happened then?

A. He declined.  He didn’t wish to make a statement at that time.

Q. What happened then?

A. I asked him for some particulars, his date of birth and he told me that he lived with his sister Cheryl and her...

Q.  I’m just going to stop you there, officer, because of a previous ruling, it is important that no evidence with respect to any past conduct be introduced at this point.  So I am just going to caution you not to do that.  Go ahead?

A.  He said that he lived with his sister Cheryl and her five year old son.  We had other conversation about his...

Q.  I understand he asked if he could go for a smoke?

A.  Yes, he did.  So he went into the garage so that he could have a smoke.  I asked him if he knew this girl Josie and he said no, that he met her that night.  He said, “I know I’m not getting out tomorrow.”  He told me he was on a “binge” last night.  I said, “Drinking plays a part in these things.”  He said, yes it did.  I asked him about some handcuffs that were in his possession when he was arrested. 

Q.  What response did he give you?

A.  He said he liked to be tied up by his girlfriend.  I asked him about a plastic tube that was also in his –  I believe it was in a bag he had been carrying.  He said that it was broken, it was a toy sword his girlfriend used to beat him with.  The only other conversation I noted was I asked him about his lunch because he didn’t eat lunch and he said he didn’t want anything to eat and it was at 2:05 p.m. he was returned to the cell.

Defence counsel did not object to any of this evidence. 

[16]          The appellant concedes the admissibility of his conversation with the officer.  However, he submits that the trial judge erred by permitting the Crown to lead evidence of his unwillingness, on the advice of counsel, to give a statement to the police.  He contends that this evidence was irrelevant and prejudicial.  He says that Crown counsel should simply have led the evidence of his informal conversation with the officer.

[17]          The appellant also submits that Crown counsel’s caution to the officer not to give evidence “with respect to any past conduct” exacerbated the prejudice.  The Crown’s caution, the appellant argues, gave the jury the impression that he had engaged in this conduct before.  Any words of caution should have been given in the absence of the jury. 

[18]          The principle that applies to this ground of appeal is well established.  Unless the Crown can demonstrate their relevance to an issue in the case, both the officer’s questions after cautioning an accused and an accused’s refusal to answer or give a statement are inadmissible.  To admit this evidence without good reason would undermine the accused’s right to silence.  In R. v. Chambers, [1990] 2 S.C.R. 1293 at 1316, Cory J. explained the rationale for the principle: 

It has as well been recognized that since there is a right to silence, it would be a snare and a delusion to caution the accused that he need not say anything in response to a police officer’s question but nonetheless put in evidence that the accused clearly exercised his right and remained silent in the face of a question which suggested his guilt. 

[19]          In setting out this principle, Cory J. relied on two decisions of this court, R. v. Robertson (1975), 21 C.C.C. (2d) 385 and R. v. Symonds (1983), 9 C.C.C. (3d) 225.  In Roberson at 400, Dubin J.A., (dissenting in part, but not on this issue) put the principle succinctly: 

In the absence of any issue raised by the defence, the mere silence of an accused during police interrogation cannot be said to advance the case for the Crown.  It cannot be a step on the way to the proof of the accused’s guilt.  It, therefore, becomes, in my opinion, irrelevant to any issue at trial.  To prove as part of the Crown’s case the fact that the accused has exercised his common law right to remain silent would constitute silence a trap if his silence is placed in evidence against him at trial.

[20]          In the case before us, Crown counsel relies on the caveat.  Ms. Hallett submits that evidence about the appellant’s unwillingness to make a formal statement was relevant to show the context in which he engaged in an informal conversation with the officer, a conversation defence counsel conceded was admissible. 

[21]          I do not accept this submission.  Crown counsel should have led only the evidence of the conversation.  No further context was needed.  That the accused declined to give a formal statement to the police was irrelevant to the jury’s appreciation of the appellant’s informal statements to the officer. 

[22]          I therefore conclude that the evidence of the appellant’s unwillingness to give a statement should not have been permitted, and the admission of this evidence amounted to an error of law.  However, I have also concluded that this error caused no substantial wrong.  For that reason I would apply the proviso in s. 686(1)(b)(3) of the Criminal Code.  I would do so for four reasons.

[23]           First, the Crown did not rely on this evidence to prove or even to suggest the appellant’s guilt.  Second, defence counsel neither objected to this evidence nor asked the trial judge to caution the jury about it.  Although defence counsel’s failure to object is never fatal to an argument on appeal, it often provides a reliable guide to whether evidence whose admissibility is now challenged caused any unfairness at trial.  Here, defence counsel did not perceive that the evidence prejudiced his client.  Third, the jury acquitted the appellant of the threatening charge, which was alleged to have occurred at the same time as the sexual assault.  The acquittal shows that the jury decided the case on the strength of the evidence and was not improperly influenced by evidence of the appellant’s unwillingness to give a formal statement during his police interview.  Fourth, the trial judge gave the jury the standard charge on the presumption of innocence.  For these reasons, I would not give effect to this ground of appeal. 

Second Issue: Did the trial judge contravene s. 4(6) of the Canada Evidence Act? 

[24]          The appellant did not testify.  Section 4(6) of the Canada Evidence Act prescribes that “the failure of the person charged...to testify shall not be made the subject of comment by the judge.”

[25]          During the trial judge’s charge on mistaken belief in consent he commented that the appellant did not testify.  He stated, “The question of intention.  The accused did not testify, and the Crown has to prove lack of consent on the part of the complainant beyond a reasonable doubt.”  The appellant submits that the trial judge’s comment contravened s. 4(6) of the Act.  I do not agree. 

[26]          The Supreme Court has said repeatedly that the prohibition in s. 4(6) must be applied purposively, not literally.  The section was obviously enacted for the protection of an accused: a jury may improperly conclude from a judge’s comment on an accused’s failure to testify that the accused is using silence as a “cloak” for guilt.  Therefore, to run afoul of s. 4(6) more is required than a mere offhand comment on an accused’s failure to testify.  To contravene the section, the comment must give rise to a realistic risk of prejudice to the accused’s defence.  See McConnell and Beer v. Her Majesty the Queen, [1968] S.C.R. 802 and R. v. Potvin, [1989] 1 S.C.R. 525.

[27]          I view the trial judge’s comment as an offhand remark that in its context could not have prejudiced the appellant.  I point to three additional considerations that negate any suggestion of prejudice.  Defence counsel did not object to the comment; throughout his charge, the trial judge instructed the jury on the presumption of innocence and the Crown’s burden of proof; and, the jury acquitted the appellant on one of the three counts with which he was charged. 

[28]          I would not give effect to this ground of appeal.

Third Issue: Were the trial judge’s instructions on consent too confusing?

[29]          The appellant contends that on the sexual assault charge the sole issue was consent.  He submits that instead of giving the jury a clear and straightforward charge on the elements of sexual assault, including the lack of consent, the trial judge gave the jury a convoluted and confusing charge that contained instructions on the defence of honest but mistaken belief in consent, and invited comparison with accident and the ability of a child to consent in law. 

[30]          The appellant buttresses his submission by pointing to the closing addresses of counsel: both counsel focused on the question of consent; neither went to the jury on honest but mistaken belief in consent.  Looking at the judge’s instructions on consent as a whole, the appellant submits that he was deprived of a fair charge on the sexual assault charge. 

[31]          I am inclined to agree with the appellant to this extent: the trial judge’s instructions could have been clearer, and he might well have omitted any reference to accident, to a child’s capacity to consent, and even to the defence of honest but mistaken belief in consent. 

[32]          That said, I understand why the trial judge thought he should leave honest but mistaken belief in consent with the jury.  A trial judge is required to put before the jury all viable defences arising from the evidence.  As the Crown points out, the following considerations, taken cumulatively, suggested an air of reality to the defence of honest but mistaken belief in consent: at the bar, J.M. was sitting on the appellant’s knee; she kissed the appellant; when she discovered him in her apartment she didn’t ask him to leave; instead she laid him down on the couch; she voiced no objection when they laid on the couch together or when they ended up on the floor together. 

[33]          However, even if the trial judge wrongly left the defence of honest but mistaken believe in consent with the jury, his doing so caused no harm.  On a fair reading of the entire charge, I think the jury would have understood that to obtain a conviction on the sexual assault charge, the Crown had to prove lack of consent beyond a reasonable doubt. 

[34]          I would not give effect to this ground of appeal. 

D. Conclusion

[35]          The appellant raised three grounds of appeal against his conviction for sexual assault.  I would not give effect to his second and third grounds.  On his first ground of appeal, I agree that in permitting the Crown to lead evidence that the appellant declined to make a formal statement to the police, the trial judge committed an error of law.  However, in my view, the error was harmless and I would, therefore, apply the proviso.  Accordingly, I would dismiss the appeal.
