On appeal from the said acquittals by Justice Arthur Gans of the Superior Court of Justice, sitting without a jury, dated April 14, 2003.       

BY THE COURT:

[1]               The Crown appeals from the respondent’s acquittal on charges of perjury and attempt to obstruct justice. The Crown argues that the trial judge erred in law in requiring as an element of these offences that the respondent intended to mislead the court on a material fact.

[2]               These charges arose as a result of the respondent’s testimony at his own bail hearing on charges relating to the robbery of two convenience stores in the summer of 2001. The respondent was charged along with several of his friends, having been arrested when he went to the police station to look for them.

[3]               At his bail hearing, the respondent lied to the justice of the peace about where he had gone in an attempt to find his friends, before going to the police station to do so. When confronted by the Crown with contradictory evidence on this issue, he  acknowledged his lie to the justice of the peace. These charges resulted.

[4]               At his trial on the perjury and obstruct justice charges the respondent admitted that he knowingly lied. Thus the trial judge properly defined the only remaining issue this way:

The sole question for determination is whether he uttered this extended lie, as it were, with the intent to mislead (perjury) or whether he intended to obstruct, pervert or defeat the course of justice (obstruct justice) by his lying.

[5]               He then set out his answer to this question as follows:

The question now to determine is whether or not the “big lie” was aimed at Mr. Prashad’s desire to obtain his own judicial release by somehow minimizing his involvement in some or all of the charges. I am not satisfied beyond a reasonable doubt that it was intended to meet the concerns engendered by the secondary or tertiary grounds of s. 515 of the Code. … his own evidence during cross-examination after he was caught in the lie is confirmatory of the notion that he himself viewed the testimony of little or no consequence.

I would observe parenthetically that the above proposition is consistent with my initial assessment of the evidence when I read the transcript of the show cause hearing on the initial application upon which I have already ruled. I was then of the view that the testimony which gives rise to the instant charge concerned but collateral matters and spoke at best to the credibility of Mr. Prashad. In any event, I am possessed of a reasonable doubt as to his guilt and therefore must acquit.

[6]               Despite Ms. Page’s able argument, we read the trial judge as requiring the Crown to prove an intention to mislead on a material fact, in this case material to the secondary or tertiary ground for judicial release. An intention to mislead on a collateral matter was not seen to be enough.

[7]               In our view, this constitutes an error of law. The required mental element is that the accused intend to mislead the court by having the court accept as credible, evidence he gives which he knows to be dishonest. It need not be evidence on a material point. See R. v. Wolf (1974), 17 C.C.C. (2d) 425 (S.C.C.). In addition, that case makes clear that although it is possible for an individual to deliberately lie without intending to mislead, such a scenario will be extremely rare.

[8]               The appeal must therefore be allowed. However, since we cannot conclude on this record that a conviction would necessarily result from the application of the proper legal test, a new trial must be ordered.
