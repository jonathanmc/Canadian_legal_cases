CRONK J.A.:

I.          Introduction

[1]               Following the termination of a two-year relationship with his girlfriend, M.O., the respondent threatened to disseminate nude photographs of M.O. to her friends and acquaintances unless she had sex with him.  The photographs depicted M.O. in various sexual acts with the respondent.  Thereafter, M.O. engaged in one act of anal intercourse and two acts of vaginal intercourse with the respondent.

[2]               The respondent was charged with two counts of sexual assault relating to these incidents, as well as three counts of assault and one count of uttering a death threat concerning encounters with M.O. in January 2002.  He pleaded guilty to the three assault charges and was acquitted of both sexual assault charges after a three-day trial before Cumming J. of the Superior Court of Justice.  The threatening death charge was dismissed on a non-suit motion at the close of the Crown’s case.  At sentencing, the respondent received a conditional discharge and two years probation in connection with the assault charges. 

[3]               The Crown appeals from the acquittals on the sexual assault charges.  The main issues on appeal are whether M.O. consented to the sexual acts in question and, if so, whether the respondent’s threats to disseminate the nude photographs vitiated her consent under s. 273.1(2)(c) or s. 273.1(3) of the Criminal Code, R.S.C. 1985, c. C-46.

[4]               M.O. and the respondent were the only witnesses at trial.  M.O. testified that she did not consent to sexual activity with the respondent and that, given the respondent’s threats concerning the photographs, she had no alternative but to engage in sex with him.  The respondent acknowledged the sexual acts and his threats to disseminate the photo-graphs.  He also admitted that he “blackmailed” M.O. into having sex with him, but claimed that the sexual activity was consensual. 

[5]               The Crown argued at trial that M.O. never truly consented to the sexual acts in question, maintaining that M.O.’s consent was vitiated under s. 273.1(2)(c) of the Code because the respondent’s admitted threats to expose the photographs constituted an inducement to engage in sexual activity through the abuse by him of a position of power.  The Crown also asserted that M.O.’s consent was vitiated because it was obtained as a result of extortionate conduct by the respondent.

[6]               The defence position at trial was that the sexual assault charges had not been made out to the requisite criminal standard of proof and that the type of activity engaged in by the respondent did not come within the scope of the Code so as to attract sanction under the criminal law.

[7]               The trial judge found that the respondent was in a position of power over M.O. and that he abused this position by threatening to disseminate the nude photographs if M.O. refused to have sex with him.  However, the trial judge acquitted the respondent of the sexual assault charges on two bases: first, although he emphasized the reprehensible nature of the respondent’s conduct, the trial judge was “not convinced beyond a reasonable doubt that Parliament intended to criminalize this situation”; and second, he was “not sure that [the respondent’s] conduct amount[ed] to extortion [that] should vitiate consent”.

[8]               For the reasons that follow, I consider that sexual assaults were made out in this case.  The respondent’s conduct in threatening to disseminate the photographs unless M.O. had sex with him amounted to extortion as defined in s. 346(1) of the Criminal Code.  M.O.’s participation in the sexual acts in question was the direct result and the intended consequence of this extortionate conduct.  On these facts, it cannot be said that M.O. voluntarily agreed to sexual activity with the respondent.  Accordingly, I would allow the appeal and set aside the acquittals on the two counts of sexual assault.  In the circumstances of this case, I would also direct that convictions be entered on these counts and remit this matter to the trial judge for sentencing.   

II.        Factual Background

[9]               Many of the background facts relevant to this appeal are essentially uncontested.

[10]          The respondent and M.O. were involved in an intimate relationship for about two years.  The relationship ended in December 2001 when M.O. left the apartment that she was then sharing with the respondent to move into her sister’s home.  The respondent was upset and traumatized by the break-up and hoped to reconcile with M.O..  He threatened several times to commit suicide, as he had sometimes threatened in the past. 

[11]          The respondent was obsessed with M.O..  Following their break-up, he sent her about two hundred e-mails to which, on many occasions, he attached nude photographs of M.O. that depicted her engaging in sexual acts with him.  In response to M.O.’s request that he delete the photographs from his computer, the respondent told M.O., “[W]ell, come over and have sex and we can delete them, or whatever.”  The respondent also ‘hacked’ into M.O.’s computer e-mail account to view her e-mail correspondence and attended several times, uninvited, at her sister’s home. 

[12]          M.O. maintained that prior to her receipt of the respondent’s e-mails, she was unaware of the existence of the nude photographs.  She claimed that the respondent surreptitiously videotaped some of their sexual encounters while they were living together.  In contrast, the respondent said that he took the photographs with a hand-held video camera and that M.O. was aware that he had done so. 

[13]          In mid-January 2002, following her receipt of the respondent’s e-mails, M.O. met the respondent at his request in a local park.  An argument ensued during which the respondent twice pushed M.O., causing her to fall down.  Thereafter, he apologized to M.O., telling her that he felt terrible about his behaviour and threatening to commit suicide.  This incident led to the first assault charge against the respondent. 

[14]          M.O. was concerned about the respondent and his state of mind and, as a result, communicated with him that evening on an Internet ‘chat-line’.  According to M.O., during their discussion via the Internet, the respondent reminded M.O. of the photographs and told her that if she wanted them deleted from his computer, she had to have sex with him the next day.  In contrast, the respondent testified that he suggested to M.O., “Maybe we can have sex one last time”, and that M.O. agreed to meet him for that purpose the following morning.

III.       Issues

[29]          The central issue on this appeal is whether M.O. consented to the sexual acts that occurred in January 2002.  If she consented, the question to be decided is whether the respondent’s threats to expose the nude photographs vitiated M.O.’s consent under s. 273.1(2)(c) or s. 273.1(3) of the Criminal Code.  

IV.       Analysis

            (1)       Relevant Statutory Provisions

[30]          Sexual assault is an assault within any of the definitions set out in s. 265(1) of the Code that is committed in circumstances of a sexual nature, such that the sexual integrity of the victim is violated: see R. v. Ewanchuk (1999), 131 C.C.C. (3d) 481 at para. 24 (S.C.C.) and R. v. Chase (1987), 37 C.C.C. (3d) 97 at 103 (S.C.C.).  Section 265(1) reads, in part:

265(1) A person commits an assault when

(a) without the consent of another person, he applies force intentionally to that other person, directly or indirectly;

(b) he attempts or threatens, by an act or a gesture, to apply force to another person, if he has, or causes that other person to believe on reasonable grounds that he has, present ability to effect his purpose;

…

By the terms of s. 265(2), s. 265(1) applies to all forms of assault, including sexual assault.

[31]          Section 265(3) of the Code lists certain circumstances that may vitiate consent to a sexual act:

265(3) For the purposes of this section, no consent is obtained where the complainant submits or does not resist by reason of

(a) the application of force to the complainant or to a per-son other than the complainant;

(b) threats or fear of the application of force to the com-plainant or to a person other than the complainant;

(c) fraud; or

(d) the exercise of authority.

[32]          Section 273.1(1) of the Code provides a specific definition of consent for the purpose of sexual assault:

273.1(1)  Subject to subsection (2) and subsection 265(3), “consent” means, for the purposes of sections 271, 272 and 273, the voluntary agreement of the complainant to engage in the sexual activity in question.

[33]          Consent in connection with sexual assault is also limited by s. 273.1(2) of the Code.  This section states, in part:

273.1(2) No consent is obtained, for the purposes of sections 271, 272 and 273 where

….

(c) the accused induces the complainant to engage in the activity by abusing a position of trust, power or authority;

….

[34]          In addition, s. 273.1(3) provides that, “Nothing in subsection (2) shall be construed as limiting the circumstance in which no consent is obtained.”

[35]          Section 346(1) of the Code defines “extortion” as follows:

Every one commits extortion who, without reasonable justification or excuse and with intent to obtain anything, by threats, accusations, menaces or violence induces or attempts to induce any person, whether or not he is the person threat-ened, accused or menaced or to whom violence is shown, to do anything or cause anything to be done.

(2)       Crown’s Position at Trial

[36]          Crown counsel argued at trial that no “true consent” was given by M.O. to any of the January 2002 sexual activity with the respondent. 

[37]          In respect of the incidents of vaginal intercourse, the Crown argued that the respondent induced M.O. to engage in sexual activity by abusing his position of power over her, thereby vitiating her consent under s. 273.1(2)(c) of the Code.  The Crown also asserted that M.O.’s consent was obtained “by means of an illegal act, extortion, in effect”, thereby vitiating her consent on this ground, as well.  This argument was also based on s. 273.1(2)(c), rather than on s. 273.1(3), of the Code.

[38]          With respect to the incident of anal intercourse, the Crown relied on s. 265(3) of the Criminal Code to argue that M.O.’s consent was obtained and vitiated by threats or fear of the application of force, based on M.O.’s evidence that she told the respondent “no” once penetration occurred and that, thereafter, the respondent did not stop immediately but continued over M.O.’s objections for several minutes. 

(3)       Trial Judge’s Decision 

[39]          The trial judge framed the issue before him in the following fashion (at para. 34):

   The essence of the factual situation is that [M.O.] agreed to have sexual intercourse with the defendant January 15 and 20, 2001, [sic] to secure a deletion of the photos of her stored in his computer that he threatened to disseminate to her friends.  Does either or both incidents amount to a sexual assault within the meaning of the Criminal Code? [citations omitted].

[40]          The trial judge next reviewed the nature of the legal arguments advanced by the Crown at trial.  He then undertook an analysis of the scope and legislative intent of s. 273.1(2)(c) of the Code.  In so doing, he stated (at paras. 46-47):

   The words “trust”, “power”, and “authority” have separate meanings.  [R. v. Hogg (2000), 148 C.C.C. (3d) 86 (Ont.  C.A.)]  at para. 16 holds that “power” is “probably the broader term” and notes that there is “little direct authority on the meaning of ‘position of power’…”  In my view, “power” is the most useful term in describing the situation here, though I am aware of the wide scope of “authority” as set out in R. v. Matheson (1999), 134 C.C.C. (3d) 289 (Ont. C.A.).  Suffice it to say that the sway that Mr. Stender held over [M.O.] was the ability to humiliate her by disseminating the photos, and it is apt to describe Mr. Stender’s position as one of “power” over [M.O.].  I also have little doubt that the threatened dissemination of the pictures would be an “abuse” of that power.

   That being said, the question remains whether this particular power dynamic was one that Parliament intended to criminalize.  As with most offences, conduct that may fit the letter of the Criminal Code nevertheless may not rise to the level of conduct for which convictions are imposed.  Thus, some positions of power and abuses thereof would not lead to vitiated consent.  Where is the line to be drawn? [emphasis added]

[41]          The trial judge concluded at para. 56:

   In short, I have my concerns…about imposing the heavy sanction of a conviction for sexual assault on these facts.  I do not mean to diminish the horrible choice that [M.O.] had to make.  Nor can I overemphasize my revulsion at Mr. Stender’s actions.  His actions and motives were morally reprehensible.  I simply mean that I am not convinced beyond a reasonable doubt that Parliament intended to criminalize this situation.  Absent clearer statutory delineation, and following [R. v. Cuerrier (1998), 127 C.C.C. (3d) 1 (S.C.C.)] I cannot sustain a conviction on this basis [emphasis added].

[42]          Finally, the trial judge considered, and rejected, the Crown’s submission that the respondent’s conduct constituted criminal extortion that operated under s. 273.1(2)(c) of the Code to vitiate M.O.’s consent to intercourse with the respondent.  He stated (at paras. 57-59):

In my view, such a conclusion would read an element into s. 273.1 that is simply not there.  Had Parliament wanted to vitiate consent obtained through extortion, it would have clearly done so.  The substantive offence of extortion is long established, and Parliament could easily and explicitly have traversed that concept into their [sic] formulation of s. 273.1.  Moreover, Mr. Stender was not charged with extortion, and I am loath to, in effect, convict him of an offence for which he perhaps ought to have been charged by means of a different offence.

   On the other hand, I note that the Crown did not attempt to argue vitiation under s. 273.1(3). …

[T]he Crown’s argument that conduct amounting to extortion should vitiate consent might have found a better home in [s. 273.1(3)] rather than [s. 273.1(2)].  On the facts of this case, however, I would not be convinced even if the issue had been argued.  For many of the same reasons that leave me with doubt that Mr. Stender’s conduct amounts to sexual assault, I am not sure that his conduct amounts to criminal extortion.  I would need a more clear-cut case of extortion before using [s. 273.1(3)] to expand the law.  Again, I emphasize that the Crown never raised s. 273.1(3) [emphasis added].

(4)              Discussion

(i)        Absence of Consent

[43]          In her written submissions on this appeal, Crown counsel submitted that, having found that the respondent induced M.O. to have sexual intercourse with him by abusing his position of power over her, the trial judge erred by misdirecting himself on the correct legal test for determining whether M.O.’s consent was vitiated.  Crown counsel submitted that this misdirection had several dimensions, including misplaced reliance by the trial judge on Cuerrier, supra, to support his findings that not all abuses of a position of power will lead to vitiated consent and that only where “the imbalance of power [is] serious, in the sense that the exercise of that superior power must result in potentially serious consequences to the other party”, will consent be vitiated.

[44]          In oral argument before this court, Crown counsel also attacked the trial judge’s decision on the basis that M.O. never consented, from the outset, to intercourse with the respondent.  As I understood Crown counsel’s submissions, she maintained that the trial judge erred in law in his approach to the absence of consent requirement for the actus reus of sexual assault.  She asserted, as the Crown had argued at trial, that the respondent’s threats to expose the nude photographs constituted the offence of extortion, thereby vitiating M.O.’s consent to sexual activity with the respondent.  She also argued, however, that such conduct by the respondent essentially foreclosed voluntary consent by M.O..  This latter submission rests on the same theory of liability advanced by the Crown at trial, albeit with a different focus concerning the legal effect of the respondent’s alleged extortionate conduct.

[45]          In my view, it is unnecessary to consider the arguments advanced by the parties concerning vitiation of consent.  I agree with Crown counsel that on the admitted facts and factual findings of the trial judge, consent to sexual intercourse within the meaning of s. 273.1(1) of the Criminal Code was not given by M.O..  It follows, therefore, `that the actus reus and the mens rea of sexual assault were established and the trial judge erred in law in concluding that the admitted incidents of sexual intercourse did not “amount to a sexual assault within the meaning of the Criminal Code”.  I reach these conclusions for the following reasons. 

[46]          In framing the issue before him, the trial judge assumed that M.O. ‘agreed’ to have sexual intercourse with the respondent.  At no point, did the trial judge consider whether consent, within the meaning of that term under s. 273.1(1) of the Code, was actually given by M.O..  Section 273.1(1), as I have said, defines “consent” to mean “the voluntary agreement of the complainant to engage in the sexual activity in question”.  No finding of such a “voluntary agreement” by M.O. was made by the trial judge.

[47]          These omissions are understandable, given the way in which the case was argued at trial.  In their submissions to the trial judge, counsel confirmed that the key issue concerned vitiation of consent and the trial judge proceeded on this basis.  This approach misconceived the threshold question for determination, namely, whether M.O. consented to sexual intercourse with the respondent at all.  Unless M.O.’s sexual activity with the respondent was consensual, the necessity of a s. 273.1(2)(c) analysis did not arise.  Expressed somewhat differently, resort to s. 273.1(2)(c) of the Code was only required in this case if the trial judge found that M.O. had consented: see Hogg, supra, at paras. 8 and 10. 

[48]          In Ewanchuk, supra, the Supreme Court of Canada confirmed that proof of three elements is required to establish the actus reus of sexual assault: (i) touching; (ii) the sexual nature of the contact; and (iii) the absence of consent.  The first and second of these are not in issue in this case.  In the context of the actus reus of sexual assault, the third element, the absence of consent, is a subjective factor that is determined by refer-ence to the complainant’s subjective internal state of mind towards the sexual touching, at the time that it occurred: see Ewanchuk at para. 27. 

[49]          M.O. advanced a version of events in which she vehemently denied that she freely consented to the sexual acts in question.  Throughout her testimony, she repeatedly stated her belief that she had no choice but to submit to intercourse with the respondent; that her purpose in going to the respondent’s apartment (on both occasions) was to secure the deletion of the respondent’s computer files containing the nude photographs so as to avoid their dissemination and the reputational injury that would follow; that she told the respondent during both attendances at his apartment that she did not want to have sex with him; that the respondent knew that she “fought him off”; and that he pressured her to engage in sex as a condition for the deletion of the computer files.  On M.O.’s evidence, therefore, she did not wish the sexual touching to occur, and no actual consent to the touching was ever given. 

[50]          In R. v. Saint-Laurent (1993), 90 C.C.C. (3d) 291 (Que. C.A.), leave to appeal to S.C.C. refused [1994] C.S.C.R. No. 55, Fish J.A. emphasized that Parliament intended to criminalize coerced sexual activity.  He said (at p. 311):

Mutual agreement is a safeguard of sexual integrity imposed by the state under the threat of penal sanction.  In the absence of consent, an act of sex is, at least, prima facie an act of assault.

   As a matter both of language and of law, consent implies a reasonably informed choice, freely exercised.  No such choice has been exercised where a person engages in sexual activity as a result of fraud, force, fear, or violence.  Nor is the consent requirement satisfied if, because of his or her mental state, one of the parties is incapable of understanding the sexual nature of the act, or of realizing that he or she may choose to decline participation.

   “Consent” is, thus, stripped of its defining characteristics when it is applied to the submission, non-resistance, non-objection, or even the apparent agreement, of a deceived, unconscious or compelled will [emphasis added].

[51]          In this case, the respondent acknowledged that he “blackmailed” M.O. into having sex with him by threatening to disseminate the nude photographs of her if she did not do so; that he intended to have sex with M.O.; and that he knew that M.O. did not wish to engage in sex with him, “except for the reason of getting rid of those files”.

[52]          In R. v. Davis (1999), 139 C.C.C. (3d) 193, the Supreme Court of Canada held that the threat to expose nude photographs in order to gain sexual favours constituted the offence of extortion. [1]   Chief Justice Lamer, writing for a unanimous court, stated at para. 45:

Extortion criminalizes intimidation and interference with freedom of choice.  It punishes those who, through threats, accusations, menaces, or violence induce or attempt to induce their victims into doing anything or causing anything to be done.  Threats, accusations, menaces and violence clearly intimidate…When threats are coupled with demands, there is an inducement to accede to the demands.  This interferes with the victim’s freedom of choice, as the victim may be coerced into doing something he or she would otherwise have chosen not to do [citations omitted].

[53]          The respondent’s conduct in threatening to disseminate the nude photographs of M.O. if she did not engage in sex with him constituted extortion as defined in s. 346(1) of the Code.  His threats to expose the photographs were coupled with demands for sexual favours.  By his threats, he induced M.O. to accede to his demands for sex, thus interfering with her freedom of choice and coercing her into doing something that she clearly testified she would otherwise have chosen not to do.

[54]          M.O. did not believe that the choice to decline participation in sexual intercourse with the respondent was available to her in the circumstances.  It cannot be said, therefore, that she was knowledgeable about her options in the face of the respondent’s admitted blackmail and that, being aware of her choices, she voluntarily agreed to sexual activity with the respondent.  As Green J.A. of the Newfoundland Court of Appeal stated in Davis (1998), 159 Nfld. & P.E.I.R. 273 at para. 97:

At the least, the notion of consent in sexual assault encompasses the making of a conscious decision freed from external extortionate pressure that is intended by the very person seeking compliance to achieve contact which, but for that pressure, would not have been agreed to.

I agree.

[55]          The respondent points out that he was not charged with extortion or attempted extortion.  He argues that the trial judge did not find that his conduct did not amount to extortion but, rather, correctly concluded that the exposure of the nude photographs, if carried out, did not amount to a criminal offence.  I would reject these submissions.

[56]          The trial judge’s reasons clearly indicate his concern that the respondent was not charged with extortion.  As I have mentioned, he observed in his reasons at para. 57: “Mr. Stender was not charged with extortion, and I am loath to, in effect, convict him of an offence for which he perhaps ought to have been charged by means of a different offence.”  Subsequently, he stated: “I am not sure that his conduct amounts to criminal extortion.”

[57]          I have already concluded that the respondent’s conduct constituted extortion.  The fact that he was not charged with extortion or attempted extortion is irrelevant to the issue of whether his extortionate conduct, which would have supported a separate charge of extortion, also precluded voluntary consent by M.O. to the sexual activity demanded by the respondent, so as to make out the offences of sexual assault with which the respondent was actually charged.  While, as argued by the respondent, M.O.’s participation in sexual activity may also have been influenced by the respondent’s threats of suicide, on all of the evidence in this case her purported ‘consent’ to sex with the respondent was the direct result and intended consequence of the respondent’s extor-tionate conduct.  In these circumstances, her alleged ‘consent’ to sexual activity was not genuine and freely given.  Accordingly, it was no ‘consent’ at all within the meaning of s. 273.1(1) of the Criminal Code.

(ii)      Other Considerations

[58]          It is appropriate to comment on two other matters raised by the parties.  First, the respondent argues that the trial judge was correct to take guidance from Cuerrier, supra, in deciding that the kind of power imbalance that existed in this case was not one that ought to attract a conviction for sexual assault.  This submission concerns the scope of s. 273.1(2)(c) of the Code and the types of abuse of power that will be deemed under that section to vitiate consent to sexual activity.  I have already concluded that, in the circumstances here, consideration of abuse of power under s. 273.1(2)(c) is unnecessary.

[59]          In any event, in Cuerrier, the complainants initially consented to sexual activity.  Unlike this case, neither complainant in Cuerrier claimed that they had no choice regarding participation in sexual acts with the accused, or that their participation was coerced.  The issue in Cuerrier was consensual sexual activity that would not constitute assault, were it not for the effect of fraud.  It was only because the complainants’ consent in that case was obtained by fraud that it was vitiated: see Cuerrier at para. 132.  That is not this case. 

[60]          Second, the respondent correctly points out that the jurisdiction of this court on a Crown appeal from an acquittal is limited to a question of law alone.  I am satisfied that the trial judge’s failure to properly consider and apply s. 273.1(1) of the Criminal Code was an error of law and that upon the application of a correct interpretation of this section, a different result is mandated in this case.

V.        Disposition

[61]          Accordingly, for the reasons given, I would allow the appeal and set aside the acquittals entered on both sexual assault charges.  In the exercise of this court’s discre-tion, I would also direct that convictions be entered on the two sexual assault charges and that the matter be remitted to the trial judge for sentencing.
