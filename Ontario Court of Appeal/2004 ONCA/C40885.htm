On appeal from the order of Justice Peter B. Hockin of the Superior Court of Justice dated September 25, 2003.

LANG J.A.:

[1]               Deloitte & Touche LLP, the trustee in bankruptcy, appeals the dismissal of its motion for possession and directions for the sale of the Marino home and the distribution of the sale proceeds to the Marinos’ creditors.  This relief was sought almost five years after the Marinos first went bankrupt.  Since declaring bankruptcy, the Marinos, relying on the trustee’s assurances, continued to live in the home and expend funds on its upkeep and improvement.  When faced with the trustee’s motion, the Marinos brought a cross-motion seeking an order vesting the home in their names.

Issues

1.      What is the standard of review?

2.      Was the trustee precluded from moving to sell Mrs. Marino’s interest in the home by reason of its discharge as her trustee?

3.      Did the motions  judge err in the application of s. 40?

4.      Is the trustee estopped, or otherwise prohibited in equity, from realizing its interest in the home?

Relevant Statutory Provisions

[2]               The provisions of the Bankruptcy and Insolvency Act, R.S.C. 1985, c. B‑3 (“the Act”) are relevant in the following order, with emphasis added:

71.(2) On a receiving order being made or an assignment being filed with an official receiver, a bankrupt ceases to have any capacity to dispose of or otherwise deal with his property, which shall, subject to this Act and to the rights of secured creditors, forthwith pass to and vest in the trustee named in the receiving order or assignment, and in any case of change of trustee the property shall pass from trustee to trustee without any conveyance, assignment or transfer.

74.(2) Where a bankrupt is the registered owner of any land or charge, the trustee, on registration of the documents referred to in subsection (1), is entitled to be registered as owner of the land or charge free of all encumbrances or charges mentioned in subsection 70(1).

40.(1) With the permission of the inspectors, any property of a bankrupt found incapable of realization shall be returned to the bankrupt prior to the trustee’s application for discharge.

(2) Where a trustee is unable to dispose of any property as provided in this section, the court may make such order as it may consider necessary.

41.(10) Notwithstanding his discharge, the trustee remains the trustee of the estate for the performance of such duties as may be incidental to the full administration of the estate.

37. Where the bankrupt or any of the creditors or any other person is aggrieved by any act or decision of the trustee, he may apply to the court and the court may confirm, reverse or modify the act or decision complained of and make such order in the premises as it thinks just.  

41.(11) The court, on being satisfied that there are assets that have not been realized or distributed, may, on the application of any interested person, appoint a trustee to complete the administration of the estate of the bankrupt, and the trustee shall be governed by the provisions of this Act, in so far as they are applicable.

Background

[3]               When the Marinos declared bankruptcy in April 1997, Deloitte & Touche LLP (“Deloitte & Touche”) became their trustees in bankruptcy. Over the course of the ensuing years, several different employees of Deloitte & Touche worked with the Marinos as the trustee’s representative: Mr. Shaw, Mr. Allen, Mr. Sholdice, Mr. Crocker and Mr. Bell. It was the frequent changes in representative, and the change in the representative’s position, that led to this litigation about the property.

[4]               Upon the Marinos’ bankruptcy, pursuant to s. 71(2) of the Act, their home automatically vested in the trustee.  At that time, the Marinos valued their home at $499,000, less outstanding mortgages and tax arrears of about $427,000.  No issue is taken with those values.  The trustee’s first report said that there “may be equity” in the home and, indeed, the inspectors instructed the trustee to register its interest on title, although it did not then proceed to do so.

[5]               The Marinos expected that they would lose their home and began looking for rental accommodation. Two months after their bankruptcy, in June 1997, Deloitte & Touche (through Mr. Allen) told the Marinos that they could remain in the home and that the trustee would make no claim against the home, as the trustee did not feel it worthwhile to realize on the home.

[6]               In January, 1998, when the Marinos received their absolute discharges, which appeared to discharge them from all debt, Mr. Marino called the trustee (then Mr. Crocker) to confirm the status of the home.  He was assured by Mr. Crocker that the trustee was claiming no interest.  Mr. Crocker suggested that, to be sure, the Marinos should retain a lawyer, specifically Mr. Crocker’s sister, to search the property’s title. If that search revealed that the trustee had  registered against the property, Mr. Crocker undertook to quitclaim the house back to the Marinos.

[7]               In the summer of 1998, the trustee sent Mrs. Marino its Final Statement of Receipts and Disbursements (“Final Statement”).  That document stated that there was no equity in the home available for the creditors: this confirmed the Marinos’ belief that there was no claim against their home.  That year, in reliance on the trustee’s statements, the Marinos planned to pay all outstanding mortgage arrears and planned home renovations. 

[8]               In furtherance of those plans, and after the trustee was automatically discharged with respect to Mrs. Marino in October 1998, the Marinos retained Mr. Crocker’s sister, who, after conducting a title search, confirmed that the trustee had not registered against the property. The lawyer told the Marinos that the property was registered in their names so it was unnecessary to prepare a transfer back from Deloitte & Touche.

[9]               In June 1999, twenty-six months after the Marinos’ bankruptcy, sixteen months after their discharge, and eight months after the trustee’s discharge as Mrs. Marino’s trustee, the trustee finally registered against the home, pursuant to s.74(2) of the Act. The trustee did not tell the Marinos it had done so.  When the trustee applied for its discharge with respect to Mr. Marino on September 2, 1999, it sent a Final Statement that contained a footnote about the registration, but Mr. Marino did not notice that change in the trustee’s position, and the trustee did nothing to bring that change in position to his attention.

[10]          In mid-2000, another representative of the trustee (Mr. Sholdice) wrote the Marinos asking for information about the house.  While acknowledging an apparent lack of equity in the home, Mr. Sholdice cautioned the Marinos that the trustee “would not release the property until we can satisfactorily settle our concerns”.

[11]          After a further exchange of correspondence, the Marinos heard nothing further until February 2002, when the trustee’s then representative (Mr. Bell) initiated correspondence and discussions about the Marinos’ purchase of the equity in their home. The Marinos contend that this is when they first became aware that the trustee had registered an interest in the home.  Mr. Bell said he was “trying to get the file closed” because he was leaving Deloitte & Touche.  This litigation eventually resulted.

[12]          It was common ground that, upon the Marinos’ bankruptcy, the home vested and remained vested in the trustee (s. 71(2) of the Act).  The trustee maintains that this vesting continued after the discharge of both Marinos and after the trustee’s discharge with respect to Mrs. Marino, despite the trustee’s representation to the Marinos that it was not pursuing any interest in the equity.  Any delay by the trustee, it argues, was irrelevant, because the property was vested and remained with the trustee throughout.  Had the Marinos taken exception to this, argues the trustee, they had the option of applying to the court for a vesting order under s. 37 of the Act. They did not do so.

[13]          The Marinos, on the other hand, take the position that they had relied upon the trustee’s representations and, but for those representations, they would not have paid arrears or principal on the mortgages, nor have undertaken the substantial improvements they made to the home.

Reasons of the Motions Judge

[14]          The motions judge found as a fact that the trustee had always known there was equity in the property and that, in fairness, “too much time” had passed for the trustee to now advance a claim against that equity (para. 15).  The equities, found the trial judge, flowed “in the direction of the Marinos” (para. 18); the trustee’s late effort to realize against the house was inconsistent with the Act’s philosophy of providing bankrupts with a “fresh start”.  The judge was of the view that had the trustee intended to proceed against the equity, it should have opposed the Marinos’ discharges from bankruptcy. In the result, the motions judge concluded that the home was “incapable of realization” within the meaning of s. 40(1) of the Act and ordered the property vested in the Marinos.

The Statutory Scheme

[15]          Upon their bankruptcies, when the Marinos’ interests vested and remained vested in the trustee, the Marinos were not entitled to the property unless it was released to them by the trustee, or if it was re-vested by court order under either s. 40 or s. 37 of the Act.  In other words, some positive act was required to return the property.  If that positive act was not forced by the bankrupt, the trustee was required to consider it at the time of application for its discharge.

[16]          The requirement to consider the property is set out in s. 40(1); where property is “incapable of realization”, the trustee is required to return it to the bankrupt before the trustee applies for discharge: Thompson v. Coulombe, [1984] Q.J. No. 11 (C.A.), Re Zemlak and Deloitte, Haskins & Sells Ltd. (1987), 42 D.L.R. (4th) 395 (Sask. C.A.).  Alternatively, under s. 40(2), where the trustee is unable to dispose of property the court “may make such order as it may consider necessary”.  If the trustee takes no action, an “aggrieved” bankrupt has the option of applying to a court and “the court may confirm, reverse or modify the act or decision complained of and make such order in the premises as it thinks just” (s. 37).

[17]          The Marinos were in different positions on this motion, however, because Deloitte & Touche had been discharged as Mrs. Marino’s trustee in October 1998, but remained undischarged with respect to Mr. Marino.

Analysis

1.      Standard of Review

[18]          A judge must be correct in his or her determination of the law. Findings of fact, however, are subject to a less onerous standard of review. Whether made from oral or documentary evidence, findings of fact and inferences drawn from them cannot be overturned absent “palpable and overriding error”. Findings of mixed fact and law are also accorded significant deference and, absent legal error or palpable and overriding factual error, are not to be disturbed on appeal: see Waxman v. Waxman, [2004] O.J. No. 1765 (C.A.) and Housen v. Nikolaisen, [2003] 2 S.C.R. 235.

2.      Did the trustee’s discharge as Mrs. Marino’s trustee preclude it from moving to sell her interest in the home?

[19]          When the trustee was discharged in October 1998, it took no action with respect to Mrs. Marino’s interest in the property.  It did nothing to contradict its earlier assurance given to Mrs. Marino that it was making no claim against the home and even confirmed that assurance by way of its Final Statement.  Accordingly, at the time the trustee applied for discharge from Mrs. Marino’s bankruptcy, and at the time it was discharged, it considered her interest to be “incapable of realization”.  It did not, however, return to her her interest in the property.

[20]          After discharge as Mrs. Marino’s trustee, Deloitte & Touche continued in its position only for “such duties as may be incidental to the full administration of the estate” (s. 41(10)). If realization of the property was more than “incidental” to the administration of this estate, or if a court was satisfied that assets of the bankrupt remained unrealized or undistributed, the trustee could seek re-appointment as a trustee to complete the estate’s administration (s. 41(11)).  No such relief was or has been requested.

[21]          Absent any application for re-appointment, and even though the property vested in the trustee, the trustee had no authority to deal with it unless such dealing was “incidental” to the administration of Mrs. Marino’s estate. 

[22]          The sale of Mrs. Marino’s interest in the home was not, however, merely incidental to the administration of her estate. On the facts, Mrs. Marino’s interest in the home was her only asset of any significance:  it was her estate.  Accordingly, the trustee had no authority to make a claim against Mrs. Marino’s interest in the home.

[23]          In addition, the principles of estoppel discussed later in these reasons apply equally to Mrs. Marino’s interest in the home as I find they do to Mr. Marino’s interest.

3.      With respect to Mr. Marino’s interest, did the motions judge err in the application of s. 40?

[24]          The trustee’s claim against Mr. Marino’s interest is different from its claim against Mrs. Marino’s interest because the trustee had not been discharged with respect to Mr. Marino’s bankruptcy, although it had applied for discharge. Accordingly, with respect to Mr. Marino’s estate, the trustee was not restricted to “incidental” duties, as it was with respect to Mrs. Marino’s estate (s. 41(10)).

[25]          The issue therefore arises as to whether the motions judge erred in his conclusion that the property was now “incapable of realization” and, for that reason, ordering its re‑vesting under s. 40(2).

[26]          The plain wording of s. 40(1) addresses realization at the time of the trustee’s application for discharge.  In June 1997, January 1998, and July 1998, the trustee repeatedly represented to the Marinos that the property was not capable of realization.  Subject to the question of promissory estoppel, the trustee is entitled to change its mind as was evident from its 1999 registration against title, its 1999 Final Statement to Mr. Marino, its 2000 correspondence and its 2002 position.  The property was not “incapable of realization” in the sense of an absence of palpable equity, in the trustee’s view, at the time the trustee applied for its discharge in respect of Mr. Marino’s estate.

[27]          This is consistent with this court’s decision in Re Shelson, [2004] O.J. No. 850 (C.A.), which was brought to our attention by counsel subsequent to the hearing of this matter. That case involved a trustee’s entitlement to the bankrupt’s interest in a civil proceeding (“the asset”) that the bankrupt had started prior to his bankruptcy. At the time of the bankruptcy, the trustee undertook to return the asset to the bankrupt upon the bankrupt’s discharge provided the creditors did not, in the meantime, require its realization. The trustee, however, did nothing about the asset prior to the bankrupt’s discharge or prior to its own application for discharge. The bankrupt continued to pursue the action for some five years subsequent to his discharge, at which time the trustee purported to realize upon the asset.

[28]          This court expressed its concern with the breadth of the proposition stated in Zemlak, supra, that when a trustee applies for its discharge, if it fails to elect to realize an asset, or to return it to the bankrupt, then the trustee holds that asset as trustee for the bankrupt and must return it to the bankrupt. Catzman J.A. said at para. 30:

In my view, the proposition cannot be framed as a “bright-line” rule that admits of no exceptions. Even the court in Zemlak qualified its statement of the proposition by the introductory phrase “[I]n the circumstances of this case”…. This court distinguished Zemlak and permitted a trustee in bankruptcy, following his discharge and that of the bankrupts, to take proceedings to pursue two properties, the values of which had been misrepresented by the bankrupts in their statements of affairs: Rocher v. H. & M. Diamond & Associates (2003), 43 C.B.R. (4th) 134. Even in instances short of misrepresentation or other misconduct by the bankrupt, it is not difficult to think of examples where an exception to the proposition would be appropriate. One is where an asset previously unknown to the trustee is discovered within a reasonable time of his application for discharge. Another is where the trustee learns within a reasonable time of his application for discharge that an asset previously considered to be unrealizable in fact had realizable value. In both examples, assuming that the asset is one which should, in the interests of the creditors of the estate, be realized, the court must retain some discretion to decide whether the proposition does or does not apply…. Failure to imbue the rule with some measure of flexibility renders a trustee's application for discharge a trap for the unwary [footnotes omitted].

[29]          I agree. On the facts of this case, including the fact that the trustee was not discharged and that the trustee’s Final Statement to Mr. Marino stated its newfound view that there was equity in the property, the trustee cannot be said to have determined that the asset  was “incapable of realization” so as to found the basis for a s. 40(2) vesting order. 

[30]          Indeed, the motions judge appreciated that this property may have had realizable equity at the time of the trustee’s application for discharge.  The essence of his reasons addressed whether the trustee was precluded by its delay in realizing on that equity.

4.      Is the trustee estopped, or otherwise prohibited in equity, from realizing its interest in the home?

[31]          Mr. Marino argues that even if Deloitte & Touche had legal entitlement to realize upon the equity in the home, it is estopped from doing so.

[32]          To rely on promissory estoppel Mr. Marino must establish:

            (a)  Deloitte & Touche, by words or conduct, made a promise or assurance which was intended to affect its legal relationship with Mr. Marino and intended Mr. Marino to act upon it; and

            (b)  Mr. Marino, relying on the representation, acted on it or in some way changed his position.

See Maracle v . Travellers Indemnity Co. of Canada, [1991] 2 S.C.R. 50 at 57.

[33]          Where appropriate, the principles of promissory estoppel have been considered both for and against a trustee in bankruptcy: Rocher v. H & M Diamond & Associates, [2003] O.J. No. 1049 (C.A.); Re Country Kitchen Donuts Ltd., [1980] O.J. No. 171 at para. 21 (H.C.J.).

[34]          In this case, the motions judge found that the trustee had, through its representatives, until at least 2000, given assurances to the Marinos that it would make no claim against the equity in the property.  The trustee did not tell them of its 1999 registration on title.  Even though the trustee did raise the possibility in 2000 of requiring further information about the property’s equity, it took no further action until 2002.

[35]          Not only did the trustee give assurances to the Marinos, but the Marinos also did not engage in any conduct that would have misled the trustee.  The trial judge found as a fact that the Marinos acted honestly. 

[36]          After the Marinos, and specifically Mr. Marino, received the trustee’s 1997 assurance that the trustee would not proceed against the property, the Marinos acted upon that assurance. They continued to reside in the home, satisfied the outstanding mortgage arrears on the property and proceeded with upgrades to the property. But for the trustee’s assurance, the Marinos could have earlier begun the “fresh start” contemplated by the Act, abandoned the premises to the trustee, and begun the process of building an equity in another property.

[37]          In all those circumstances, the motions judge made no error in deciding that the equities favoured the Marinos.

[38]          Accordingly, the representations and conduct of the trustee and the Marinos’ actions in reliance on them, estops the trustee from now realizing upon its registered interest in the property.

Conclusion

[39]          I would dismiss the appeal.

Costs

[40]          We have received and considered the parties’ written submissions on costs. In the circumstances, the Marinos are entitled to their costs, which we award on a partial indemnity basis fixed in the amount of $10,000, inclusive of disbursements and G.S.T.
