On appeal from the sentence imposed by Justice Paul F. Lalonde of the Superior Court of Justice,  sitting without a jury, on March 19, 2004.

ENDORSEMENT

[1]               The appellant was convicted of two counts of assault causing bodily harm and one count of assault, for which he was sentenced to three years imprisonment.  He was also ordered to provide a DNA sample and was prohibited from possessing weapons for life.  He seeks leave to appeal his sentence and, if leave be granted, appeals his sentence.

[2]               The appellant argues that the trial judge erred in his consideration of the appellant’s lack of remorse for the offences in question.  We agree.  This is not a case where the trial judge treated the appellant’s lack of remorse as relevant to rehabilitation and specific deterrence on the basis that it indicated a failure by the appellant to accept responsibility for his actions; nor is this a case where the trial judge regarded the appellant’s lack of remorse as disentitling him to mitigation consideration on sentencing.  To the contrary, the reasons of the trial judge indicate that he accepted Crown counsel’s submission at trial that the appellant’s lack of remorse was an aggravating factor relevant to fashioning an appropriate sentence.  This was an error in principle.

[3]               Given this error, this court is obliged to determine a fit sentence for the appellant.  In our view, a global sentence of three years imprisonment, as imposed by the trial judge, is appropriate here having regard to the circumstances of these offences and this offender.  In particular, the agreed facts filed in support of the appellant’s guilty plea detail the prolonged abuse of three separate partners with whom the appellant enjoyed domestic relationships.  In all three instances, one or more weapons were used, or their use was threatened by the appellant either upon himself or his victim.  In addition, in all three instances, the abusive acts were serious, involved considerable violence and inflicted significant physical or continuing psychological trauma on the victims.  These facts alone warrant a substantial term of imprisonment.

[4]               As well, the appellant’s record before this court indicates that he has a history of abusive behaviour for which he has never been treated by appropriate experts.  He has only recently registered in or become wait-listed for various counselling programmes.  Although the appellant has made some progress in his rehabilitation efforts since his sentencing hearing, the fact remains that he engaged in savage, abusive behaviour, involving multiple victims, during a period of about six years.

[5]               Accordingly, for the reasons given, leave to appeal sentence is granted and the sentence appeal is dismissed.  The sentence imposed by the trial judge remains in place.
