On appeal from a judgment of Justice Russell G. Juriansz of the Superior Court of Justice, sitting with a jury, acquitting the respondent, dated October 16, 2001.

FELDMAN J.A.:

OVERVIEW

[1] The charges in this case arose out of an incident on January 13, 2001, when the respondent went to the complainant's store. At trial, the respondent and the complainant told diametrically different versions of what happened at the store and the events leading up to the incident. On October 16, 2001, after a two week jury trial before Juriansz J., the respondent was acquitted on charges of robbery with a firearm, forcible confinement, assault causing bodily harm, extortion and possession of stolen property over $5,000 (cash). He was found guilty of theft (a watch) (as an included offence to the robbery charge), possession of stolen property over $5,000 (a watch), possession of a prohibited firearm and possession of a prohibited device. The respondent spent nine months in pre-trial custody and on October 18, 2001, was sentenced to one day in jail and twelve months of probation.

[2] The Crown appeals against the acquittals on a question of law alone: that the trial judge erred in law by charging the jury that evidence of the respondent's prior consistent statement could be used by the jury for the truth of its contents. The Crown submits further that it can meet its onus on a Crown appeal from acquittal of showing that the jury verdict would likely have been different had the erroneous instruction not been given: Section 686(4) of the Criminal Code, R.S.C. 1985, c. C-46.

[3] For the reasons that follow, I would dismiss the appeal. Although there was an error in law, the Crown has not met its onus for overturning a verdict of acquittal.

FACTS

[4] The twenty-nine year old complainant, Catherine (Cathy) Cosburn, owned and operated the "Funky Skunk," a store in a Scarborough strip mall that sold items related to hemp and marijuana. The respondent, Alex Divitaris, was twenty-two years old at trial and lived in close proximity to the Funky Skunk with his mother and two brothers.

[5] The respondent completed the eleventh grade in school and has a substantial criminal record consisting of seventeen entries dating back to 1994. It includes convictions for theft, failure to comply, escape lawful custody, obstructing an officer and failure to attend court. At trial, the record was put to him and accepted as true in his examination-in-chief.

[6] After being in jail for approximately nine months, the respondent was released in mid-August 2001 and began working at his brother's cleaning company three to five days a week. The complainant knew the respondent as "Alex" or "Diva". They had known each other for approximately four years before the incident; however, they differed in their views of how well they knew each other over the first three years of that time.

The Events at the Funky Skunk on January 13, 2001

[7] On the evening of January 13, 2001, the respondent went to the complainant's store where a violent incident occurred that gave rise to this case. Those were the only facts agreed to at trial; the complainant and the respondent each had their own versions of how and why the incident occurred.

The Complainant's Version of the Events

[8] According to the complainant, as she was closing her store for the evening at roughly six o'clock p.m. on January 13, 2001, the respondent entered. He appeared very angry and took her keys, locking the door behind him. He pushed her back and lifted up his jacket revealing a gun tucked in the top of his pants. The respondent yelled at her and insisted that she owed him $15,000, because she had caused him trouble by saying bad things about him. She insisted that was impossible - she had been in the hospital for the previous two weeks and had not spoken to anyone.

[9] After pulling the complainant by her hair to the back of the store, the respondent began punching her, waving his gun around, throwing her into the wall, yelling at her, and kicking her when she was down on the floor trying to sit up. Then the respondent removed just under $1,300 from the complainant's purse on the table beside her. This money was for a COD order she was expecting. He also took her watch (which she was wearing at the time), her ring, her prescription, and her driver's license. He told her to meet him the next day to give him more money and threatened to hurt her family if she disclosed what had transpired. He reminded her to meet him the next day and made her write it down so she would not forget. At trial, the complainant identified a note seized from the store as the one she made at the time.

[10] Eventually the complainant told the respondent that she would do what he wanted - borrow the money and pay him - if he would just leave her alone. After the respondent left, the complainant waited two or three minutes before driving to her friend Gayle Mackenzie's house a few blocks away.

The Respondent's Version of the Events

[11] According to the respondent, at five o'clock p.m. on the day of the incident, while he was out shopping with his girlfriend, Tahnee (Tanya) Foss-Liboiron, for a birthday present for her daughter, he received a call on his cell phone from the complainant. The respondent explained that the complainant owed him money because she lost casino chips that he had given her to cash in for him, and that she had persistently refused or been unable to pay him. His girlfriend knew about the complainant; she was aware of the telephone calls he was receiving from her and of the sexual encounter they had once had at a motel.

[12] On the phone the complainant asked the respondent to come to the store. The respondent and his girlfriend took a cab over to the Funky Skunk; the respondent thought the complainant was planning on paying him the money that she owed him. Once they reached the store, the respondent went into the store and told his girlfriend to wait in the cab. He quickly realized the complainant was high on cocaine and unable to give him cash; instead, the complainant insisted that, as collateral for the debt, he take an expensive watch, which she told him was stolen. Annoyed, the respondent threw the complainant's bag of cocaine on the floor. The enraged complainant then pulled out a gun and threatened to shoot the respondent.

[13] The respondent struggled with her, shoving her into a wall at one point and eventually disarming her. After taking the gun from the complainant, the respondent left the store with the intention of getting rid of it. When the respondent and his girlfriend arrived at her apartment, he waited for his girlfriend's mother to leave and then told her what had happened, in the course of which he showed her the gun. During the respondent's examination-in-chief, he testified that he told his girlfriend what had occurred, although in his testimony he did not give the contents of that conversation:

Q: What happened after Tonya's mother left?

A: Um, she was all over me. She asked me - she asked me: "Well, what happened?" And I told her what happened, basically. And as I was explaining her the story I - I pulled out the gun and I showed it to her, and she kind of freaked out; she was shocked when I showed it to her. And she was mad at this girl. She doesn't really like this girl just from what I told her. And she was just really upset at this girl, and she was upset at me with having the gun.

[14] Shortly after their conversation, the respondent took the subway home to his mother's apartment where he was arrested several hours later. The gun was found under the sofa where the respondent was sleeping. Also discovered were the watch and some cash ($740 all in twenty dollar bills).

The Complainant's Injuries

[15] When the complainant left the store after the incident, she went to her friend Gayle Mackenzie's house. Ms. Mackenzie confirmed that the complainant showed up at her door at around 7:30 p.m. on January 13, 2001. The complainant was crying, her hair was a mess, her hands were swollen, she vomited, and she had blood on her face.

[16] At around six o'clock a.m. the next day, the complainant went to the hospital. She described herself as having a concussion, lots of bumps, bruising and swelling, a broken nose, swollen eyes, a cut lip and nose, and cuts to her back. She testified that her head was split from being hit with the gun and that there was a significant amount of blood matted in her hair. The treating physician, Dr. Fonberg, testified that he ordered a CAT scan and X-rays on the basis of the complainant's information. According to the doctor, the complainant's injuries were not severe, but her distress was understandable if she had in fact been threatened in the manner reported.

Evidence Tendered in Support of Respondent's Version of Events

The Rental Car

[17] To support his position that the complainant owed him money, the respondent testified that the complainant agreed to rent him a car as partial payment for the debt. He recalled the date that she rented the car - October 23, 2000 - because his younger brother found the rental agreement in their apartment. It was in the course of renting the car that the respondent and complainant drove to the motel and engaged in a brief sexual encounter.

Michael McCollum

[18] Mr. McCollum was in jail at the time of trial and had spoken to the respondent on one occasion in the yard, although he testified that they did not discuss the trial. Mr. McCollum acknowledged that he had dated the complainant, had been arrested with her, and had an extensive criminal record. According to Mr. McCollum, he had seen the complainant with a gun in her store; she had asked him if he knew anywhere she could get nine-millimeter bullets.

Michael Sparkes

[19] Michael Sparkes testified that the complainant was a friend or acquaintance whom he had known for about four years since she opened the store. He related an incident from December 2000, when he told the complainant about a fight he had been involved in. During the conversation, she said: "I wish I was there, I would have capped their asses" or, "[y]ou should have run into here I would have capped their asses." On the same day, a few minutes later, the complainant gave Mr. Sparkes some marijuana and he asked her if she kept a gun in the store; in response, she showed him a gun, which Mr. Sparkes identified at trial. The complainant also asked Mr. Sparkes if he knew anyone who could get bullets for her.

[20] Mr. Sparkes went to see the respondent in jail about a month and a half after the respondent's arrest to ask him what happened. They talked about the gun, and Mr. Sparkes realized that the complainant had pulled out the same gun as the one he had seen her with in December 2000. Mr. Sparkes spoke to or visited the respondent three times while the respondent was in jail awaiting trial. He only revealed his information about the gun to the respondent's lawyer about six months after his visits to the respondent in jail.

Prior Consistent Statement

[21] Crown counsel cross-examined Mr. Sparkes and suggested that he and the respondent had "cobbled together" his version of events in jail, in anticipation of testifying at the respondent's trial. The Crown's cross-examination of Mr. McCollum also suggested that he and the respondent had spoken about their evidence prior to trial, while in custody together.

[22] To rebut the Crown's suggestion that the respondent had recently fabricated his version of events, the trial judge permitted the defence to lead evidence of the respondent's prior consistent statement to Ms. Foss-Liboiron, the respondent's girlfriend at the time of the incident. She testified that on the day of the incident, when they returned home from the Funky Skunk, the respondent showed her the gun and told her what had happened in the store. She recited essentially the same story as that later provided by the respondent in the course of his testimony:

A: Um, I asked him what happened with Cathy, he said that he - he went up to her store, and that she had - he had asked her if she had the money for him, she said: No. Um, she started talking about some stolen jewellery, saying that he could buy this jewellery and in return sell it. Alex said that that pissed him off. Um, he said that - let me just think back. He said that there was drugs on a table - or a counter. Um, he had said to her: If you don't have the money to pay me, how do you have the money for drugs? Um, and which in that time he threw the drugs on the floor.

Q: Okay. Did he tell you what kind of drugs they were?

A: It was cocaine.

Q: Did he tell you what happened after the drugs were thrown on the floor?

A: Yes, he said that Cathy went to the floor to try to salvage whatever drugs she could from the floor. Um, he said that she wasn't successful with doing that and she got quite angry and started screaming at him.

Q: Did he tell you anything else that took place?

A: Yes, he told me that she pulled a gun on him….

Q: Did he tell you what happened after she produced this firearm?

A: Yes, he told me that he tackled her that he got the gun from her.

Q: Okay. Did he use the word "tackle," or is that your -

A: No, that would be my word. He said there was a struggle, and that he ended up with the gun.

Q: Did he tell you anything about the struggle?

A: Yes. He told me that he threw her into a wall, or up against the wall.

…

Q: Now, was there anything else in this conversation that he told you?

A: Yes. He told me that she had - she had produced a watch, and she had asked him to take it for collateral.

[23] During his cross-examination of Ms. Foss-Liboiron, Crown counsel questioned the witness as to how honest the respondent was with her:

Q: So you don't think that Mr. Divitaris deceived you?

A: No, I don't.

Q: And the degree of honesty he showed to you, you're satisfied with that degree of honesty?

A: Yes, I am.

…

Q: Would you be surprised if I suggest to you that, in fact, - in fact, knowing that that watch, knowing in his mind that that watch was the product of a robbery, nevertheless Alex took that watch with him and had it in his pocket. Would that surprise you?

THE WITNESS: I don't understand the question.

CROWN COUNSEL: It's relevant, Your Honour, because it's yet another instance of where Mr. Divitaris has seen fit to deceive this witness.

DEFENCE COUNSEL: I don't know that it's been established that he did deceive the witness, Your Honour.

CROWN COUNSEL: He certainly deceived about his criminal record, there is no doubt.

THE COURT: Whether the witness would be surprised has slight evidential value.

THE TRIAL JUDGE'S RULING

[24] Despite repeated objections from the Crown, the trial judge charged the jury that they could use the evidence of the respondent's prior consistent statement to Ms. Foss-Liboiron not only to rebut the allegation of recent fabrication, but for the truth of its contents. The trial judge held that a prior statement made by an accused was subject to a different rule than that of a witness. He also stated that since the respondent's version of events at trial was basically the same as the one he had given to Ms. Foss-Liboiron, the issue of the permissible use of that evidence was "academic".

[25] The trial judge charged the jury, with respect to prior consistent statements, as follows:

What a witness said in an out-of-court statement is not admissible for the truth of its contents unless the witness has adopted it as true at this trial. An out-of-court prior statement may be admissible and may be used only to help you assess the witness' credibility. In other words, where the witness has made a prior consistent statement that consistency may be viewed as tending to enhance the credibility of the witness. However, it is important to note that the fact that the prior statement is consistent with the witness' testimony at trial does not mean the prior statement is corroborative or confirmatory of the truth of the witness' testimony at trial. This is because a deliberate lie or even an honest mistake, if that's the case, does not become truthful merely because it is repeated….

A different rule applies to the accused. In this case, several witnesses have testified about what they say Mr. Divitaris told them on an earlier occasion. In the circumstances of this case, of course, the first thing you have to decide is whether you accept the testimony that the accused did make the previous out-of-court statements that some of the witnesses said he did. If you decide that you accept this testimony or some of it, then what the accused said in his out-of-court statements is admissible for two purposes. First, the prior statement is admissible for the purpose of helping you assess the accused's credibility. If you accept that he did make these statements at an earlier time it may help you to decide whether his testimony at trial was or was not a recent fabrication. It may affect his credibility depending on whether the particulars in the early statement are consistent or inconsistent with his statement at trial. Second, in the case of the accused a previous statement is also admissible for the truth of its contents. In other words, if you accept that the accused did make a previous out-of-court statement about the events you may consider the content of that statement to help you decide what happened, what the facts are in this case. Thus, the prior statement is admissible for or against the accused on the issue of whether the Crown has proved his guilt beyond a reasonable doubt.

ISSUES

[26] The Crown appeals the respondent's acquittals under s. 686 of the Criminal Code, on the basis that the trial judge erred in law in instructing the jury that they were entitled to use the accused's statement to his girlfriend of what transpired in the Funky Skunk for the truth of its contents.

[27] The respondent submits that the trial judge made no error of law in his instruction, but that if he did, the Crown has not met its onus when appealing from an acquittal, as required by the Supreme Court in R. v. Vezeau, [1977] 2 S.C.R. 277, to show that the jury verdicts would likely have been different had the error not been made.

ANALYSIS

Prior Consistent Statement

[28] A prior consistent statement is normally not admissible because it is viewed as a form of self-serving evidence. It is considered (a) unhelpful because it adds nothing new to the evidence, and (b) potentially dangerous, because it may mislead the trier of fact into thinking that because a person said the same thing on more than one occasion, it is more likely to be true. On the contrary, a concocted statement, repeated on more than one occasion, remains concocted: See John Sopinka, Sidney N. Lederman & Alan W. Bryant, The Law of Evidence in Canada, 2nd ed. (Toronto: Butterworths, 1999) c. 7 at 313.

[29] However, as with most exclusionary rules, where there are circumstances that make a prior consistent statement relevant, it can be admissible for a limited purpose: See e.g. R. v. Demetrius (2003), 176 O.A.C. 349.

[30] One such circumstance is to rebut an allegation of recent fabrication. In that case, the purpose of allowing evidence of the prior consistent statement is to demonstrate that the statement was not recently fabricated because the person made a similar statement at a time before the impugned recent statement. For that purpose, it is still not admissible for the truth of its contents, but to rebut the allegation of recent fabrication and to assess the credibility of the person who made the impugned statement: The Law of Evidence in Canada, at 319. This applies equally to a witness and to the accused: R. v. Rosik (1970), 2 C.C.C. (2d) 351 (Ont. C.A.).

[31] Because the jury is only entitled to make limited use of a prior consistent statement, where the statement is admitted, the trial judge is required to include in the charge to the jury a limiting instruction explaining that the prior statement is not evidence of the facts it relates but may only be used to assist in determining the credibility of the impugned statement and its maker.. Rosik[1], R. v. A.(J.) 112 C.C.C. (3d) 528 at 536 (Ont. C.A.), R. v. Wait (1994), 69 O.A.C. 63 at paras. 4-5.

[32] The reason that the repetition of a statement becomes probative of the credibility of its maker, when according to the original rule, repetition of a statement does not make it more likely to be true, can be explained by the timing of the prior statement. When the prior consistent statement was made at a time before the person who made it would have had a reason to concoct the statement, the prior statement has some indicia of reliability.

[33] For example, in Rosik,[2] the accused did not testify but gave statements to the police that were admitted into evidence. In one of those statements, he said that he had ingested drugs before the shooting. Jessup J.A. (with whom Gale C.J.O. concurred on this point) was satisfied that an imputation of recent contrivance was implicit in the Crown's conduct of the trial. He therefore would have allowed the accused's wife and two others to give evidence that the accused told them before the crime that he had injested the drugs. The evidence would be admitted with an instruction from the trial judge that "such evidence was not evidence of the fact of the injestion of drugs, but was simply consistent with what the accused said in his statement to the police and should be considered for the sole purpose of deciding the truth of that part of the statement" (p. 390).

[34] This instruction appears to contradict the premise for the rule that excludes prior consistent statements - that repetition does not make the statement more likely to be true. However, because the prior statement was made at a time when there was no motive to fabricate, the likelihood that the prior statement was true, together with the consistency of the statements, could make the prior statement probative of the veracity of the second statement.

[35] However, when the prior consistent statement is made after the crime, when arguably there was a motive to fabricate, it is as unlikely to be true as the statement that the Crown impugns as a recent fabrication. That was what occurred in this case.

[36] In R. v. Campbell (1977), 38 C.C.C. (2d) 6, Martin J. A expressed the view that the exception regarding the admissibility of previous statements to rebut an allegation of recent fabrication can be difficult to apply. However, in explaining the concept, the examples he refers to all describe situations where the prior statement was made at a time before there was a motive or opportunity to fabricate (p.19-20).

[37] In this case, the issue arose through the Crown's suggestion to the defence witness, Mr. Sparkes, that he and the accused had cobbled together the story that the gun belonged to the complainant when Mr. Sparkes visited the accused in jail, i.e., the Crown suggested that the story was recently fabricated. Once that suggestion was made, defence counsel sought to lead from the witness Ms. Foss-Liboiron, the accused's girlfriend who was with him that day, waiting for him in a taxi, what the accused told her after they returned to her mother's apartment about what had transpired in the Funky Skunk. Ms. Foss-Liboiron was permitted to recount the accused's version of the story. Although Crown counsel at trial conceded that he had suggested recent fabrication, he took the firm position with the trial judge that the jury had to be given a limiting instruction as to the purpose of the evidence of the prior consistent statement and the use that could be made of it.

[38] The trial judge rejected this submission. In his charge to the jury, the trial judge told them that where a prior consistent statement is made by an accused, as opposed to another witness, the statement is admitted into evidence for the truth of its contents.

[39] In his questioning of the witness, Ms. Foss-Liboiron, and in his address to the jury, Crown counsel suggested that the accused was lying to Ms. Foss-Liboiron and that she had no independent knowledge of whether the facts were true other than what the accused had told her. The respondent on the appeal submits that because the Crown put the truth of the statement in issue, the jury was entitled to use it either for or against the accused, depending on whether they believed it was true or not. The respondent says that the trial judge was therefore correct in this case, that the accused's statement to Ms. Foss-Liboiron could be used by the jury for the truth of its content.

[40] In my view this analysis is flawed. The trial judge erred by instructing the jury that they could use a prior consistent statement by the accused for the truth of its contents. The statement was tendered to rebut the Crown's suggestion that some time after the event, Mr. Sparkes and the accused concocted the story that the gun found in the accused's apartment belonged to the complainant. Although the Crown concedes that the evidence was admissible for that purpose, it is clear that the evidence would not bolster Mr. Sparkes' credibility; rather, it seems to have been agreed that because it was alleged that Mr. Sparkes and the accused concocted the story together, the accused's prior statement to his girlfriend could be led to rebut the allegation that the accused concocted the story with Mr. Sparkes.

[41] However, because the accused told his girlfriend the story following the events of the alleged crimes, when he already had a reason to fabricate, there is no basis to conclude that the statement was likely true. In other words, one of the reasons why prior consistent statements are considered non-probative applies here. In these circumstances, unlike in Rosik, there was no reason for the jury to consider the consistency of the statements, taken alone, as probative of the truth of the impugned evidence.

[42] Therefore, even the instruction suggested in Rosik that the jury could use the prior statement, not for its truth but to assess the credibility of the accused's version of what occurred, would not have been appropriate in this case. The most the jury could do with the accused's prior statement to his girfriend, if they accepted that it was made (or had a reasonable doubt about it) was to use it to rebut the allegation that the accused concocted the story about the gun with Sparkes, i.e. to rebut the allegation of recent fabrication with Sparkes, and in that context, to use the consistency of his statements as a factor in assessing his credibility.

The Vezeau Test

[43] Where the Crown appeals against an acquittal on a question of law, in order to set aside the verdict, the court must be satisfied that without the error, "the verdict would not necessarily have been the same". Vezeau, supra, at 292. The Supreme Court has emphasized that the Crown's onus is a heavy one: R. v. Morin, [1988] 2 S.C.R. 345 at para. 80. See also R. v. MacKenzie, [1993] 1 S.C.R. 212 at 217.

[44] The Crown submits that it has met the onus on the following analysis:

  • the case turned on credibility;
  • the trial judge told the jury: "The case for the defence also depends entirely on the credibility of Mr. Divitaris, and his witnesses";
  • in his jury address, defence counsel suggested that Ms. Foss-Liboiron was perhaps the most important witness after the accused;
  • because the trial judge misdirected the jury as to how to approach her evidence, it is logical that the jury misused her evidence;
  • consequently, with a proper instruction, the jury's verdict would not necessarily have been the same.

[45] I do not accept this submission for four reasons. First, while it is true that the case turned on the credibility of the complainant and of the accused, the verdicts demonstrated that the jury did not accept all of the evidence of either of them. The jury acquitted the appellant on the armed robbery and assault causing bodily harm charges, but convicted him of theft of the complainant's watch.

[46] His story was that the complainant had asked him to take the watch as collateral against the money she owed him. Her story was that he took it from her. From the verdict, although the jury had a reasonable doubt about everything else that occurred during the encounter, they were satisfied beyond a reasonable doubt that the accused was not telling the truth about the watch. This is particularly significant because in her evidence, Ms. Foss-Liboiron said that the accused told her that Ms. Cosborn offered the watch to him as collateral. The jury must have rejected this fact as untrue.

[47] Second, there were several apparent discrepancies in the evidence given by the complainant, particularly in respect of the rental car and regarding the extent of her injuries. These problems weakened the Crown's case.

[48] Third, the respondent's credibility was the main focus of Crown counsel's address to the jury. Crown counsel made it clear to the jury in his address that because Ms. Foss-Liboiron was not present during the encounter between the accused and the complainant, she did not know what happened other than what the accused had told her. Crown counsel's position was that the accused was not telling the truth and that his repetition of the story to his girlfriend after the fact did not add anything new to the story. He also cross-examined Ms. Foss-Liboiron on the accused's lack of candour to her, again emphasizing that it was the accused's credibility that was in issue. Furthermore, in his closing to the jury, counsel for the accused agreed, when discussing Ms. Foss-Liboiron's credibility, that she had no independent knowledge of what happened in the store that night. Therefore, it was emphasized to the jury that they needed to look at the accused's credibility on the issue of what occurred.

[49] Fourth, in response to the Crown's objection to the judge's charge regarding the instruction on the prior consistent statement, the trial judge declined to reconsider his previous ruling on the basis that he did not think the matter was important in this case. I agree with respondent's submission that the trial judge was in a good position to assess the significance of this aspect of the evidence in the context of the entire record before the jury. In any event, I agree with the trial judge's observation. The instruction that Crown counsel was seeking was in accordance with the instruction proposed by Jessup J.A. in Rosik, where the trial judge would have told the jury that they could use the evidence to assess the credibility of the accused's version of the events. As discussed above, in my view that instruction would not have been appropriate here where the prior consistent statement was made after the event and had no enhanced indicia of reliability. All that was appropriate was that the jury could use the prior statement to rebut the allegation of recent fabrication with Sparkes, and in that context, to consider the consistency of his statements in their assessment of his credibility. However, had the trial judge given the instruction requested by the Crown, it would have been very similar in meaning and effect to what the jury was actually told, since Ms. Foss-Liboiron recounted a version of the event that the respondent told her that was almost identical to the version presented in the respondent's testimony.

CONCLUSION

[50] Although an error of law was made, this is not a case where the Crown has met its burden to show that without the error, the verdict of the jury would likely have been different. The appeal is therefore dismissed.
