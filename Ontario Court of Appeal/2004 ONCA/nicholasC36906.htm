On appeal from acquittals entered by Justice Peter G. Jarvis of the Superior Court of Justice on February 22, 2001, and March 23, 2001, and from the conviction entered on March 23, 2001.

ABELLA J.A.:

[1] In the summer of 1999, the police set up a Task Force to investigate a series of break and enters and sexual assaults believed to be committed by the "Scarborough Bedroom Rapist". As a result of that investigation, Eli Stewart Nicholas was arrested on September 25, 1999.

BACKGROUND

[2] The charges against Mr. Nicholas arose out of three incidents of an intruder breaking into three different homes in Scarborough and physically assaulting, and, in two of the cases, sexually assaulting the female occupants. The first incident, involving A.U., occurred on June 2, 1999. The second incident, involving G.W., occurred on August 21, 1999. The third incident, involving F.G., occurred on September 13, 1999.

[3] Mr. Nicholas was convicted of sexual assault and break and enter in connection with the events on September 13, 1999 relating to F.G. He appeals from these convictions on the basis that the trial judge erred in admitting DNA evidence taken while he was detained and in violation of his s. 10 (b) Charter rights. The Crown concedes that if Mr. Nicholas was detained when he gave his DNA sample, his rights were violated.

[4] Mr. Nicholas was acquitted of the charges relating to G.W. The Crown brought an application to admit out-of-court statements made by G.W. This application was dismissed. The Crown appeals from that acquittal on the basis that the trial judge erred in refusing to admit the evidence as an exception to the hearsay rule.

[5] Mr. Nicholas was also acquitted of the charges relating to A.U. During the proceedings, the Crown brought an application to admit similar fact evidence from the F.G. incident for the purpose of identifying the accused as the perpetrator of the A.U. incident. The trial judge dismissed the application. The Crown appeals from the acquittal on the basis that the trial judge erred in refusing to admit the similar fact evidence.

Investigation

[6] The Task Force received hundreds of tips. Based on them, the police identified between one and two hundred "persons of interest". At trial, the distinction between a person of interest and a suspect was explained as being that a person of interest has no incriminating evidence linking him or her to the offence, whereas a suspect does.

[7] As part of the investigation, persons of interest were asked to provide a buccal swab for a DNA analysis. If they agreed to do so, they were asked to sign a consent form. At the time the consent DNA samples were obtained, the police did not have any suspects.

[8] People who refused to consent were placed under surveillance for the purposes of obtaining a discard sample. Two officers testified at trial that out of seven individuals who refused, discard samples were subsequently obtained from four of them. No follow-up was conducted on the other three individuals.

[9] Mr. Nicholas, who was nineteen years old, was first identified as a person of interest on September 20, 1999 as a result of a Crime Stoppers tip. At 12:47 p.m. on September 21, 1999, Cst. Catalano went to Mr. Nicholas's home, where his mother indicated that he was at the library but had a curfew. The officer left his card and asked that Mr. Nicholas call him and that he would return to see him.

[10] On September 22, Cst. Catalano confirmed that Mr. Nicholas was on a recognizance with a curfew of 9:00 p.m. to 6:00 a.m. Since Cst. Catalano's shift ended at 4:00 p.m., two other police officers, D/Cst. Tanel and D/Cst. Dyck, attended at Mr. Nicholas's home at 10:10 p.m. A man named Peter Powell answered the door, invited the officers in, and directed the officers upstairs to Mr. Nicholas' room. Testimony given by the officers indicated that Mr. Powell, whose relationship with Mr. Nicholas was unclear from the evidence, appeared very animated and seemed to be angry with Mr. Nicholas.

[11] When the officers arrived upstairs, Mr. Nicholas was coming out of the bathroom and was wearing a towel. After identifying themselves with their badges, the officers informed him that they were investigating a series of sexual assaults and break and enters in the Scarborough area. They told him his name had come up as a person of interest and that they would like to speak to him. Mr. Nicholas replied "sure, no problem" and asked if he could get dressed first. When he went to get dressed, the officers waited outside on the landing. Since the light in Mr. Nicholas' bedroom was not working, D/Cst. Dyck suggested that they go to the kitchen to talk.

[12] When they arrived in the kitchen, Mr Powell could be heard talking to a friend in the other room so the officers suggested he move to the basement. D/Cst. Dyck again explained to Mr. Nicholas that they were investigating a series of sexual assaults and that he was a person of interest they wanted to speak to. D/Cst. Dyck read the consent form to him and Mr. Nicholas agreed to provide a buccal swab. D/Cst. Dyck then turned the audiotape on, read the consent form again, and asked Mr. Nicholas if he understood it. Mr. Nicholas answered "yes".

[13] The key portion of the audiotape transcript reads as follows:

Dyck: I'm just gonna read something to you here. It's a consent form, and-ah I'm just gonna read this. Toronto Police Service is investigating a series of break and enters which have occurred between the twenty-third of June and twenty-first of August Nineteen-ninety-nine, inclusive. During the course of these break and enters female persons have been subjected to either physical or sexual assault. We are seeking permission from you to obtain bodily samples including blood or saliva for scientific testing, including DNA analysis. The samples obtained from you will be scienti-fically tested and analysed and compared with evidence obtained from the other crime scenes. The purpose of this analysis and comparison is to enable investigators to attempt to positively identify the person responsible for the break and enters, sexual assaults and to also enable the investigators to eliminate the persons as possible suspects. You understand that so far Sir?

Background Noise: (Unintelligible)

Nicholas: Yes

Dyck: You are not required to give us these samples. You may refuse to provide these samples. If you agree to provide samples for analysis, the results of the analysis may be used against you in criminal proceedings. You may wish to discuss this request with anybody including a lawyer and you are free to do so now. Do you understand what I've just read to you sir?"

Nicholas: Yes Sir.

Dyck: Do you have any questions?

Nicholas: No.

[14] Mr. Nicholas's mother, who had come into the kitchen at some point during the interview, then asked the police "[S]houldn't he have a lawyer?" The officers replied that it was "up to him" and told her that Mr. Nicholas had stated that he understood his opportunity to speak "to a lawyer or anyone else for that matter".

[15] Mr. Nicholas then signed the form, swabbed the inside of his mouth, and handed the swabs to the officers. The officers left his house at approximately 10:32 p.m.

[16] Mr. Nicholas's swabs were forwarded to the lab along with consent samples from other persons of interest, and the Task Force continued its investigation.

[17] On September 25, 1999, the police learned that the DNA samples they had obtained from Mr. Nicholas matched DNA found at the crime scene involving G.W. on August 21, and F.G. on September 13, 1999.

[18] That same day, a search warrant was executed at Mr. Nicholas's residence and a loaded handgun was found in his bedroom closet. He was arrested and charged with thirteen counts arising from the three incidents, including sexual assault, break and enter and weapons related charges.

[19] Afterwards, the police realized that the consent form only referred to incidents between June 23 and August 21. This excluded the September 13 incident involving F.G. To rectify this omission, the police seized a blood sample from Mr. Nicholas, pursuant to a warrant executed under s. 486.05 of the Criminal Code. The DNA profile obtained from the blood sample was matched to the DNA profile obtained from the G.W. and F. G. crime scenes.

THE CONVICTION APPEAL: The "F.G." Incident: September 13, 1999

[20] F.G. was seventy-five years old and lived with her physically disabled husband. Her two-storey home was close to two parks. She awoke on September 13, 1999, to find a male lying on top of her. The male fled after she began to yell and struggle. She discovered that twenty dollars was missing from her wallet and that an orange juice carton had been removed from the refrigerator and left in her bedroom. DNA was taken from the carton. It matched Mr. Nicholas's DNA.

The DNA Sample

[21] At trial, counsel for Mr. Nicholas argued that Mr. Nicholas was detained when the police came to his home. Consequently, since the Crown acknowledged that Mr. Nicholas was not properly informed of his right to counsel as required by s. 10(b) of the Charter, the DNA sample was improperly admitted. His alternative argument was that Mr. Nicholas did not provide a voluntary and informed consent to the taking of the sample, and in the absence of such consent, the seizure was unreasonable and a violation of s. 8 of the Charter.

[22] The trial judge held a voir dire to examine the circumstances surrounding the taking of the sample. Mr. Nicholas did not testify at the voir dire.

[23] The trial judge observed that Mr. Nicholas was allowed to enter his bedroom without supervision or control and that the police never assumed any physical control over his actions. He also found that the only police directions were about where their conversation with Mr. Nicholas would take place since his bedroom was unavailable. He concluded:

In fact, the actions of the police indicate a sensitivity to Mr. Nicholas' privacy interests, as did their efforts to clear the other occupants of the main floor in advance of the interview. At some point Mr. Nicholas' mother appeared and her presence was countenanced. All of this evidence satisfies me that there was no demand or direction implicit or explicit in the actions of the police which had any bearing on the signing of the consent by Mr. Nicholas [emphasis added]

[24] The trial judge also examined whether the consent provided by the accused was voluntary and informed.

[25] Dr. Nathan Pollock testified at the voir dire on behalf of the defence. His opinion was that there was a possibility that Mr. Nicholas' personality profile might have affected his ability to give an informed consent to the request for the DNA. Dr. Pollock based his opinion that Mr. Nicholas did not understand the consequences of giving a DNA sample on some rap lyrics written by Mr. Nicholas which contained the words, "DNA, ain't gonna solve Ya Murda". The trial judge rejected Dr. Pollock's suggestion.

[26] Applying the test in R. v. Borden (1994), 92 C.C.C. (3d) 404 (S.C.C.), and after considering the evidence of intelligence test results, the testimony from Dr. Pollock, and audio and videotapes, the trial judge concluded that the accused had sufficient understanding to give a voluntary and informed consent.

[27] Most significantly, he relied on the fact that Mr. Nicholas' failure to testify at the voir dire, resulted in an absence of evidence of compulsion. The trial judge noted that Mr. Nicholas:

[d]id not testify in the voir dire and there is no direct evidence of his subjective state of mind other than the external objective evidence which shows that he knew and understood what he was doing and saying. There was evidence from Dr. Pollock bearing on this, which I deal with below. The only indication from Mr. Nicholas regarding his consent or otherwise, consist of his utterances immediately after his arrest and his statements during his interview on September 27, 1999, in which he stated several times that he had voluntarily consented to the sample.

It was argued with some force on Mr. Nicholas's behalf that the very fact that he had consented made no sense and could only be evidence of a complete failure to understand the consequences of what he was doing. With the greatest respect, human actions are not always logical, nor are they always consistent with self-interest. I am convinced on all the evidence that Mr. Nicholas did not feel compelled to co-operate and acted voluntarily.

[28] However, he also concluded that since the dates on the written consent form were limited to June 23 and August 21, 1999, Mr. Nicholas's s. 8 Charter right was breached by the use of the DNA sample in connection with the offences that took place on September 13, 1999.

[29] The trial judge accordingly engaged in a s. 24 (2) analysis, applying the three-stage inquiry in R. v. Collins (1987), 33 C.C.C. (3d) 1 (S.C.C.): the fairness of the trial; the seriousness of the violation; and the effect of the exclusion on the administration of justice.

[30] Relying on Cory J.'s comments in R. v. Stillman (1996), 113 C.C.C. (3d) 321 (S.C.C.) at p. 360, the trial judge concluded that the Crown had established, on a balance of probabilities, that alternative non-conscriptive means, like discard samples, were available and that the police would have availed themselves of them, as they had during the course of their investigation when other people refused to consent to providing a buccal swab.

[31] The trial judge also considered the manner in which the search was conducted; the bona fides of the investigators; the duration and intensity of the Charter violation; and the regard for the suspect's rights demonstrated by the officers. He observed that the written consent was detailed, informative, and, although missing the September 13th date, the date was mentioned orally to the accused during the meeting, albeit after he had signed the consent. He found that no force was used and that there was no intention on the part of the police to mislead.

[32] The breach, therefore, was not a serious one.

[33] Citing Cory J.'s conclusion in R. v. Belnavis (1997), 118 C.C.C. (3d) 405 at 426 (S.C.C.), that evidence necessary to substantiate a serious charge should not be excluded where the breach is trivial, inadvertent or technical, the trial judge concluded that the exclusion of the evidence based on the missing date in the consent form, would bring the administration of justice into disrepute.

[34] The DNA evidence was therefore admitted in connection with the incidents involving F.G. and G.W. DNA was not obtained from the A.U. crime scene. Mr. Nicholas was convicted of break and enter and sexual assault in relation to the F.G. counts, as well as possession of a loaded prohibited weapon.

Analysis

[35] The primary issue is whether Mr. Nicholas, at the time he provided his DNA sample, was detained. The Crown conceded that if Mr. Nicholas was detained, his s. 10(b) Charter right to counsel was violated. The appellant acknowledges that the only aspect of detention which arises on this conviction appeal is whether Mr. Nicholas was "psychologically detained" and under a psychological compulsion to comply with the police request. If he was not detained, the appellant argues that he did not give a valid and informed consent to providing a sample and therefore his s. 8 Charter right was violated.

[36] Le Dain J. observed in R. v. Therens (1985), 18 C.C.C. (3d) 481 (S.C.C.) at 505 that,

[D]etention may be effected without the application or threat of application of physical restraint if the person concerned submits or acquiesces in the deprivation of liberty and reasonably believes that the choice to do otherwise does not exist.

See also R. v. Thomsen (1988), 40 C.C.C. 3d 411 (S.C.C.) at p. 417.

[37] In R. v. Bazinet (1986), 25 C.C.C. (3d) 273 (Ont. C.A.) Tarnopolsky J.A. emphazised at p. 283 that Le Dain J.'s extension of detention to instances of psychological detention is predicated upon "two requirements: (1) a 'demand or direction', in response to which (2) 'the person concerned submits or acquiesces in the deprivation of liberty and reasonably believes that the choice to do otherwise does not exist'".

[38] In R. v. Moran, (1987), 36 C.C.C. (3d) 225 (Ont. C.A.) at pp. 258 - 259 Martin J.A. set out a non-exhaustive list of factors to be considered when determining whether an accused has been psychologically detained:

1. the precise language used by the police officer in requesting the person who subsequently becomes an accused to come to the police station, and whether the accused was given a choice or expressed a preference that the interview be conducted at the police station, rather than at his or her home;

2. whether the accused was escorted to the police station or came himself or herself in response to a police request;

3. whether the accused left at the conclusion of the interview or whether he or she was arrested;

4. the stage of the investigation, that is, whether the questioning was part of the general investigation of a crime or possible crime or whether the police had already decided that a crime had been committed and that the accused was the perpetrator or involved in its commission and the questioning was conducted for the purpose of obtaining incriminating statements from the accused;

5. whether the police had reasonable and probable grounds to believe that the accused had committed the crime being investigated;

6. the nature of the questions: whether they were questions of a general nature designed to obtain information or whether the accused was confronted with evidence pointing to his or her guilt;

7. the subjective belief by an accused that he or she is detained, although relevant, is not decisive, because the issue is whether he or she reasonably believed that he or she was detained. Personal circumstances relating to the accused, such as low intelligence, emotional disturbance, youth and lack of sophistication are circumstances to be considered in determining whether he had a subjective belief that he was detained [emphasis in original].

[39] These factors were applied by the trial judge and he concluded, properly in my view, that several facts indicate that Mr. Nicholas was not detained.

[40] In these circumstances, where the trial judge made findings manifestly endorsing the propriety of the police conduct in the circumstances, it was important to have evidence from Mr. Nicholas as to his state of mind when he signed the consent form (see R. v. Caputo (1997), 114 C.C.C. (3d) 1 (Ont. C.A.)). As has been previously pointed out, Mr. Nicholas gave no evidence on the voir dire.

[41] Steel J.A. in R. v. H. (C.R.) (2003), 174 C.C.C. (3d) 67 (Man. C.A.) at paras. 45-6, discussed the importance of evidence of compulsion from an accused for purposes of determining whether he or she was detained as follows:

In most of the cases where a detention has been found, evidence of compulsion arises from the testimony of the accused on the voir dire. Although not conclusive, the testimony of the accused is often crucial to the question of whether there was a reasonable belief that a compulsion to reply was present…I am not suggesting that there can never be a finding of detention in the absence of testimony from the accused, but such a determination would require stronger facts than exist in this case. The court must still have evidence from which it can infer that the circumstances were such that the accused could reasonably have concluded that his freedom had been restrained. (See also R. v. Bazinet (1986), 25 C.C.C. (3d) 273 (Ont. C.A.) and R. v. Esposito (1985), 49 C.R. (3d) 193 (Ont. C.A.)).

[42] There is no evidence of any kind in this case indicating that Mr. Nicholas felt psychologically compelled to speak to the police or to provide a sample. D/Cst. Dyck testified that the police had not made any demand of Mr. Nicholas, nor had they ordered him to do anything, and Mr. Nicholas did not express any concern or confusion either before sitting down at the kitchen table or after having the consent form read to him.

[43] While it was undoubtedly disconcerting to Mr. Nicholas, dressed in a towel, to find the police waiting for him outside his bathroom door, this alone is an insufficient fact to ground a finding of detention given what happened next. He was permitted to change, and while it is true that the police waited for him outside his bedroom while he got dressed, the only direction they gave him was that they speak in the kitchen because the light in his bedroom was not working.

[44] The trial judge found that the suggestion by the police that they move to the kitchen was not a demand or direction, and that the police were polite and non-threatening to Mr. Nicholas. The consent form, which was read twice to Mr. Nicholas, clearly stated that he could refuse to provide the sample. Mr. Nicholas was not arrested at the end of the interview and the police continued their investigation. The police were conducting the consent DNA sampling procedure with other residents and had over one hundred persons of interest.

[45] In view of the trial judge's findings and in view of the absence of any evidence of psychological compulsion, I find no basis for disturbing the trial judge's finding that Mr. Nicholas was not detained.

[46] The trial judge also found that Mr. Nicholas provided a voluntary and informed consent to the DNA sample. I see no basis for disturbing this finding.

[47] This Court in R. v. Blackstock, [1997] O.J. No. 3597 (C.A.) at para. 5 held that the Crown "bears the burden of proving an effective consent to the taking of such samples and in many cases, will fail to meet that burden where the police have not expressly told a person that he or she has a right to refuse".

[48] In R. v. Wills (1992), 70 C.C.C. (3d) 529 (Ont. C.A.), Doherty J.A. listed six factors at p. 546 which the Crown must establish on the balance of probabilities for a finding that an individual waived his or her right to be secure against an unreasonable seizure:

(i) there was a consent, express or implied;

(ii) the giver of the consent had the authority to give the consent in question;

(iii) the consent was voluntary in the sense that that word is used in Goldman, [1980 1 S.C.R. 976] supra, and was not the product of police oppression, coercion or other external conduct which negated the freedom to choose whether or not to allow the police to pursue the course of conduct requested;

(iv) the giver of the consent was aware of the nature of the police conduct to which he or she was being asked to consent;

(v) the giver of the consent was aware of his or her right to refuse to permit the police to engage in the conduct requested, and

(iv) the giver of the consent was aware of the potential consequences of giving the consent.

[49] The arguments of defence counsel relate primarily to the last three factors.

[50] The application of the factors in Wills to this case demonstrates that the Crown has established that Mr. Nicholas consented to the seizure of his DNA, at least in relation to the A.U. and G.W. incidents. The police informed Mr. Nicholas twice that they wanted to speak to him about a series of break-ins and sexual assaults. Nicholas was informed that the police were investigating a series of assaults, that he was a person of interest, that the sample would be scientifically analysed, and that it would be compared with evidence obtained from the crime scenes. The police explicitly told Mr. Nicholas that this comparison was to enable the police to identify the person responsible for the crimes and that the DNA sample could be used in criminal proceedings against him. The consent form stated that the police were seeking permission, and that Mr. Nicholas did not have to provide the sample.

[51] The trial judge found, however, that the use of the sample in the investigation of the September 13, 1999 assault against F.G. breached Mr. Nicholas's Charter right to be secure against unreasonable search and seizure because the consent form did not refer specifically to that date. The appellant submits that he erred in failing to exclude the evidence under s. 24(2).

[52] The trial judge found the police conduct to be, on the whole, beyond reproach. He found the violation not to be a serious one. Moreover, the Crown's evidence was that the police had already successfully obtained discard samples from other individuals who had refused to provide a sample, and planned to obtain discard samples had Mr. Nicholas refused. This formed the basis for the trial judge's conclusion that the evidence was otherwise discoverable.

[53] The trial judge conducted a thorough and well-reasoned s. 24(2) analysis and I see no basis for interfering with his conclusion.

[54] In conclusion, I would dismiss Mr. Nicholas's appeal from the convictions.

THE ACQUITTALS

(i) Similar Fact Evidence: The "A.U." Incident, June 2, 1999

[55] During the early morning hours of June 2, 1999, sixty-eight year old A.U. awoke to find an intruder holding a pillow over her face. She began to struggle and the intruder fled the residence. She discovered that twenty-five dollars had been stolen and that an orange juice carton had been taken from the refrigerator, partially consumed, and left in a spare bedroom. The container was not tested for DNA.

[56] A.U. lived with her physically and mentally disabled husband. They both slept in the upper level of their home, which bordered on a park.

[57] A.U. was unable to identify her attacker and there was no DNA evidence obtained from her home. The Crown sought to adduce evidence of the offences on September 13, 1999 involving F.G. as similar fact evidence relevant to the identity of A.U.'s attacker. Mr. Nicholas's DNA had been found on the orange juice container in F.G.'s home.

[58] The Crown argued that there were both unique trademarks and sufficient similarities between the F.G. and A.U. incidents to render objectively improbable the likelihood that two different perpetrators committed the offences.

[59] The trial judge excluded the similar fact evidence on the basis that none of it was sufficiently unique to satisfy the requirements in R. v. Arp (1998), 129 C.C.C. (3d) 321 (S.C.C.) and found that its probative value was insufficient to outweigh its prejudicial effect.

[60] He first considered whether there was a unique signature or trade mark and concluded that the:

[o]nly 'unique' factor…was that in each case the perpetrator took a container of orange juice from each complainant's refrigerator and apparently consumed part or all of which as he proceeded through each house. I take judicial notice of the fact that orange juice is probably to be found in the vast majority of kitchen refrigerators in Canada…I do not consider the consumption of a beverage from a victim's refrigerator by a perpetrator to be sufficiently unique to satisfy the requirements in Arp.

…

Of more interest and difficulty is whether the other similarities in these events are such that coincidence is an objective improbability.

Let me say at first that many of the details of the evidence are similar and no doubt have some probative value. I am not, however, convinced that their probative value comes close to outweighing their prejudicial effect. The most obvious simi-larities are the following:

(a) the age of the each victim;

(b) each lived with a disabled husband;

(c) each offence occurred during the early hours of the morning;

(d) the perpetrator fled at the first sign of resistance;

(e) in each case the perpetrator was in the victim's bedroom when discovered, in one case lying atop of the victim, and in the other standing beside her bed. In both cases an attempt was made to cover the face of the victim.

…

In my view, none of the above factors are sufficiently unique, either alone or cumulatively to satisfy me on the balance of probabilities that the same person necessarily committed these acts. In other words, I consider the probative value of the proposed evidence to be slight and its prejudicial effect likely to be correspondingly high. Assuming the [F.G.] DNA evidence were admitted, the proposed evidence would make a conviction by the jury on the [A.U.] counts overwhelmingly likely, even with the most careful instructions from me.

[61] The trial judge concluded that the evidence fell short of meeting that threshold. Having excluded the similar fact evidence, Mr. Nicholas was acquitted of the A.U. incident.

[62] The Crown argues that the trial judge erred in two ways. First, in concluding that the consumption of orange juice was not a unique trademark, and by focusing on the uniqueness of the similarities when assessing their probative value. Second, by relying solely on the increased chance of conviction when determining the prejudicial effect of admitting the evidence.

Analysis

[63] I am mindful that a trial judge's exercise of discretion regarding the admissibility of similar fact evidence is entitled to a high level of deference: R. v. Arp, at para. 42; R. v. Harvey (2001), 57 O.R. (3d) 296 (C.A.), affd (2002), 169 C.C.C. (3d) 576 (S.C.C.) at para. 50.

[64] Applying Arp, the trial judge took a two-step approach and considered first whether the orange juice was a unique trademark. He concluded it was not, but then observed, as has been previously noted: "Of more interest and difficulty is whether the other similarities in these events are such that coincidence is an objective probability [emphasis added]." He listed five similarities, but does not list the orange juice. He observed that:

none of the above factors are sufficiently unique, either alone or cumulatively to satisfy me on the balance of probabilities that the same person necessarily committed these acts.

[65] A higher degree of scrutiny of the similarities is required where identity is the issue. This requirement is explained by Justice David Watt in Watt's Manual of Criminal Evidence, (Toronto: Carswell, 2002) at p. 479 as follows:

Where similar act evidence is adduced to prove identity, a high degree of similarity between the acts is required. The similarity may consist of

i. a unique trademark or signature; or

ii. a series of significant similarities.

In assessing similarity, the trial judge should only consider the manner in which the acts were committed, not the evidence of D's involvement in each act. As a general rule, where the trial judge is satisfied that there is a sufficient degree of similarity between the acts that they were likely committed by the same person, similar fact evidence will usually have sufficient probative value to outweigh its prejudicial effect and justify its admission [emphasis in original].

[66] The trial judge relied on the following passage from Arp at para. 45:

… a principled approach to the admission of similar fact evidence will in all cases rest on the finding that the accused's involvement in the alleged similar acts or counts is unlikely to be the product of coincidence. This conclusion ensures that the evidence has sufficient probative force to be admitted, and will involve different considerations in different contexts. Where, as here, similar fact evidence is adduced on the issue of identity, there must be a high degree of similarity between the acts for the evidence to be admitted. For example, a unique trademark or signature will automatically render the alleged acts 'strikingly similar' and therefore highly probative and admissible. In the same way, a number of significant similarities taken together, may be such that by their cumulative effect, they warrant admission of the evidence. Where identity is at issue ordinarily, the trial judge should review the manner in which the similar acts were committed - that is to say, whether the similar acts involve a unique trademark or reveal a number of significant similarities.

It appears from the trial judge's reasons, with respect, that he interpreted this passage to mean that in order to find significant similarities, there must be a uniqueness to the factors, either individually or cumulatively.

[67] His error, in my view, was in his emphasis on uniqueness, rather than on considering whether the cumulative evidence, including the close timing of the offences, revealed a series of significant similarities sufficient to render the objective possibility of coincidence improbable.

[68] I agree that in itself there is nothing unique about the consumption of orange juice, but when taken together with the other similarities, it seems to me to reduce substantially the likelihood of coincidence.

[69] The Crown also argued that the trial judge erred in finding that the increased risk of conviction increased the prejudicial impact of the similar fact evidence. As Binnie J. wrote in R. v. Handy (2002), 164 C.C.C. (3d) 481(S.C.C.) at para. 139, a decision which, it is important to note, had not been released when the trial judge made his ruling, "[i]t is frequently mentioned that 'prejudice' in this context is not the risk of conviction. It is, more properly, the risk of an unfocussed trial and a wrongful conviction."

[70] In balancing prejudice and probative value, the inquiry into probative value necessarily involves assessing the strength of the similar fact evidence; identifying the issue at trial to which the proposed similar facts are said to be relevant; and identifying the factors that connect or distinguish the similar fact evidence from the facts alleged in the charge (Handy at para. 99). The relative importance of the issue in the particular trial is also influential in the weighing exercise (Handy at para. 74).

[71] The dissimilarities between the incidents must be taken into account:

1) In one incident, the intruder did not say anything; in the other, he said "Relax"

2) In one incident, there was more wandering by the intruder through the house

3) In one incident, the orange juice was left in the bedroom where the assault took place, in the other the orange juice was left in the empty spare bedroom

4) In one case, the intruder lay on top of the victim; in the case of the other, he was holding a pillow over her head

5) In one case, the victim's husband was sleeping in a separate bedroom; in the other, the husband was sleeping next to the victim

[72] There are, however, a number of significant similarities between the subject matter of the charge and the similar acts, as well as the circumstances surrounding the acts:

1) The intruder in both cases took orange juice out of the fridge, consumed some of it, and carried it upstairs

2) Both victims were female

3) The victims were sixty-eight and seventy-five years old

4) Both victims had disabled husbands who were sleeping upstairs at the time of the attack

5) Both incidents took place in Scarborough within weeks of each other

6) The homes of both victims were within walking distance of the residence of Mr. Nicholas

7) Both victims lived in close proximity to a park

8) The victims lived within blocks of each other

9) Both acts occurred in the early a.m. hours

10) The intruder in both cases took a small amount of money and did not take anything else of value

11) Both victims described their attacker in similar terms, such as "not a very big person"

[73] There is no doubt that some of these similarities are more significant than others. For example, the age of the victims, the disability of their husbands, the location and timing of the offences, and, in particular, the drinking of the orange juice.

[74] In my view, the evidence is so strikingly similar as to elevate the probative value over the inevitable prejudice. The modus operandi of the intruder in assaulting elderly female victims with disabled husbands, stealing only a small amount of money, and wandering throughout the house drinking orange juice, as well as the time, geographic location, and pattern of the acts, render it objectively improbable that Mr. Nicholas's involvement is the product of coincidence (see also R. v. Fisher, [2003] S.J. No. 597 (Sask. C.A.)).

[75] Accordingly, with respect, I think the trial judge erred in law by excluding this evidence.

(ii) Hearsay: The "G.W." Incident, August 21, 1999

[76] G.W. was fifty-four years old. On August 21, 1999, she awoke to find someone standing by her bed. The intruder repeatedly hit her on the head, covered her face with a pillow and had intercourse with her. G.W. made an eleven minute 911 call approximately ten minutes after the intruder left. Later, G.W. discovered that her wallet had been stolen.

[77] After attending at the hospital for a rape kit examination, G.W. gave an unsworn videotaped statement to the police. This occurred approximately six hours after the assault.

[78] Mr. Nicholas's semen was found in G.W.'s vagina.

[79] After the attack, G.W. suffered from post-traumatic stress disorder. At trial, G.W.'s physician gave evidence indicating that forcing G.W. to testify could result in a complete inability to function and that the prospect of having to testify caused "a total deterioration in [G.W.'s] psychological status".

[80] At trial, the Crown sought to rely on the 911 call, and on the videotaped statement she gave to the police. During the videotaped interview, injuries on G.W.'s nose, inner thigh, wrist, and ear were noted. The purpose of the evidence was to establish the time of the offence, that her home was broken into, that she was sexually assaulted, and that her wallet was stolen.

[81] Since she did not see the assailant's face, her statements were not relevant to identification. To establish identity, the Crown was relying on the match between Mr. Nicholas's DNA and the DNA obtained from G.W.'s vaginal swab.

[82] At trial, the Crown argued that both the 911 call and the videotaped police statement were admissible under the principled exception to the hearsay rule. The Crown's alternative argument was that the 911 call was admissible under the res gestae exception.

[83] In order to satisfy the necessity and reliability requirements, the Crown called Dr. P. Voorneveld, G.W.'s psychologist, who was qualified as an expert in post-traumatic stress disorder and who testified that it was "impossible" for G.W. to testify due to her severe anxiety. To establish necessity, Dr. Voorneveld testified that there was a risk that G.W. would commit suicide if forced to testify and that aids such as a closed courtroom or screen would not help.

[84] As to reliability, Dr. Voorneveld testified that the fact G.W. was able to describe the incident during the call and videotaped statement indicated that although she was in shock, the shock did not have a negative impact on the accuracy of her statements.

[85] Defence counsel at trial argued that the criteria of necessity and reliability were not met. Moreover, he asserted that since consent and identification were live issues, cross-examination was important.

[86] The trial judge concluded that the videotaped statements were both reliable and necessary. Further, the 911 call could be categorized as res gestae. Ultimately, however, the trial judge exercised his residual discretion and excluded both statements on the basis that the accused's right to full answer and defence would be violated if he were denied the opportunity of cross-examining G.W based on the defence's submissions as to what the issues in the trial were:

It is my finding that both tapes are reliable within the meaning of the law. The videotape would be admissible under the rubric of the principled approach to hearsay, and the audiotape constitutes res gestae and is admissible for that reason.

These findings are not, however, the end of the inquiry. It is my overarching responsibility to ensure the fairness of the trial process for the accused. The right to full answer and defence, particularly the right to face and cross-examine one's accuser, ought not to be lightly diminished. It is quite difficult for me to imagine the utility of what cross-examination may be undertaken given the facts of this. But this is entirely within the province of defence counsel and is not for me to say. My sympathy for the victim can be no justification for denying this accused due process [emphasis added].

For these considerations, I must find that these tapes may not be admitted as a substitute for viva voce evidence from [G.W.]. While the tapes may be necessary in the legal sense and may well be reliable, I find those factors insufficient to override the rights of the accused to make full answer and defence.

[87] On appeal, the Crown argued that the trial judge erred in exercising his residual discretion to exclude the statements. Defence counsel argued that the trial judge was correct in excluding the statements, or, in the alternative, that the trial judge erred in finding the statements to be necessary and reliable, and in characterizing the 911 call as res gestae.

Analysis

[88] In R. v. Dakin (1995), 80 O.A.C. 253 (Ont. C.A.) the accused was charged with the murder of two women who died as a result of burns. The Crown sought to introduce statements made by one of the women an hour after the fire. The Court of Appeal upheld the trial judge's decision to admit the statement under the spontaneous statements exception referred to in R. v. Clark (1983), 7 C.C.C. (3d) 46 (Ont. C.A.), citing R. v. Khan (1988), 42 C.C.C. (3d) 197 (Ont. C.A.) at 207:

[A] spontaneous statement made under the stress or pressure of a dramatic or startling act or event and relating to such an occasion may be admissible as an exception to the hearsay rule. The stress or pressure of the act or event must be such that the possibility of concoction or deception can be safely discounted. The statement need not be made strictly contem-poraneous to the occurrence so long as the stress or pressure created by it is ongoing and the statement is made before there has been time to contrive or misrepresent. The admissibility of such statements is dependent on the possibility of concoction or fabrication. Where the spontaneity of the statement is clear and the danger of fabrication is remote, the evidence should be received.

[89] In my view, the trial judge did not err in classifying the 911 call as falling within the res gestae exception. The call was made within ten minutes of the attack and there has been no suggestion that G.W. had any motive for misrepresenting what happened that night.

[90] Nor do I see any error in the trial judge's finding that the statements she made in the 911 call or in her videotaped statement were necessary and reliable.

[91] With respect to reliability, the 911 call was made approximately ten minutes after the attack, indicating that there was little time to fabricate. It was audiotaped and the police statement was videotaped. The police statement, although not under oath, was not elicited by leading questions. The location of the statement, taken at the police station and made to a person in authority is also indicative of reliability (see R. v. Fleet (2001), 48 C.R. (5th) 28 (N.S. C.A.)). The trial judge found that G.W.'s demeanour was credible. Perhaps most significantly, he found an absence of any motive to fabricate and that G.W.'s statement was not consistent with other hypotheses. (See Justice David Watt, Watt's Manual of Criminal Evidence, (Toronto: Carswell, 2002) at pp. 368-372)).

[92] The trial judge was satisfied, based on the evidence of Dr. Voorneveld, that the complainant was unable to attend court. Her out of court statements were necessary, not for identity, but to establish that there was a break in by an unknown assailant, that there was an assault, and the time of the assault.

[93] In R. v. Dubois (1997), 118 C.C.C. (3d) 544 (Que. C.A.) a child's out of court statements were found to be necessary when a psychologist testified that the trauma of testifying would aggravate the boy's trauma and impede his development. (See also R. v. R. (R.) (2001), 159 C.C.C. (3d) 11 (Ont. C.A.) aff'd [2003] S.C.J. No. 3 (S.C.C.)

[94] Although it is rare for a complainant to be found unable to testify, where the hearsay statements were found to be reliable and where the evidence of the psychologist that the complainant could not testify was uncontradicted, the trial judge was entitled to conclude that the necessity and reliability tests have been met.

[95] Notwithstanding his conclusion that the necessity and reliability requirements were met, the trial judge decided to exclude the evidence nonetheless based on its detrimental impact on the defence's ability to make full answer and defence. This conclusion was, with respect, based on an erroneous principle, namely, that he was obliged to defer to defence counsel assertions about the utility of cross-examining G.W. in spite of his own serious misgivings about its value.

[96] In R. v. Smith, [1992] 2 S.C.R. 915 at p. 934, the Supreme Court concluded that "where criteria of necessity and reliability are satisfied, the lack of testing by cross-examination goes to weight, not admissibility, and a properly cautioned jury should be able to evaluate the evidence on that basis".

[97] As the Crown indicated, the statements are relevant only to the following issues: the fact that the assault occurred, that it occurred to G.W., and when it occurred. Defence counsel, however, relied on the need to cross-examine G.W. based on the issues of identification and consent.

[98] As previously noted, it is clear from both statements that G.W. was unable to describe her attacker since he was holding a pillow over her face. Her statements, therefore, were of no relevance to identity. What the Crown relied on exclusively to establish identity was the match between Mr. Nicholas' DNA and the DNA from G.W.'s vaginal swab.

[99] Moreover, the issue of consent, or honest but mistaken belief in consent, cannot arise on these facts, where the victim had a pillow over her face during the entire assault, facts which are clearly inconsistent with consent or mistaken belief in consent, or with her ability to identify the accused. The inability to cross-examine, therefore, had little prejudicial impact in these circumstances.

[100] The trial judge did not find any actual prejudice to the accused or to the trial process. As the trial judge himself stated, "[I]t is quite difficult for me to imagine the utility of what cross-examination may be undertaken given the facts of this case."

[101] In the absence of any evidence indicating how the admission of the statements would actually prejudice the accused and the trial process, having found them to be necessary and reliable, there was no basis for excluding them.

CONCLUSION

[102] Accordingly, Mr. Nicholas's appeal from conviction is dismissed. I would allow both Crown appeals, set aside the acquittals, and direct new trials.
