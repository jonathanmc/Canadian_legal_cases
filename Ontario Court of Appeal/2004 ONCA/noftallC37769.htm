A. Introduction

[1] In early 1997 Cindy Greaves, then seventeen years old, left the Children's Aid Society residence where she had been staying and went to live in a home occupied by her friend Lanelle Chiasson, Lanelle's three siblings and mother, Elizabeth Chiasson, and Elizabeth Chiasson's boyfriend, the appellant, Robert Noftall. Ms. Greaves alleged that over the course of three months the appellant sexually assaulted her numerous times. These assaults included two incidents of forced sexual intercourse. After the second incident Ms. Greaves left the Chiasson home. Soon afterwards she recounted what had occurred in a videotaped statement to the police.

[2] At the appellant's trial before O'Driscoll J. and a jury in the fall of 2001, Ms. Greaves' testimony in chief consisted almost entirely of her videotaped statement, which she adopted and was admitted in evidence under s. 715.1 of the Criminal Code. The trial judge then gave the jury a copy of the videotape for its use during its deliberations.

[3] The jury convicted the appellant of sexual assault. The trial judge sentenced him to eight years in a penitentiary. The appellant appeals both his conviction and his sentence. The main issue on the conviction appeal is whether giving the jury Ms. Greaves' videotaped statement deprived the appellant of a fair trial.

B. Background

(a) Factual overview

[4] The complainant, Cindy Greaves, had been a depressed, vulnerable and needy teenager. Her relationships with her own family had been fraught with difficulty. At fifteen she became a ward of the Children's Aid Society, living in one of its homes. But she was unhappy there. She wanted to move in with a member of her family. Unfortunately, no one in her family wanted her.

[5] The complainant met Lanelle Chiasson at school and they became friends. At the end of January 1997, without the permission of the Children's Aid Society, Ms. Greaves accepted an invitation to live in the Chiasson home. She was looking for the comfort and security of a happy family environment. The Chiasson home, however, was anything but happy.

[6] The appellant had recently moved in. Although thirty-four years old, he had very little parenting experience. He had last seen his own three children in 1984 when he separated from his wife. He had spent half his adult life in jail.

[7] The appellant tried to be a father figure in the Chiasson household by being a strict disciplinarian. This created tension. His more lenient treatment of the complainant, whom he perceived to be his "little girl", only added to the tension. So too did the appellant's frequent use of drugs, his admitted violent temper, and his controlling manner in the home. The complainant, too, was unstable, prone to sudden fits of anger and to bouts of depression.

[8] In the midst of this unhappy home life, the complainant alleged that the appellant engaged in an escalating course of sexual abuse, culminating in the two incidents of sexual intercourse, which took place in the van the appellant used for his moving business and which prompted the complainant to leave the Chiasson home at the beginning of May 1997.

(b) Positions at trial

[9] At trial, the Crown's case rested primarily on two planks: Ms. Greaves' videotaped statement to the police given on June 19, 1997, and admitted under s. 715.1 of the Code; and Elizabeth Chiasson's testimony that, while having sex with her shortly after Ms. Greaves had left the household, the appellant cried out the name "Cindy".

[10] The appellant testified and denied that he had sexually assaulted the complainant. He acknowledged his lengthy criminal record but pointed out that he had never been convicted of a sexual offence and claimed that sex offenders disgusted him.

[11] The appellant admitted that he had paid special attention to Ms. Greaves, but he said that he had done so because she was needier than the other children in the home. He had tried to console her when she became upset, and had gone to her room at night when she cried. The defence's theory was that the complainant had fabricated her allegations to regain benefits from the Children's Aid Society that she might otherwise have lost because she had left the Society's care without permission. She was approaching her eighteenth birthday and unless she returned to the Society before she turned eighteen it would not take her back.

(c) The appellant's submissions on appeal

[12] The appellant advances three submissions on his appeal against conviction:

(1) The trial judge erred in giving Ms. Greaves' videotaped statement to the jury for its use during its deliberations; alternatively, he erred in not also giving the jury the transcript of the cross-examination of Ms. Greaves on her statement.

(2) The trial judge erred in permitting Elizabeth Chiasson to testify about the appellant's inculpatory utterances while they had sex; alternatively, he failed to properly instruct the jury on its use of these utterances.

(3) The trial judge erred in his instruction to the jury on reasonable doubt.

We called on the Crown to respond only to the first submission.

[13] On his sentence appeal the appellant submits that eight years is outside the recognized range for his offence and is therefore unfit.

C. The conviction appeal

Appellant's first submission: The trial judge erred in giving Ms. Greaves' videotaped statement to the jury for its use during its deliberations.

(a) Introduction

[14] This submission has three branches. First, the appellant argues that the trial judge had no discretion to let the videotape go to the jury room. Second, he argues that if the trial judge had a discretion, he exercised it improperly by giving the jury the videotape. Third, he argues in the alternative that the trial judge erred by not also giving the jury the transcript of Ms. Greaves' cross-examination.

[15] As the appellant acknowledges, his first argument is foreclosed by the decision of this court in R. v. Toten (1993), 14 O.R. (3d) 225 at 268. There, Doherty J.A. writing for a five member panel held that a trial judge had a discretion to permit the jury during its deliberations to view a videotaped statement taken under s. 715.1 of the Code. He justified this discretion by observing that a videotape is not "so different from other forms of testimonial evidence which routinely go into the jury room as exhibits". In exercising this discretion, in Doherty J.A.'s words "the trial judge must decide how to best facilitate the jury's deliberations while maintaining the fairness of the process." I know of no case law or other developments since Toten that either casts doubt on its authority or suggests its reconsideration.

[16] Thus, the gravamen of the appellant's submission on the videotaped statement centres on his contention that the trial judge exercised his discretion improperly either by giving the jury the videotape at all, or by giving the jury the videotape but not the cross-examination on it.

[17] The potential unfairness of giving the jury only the videotape - especially in a case where it comprised virtually all of the complainant's evidence in chief - arises from the possibility that a jury would give it far more emphasis than other equally relevant evidence. Morden J.A. discussed this risk in R. v. Pleich (1980), 55 C.C.C. (2d) 13 (Ont. C.A.). In that case the trial judge had permitted tapes of intercepted telephone conversations to go to the jury room. Although Morden J.A. concluded that the trial judge did not err in doing so, he pointed out at 32 that the practice of permitting the jury to use a tape carried "the risk that such evidence will thereby receive such an undue emphasis compared to the oral evidence which the jury has heard."

[18] Applied to this case, Pleich and Toten stand for the proposition that the trial judge's exercise of discretion is reviewable if there is a reasonable likelihood the jury gave undue emphasis to Ms. Greaves' videotaped statement and in doing so deprived the appellant of a fair trial.

[19] To test this proposition in this case I will discuss the contents of the statement, the trial judge's ruling and whether that ruling amounted to a reversible error.

(b) Ms. Greaves' statement

[20] Section 715.1 of the Criminal Code sets out the conditions for admissibility of Ms. Greaves' videotaped statement:

In any proceeding relating to an offence under section 151, 152, 153, 155 or 159, subsection 160(2) or (3), or section 163.1, 170, 171, 172, 173, 210, 211, 212, 213, 266, 267, 268, 271, 272, or 273, in which the complainant or other witness was under the age of eighteen years at the time the offence is alleged to have been committed, a videotape made within a reasonable time after the alleged offence, in which the complainant or witness describes the acts complained of, is admissible in evidence if the complainant or witness, while testifying, adopts the contents of the videotape.

These conditions were met. Sexual assault is a listed offence (under s. 271 of the Code). Ms. Greaves was seventeen at the time of the alleged offence. She gave the statement on June 19, 1997, about a month and a half after she had left the Chiasson household. And she adopted the statement at trial. Indeed, save for one additional allegation, which I will refer to, her statement amounted to her entire evidence in chief. Once admitted under s. 715.1 her statement became testimonial evidence as if she had testified in open court.

[21] In her statement, Ms. Greaves described in great detail the appellant's sexually abusive conduct. It began with rude and inappropriate comments. The appellant told Ms. Greaves she had "a cute little sweet butt" and "sexy legs". He spoke vulgarly to her about his sexual proclivities with remarks such as "If I can't eat it I won't fuck it." When they hugged he caressed her back "like you would hug your lover". When they parted his penis was erect.

[22] Once the family moved, Ms. Greaves had her own bedroom and the appellant's sexual abuse became more serious. The appellant began coming to her room at different times during the night to "cuddle" with her. He got under the covers wearing just his underwear and sometimes his shirt. Ms. Greaves complained that she did not like some of the ways he touched her. He replied that he was just showing how much he cared and that there was "no difference between your arm, your hand, your breast and your behind".

[23] Later, while in the complainant's bed, the appellant started "pumping" against her, "pressing himself in and out towards [her]". He also "went down" on her at least twice, fingered her, digitally penetrated her, touched her breast, lay on top of her, rubbed her vaginal area and performed oral sex on her.

[24] The complainant said that the appellant had enlisted her help in his moving business. She accompanied him in his van to various moving jobs. At first she enjoyed the work. But that changed.

[25] Often other workers were in the front seat and for lack of room she was obliged to sit on the appellant's lap. While there, "he'd get his crotch right up against my butt". She described the appellant digitally penetrating her under a coat while two workers were seated beside her in the van. She claimed that the appellant said, "Oh God you made me lube. I had lube coming out of me like anything." He exposed his erection telling her, "You know, you give me an instant hard on."

[26] When just the complainant was in the van the appellant would park and ask her questions such as, "Can I come inside you?…[O]h please, just for a minute and I'll take it back out."

[27] The two most serious sexual assaults - forced intercourse - took place in the van after the appellant had dropped off his co-workers and was alone with the complainant. The first occurred at the end of April 1997. While they were in the back of the van the appellant had sexual intercourse with Ms. Greaves. She protested, telling him "no, no, no" throughout.

[28] The second incident occurred a few days later. The appellant twice had vaginal intercourse with Ms. Greaves without her consent. During her evidence in chief at trial the complainant added the allegation that during one of the incidents of sexual intercourse the appellant had also performed oral sex on her.

[29] Ms. Greaves said that the morning after the second incident of intercourse the appellant came into her bedroom and asked her whether having made love meant anything to her. He wanted to touch her and wanted her to roll on top of him. By then she had had enough. She decided to leave the household and to try to get her life back on track.

(c) Did the trial judge's ruling amount to a reversible error in the exercise of his discretion?

[30] After the complainant's videotaped statement was admitted the trial judge told the jury that it would be given a copy during its deliberations. The defence immediately objected on two bases: the jury would have the complainant's evidence in chief but not the cross-examination; and the jury would have that evidence in a most compelling form. The trial judge declined to rule at that time.

[31] After all the trial evidence was in, the defence renewed its objection, adding the submission that because the trial had been short, the jury did not need the videotape to remember the complainant's evidence. Again, the trial judge did not rule on the objection.

[32] The following day, for the first time, the Crown joined defence counsel in submitting that neither the videotaped statement nor a transcript of it should go to the jury room "primarily because the videotape makes up the entire examination in chief".

[33] Despite the Crown's concession, the trial judge exercised his discretion by giving the jury the videotaped statement for its use during its deliberations. In his view the rationale underlying s. 715.1 of the Code would be undermined if the jury did not have the videotape. He held:

When one considers the reasons for and the purposes of s. 715.1 of the Criminal Code, the videotaped interviews of complainants, I would think that Parliament anticipated that, in most cases, the evidence of a person under eighteen years of age, the complainant, would go before the trier of fact as "the evidence" in chief of the complainant, because the purpose of this is to capture the best recollections of the complainant at the time closest to the alleged offence. That purpose would be negated if the trier of fact is allowed only a one-time viewing in court and later viewing only by request in returning to the courtroom; see R. v. F. (C.C.) (1997), 220 N.R. 362 (S.C.C.).

In this case, the trial is four years and five months after the date of the alleged offence. It seems to me that the time factor alone is good reason why the jury should be entitled to what Parliament feels are the best recollections of the complainant at the time of the alleged offences. Moreover, in this case I am influenced by the fact of the nature of the defence, that is that the matters alleged never happened, it is all made up of whole cloth. In my view, the jury's task will be made easier if they are allowed to take Exhibit A to the jury room for private hearing and viewing. In my view, any prejudice to the accused is negligible.

[34] The trial judge's ruling raises two concerns. The first concern is that he overrode the joint position of Crown and defence - and a position taken by the Crown after careful consideration. The trial judge of course was not bound by counsels' position, but it seems to me that he lacked a compelling reason for departing from it.

[35] The second concern is that his reasoning for giving the jury the videotape was erroneous. His reasoning explains why the court had discretion to admit the videotaped statement, not why it should go to the jury room. Worse, his reasoning indicates that during their deliberations the jurors needed more access to the videotape than to other pieces of evidence, thus suggesting that the videotape had special status. In his words, the purpose of the videotaped statement "would be negated if the trier of fact is allowed only a one-time viewing in court and later viewing only by request". Yet the risk the jury would give the videotape special status - "undue emphasis" - is precisely the risk Pleich cautions against.

[36] Despite these concerns about the trial judge's reasoning, the issue remains whether giving the jury the videotape deprived the appellant of a fair trial. The appellant says that it did. He asserts that the jury likely gave the videotape undue emphasis, and that it either should not have been given the tape or it should have been given both the tape and the transcript of Ms. Greaves' cross-examination. The appellant contends that the Supreme Court of Canada's decision in R. v. Olbey, [1980] 1 S.C.R. 1008 requires that if the jury is given one it has to be given the other.

[37] I do not accept the appellant's position. I do not consider it reasonably likely the jury gave the videotape undue emphasis. And because I do not think that the complainant's cross-examination undermines her evidence on the videotape, it was not necessary to give the jury the transcript of the cross-examination along with the videotape.

[38] I agree with Ms. Klukach for the Crown that there would be a risk the jury gave the videotape undue emphasis if cogent evidence had been pitted against the videotaped statement, and the jury did not have equal access to this evidence. However, there was no such evidence here. The cross-examination of the complainant did not weaken or qualify the substance of Ms. Greaves' detailed allegations. Her account of the sexual assaults was virtually unchallenged. Also, the appellant did not offer a contradictory version of the incidents in question. Instead, his defence was an outright denial of any sexual contact with the complainant.

[39] Moreover, in his charge, the trial judge cautioned the jurors that the videotaped statement was not special and that it ought not to unduly influence their deliberations. He told the jury that it was not required to view the videotape unless it wanted to. He also highlighted the parts of the complainant's cross-examination that were helpful to the defence: her evidence that she lied continuously to the Children's Aid Society and that she had threatened to falsely accuse a group home worker.

[40] Finally, the principle in Olbey does not assist the appellant. In Olbey, an Ontario case, McIntyre J. accepted that when a jury asks to re-hear part of the evidence of a witness it must also be given any qualifying evidence. McIntyre J. expressly agreed with the following passage from the reasons of Martin J.A. at 1027-1028:

I accept, of course, the proposition that where the jury requests that the evidence of a witness be read back, it is incumbent on the trial Judge not to allow the jury to hear a part only of the evidence of the witness without also hearing those portions of the evidence of the witness, whether given in-chief or in cross-examination, which weaken or qualify the part read, even though the jury indicates that it does not wish to hear any more of the witness's evidence. Otherwise, the jury is not hearing the evidence of the witness, but only an incomplete, or even an inaccurate account of his or her testimony.

[41] Olbey deals with the situation where the jury asks to have some of the witness's evidence read back. The very request suggests the jury might give the evidence undue emphasis and ignore or give less weight to other contrary or qualifying evidence. To guard against that risk Olbey requires that the jury be given the witness's complete account.

[42] Olbey does not directly apply here because the jury did not request a copy of the videotaped statement for its deliberations. Nonetheless, had I concluded that there was a reasonable likelihood the jury would have given the statement undue emphasis or that the complainant's cross-examination qualified her evidence in chief, then I would have held, consistent with Olbey, that the transcript of her cross-examination should also have gone to the jury room. But because the risks that concerned the court in Olbey, Pleich and Toten are not significant here, the trial judge's ruling did not deprive the appellant of a fair trial. I would therefore not give effect to this ground of appeal.

[43] I will now deal briefly with the appellant's other two submissions on his conviction appeal.

Appellant's second submission: the trial judge erred in permitting Elizabeth Chiasson to testify about the appellant's inculpatory utterances.

[44] The background of this submission is as follows. Over the defence's objection the trial judge allowed Elizabeth Chiasson to testify about an incident that allegedly occurred about three weeks after Ms. Greaves left the household. The appellant and Ms. Chiasson were in bed. He was high on crack cocaine; she was asleep face down. He began having anal sex with her and wouldn't stop though she protested that it was painful. Then, as if "in a daze", he said things like, "Oh Cindy, you're so sweet", "Cindy, my girl", "Cindy I love you". Ms. Chiasson testified that the same thing happened a few more times and each time the appellant's words were "exactly the same".

[45] The appellant's position at trial, repeated on appeal, was that this evidence was highly prejudicial and should not have been admitted. The trial judge held that the probative value of Ms. Chiasson's evidence exceeded its potential prejudicial effect. We agree.

[46] The context in which the Crown sought to elicit the evidence was important. As the trial judge recognized, the evidence would have had much less probative value if the issue in this case was consent. But the issue was not whether Ms. Greaves consented to sexual activity with the appellant, but whether sexual activity occurred at all. In this context, the jury could conclude that the appellant's utterances were tantamount to an admission of guilt. At a minimum, the jury could infer from this evidence that the appellant's claim that he had only a paternal interest in the complainant was false.

[47] Balanced against its probative value, the risk that the jury would misuse the evidence by engaging in prohibited propensity reasoning was small. In his recharge - just before the jury began its deliberations - the trial judge forcefully instructed the jury not to use Ms. Chiasson's evidence to infer that the appellant was "the type" of person to commit the offence charged. The defence did not object to this recharge. Moreover, the appellant had already acknowledged that a good deal of his conduct - drug use, a criminal lifestyle, failings as a parent - had been discreditable.

[48] We therefore find no reviewable error in the trial judge's decision to admit the evidence or in his instruction to the jury on its use.

Appellant's third submission: the trial judge erred in his instruction to the jury on reasonable doubt.

[49] This submission springs from the appellant's contention that in his discussion of circumstantial evidence the trial judge impermissibly lowered the burden of proof by telling the jury:

The law does not demand that you act upon certainties alone. In our daily activities we act upon reasonable grounds. So too, in court, a fact may be proved by circumstantial evidence.

[50] There are two answers to the appellant's contention. First, the passage the appellant complains of is a small part of a much longer instruction in which the trial judge was trying to explain to the jury that circumstantial evidence could be as cogent as direct evidence and that the jury was entitled to establish facts based on reasonable inferences from the evidence. The trial judge's discussion of circumstantial evidence, therefore, did not undermine or even qualify his separate instruction on the meaning of reasonable doubt.

[51] The second answer to the appellant's submission is that the trial judge gave a reasonable doubt instruction almost identical to the instruction approved of by the Supreme Court of Canada in R. v. Lifchus, [1997] 3 S.C.R. 320. The hurdle for appellate intervention on a reasonable doubt instruction is whether there is any reasonable likelihood the jury misapprehended or misapplied the burden or standard of proof. The appellant did not meet this hurdle. His submission on this point therefore fails.

[52] I would dismiss the conviction appeal.

D. The sentence appeal

[53] The appellant submits that his eight-year sentence is outside the range recognized by this court. As no facts take it outside this range, his sentence is unfit. I agree with this submission.

[54] The appellant undoubtedly committed a serious offence. He occupied a position of trust towards a vulnerable victim. His sexual abuse amounted to a breach of that trust. His sexual assaults included two incidents of sexual intercourse. A significant penitentiary sentence was warranted.

[55] In R. v. B.(J.) (1990), 36 O.A.C. 307 this court confirmed that in cases of sexual abuse of a young person, where the abuse includes sexual intercourse and a convicted person occupies a position of trust toward the victim, the usual range of sentence is three to five years. Aggravating or mitigating factors may put the sentence at the higher or lower end of that range. Thus, Blair J.A. wrote at para. 5:

The decisions of provincial appellate courts establish that, except in unusual circumstances, a penitentiary sentence is called for in all cases of sexual abuse of children to whom the convicted person stands in loco parentis if the abuse involves sexual intercourse. Such sentences reflect society's denunciation of this abhorrent conduct and the breach of trust reposed on parents or guardians of children. Both counsel agreed that the usual range of sentences for this type of offence is from three to five years. The length of sentence within the range of three to five years depends on a number of factors. These include the age of the victim, the duration and frequency of the sexual assaults, the criminal record of the offender, the effects on the victim and the presence or absence of collateral violence or remorse.

[56] Subsequent decisions of this court have affirmed this range. See R. v. M.(D.) (1999), 136 C.C.C. (3d) 412, R. v. D.A.H. (2003), 171 C.C.C. (3d) 309 and R. v. B.L. (2000), 138 O.A.C. 383.

[57] Here, two aggravating factors put the sentence at the high end of the range: the appellant had a lengthy criminal record; and the complainant suffered traumatic psychological scars, in part because of the sexual assaults and in part because the appellant terrorized the Chiasson household.

[58] Additional aggravating factors may entitle a court to impose a sentence exceeding the usual range. In R. v. B.(J.) itself this court upheld a sentence of eight years where the accused had sexually abused his stepdaughter for eight years, despite an intervening conviction for attempted rape, and where the acts of intercourse were accompanied by threats of punishment if the victim did not comply. In R. v. B.L. this court upheld a sentence of six and a half years where the incest lasted seven years and twice resulted in the victim becoming pregnant.

[59] Here, no such additional aggravating factors were present. The assaults lasted for three months; they were not accompanied by violence or threats of violence beyond the offence itself; the complainant did not suffer any physical injury; and the appellant's long criminal record contained no previous conviction for sexual offences.

[60] In my view, the trial judge erred in imposing a sentence beyond the usual range accepted by this court. I would grant leave to appeal sentence, allow the sentence appeal, and reduce the appellant's sentence to five years.

E. Conclusion

[61] I would dismiss the conviction appeal. I would allow the sentence appeal by reducing the appellant's sentence to five years in the penitentiary.
