HEARD: March 19, 2004

On appeal from conviction by Justice David J. Nadeau of the Superior Court of Justice dated March 28, 2003. The Crown appeals from sentence imposed by Justice David J. Nadeau on May 27, 2003.

ROSENBERG J.A.:

[1] On March 28, 2003 the appellant was convicted of sexual assault and sexual exploitation. On May 27, 2003, he was sentenced to ninety days imprisonment concurrent on each count, the sentences to be served intermittently, to be followed by twenty-four months probation. The appellant appeals against the conviction. The Crown applies for leave to appeal against the sentence and also seeks to have fresh evidence admitted. At the conclusion of the argument, we informed the parties that the appeal from conviction was dismissed and that we would provide reasons at a later date. We reserved our decision on the Crown sentence appeal. These are our reasons for dismissing the conviction appeal and for allowing the sentence appeal and increasing the sentence to twelve months imprisonment.

THE FACTS

[2] Although there is an appeal by both R.D. and the Crown, for convenience I will refer to R.D. as the appellant throughout these reasons. The complainant is the appellant's daughter. The allegations of sexual assault and sexual exploitation arise primarily out of massages that the appellant began to give his daughter when she was twelve or thirteen. The massages initially included her legs and buttocks. Eventually, during the massages, the appellant began to put his fingers beneath the complainant's underwear and touch her vagina. During one particular massage he touched her breasts.

[3] At some point, according to the complainant, the appellant began to massage the complainant while viewing a video showing "sensual" massages in which the participants were naked. The appellant began to massage the complainant when she was only wearing underwear and later when she wore only a towel placed over her buttocks. On some occasions, even the towel was removed. One time, the appellant attempted to massage her naked breasts but the complainant resisted and started to cry. The complainant's mother, who had been out, came into the bedroom and saw that her daughter was unclothed. By this time the complainant was seventeen years of age.

[4] According to the complainant, during the same period that the massages were taking place, the appellant was taking showers with her. The appellant was a custodian at the local public school and also taught karate after hours. When the karate sessions ended, the appellant would take a shower in the girls' shower room. The complainant was often at the school during these times because there she could access the Internet. On perhaps twenty occasions, the appellant persuaded the complainant to have showers with him. There were curtains separating the appellant from the complainant but he would come over to her side and wash her back and buttocks. On one occasion, the complainant had a vaginal infection and the appellant insisted on the complainant letting him examine her vaginal area. The appellant told the complainant not to tell her mother about the showers as she was closed minded and would not understand.

[5] There were two other incidents. When the complainant was seventeen years of age, the appellant took the complainant to a local hotel. They sat in the hotel whirlpool and the appellant gave the complainant five or six drinks of hard liquor. The appellant massaged different parts of her body and then put his hands underneath her bathing suit and inserted his thumbs in her vagina.

[6] On another occasion the appellant and the complainant went to Belleville to see the complainant's brother, who was attending a special school. The complainant and the appellant stayed in the same hotel room. The appellant again gave the complainant alcohol and she fell asleep. At some point, the complainant woke up and found the appellant rubbing her belly with his hands underneath her nightgown and caressing her breasts. He also put his hand beneath her underwear and touched her clitoris. She tried to pull away but could not. The appellant said there was nothing wrong with what he was doing. She fell asleep again but when she awoke, the appellant was in bed with the complainant with his arm on her belly, and her nightgown was hiked up to her armpits.

[7] The last incident was another shower when the complainant was eighteen years of age. After that, the complainant left for college. In 2002, the appellant and his wife separated. Some months later, the complainant went to the police.

[8] The appellant testified and confirmed much of the complainant's evidence. He admitted to performing massages on the complainant when she wore only a towel, but he claimed they were proper massages and not performed for a sexual purpose. He denied massaging the complainant while viewing the sensual massage video. He admitted taking at least one shower at the school at the same time as the complainant but he claimed that they were in separate stalls. He denied the sexual touching at the hotels.

THE CONVICTION APPEAL

[9] The appellant raises a number of grounds of appeal relating to the trial judge's reasons. In general, the appellant submits that the trial judge did not properly apply the standard of proof beyond a reasonable doubt to the questions of credibility, held the appellant's evidence to a much higher standard than that of the Crown witnesses and failed to come to grips fairly with the issues of credibility. I do not agree with these submissions. The trial judge gave lengthy and clear reasons and his approach to assessing the evidence was correct.

[10] The appellant's principal submission relates to the following passages of the reasons for judgment:

In many cases involving sexual offences there will be no physical evidence of the alleged offence. As mentioned, what is sought is supporting evidence or independent evidence making it more probable that the complainant's testimony is true.

Furthermore, in a case such as here, where the accused has chosen to testify, the evidence is to be properly assessed in light of the other evidence as supporting one version of the events over the other.

[11] The appellant submits that in these passages the trial judge has applied the civil standard of proof. The appellant misconstrues what the trial judge is doing in this part of his reasons. Both prior to and after the trial judge's comments, above, he referred to the criminal standard of proof and applied the principles in R. v. W. (D.) (1991), 63 C.C.C. (3d) 397 (S.C.C.). In the first paragraph of his reasons set out above, the trial judge addressed the question of confirmatory evidence. For evidence to be confirmatory, it need not be capable on its own of proving the case beyond a reasonable doubt but should be capable of "restoring the trier's faith in the witness' account". See R. v. Kehler, [2004] S.C.J. No. 1 at para. 12.

[12] The expression used by the trial judge in the passage quoted above is entirely consistent with this approach to confirmation. I would point out that this is not a case where corroboration or confirmation was required to restore faith in the complainant's testimony. All the trial judge was doing was looking for some independent evidence that would assist him in determining whether the complainant was telling the truth.

[13] In the second paragraph, the trial judge was doing nothing more than making the important point that the appellant's evidence had to be assessed having regard to all of the evidence, and not just in isolation. Again, this was the correct approach to determining the issues of credibility. The formula in W. (D.) does not mandate that the accused's evidence is to be viewed in isolation, divorced from the context or other evidence in the case.

[14] The appellant also submits that the trial judge erred in finding confirmation from evidence such as the appellant's wife's observation of the complainant when the appellant was giving his daughter a massage, the appellant's admission to giving the massages when his daughter was wearing only a towel and his admissions concerning the shower. The appellant submits that this evidence is neutral at best and shows no more than opportunity. I do not agree. This was highly unusual conduct by a parent. The evidence went far beyond mere opportunity. It confirmed important parts of the complainant's story as to the nature of the massages and other conduct. The evidence did not have to implicate the appellant on matters in dispute to be capable of confirming the complainant's version of events. See R. v. Kehler at para. 15.

[15] The appellant also submits that the trial judge focused solely on the reliability of the complainant's evidence when the real issue was her credibility in the sense of sincerity. However, the trial judge made it clear that he was concerned about both reliability and sincerity. Both aspects of credibility were in issue. While parts of the complainant's and appellant's stories were diametrically opposed, it was also part of the appellant's defence that this was simply innocent conduct on his part. The trial judge had to be sure that the complainant, when relating events that occurred several years before, had accurately recounted the incidents.

[16] Finally, the appellant submits that the trial judge applied a stricter test in analysing the evidence of the appellant as opposed to that of the complainant. However, Mr. Xynnis fairly concedes that there were no major inconsistencies in the evidence of the complainant. By contrast, the appellant's story of innocent touching was inherently improbable. I see nothing in the trial judge's reasons to support this submission.

[17] Finally, the appellant submits that the trial judge improperly relied on the appellant's demeanour when he referred to having observed the appellant's "lecherous nature". The trial judge had earlier cautioned against relying on demeanour to make credibility assessments. It is apparent that the reference to the appellant's lecherous nature related to the conduct as described by the complainant and even by the appellant. The impugned expression was no more than a somewhat inelegant way of finding that the Crown had proved that the touchings were for a sexual purpose.

[18] Accordingly, I would dismiss the appeal from conviction.

THE SENTENCE APPEAL

[19] Crown counsel makes two submissions with respect to sentence. First, Ms. Wassenaar submits that a sentence of ninety days intermittent is manifestly inadequate given the serious nature of the conduct and the gross breach of trust. Second, relying on the fresh evidence, she submits that the trial judge was misled as to the true state of the appellant's employment.

[20] I will deal first with the adequacy of the sentence. I agree with the Crown that this sentence is manifestly inadequate. I have described the conduct above. This was a prolonged course of conduct involving the appellant's own daughter. It represented a serious breach of trust. In addition, the appellant used alcohol on two occasions to put the complainant in an even more vulnerable position. The offences have had a serious impact on the complainant. The range of sentence suggested by Crown counsel at trial of fifteen months to two years less one day was the appropriate range given the seriousness of the conduct, even taking into account the appellant's lack of criminal record and good work record.

[21] The issue to which the fresh evidence is directed is as follows. The appellant was the head custodian at the local public school. After the appellant was convicted on March 28, 2003, the school board met and decided to terminate his employment. The appellant was notified of this decision by a letter dated May 1, 2003 and sent by registered mail. The appellant admits receiving this letter before the sentencing hearing on May 27, 2003. Nevertheless, the appellant permitted his counsel to make submissions to the court to the effect that he was merely suspended without pay and that a final decision had not yet been made. There is no suggestion that the appellant's trial counsel (not Mr. Xynnis) was aware of the May 1st letter or the true state of affairs.

[22] The trial judge held that this was not a proper case for a conditional sentence. He was, however, very concerned about the impact of a jail sentence on the appellant's employment. In his reasons, the trial judge placed great emphasis on the fact that the appellant would lose his job if given a period of imprisonment. The only reasonable inference to draw from the proceedings is that the trial judge settled on the maximum intermittent sentence because this was the only way that the appellant could keep his employment.

[23] As I have said, the fresh evidence shows that the appellant no longer had employment with the school board when he appeared before the trial judge for sentencing. In an affidavit filed in response to the Crown's fresh evidence application, the appellant explains that he did not consider that the Board's decision was final because he had asked the union to file a grievance on his behalf. His view was that until the grievance was dealt with it was still possible he could maintain his employment. He also says that even after the charge was laid he was permitted to work, albeit at a time that would not bring him in contact with students.

[24] I have some doubt about the truth of the appellant's explanation but I am prepared to proceed on the basis that the appellant did not deliberately set out to mislead the trial judge. The fact remains that the trial judge was misled and that this misinformation contributed to the imposition of a wholly inadequate sentence.

[25] The Crown has met the test for admission of fresh evidence on a sentence appeal. See R. v. Levesque, [2000] 2 S.C.R. 487. The appellant concedes that the Crown's fresh evidence should be admitted and likewise the Crown agrees to the admission of the appellant's responding affidavit.

[26] The question of an appropriate sentence is a difficult one. The appellant completed serving his intermittent sentence in December of last year. While the court is always very concerned about returning an offender to prison in such circumstances, that factor takes on less importance where, as here, the appellant has contributed to the situation that led to the improper sentence. However, the additional hardship of returning the appellant to custody must be given some consideration and warrants imposition of a lesser sentence than might otherwise be imposed.

[27] Mr. Xynnis forcefully argued on behalf of the appellant that if this court was of the view that the 90-day sentence was inadequate, the court should impose a conditional sentence. In my view, this is not a case for a conditional sentence. No one factor is dispositive of this issue. It is the combination of factors that, in my view, make a conditional sentence inappropriate, including the serious breach of trust, the length of time over which the offences occurred, the number of incidents, the seriousness of the conduct and the use of alcohol to increase the vulnerability of the complainant. I am not satisfied that a conditional sentence would meet the objectives of denunciation and deterrence. I agree with the trial judge that this was also a case where specific deterrence was an important factor. The fact that the appellant did not seem to recognize the inappropriateness of his conduct, even on his own version of events, is of real concern and indicates that specific deterrence was an appropriate objective.

[28] Accordingly, I would grant leave to appeal the sentence, allow the appeal and increase the sentence to twelve months imprisonment. Having regard to the fact that the appellant was on probation during the time when he was serving the intermittent sentence I would reduce the term of probation to twelve months on the same terms as imposed by the trial judge.
