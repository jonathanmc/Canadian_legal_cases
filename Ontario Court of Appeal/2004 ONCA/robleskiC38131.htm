On appeal from the surrender order of the Honourable Martin Cauchon, Minister of Justice for Canada, dated April 12, 2002 and on application for judicial review of the committal order for extradition of Justice Arthur C. Whealy of the Superior Court of Justice dated November 14, 2001.

BY THE COURT:

A. Introduction

[1] The United States of America seeks to extradite Daniel Robleski for six counts of incest alleged to have been committed on his daughter in the State of Missouri between April 1, 1998 and May 29, 1998. After a hearing, Whealy J. committed Mr. Robleski for extradition on six counts of the Canadian offence of sexual assault. The Minister of Justice then ordered his surrender on the six incest counts. Mr. Robleski appealed the committal order and sought judicial review of the surrender order. After hearing from his counsel we dismissed both his appeal and his application for judicial review, with reasons to follow. These are our reasons.

B. Background Facts

[2] The record before the extradition judge disclosed the following salient facts.

[3] The complainant had lived away from her father from the age of three. However, in early 1998 she moved into his home, an apartment in St. Charles, Missouri. She was eighteen years old at the time, but went to a learning disabled school and had a maturity level much lower than eighteen years.

[4] Soon after she moved in, the appellant began to sleep in her bed. About a month later he began licking her vagina and buttocks, inserting his finger into them and touching her breasts. He also had her stroke his penis, until he would "cum". The appellant continued this inappropriate sexual touching almost nightly for about a month and a half ending on May 29, 1998. Two days later the complainant got in touch with the police.

[5] She explained that the appellant would get "pissed off" and yell at her if she did not do what he wanted her to do. She complied with his requests because she did not want her father to be angry with her. She and her brothers had just gotten back together and she was afraid of losing this family bond. Several times she faked being asleep to avoid having anything to do with the appellant. At times the appellant asked her to tell him "I love it when you lick my pussy daddy" and she did so because "he scared me".

[6] After the appellant was arrested he admitted that he and his daughter slept together, that he had licked her vagina five or six times and that he had touched her vagina or her anus many times. He also acknowledged that his daughter had stroked his penis at least three times and he confirmed her evidence that the sexual touching had taken place for about a month and a half.

C. The Appeal Against the Committal Order

[7] The appellant raised two grounds of appeal against the committal order.

1.) The respondent failed to establish a prima facie case of lack of consent; and

2.) The respondent failed to establish a prima facie case of six counts of sexual assault.

1. Consent

[8] The appellant contended that there was no direct evidence of a lack of consent because in her interview with the police the complainant was not asked whether she consented to the appellant's sexual touching.

[9] In our view, the evidence in the record amply demonstrated that the complainant did not consent to the appellant's sexual activity. This evidence included the following:

  • The appellant initiated sexual activity with his dependent, vulnerable and immature eighteen year old daughter. Such conduct is inconsistent with consensual sex. See R. v. M.(M.L.) (1992), 78 C.C.C. (3d) 318 (N.S.C.A.).
  • The complainant did not resist the appellant's sexual advances because if she did not comply with his demands he would get "pissed off" and yell at her. As the extradition judge aptly observed:

    think there is some evidence that she did not consent in the sense that we use the word, she was not a willing participant, and if she submitted out of fear that is not consent according to our law.

2. Prima Facie Case on Six Counts versus One Count

[10] The appellant submitted that he ought to have been committed on one count of sexual assault, not six. He argued that, even assuming the respondent made out a prima facie case of lack of consent within the specified time period, it proved only one "global count" of sexual assault.

[11] We take a different view. The extradition judge fulfilled his statutory jurisdiction under s. 29 of the Extradition Act by holding a hearing to determine whether the evidence supported a committal on the Canadian offence described in the authority to proceed. He properly amended the authority to proceed under s. 23 of the Act and committed the appellant for his conduct during the agreed upon period. In so doing he was entitled to take into account that the State of Missouri sought the appellant's extradition on six counts, and correspondingly, to treat the relevant "conduct" as amounting to six counts of sexual assault.

[12] For these brief reasons the appeal is dismissed.

D. The Application for Judicial Review of the Surrender Order

[13] The applicant applied for judicial review of the Minister's surrender order on three grounds:

1.) Surrendering him would be unfair and contrary to s. 7 of the Charter;

2.) Alternatively, if he is surrendered on six counts of incest, the Minister should require as a condition of surrender that the applicant face concurrent, not consecutive, sentences; and

3.) The Minister should require as a condition of surrender that the applicant be given credit for his pre-trial custody in Canada.

1.) Fairness

[14] The appellant submitted that the Minister ought not to have ordered his surrender because of the combination of two considerations: the case against him on the issue of consent was weak; and under Missouri law the offence of incest, even without intercourse, does not require proof of lack of consent. Accordingly, a weak case on the issue of consent in Canada will never be assessed in the United States. Therefore, the applicant submitted that surrendering him undermines the principle of reciprocity. We saw no merit in this submission.

[15] First, for the reasons we have already outlined we do not view the case against the appellant as weak, even under Canadian law. The extradition judge found that the prosecution had made out a prima facie case, and as we have already said, the record amply supports that finding.

[16] Second, and more important, once a prima facie case has been established, unless surrendering the applicant would be "simply unacceptable" or "shock the conscience of our society" the Minister may not refuse to do so. The applicant could not meet this stringent threshold.

2.) Consecutive versus Concurrent Sentences

[17] The applicant argued that because he ought only to have been committed on one global count of sexual assault the Minister should not have surrendered him to face six separate counts of incest and the possibility of six consecutive sentences. We do not agree with this argument.

[18] The Minister surrendered the applicant on the conduct underlying the foreign offence, consistent with s. 58(b) of the Extradition Act. Moreover, the Minister held that he could find no compelling grounds under the Act, the Treaty or the Charter justifying making the applicant's surrender conditional on his receiving concurrent time if convicted. We are not persuaded that in so holding the Minister erred.

3.) Pre-trial Custody

[19] The applicant spent about two and a half months in a Canadian jail. He submitted that Missouri will not give him credit for pre-trial custody unless he waived his extradition. The applicant contended that this amounted to an abuse of process. The Minister rejected this argument. We see no reviewable error in his decision.

[20] In Missouri the State Department of Corrections determines credit for time spent in pre-trial custody. Although the department's policy is to deny credit for time served in another jurisdiction while contesting an extradition, this policy does not amount to an abuse of process. Even in Canada credit for pre-trial custody is discretionary. To turn a discretionary principle under Canadian law into a mandatory condition of surrender to a foreign jurisdiction would run counter to the proposition articulated by the Supreme Court of Canada in R. v. Burns (2001), 151 C.C.C. (3d) 97 "that individuals who chose to leave Canada must generally accept the local law, procedure and punishments which the foreign state applies". As the Minister observed:

If convicted, Mr. Stassi will be sentenced by the court in the United States of America, which will determine a fit and appropriate sentence for the offences under American law. Furthermore, the administration of the sentence is for American authorities who are entitled to consider all the facts and circumstances relating to Mr. Stassi in making decisions regarding the administration of his sentence.

[21] Accordingly, the application for judicial review is dismissed.
