WEILER J.A.:

Nature of Appeal

[1] The appellant Nicos Timotheou appeals from his conviction for sexual assault and, if necessary, seeks leave to appeal and appeals his sentence of three years incarceration imposed on January 25, 2002. In relation to the conviction appeal, the appellant alleges that the verdict is unreasonable or in the alternative that the trial judge's misapprehensions of the evidence necessitate a new trial. In addition, the appellant alleges that the trial judge erred in not considering the defence of honest but mistaken belief raised by the appellant. For the reasons that follow, I am of the opinion that the verdict is not unreasonable, but I would order a new trial based on the trial judge's misapprehensions of the evidence. As a result, it is unnecessary to deal with the other issues raised and I do not do so.

Background

[2] The appellant and the complainant had sexual intercourse in the basement of a bar on the evening of June 20, 1999. After the incident, the complainant left the bar and phoned her husband from a payphone outside. She then called the police who began an investigation that resulted in the appellant being charged with sexual assault.

[3] The main issue at trial was whether the complainant had consented to sexual intercourse with the appellant. Counsel for the appellant also raised the issue of honest belief in consent in his submissions.

[4] The 30 year old complainant, a law student, testified that she lived with her husband and their daughter, Sylvia. After she and her daughter helped her next-door-neighbour, Mike, with a yard sale, she and her daughter participated in a barbecue during which she had a shot of whiskey and a beer. Mike ran out of alcohol and suggested that the complainant accompany him to purchase more. The liquor store was closed. They went to a bar and had a shot of whiskey. Earlier in the evening, the complainant told Mike that she was looking for work and would take whatever work she could get. Mike suggested that they go to a second bar where he knew the owner of the bar who might be in a position to offer her a job.

[5] The owner of the bar, Ms. Tekle, testified that Mike and the complainant came into the bar about 11:30 and had a drink. Mike, whom she knew as a friend, introduced Ms. Tekle to the complainant and the complainant told her that she was looking for some work. Ms. Tekle offered her a couple of shifts and told her that if she was available she could start the next day or the day after. The appellant was also present and met the complainant. Mike asked the appellant to show the complainant around. Ms. Tekle noticed that the complainant was flirting with the appellant because of her body language and because she was smiling at him. Ms. Tekle left for a few minutes to get change for the pool table and asked the appellant to watch the bar while she was gone.

[6] The complainant testified that she went downstairs to use the washroom. It was a small one-person bathroom with cleaning supplies and a door that did not seem to close all the way. She pulled down her jeans to prepare to use the toilet. The complainant testified that she "blacked out" several times during the evening because of her alcohol intake. She did so in the toilet. She testified that by "blacking out" she meant that she could not remember certain parts of the evening. In cross-examination, she was asked the following questions and gave the following answers:

Q. If I asked you that question, what do you mean by blacking out? Your answer would be, I was aware, and then I was unaware, and you don't know if you were conscious or unconscious; is that correct?

A. Correct. To a degree. When I became aware, I was on the floor, as if I had passed out."

Q. Okay. So you went to the washroom?

A. Right.

….

Q. And you went to use the toilet?

A. Yes.

Q. And then what happened?

A. And then the very next thing I remember is that the accused was right there in my face, and that I had been penetrated, and then I blacked out again.

Q. Did you -- I know that you haven't said that you did this, but did you take down his zipper on his pants?

A. No.

…

Q. And if I asked you, and I am, can you tell us what transpired up to the time that he got his penis into you, and your answer is that you don't know?

A. I don't know. I blacked out.

[7] Once the complainant was conscious of what was going on, she said she "pushed at the appellant" but did not want to start a fight with him out of fear. She was shocked, stunned, angry and upset by what was happening. She testified that she was two or three days into her period, the heavy portion, and her general view on having intercourse while menstruating was that she found the idea repulsive, disgusting and gross. When asked how she felt about the appellant's penis in her vagina she stated, "Even if I had been in a normal healthy state it wasn't something I consented to, and I didn't consent to it in a drunk state either." She thought he might have ejaculated because she felt wetter than just being on her period. The next thing she recalled after the intercourse was running across the street and almost getting hit by a convertible. She went to a pay phone and called her husband but could not make herself understood. She then called the police.

[8] The police officer who met the complainant noted that she was crying and hysterical. The officer testified that the complainant was still crying and upset when a sexual assault kit examination was conducted a couple of hours later. After they arrived at the police station the complainant indicated she was going to be sick, was taken to the public washroom of the police station and threw up. The police officer said that the complainant's speech was not slurred and when asked questions the answers she gave were responsive. The officer was of the opinion that the complainant would have been too impaired to drive a car.

[9] The appellant testified that the complainant had four or five drinks while she was in his company. She left two drinks on the bar. He testified that the complainant wanted to go downstairs to see where the coolers were in anticipation for her work, that he told her no, that after the owner of the bar left Mike pointed out that the complainant had gone downstairs, that Mike was enticing him to have sex with her, gave him some marijuana to give to the complainant and told him that she liked it. The appellant says he took the marijuana, put it in his pocket and went downstairs to bring the complainant back up. The complainant was just coming out of the washroom and her zipper was undone. The appellant testified that as soon as the complainant saw him she started kissing him. He reciprocated. She backed up into the washroom and he followed. She undid his zipper and opened his trousers. He made love to her quickly and then left immediately to return upstairs as he figured he only had about 5 minutes in total before his girlfriend returned. The appellant said nothing at all to the complainant before or during sex. He did not ask if she wanted to have sex or if she wanted him to use a condom. The appellant explained that he knew the complainant was consenting because she did not object, and she gave him the "type of kiss that was asking for screwing." He did not feel her push at him.

[10] The trial judge's reasons were particularly brief. After reciting what he considered to be the salient points in the evidence of the complainant and the appellant, the trial judge held:

As I indicated above, the complainant cannot remember if she drank much at the bar. The accused said that she did. The defence of honest and mistaken belief in consent does not arise here. This is not a case where there was equivocal conduct. The defence would be consent, not mistaken identity. The accused's evidence is that the complainant was not only willing but eager. I do not believe the evidence of the accused, nor does it raise a reasonable doubt. The evidence of the complainant is convincing. She would not consent to sex while menstruating, nor in any case to stand up sex in the basement of a bar. I find the accused guilty as charged.

Analysis

[11] The appellant alleges that the trial judge misapprehended the evidence in a number of ways and that as a result the verdict was unreasonable or alternatively resulted in a miscarriage of justice.

[12] In my opinion the verdict was not unreasonable. The evidence capable of confirming the complainant's credibility includes the following circumstances:

  • the complainant's evidence of what she had to drink prior to arriving at the second bar; the evidence of the appellant as to the complainant's drinking at the bar; and Officer Newton's evidence as to the complainant's intoxication.
  • the complainant's recollection of certain details was confirmed: she testified that the door to the basement washroom did not close all the way and the bar owner confirmed this; that the appellant did not say anything to her during intercourse and the appellant confirmed this; that the appellant did something to her breasts during intercourse and red dots were noted on her right nipple; that she was pushed up against the wall of the basement bathroom and this was confirmed by a pink scratch on the right side of the complainant's back and a dark and tender area below the scratch; that she thought the appellant ejaculated during the incident was confirmed by the examination afterwards.
  • the complainant's distraught emotional state immediately after the incident.

[13] I now turn to the trial judge's alleged misapprehensions of the evidence. As stated by Doherty J.A. in Morrissey (1995), 97 C.C.C. (3d) 193 at 221:

If an appellant can demonstrate that the conviction depends on a misapprehension of the evidence then, in my view, it must follow that the appellant has not received a fair trial, and was the victim of a miscarriage of justice. This is so even if the evidence as actually adduced at trial was capable of supporting a conviction.

[14] For the reasons that follow, I am of the opinion that when the cumulative effect of the misapprehensions is considered the appellant has met the test in this case. The misapprehensions of the evidence are as follows:

1. After outlining the complainant's evidence and the appellant's evidence the trial judge stated:

The complainant testified that the idea of engaging in sexual intercourse during menstruation was repugnant. Also, she said, "Why would I want to have sex in the basement of a bar on the Danforth?"

The complainant did not testify that she would not have consented to sex in the basement of a bar.

[15] After rejecting the appellant's evidence and finding that it did not raise a reasonable doubt, the trial judge accepted the complainant's evidence. He noted it was convincing and, in accepting her evidence, referred to her explanation that "she would not consent to sex while menstruating, nor in any case to stand up sex in the basement of a bar."

[16] No issue is taken with the trial judge's reliance on the complainant's explanation that she found even the idea of sexual intercourse while menstruating repulsive. The second explanation, an incorrect quotation of the appellant's evidence, was relied on by the trial judge and was central to his reason for conviction.

[17] The respondent submits that the trial judge's quotation was in fact a reasonable summary of the complainant's evidence. In support of this submission the respondent points out that when the complainant was asked whether she was amazed that the appellant managed to penetrate her standing up in the small area of the bathroom, she responded, "I don't think about it. No. I don't think about it. If the thought crosses my mind it's only repulsion that I feel." The complainant's evidence indicated that she felt repulsion when she thought about the logistics of stand-up sex in the small basement bathroom of the bar.

[18] The evidence relied on by the Crown is that the complainant was repulsed at the very idea of what occurred as well as its location. The trial judge could have accepted this evidence but that is not what he said. The query, "Why would I have sex in a bar on the Danforth?" is really a way of saying that the complainant said she had no motive to engage in sexual intercourse with the appellant. That is not what she said.

2. The second alleged misapprehension of the evidence is with respect to the trial judge's summary of the events that led to the incident. The trial judge stated, "She had just finished her business (going to the bathroom) when she says that the accused came in and raped her up against the wall." As the passage quoted above indicates the complainant said she "went to use the toilet". Whether the complainant had finished using the toilet was not central to the trial judge's reasoning process. What is important however is that the complainant could not remember what happened immediately before she discovered the appellant was having sexual intercourse with her. Although it is obvious that the accused came into the bathroom or he could not have been having sex with her there, the complainant had no recollection that the appellant came into the bathroom.

[19] The respondent submits that given the emphasis placed on blackouts during the trial, the trial judge was aware that the complainant could not remember what happened immediately prior to discovering the intercourse and his reasons as a whole demonstrate this. Just prior to this impugned portion, the trial judge noted that the complainant could not remember much of what happened at the bar. He stated, that at the bar, "She engaged in idle conversation and she went in and out of consciousness."

[20] Whether the complainant remembered what happened immediately prior to discovering the intercourse was critical to the trial judge's assessment of her credibility that she did not consent to intercourse with the appellant. If the complainant was blacked out and had no recollection of how the intercourse began, the complainant may have been incapable of consenting to intercourse. That, however, is not the same as reciting evidence to the effect that she did remember what happened and did not consent.

[21] In his recitation of events at the hospital, the trial judge concluded: "At Women's College Hospital, [the complainant] exhibited the customary emotional effects of a rape victim." The respondent concedes that once again the trial judge used inaccurate language. The nurse at the hospital testified that the complainant was tired, depressed, worried, anxious and scared. She never suggested that these were typical symptoms of having been sexually assaulted. As a result the appellant submits that the trial judge erroneously relied on her evidence as confirming the complainant's credibility. The respondent points out that the trial judge was entitled to rely on evidence of the appellant's demeanour and that the police officer who picked her up from the telephone booth noted that she was crying and "quite hysterical" while speaking on the telephone and that she continued to cry on the ride to the station, as well as at the hospital. Again, this evidence was capable of supporting a conviction but the trial judge did not rely on the police officer's evidence.

[22] Having regard to the number and extent of the trial judge's misapprehensions of evidence, I am not prepared to say that in totality they did not affect the trial judge's reasoning process and that his decision would inevitably have been the same. Accordingly we would allow the appeal, set aside the conviction and direct a new trial.

In view of our conclusion it is not necessary to deal with the other grounds of appeal.
