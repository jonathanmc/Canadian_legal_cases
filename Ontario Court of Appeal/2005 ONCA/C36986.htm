Heard: February 28, 2005

On appeal from conviction by Justice John Ross Belleghem of the Superior Court of Justice, sitting with a jury, dated November 19, 1999 and sentence imposed by Justice Belleghem dated December 17, 1999.

BY THE COURT:

[1]               The appellant appeals from his conviction for second degree murder and from the order of parole ineligibility of twelve years made by Belleghem J.  The appellant’s principal defence was self-defence and his main grounds of appeal concern the trial judge’s charge to the jury with respect to that defence.

[2]               On September 4, 1998, the appellant became involved in a fistfight with the deceased outside a bar in Mississauga.  At a time when most of the independent evidence suggested that the appellant had the support of his friends and therefore was not in serious danger, he pulled out a large knife he had been carrying and stabbed the deceased three times in the chest and abdomen.  The deceased died a few hours later in hospital.  The appellant fled the scene. With the assistance of friends and relatives, the knife and the clothing he was wearing were disposed of and he then left the city with his family.  He intended to turn himself into police the following day but was arrested before that.

[3]               The appellant testified that he acted in self-defence.  He claimed that the deceased was the aggressor in the initial fistfight and he only resorted to the use of the knife when the deceased was severely beating him.  He testified that when he used the knife his glasses had been knocked off and he was simply swinging blindly in an attempt to ward off his attacker.  He did not intend to stab the deceased and was surprised to later learn that the deceased had died. 

[4]               The trial judge put to the jury defences of accident, self-defence and provocation.  He also gave the jury the so-called rolled-up charge on intent to the effect that even if all of these defences failed, the jury could have a reasonable doubt that the appellant had the requisite intent for murder.

Self-defence

[5]               The appellant submits that the charge to the jury on self-defence was unnecessarily long, confusing, overly complex and distracted the jury from the core issues upon which the real defence depended.  He argues that the charge did not comply with the suggestions from this court and the Supreme Court of Canada in R. v. Jacquard (1997), 113 C.C.C. (3d) 1 (S.C.C.) and R. v. Pintar (1996), 110 C.C.C. (3d) 402 (Ont. C.A.), that the instructions on self-defence should focus on the real issues in the case.

[6]               The charge to the jury generally and the directions on self-defence specifically were lengthy.  The charge included instructions on the possible application of ss. 34(1) and (2), 35 and 37 of the Criminal Code.  However, the trial judge did not err in leaving those sections to the jury.  The appellant told a relatively simple factual story, but it was a story that was legally complex.  Because the appellant claimed that he did not intend to cause death or grievous bodily harm, it was necessary for the trial judge to direct the jury on s. 34(1).  For similar reasons, the defence in s. 37 was potentially open to the appellant.  Finally, because it was open to the jury to find that the appellant had unlawfully provoked an assault on himself, the defence in s. 35 may have been open to the jury. 

[7]               We disagree with the appellant’s overall characterization of the charge on self-defence.  The self-defence sections are difficult, but the trial judge explained their application clearly, or as clearly as possible.  He pointed out the differences in the various provisions so that the jury would understand how they applied.  He focused the jury on the underlying factual issues and assembled the evidence that the jury would have to deal with to resolve those issues. 

[8]               The appellant submits that the real defence in this case was under s. 34(2), and that the emphasis placed by the trial judge on ss. 34(1) and 35 would have diverted the jury from focusing on that provision.  He submits that s. 34(2) offered a broader basis for exculpation because of the disqualifying features in ss. 34(1) and 35 and especially because those provisions require consideration of who was the initial aggressor. 

[9]               It is not at all clear from this record that the primary defence at trial was s. 34(2).  The appellant testified that he used the knife to fend off the attack and had no intention of striking or hurting anyone with it.  Had the jury taken that view of the evidence, s. 34(1) would apply.  Moreover, on that view of the evidence, s. 34(2) did not necessarily offer a broader basis for avoiding liability, given the paucity of evidence that the appellant resorted to deadly force because he was under a reasonable apprehension of death or grievous bodily harm and because he believed on reasonable grounds that he could not otherwise preserve himself from death or grievous bodily harm.

[10]          The appellant submits that the trial judge should not have put s. 35 to the jury because on any view of the facts the appellant could not meet the requirement that he “declined further conflict and quitted or retreated from it”.  That is not the case.  The retreat requirement only applies “as far as it was feasible to do so”.  On the appellant’s version of events he was essentially trapped between two cars and could not retreat when he lashed out at the deceased.

[11]          Finally, there was no suggestion at trial by counsel for the defence that the trial judge should not have left any of the various self-defence sections to the jury.  To the contrary, defence counsel, not Mr. Lockyer, commended the trial judge on his charge. 

[12]          The appellant also submits that the trial judge invited the jury to import elements of s. 34(1) into s. 34(2) because in his introduction to the latter section he told the jury to “Please recall and consider those instructions and the evidentiary references as I do not propose to repeat them.”  This direction must be considered in context.  The jury would necessarily understand that the impugned direction was a reference to the elements common to the two subsections, such as the requirement of an unlawful assault by the victim. 

[13]          The appellant also submits that the trial judge failed to make it clear that s. 34(2) is available even where the accused has provoked the initial assault.  The trial judge pointed out the differences between the various subsections and especially between subsections 34(1) and (2).  He also broke out the elements of the defence under ss. 34(1) and (2).  This clear and accurate recital of the various elements of the defences showed that provocation was not an issue under s. 34(2).  He also expressly directed the jury that ss. 34(2) and 37 applied even though the assault that led to the acts of self-defence was provoked.  We are satisfied that the jury would not have been under any misapprehension as to the application of s. 34(2).  See R. v. Cameron (1995), 96 C.C.C. (3d) 346 (Ont. C.A.) at 365‑68.

[14]          Finally, the appellant submits that the trial judge erred when on several occasions he referred to s. 34(2) applying where the appellant intended to cause death or grievous bodily harm.  This was a misdirection.  Section 34(2) applies even where the death or grievous bodily harm is unintended.  See R. v. Trombley (1998), 126 C.C.C. (3d) 495 (Ont. C.A.), affirmed (1999), 134 C.C.C. (3d) 576 (S.C.C.).  However, this error could not have affected the verdict.  The jury convicted the appellant of murder and so must have found that he had the intent for murder.  Thus, they would not have disregarded s. 34(2) because they found that the appellant lacked that intent.

[15]          The instructions on self-defence were fair and balanced and as clear as possible given the complex legal issues that arose out of the appellant’s version of the events.  We would not give effect to these grounds of appeal.

After-the-fact Conduct

[16]          The appellant submits that the after-the-fact conduct evidence was only relevant to self-defence and that the trial judge failed to make that clear.  The after-the-fact conduct was a relatively minor aspect of this case.  The trial judge explicitly told the jury that the most potentially damaging piece of evidence, the disposal of the knife, could not be used against the appellant.  The trial judge also explained to the jury at great length the appellant’s explanations for his conduct that were inconsistent with an inference of guilt.  We are satisfied, despite the references to “murder”, that the jury would understand that the after-the-fact conduct related to self-defence and accident only. 

[17]          The appellant submits that the most serious error was in answer to the jury’s question concerning the intent for murder.  In answering the question, the trial judge included reference to “the subsequent conduct of the accused” as part of a long list of pieces of evidence that the jury should consider on that issue.  However, unlike the references to the other evidence, the reference to subsequent conduct was coupled with a direction as to whether it was consistent or otherwise “with an accidental killing in self defence or an intentional killing”.  We think in context the jury would have understood the limited use of the evidence. 

[18]          In any event, that evidence played a minor role in the case and the impugned direction could not have affected the verdict.  As we have said, the reference to this evidence came as part of a very long list that included evidence that was much more germane to the intent question. In the main part of the charge, the trial judge expressly told the jury that after-the-fact conduct had “very limited application” and “has no application, whatsoever, in determining whether the offence committed by the accused, should you so find that he committed any offence at all, was either murder or manslaughter”.  While it would have been preferable for the trial judge to have omitted reference to subsequent conduct from the list, we are satisfied that no substantial wrong or miscarriage of justice was occasioned.

Parole Ineligibility

[19]          The appellant submits that the trial judge erred in principle in placing undue emphasis on the appellant’s character.  The offender’s character is one of the statutory factors to be considered under s. 745.4 of the Criminal Code.  The Crown led additional evidence at the sentencing hearing that tended to demonstrate that the appellant, if not obsessed, was at least preoccupied with violence.  The fact that the appellant habitually drove around with a knife was itself a matter of real concern.  All this evidence had to be balanced against the evidence in the appellant’s favour, such as the strong family support, his good work history and his lack of a related criminal record.  The trial judge’s decision as to the weight to attach to the various factors under s. 745.4 is entitled to deference unless it can be said that the decision is unreasonable.  In his very careful reasons, the trial judge did not focus solely on the appellant’s character.  He looked at the circumstances of the offence and the other factors.  This was a brutal, senseless killing of an unarmed man in a public place.  The appellant and the deceased were complete strangers.  It was a case where it was open to the trial judge to find that an increase in parole ineligibility was warranted as a matter of denunciation.  We see no reason to interfere with his decision.

[20]          Accordingly, the appeal from conviction and from the increase in parole ineligibility is dismissed.
