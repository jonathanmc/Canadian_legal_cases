ENDORSEMENT

The Liability Appeal

[1]               The appellant (“Carosi”) was the low bidder on two construction contracts put out for tender by the respondent (“Niagara”).  Carosi did not get either contract.  The first contract was withdrawn by Niagara because of delays in obtaining the necessary environmental approvals and the second contract was awarded to the company that had placed the second lowest bid.

[2]               Under the terms of both tenders, those responding to the tenders were required to “adhere strictly” to the tender instructions.  Both tenders also reserved to Niagara the right to reject any or all tenders for any reason whatsoever and to accept the proposal that Niagara considered to be in its best interests.  Finally, both tenders provided that tenders that were incomplete could be rejected as informal. 

[3]               It was Carosi’s position at trial that his tenders were rejected because officials with Niagara had decided to “blackball” Carosi as a result of a negative reference provided by another regional municipality.  Carosi had done work for that municipality and listed them as a reference in the tender documents.  Carosi argued that the various objections to his tender documents raised by Niagara and its consultants during the tender process were all a ruse designed to disguise the decision that had been made to “blackball” Carosi because of the negative reference.  Carosi argued that this conduct demonstrated Niagara’s bad faith towards him throughout the tendering process.  He claimed damages assessed on the basis that he was entitled to be awarded the two contracts. 

[4]               Niagara acknowledged that it had obtained the negative reference in response to its inquiries.  It was Niagara’s position, however, that Carosi’s documents did not meet the tender requirements.  Niagara took the position that despite numerous efforts by its consultants to obtain the necessary information from Carosi, Carosi did not supply that information.  Niagara contended that far from acting in bad faith, it demonstrated a willingness throughout to co-operate with Carosi to finalize the documents as required by the tenders.  Niagara took the position that Carosi refused to provide all of the necessary information.  It was also Niagara’s position that in any event Carosi was not entitled to damages in relation to the first contract, since that contract was not granted to anyone. 

[5]               The trial was a hard fought affair that lasted three weeks.  The evidence consisted of essentially two conflicting descriptions of the relevant events.  One description came from Carosi’s witnesses and the other came from Niagara’s witnesses.  The stark differences in the two versions were in large measure based on different interpretations of the same events.  For example, Carosi characterized certain inadequacies in his material as insignificant and quickly remedied.  The Niagara witnesses characterized these same deficiencies as significant and unremedied, despite repeated attempts to get the necessary information.  The witnesses gave reasons for advancing their different interpretations of the events.  The trial judge made it clear in her reasons that she preferred the evidence of the witnesses called by Niagara to those called by Carosi.  She gave reasons for her preference.  We see no basis upon which we can interfere with those credibility assessments.

[6]               Mr. O’Connor, in a forceful and effective argument for Carosi, contends that the trial judge’s reasons are so inadequate as to necessitate the quashing of the judgment.  He submits that the reasons contain virtually no review of the evidence and are almost entirely conclusory in tone.  It is his contention that the reasons do not permit effective appellate review.

[7]               The reasons are brief.  Brevity cannot be equated with inadequacy.  The adequacy of reasons requires a contextual assessment.  In considering the adequacy of these reasons, we begin with the observation that the primary thrust of Carosi’s case was not tenable.  As counsel quite properly acknowledged in oral argument on appeal, Niagara was entitled to contact the references provided by Carosi and consider any information those references gave concerning Carosi’s prior work.  Counsel also acknowledges, again quite properly, that Niagara was entitled to reject Carosi’s tender bid if Niagara decided that it did not want to do business with Carosi based on the information it received from the references provided by Carosi.  Nor, was Niagara under any obligation to advise Carosi of the substance of the references.  If Niagara decided not to do business with Carosi because of the negative reference, it is wrong to characterize that decision as a “blackballing” of Carosi. 

[8]               Since Niagara was entitled to decline to do business with Carosi based on a negative reference, it makes no sense that Niagara would engage in an elaborate cover-up of that decision if in fact that was the basis for the decision not to grant the tenders to Carosi.  If the cover-up existed, it would also provide no basis in law for the damages claimed by Carosi.

[9]               The trial judge did not address Carosi’s “blackball” theory.  It would have been better had she done so.  However, her failure to analyze that theory caused no prejudice to Carosi.  Analysis of the theory could only demonstrate its untenability. 

[10]          The trial judge chose to analyze the case from a vantage point which offered Carosi a better chance of success than did his “blackball” theory.  While it is at least arguable that in law Niagara owed no obligation of good faith to Carosi until proper tender documents were filed, the trial judge proceeded on the assumption that Niagara owed a duty of good faith from the outset of the tender process.  Viewed from the perspective taken by the trial judge, the crucial factual issue was whether Niagara’s dealings with Carosi during the negotiations over the tender documents demonstrated Niagara’s bad faith. 

[11]          The trial judge referred in a general way to the competing evidence relevant to the question of Niagara’s bad faith.  She accepted the evidence provided by Niagara’s consultants concerning their day-to-day dealings with Carosi.  She accepted that evidence because of the manner in which it was given and because it was supported by documentary evidence, particularly a lengthy chain of correspondence between Carosi and the consultants.  The trial judge concluded:

Carosi failed to provide the information that was required to allow the proper evaluation of its tenders.  In both cases, the answers provided were of a tenor to raise legitimate concerns in the minds of the evaluating engineers.

… There was absolutely nothing in their testimony [the witnesses for Niagara] to suggest that they had any motive other than ensuring that the projects were carried out by the individuals or companies best able to do so.  Their testimony corroborated the defendant’s position that everything had been done to allow Carosi to correct deficiencies in its tenders both before and after the consulting reports were completed [emphasis added].

[12]          In this passage, the trial judge addressed the factual questions that were crucial to the good faith argument.  She found that Carosi did not provide essential information and that Niagara made all reasonable efforts to acquire that information.  These findings doomed Carosi’s argument that Niagara did not act in good faith in its dealings with him in the course of the discussions relating to the tender documents. 

[13]          The reasons for judgment, while brief, addressed the crucial factual questions and told the appellant why those questions were decided against him.  The reasons were adequate. 

[14]          Having concluded that the reasons were adequate, Carosi can succeed on this appeal only if he can demonstrate “clear and palpable” error in the findings of fact.  We find no such error.  There was ample evidence to support the trial judge’s findings.  Mr. O’Connor’s arguments certainly demonstrate that there was an evidentiary basis for different findings than those made by the trial judge.  The mere fact that different findings could reasonably have been made, however, does not justify appellate intervention. 

[15]          For example, counsel in his argument stressed the point that inadequacies in Mr. Carosi’s material were minor and insignificant.  His arguments in that respect are forceful.  There was, however, other evidence supporting the contention that the inadequacies were important and undermined any attempt to effectively assess the tender.  The trial judge accepted this evidence and rejected the evidence relied on by Carosi to support the insignificance of the inadequacies.  It is not this court’s function to reassess the relative merits of the competing descriptions of the significance of the various inadequacies.  That was the trial judge’s function and we see no “clear and palpable” error in her fact finding.

[16]          Carosi’s appeal is dismissed.

The Costs Appeal

[17]          Carosi also seeks leave to appeal the cost order made at trial.  The trial judge held that Niagara was entitled to its costs on a substantial indemnity basis throughout the proceedings.  Niagara claimed substantial indemnity costs of $650,515.15.  The trial judge awarded costs on a substantial indemnity basis in the amount of  $485,190.99. 

[18]          Leave to appeal costs will seldom be granted.  We are, however, satisfied that this is an appropriate case to grant leave to appeal the cost order and to vary the cost order made at trial. 

[19]          The trial judge made two errors in principle in awarding costs on a substantial indemnity basis.  First, she characterized Carosi’s allegations made in his pleadings and at trial as allegations of “improper conduct by professionals in respect of their professional duties”.  With respect, we think this is an overstatement.  Allegations of institutional bad faith made in the context of contractual negotiations do not, in our view, equate with the kind of personal allegations of dishonesty or corruption that would attract a punitive costs order.

[20]          The second error in principle arises out of the trial judge’s holding that Carosi’s previous counsel’s behaviour was so egregious as to warrant a cost sanction against Carosi.  She found that the conduct of prior counsel was unprofessional and increased the length of and complexity of the proceedings.  She also found that prior counsel acted with the full knowledge and support of Carosi. 

[21]          We take no issue with the trial judge’s description of the conduct of prior counsel or her finding that it was properly attributable to Carosi.  We also agree that counsel’s conduct merited a cost sanction against Carosi.  However, prior counsel left the case a year before the trial commenced.  There is no suggestion that the conduct of counsel who was retained a year before the trial and who conducted the trial was anything but appropriate.  The trial judge erred in principle in holding that the conduct of counsel discharged a year before the trial justified an order of costs on a substantial indemnity basis through to the end of the trial.  The trial judge should not have awarded costs on a substantial indemnity basis for the entire trial proceedings. 

[22]          Both counsel invited this court to determine the appropriate costs award at trial should the court decide that the order made by the trial judge was wrong and must be varied. 

[23]          The overall objective of a cost order as between parties is to fix an amount that it is fair and reasonable for the unsuccessful party to pay in the particular circumstances to the successful party.  A number of factors must be considered, including the complexity of the case, the conduct of the parties, the amount in issue and the result achieved.  In fixing costs in this case, we also bear in mind the amount of the partial indemnity bill submitted by counsel at trial and the fact that the trial judge reduced the substantial indemnity bill by some 25 percent.  Bearing those factors in mind and recognizing that the fixing of costs is anything but an exact science, we fix Niagara’s costs at $350,000.00.

[24]          Leave to appeal costs is granted, the cost order made below is set aside, and in its place an order awarding costs in the amount of $350,000.00, inclusive of disbursements and GST.

[25]          Niagara was successful on the appeal on liability and Carosi was successful on the costs appeal.  Bearing those results and the amount of work involved in the two appeals in mind, we would order costs of the appeals to Niagara in the amount of $20,000.00, inclusive of disbursements and GST.
