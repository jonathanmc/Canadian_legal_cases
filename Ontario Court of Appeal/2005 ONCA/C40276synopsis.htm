DATE: April 15, 2005
COA FILE NO.: C40276

SYNOPSIS OF

R. v. Anthony Kporwodu and Angela Veno

OVERVIEW

Anthony Kporwodu and Angela Veno were indicted on charges of first-degree murder in relation to the death of their three-month old daughter, Athena.  On June 23, 2003, Justice Brian Trafford of the Superior Court of Justice stayed the proceedings against them on the ground that their right to be tried within a reasonable time under s. 11(b) of the Charter had been breached. The Crown appealed from that order.

In a judgment released today, the Court of Appeal for Ontario (Justices Moldaver, Gillese, and Juriansz) unanimously dismissed the Crown’s appeal. It concluded that the four and one-half years delay from the date Mr. Kporwodu was charged to the anticipated completion of his trial, and the three and one-half years delay in the case of Ms. Veno, was unreasonable. The panel further held that the trial was delayed for the better part of two years because of the failings of the chief Crown witness, Dr. Charles Smith, the pathologist who performed the autopsy on Athena. But for his failings, the trial should have occurred within two to two and one-half years from the time that the respondents were first charged.

While upholding the stay, the panel disagreed with aspects of the trial judge’s legal analysis, particularly his treatment of pre-charge delay. The panel also took the view that the trial judge was overly critical of many of the state actors. In some instances, his findings of unacceptable conduct were overstated; in others, they were undeserved.  

REASONS FOR JUDGMENT

The panel’s reasons for judgment set out a history of certain pivotal events. There were four time periods central to the panel’s analysis within the approximately seventy-month time frame from the date of Athena’s death to the date on which it was anticipated the respondents’ trial would end. The panel’s reasons regarding each of these time periods are briefly dealt with below.

(1)              Phase 1: Pre-charge phase (March 6, 1998 to May 3, 1999)

This fourteen-month period runs from the date of Athena’s death to the date on which Mr. Kporwodu was charged with manslaughter.

The trial judge included this time in his calculation of the overall length of delay for s. 11(b) purposes.  He appeared to have done so as a means of remedying what he found to be separate breaches of the respondents’ rights under s. 7 and s. 11(d) of the Charter. These breaches consisted of the failure of state actors to preserve Athena’s remains in order that they could obtain a second autopsy report if they were dissatisfied with Dr. Smith’s report. 

The panel held that the trial judge erred in including any pre-charge time in calculating the overall length of delay for purposes of his s. 11(b) analysis. The panel concluded that the cremation of Athena’s remains did not constitute a breach of s. 7 or s. 11(d). In the panel’s view, when the cremation occurred, Athena’s remains were in the control of the respondents and the crematorium to which they had entrusted the remains. The police, and the Chief and Deputy Coroners, and Dr. Smith did not have a duty to preserve the remains.

Further, even if the state was negligent in failing to preserve the remains and thereby breached s. 7 and possibly s. 11(d), the trial judge erred in including pre-charge delay as a remedy for such breaches. The law is settled that pre-charge delay cannot be included for purposes of s. 11(b). Further, there was no evidence that the loss of Athena’s remains occasioned any prejudice to the respondents, let alone prejudice that entitled them to a stay. 

(2)              Phase 2: Transition phase (May 3, 1999 to May 15, 2000)

This twelve-month period runs from the date on which Mr. Kporwodu was charged with manslaughter to the date on which the respondents were charged with second-degree murder.

The trial judge appeared to attribute this entire period of delay to the Crown, addressing two matters in his stay ruling: delay in disclosure and delay in obtaining an addendum from Dr. Smith to his original autopsy report.

The addendum related to Dr. Smith’s opinion (which he told the Crown and the police about for the first time in July 1999) that an injury to Athena’s liver (which he observed during his autopsy in March 1998) had likely occurred within twelve hours of her death. If accurate, this meant that both respondents were present when the injury was inflicted and that Athena had been physically abused more than once. This additional information gave the police the grounds to charge Ms. Veno with second-degree murder and to elevate the charge against Mr. Kporwodu from manslaughter to second-degree murder.  (The charges against both were later elevated to first-degree murder.)

In the trial judge’s view, had this additional information been included in Dr. Smith’s original autopsy report, the respondents would have been charged with murder by January 1999, instead of May 2000. Hence the entire transition period was lost time for both respondents for which the Crown bore responsibility.

The panel upheld the trial judge’s decision to attribute this entire time to the Crown. In the panel’s view, no matter how one chose to assess this period, for practical purposes, it was lost time for Kporwodu and the delay was largely attributable to the failings of Dr. Smith, for whom the Crown bore responsibility.

(3)              Phase 3: Preliminary hearing phase (January 15, 2001 to January 17, 2002)

This twelve-month period runs from the date on which the preliminary hearing started to the date on which the respondents were committed to stand trial on second-degree murder charges. (The Crown preferred first-degree murder indictments against the respondents about a month after they were committed to trial.) 

The preliminary hearing delay resulted from the Crown’s decision to seek an independent review of Dr. Smith’s autopsy report following the withdrawal of two unrelated murder charges about two weeks after the respondents’ preliminary hearing had started. It took about eight months for the Crown to obtain this external review report.  The Crown at trial conceded responsibility for most of this delay and the trial judge agreed. On appeal, the Crown argued that the time taken to obtain the independent expert report should be treated as part of the inherent time requirements of the case and thus considered neutral.

The panel upheld the trial judge’s decision to attribute this entire time to the Crown. While the caselaw establishes that delays relating to the inherent time requirements of the case can include delay relating to extraordinary and unforeseeable events, the panel concluded that the need for an independent review of Dr. Smith’s work was not an unforeseen event. To the contrary, the panel accepted the trial judge’s finding that the police and the Crown were aware of a controversy concerning Dr. Smith as early as July 1999. The panel also took the view that, while it was legitimate for the Crown to seek an adjournment of the preliminary hearing in order to obtain an independent review of Dr. Smith’s work, the delay occasioned by this adjournment cannot be relied on to explain away delay that was otherwise unreasonable.

(4)              Phase 4: Trial phase (October 28, 2002 to June 23, 2003)

This eight-month period runs from the date on which the trial started to the date on which the charges were stayed. At issue was the seven months taken up with the respondents’ production motion and the delay that followed a change in counsel after the trial judge ruled on the production motion. (The one month taken up with the respondents’ s. 11(b) motion was not at issue on appeal.)

The respondents sought production of a review by the Coroner’s Office of files involving Dr. Smith. The trial judge concluded that the Coroner’s Office had acted as agent for the Crown in conducting the review and he ordered the Coroner’s Office to produce its files to the Crown for disclosure to the respondents. In a ruling denying a stay of his production order pending an application by the Coroner’s Office for leave to appeal to the Supreme Court of Canada, the trial judge made several disparaging comments about the professional integrity of counsel for the Crown and for the Coroner’s office, with the result that new counsel took over for each. A further delay ensued, as the trial judge undertook another murder trial before continuing with the trial of this matter. 

In his s. 11(b) analysis, the trial judge ascribed the entire time relating to the production motion to the Crown on the basis that the Crown and the Coroner’s Office acted oppressively in resisting the motion.

The panel disagreed with the trial judge’s assessment of this trial phase, although it ascribed, for reasons different from those of the trial judge, state responsibility for the three or four month delay following the change in counsel.

In the view of the panel, there were reasonable bases for resisting the production motion and the Crown and the Coroner’s Office did not act oppressively in doing so. Accordingly, the trial judge erred in ascribing the time relating to the production motion to the Crown. Rather, the time should have been attributed to the respondents or treated as neutral time.

In concluding that the Crown and the Coroner’s Office did not act oppressively, the panel made three points. First, the Crown’s motion to quash the respondents’ subpoenas relating to production was reasonable given the overbreadth of the subpoenas and the fact that the respondents had sufficient information to clarify the precise files they were looking for. Second, the trial judge’s finding that the Coroner’s Office had acted as the Crown’s agent in reviewing Dr. Smith’s files was not inevitable and the Crown did not act oppressively in arguing to the contrary. Last, the Crown did not act oppressively in raising privacy concerns relating to the families of the deceased children involved in the files reviewed by the Coroner’s Office.

The panel agreed with the trial judge that the time following the change in counsel should be borne by the state, but for different reasons. In the panel’s view, this change in counsel was necessitated by the trial judge’s unwarranted findings impugning the professional integrity of Crown counsel and counsel for the Coroner’s Office. Accordingly, the state must bear responsibility for the three or four months delay that followed this change in counsel.

(5)              Prejudice to the respondents

The panel concluded that the effect of the delays attributable to the Crown occasioned prejudice to the respondents. The panel concluded that the three and one-half years to try Ms. Veno and the four and one-half years to try Mr. Kporwodu, by themselves, are sufficiently long to infer prejudice, particularly taking into account that the Crown called no evidence to rebut this inference and that the respondents pressed to have their matters tried expeditiously. The panel was also satisfied that, as a result of the delay, the respondents suffered actual prejudice of the individual interests that s. 11(b) is intended to protect.

With respect to the respondents’ liberty interest, the panel agreed with the trial judge that the duration of the reporting requirements and the mobility restrictions prescribed by the their bail conditions impaired the respondents’ liberty interests. With respect to the respondents’ security interests, the panel reviewed the personal and emotional stresses that the respondents lived through while awaiting their trial. In declining to interfere with the trial judge’s finding that the respondents’ security interests had been seriously impaired as a result of the delay, the panel noted that it could hardly be suggested that there was no evidence of actual prejudice to the respondents’ health and well-being or that prejudice was not due to the length of time that the criminal proceedings took. With respect to the respondents’ fair trial interests, the panel concluded that the cremation of Athena’s remains and the respondents’ inability to obtain a second autopsy did not occur through any fault of the state. Nor was there evidence to show that Athena’s body was required to prepare a second autopsy report. Further, the respondents had not showed any evidentiary prejudice; virtually all witnesses were still available and there was ample documentary evidence to help them refresh their memories.

Last, the panel concluded that the trial judge did not err in his balancing of the societal and individual interests protected by s. 11(b). First, the trial judge clearly was alive to the seriousness of the charges. Second, the seriousness of the charges informed his s. 11(b) analysis. (In taking this view, the panel rejected the suggestion that the seriousness of the charge is a separate factor in a s. 11(b) analysis.) Third, while society has a heightened interest in seeing that serious offences are tried, the Crown has a heightened obligation to ensure that the trials for such offences are held in a timely fashion.  Fourth, while no doubt there are cases in which accused persons attempt to delay their trials, in this case the respondents’ interests in having their son returned to them and rebuilding their lives and their relationship depended upon the speedy resolution of the charges against them.
