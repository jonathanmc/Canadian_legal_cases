COSTS ENDORSEMENT

FELDMAN and LANG JJ.A.:

[1]               The parties made written submissions with respect to costs. Although the appellant was partially successful in having the judgment of the court below modified as to the extent of the remedy, the respondents had substantial success in maintaining the judgment in respect of the entitlement of same-sex partners to pensions without discrimination. Consequently, the respondents are entitled to their costs from the appellant on the partial indemnity scale. If the parties are unable to agree on the amount of costs within thirty days, the costs shall be assessed.

[2]               In our view, the intervenor did not add in any significant way to the costs and should not have to bear any costs.

[3]               The appellant raised two issues that we wish to address. The first is whether the costs should be reduced because the action is a constitutional challenge to legislation that need not have been brought as a class action with its attendant increased costs. Although this claim might have merit in another case, here it does not.  First, the issue has been addressed already both at the certification stage of this action, at which point the appellant consented to certification, and also by the British Columbia Supreme Court at an earlier date when dealing with the certification of the companion case: Brogaard v. Canada (Attorney General), [2002] B.C.J. No. 1775 (S.C.). The trial judge in this case, when dealing with the issue of counsel fees to be paid by the class, also confirmed that this action had to be prosecuted as a class action in order to deal effectively not only with the common issues, but with the individual entitlements of the class members: Hislop v. Canada (Attorney General), [2004] O.J. No. 1867 at para. 15 (Sup. Ct.) [“Hislop fees decision”]. Without limiting the discretion of the assessment officer on other grounds, we therefore direct the assessment officer to make no deduction on the basis that the action did not need the number of lawyers engaged, or the amount of time spent, because the action could have been brought as a test case and not a class action.

[4]               The second issue raised by the appellant is that counsel has already been awarded a significant fee from the class members by the trial judge. In response, any costs that are assessed and paid by the appellant will be applied to reduce the amount that the class members will have to pay to their counsel, and will therefore increase their recovery in the action.

[5]               However, we note that in the Hislop fees decision, which was made before the release of the reasons for judgment in the appeal, the trial judge proceeded to determine and award the multiplier to be used to fix the counsel fee payable by the class. The application of the multiplier was based on the potential recovery of pension arrears in accordance with the trial judgment. Based on the reasons for judgment of this court, that amount may now be reduced, depending on the determinations made by the trial judge when deciding the issues of individual entitlement. The appellant suggests that the fee awarded, including the multiplier of 4.8, could now potentially consume the entire amount of pension arrears owing to the class.

[6]               Although this issue does not affect the appellant directly, we agree that it is a matter of potential concern for the class members. As a matter of future practice, it may be appropriate in class actions to await the outcome of an appeal before setting the fee multiplier where the multiplier depends in part on the amount of the recovery for the class and where the issues on appeal may affect the amount recovered.

[7]               The appellant also asked this court to set aside the decision of the trial judge awarding the costs of the trial. We decline to do so.
