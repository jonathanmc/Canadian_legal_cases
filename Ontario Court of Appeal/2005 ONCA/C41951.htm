On appeal from the judgments of Justice Colin L. Campbell of the Superior Court of Justice dated May 20, 2004 and October 5, 2004.

MACPHERSON J.A.:

A.            INTRODUCTION

[1]               Terrence Pierce (“Pierce”) was an investment counsellor.  On May 19, 1999, he and his wife, the appellant Nancy Pierce, entered into a loan agreement with the respondent Canada Trustco Mortgage Company (“Canada Trustco”) in the principal amount of $775,000 for a five‑year term.

[2]               Pierce suffered from a bi‑polar disorder and experienced severe manic and depressive episodes in 1998 and 1999.  He committed suicide on August 20, 1999.  The loan and Pierce’s death gave rise to three proceedings, two of which are subjects of this appeal.

[3]               In the first proceeding, Canada Trustco commenced an action against Pierce’s estate (the “Estate”) – but not Mrs. Pierce personally – for repayment of the loan, which had gone into default in December 1999, approximately three months after Pierce’s death.

[4]               In the second proceeding, Mrs. Pierce commenced an action against Canada Trustco.  In her statement of claim, she pleaded that the loan, together with her husband’s mental disorder, caused his health to deteriorate to the point that he took his own life.  Mrs. Pierce claims for the loss of support and income provided by her husband.

[5]               The third proceeding, which is not part of this appeal, is an application brought by Mrs. Pierce to prevent Canada Trustco from enforcing the loan agreement against the Pierce matrimonial home on the basis that it knew Pierce unduly influenced Mrs. Pierce to sign it and yet failed to take reasonable steps to ensure that she entered into the agreement voluntarily and based upon independent legal advice.  By order dated June 27, 2001, this application is to be tried and heard together with the Canada Trustco action.

[6]               In 2004, Justice Colin Campbell heard three motions relating to the pleadings in the two actions.  The motions judge rendered two judgments with respect to the motions, on May 20 and October 5, 2004. [1]   The end result of these decisions was that the Estate’s proposed defence of equitable set‑off in the first action was struck out for non‑compliance with a limitation period and Mrs. Pierce’s statement of claim in the second action was struck out as disclosing no reasonable cause of action.

[7]               The appellants, Nancy Pierce as estate trustee in the first action and Mrs. Pierce personally in the second action, appeal both of these decisions.  The appeals raise issues relating to pleadings, tort law, contract law, limitation periods, and legal and equitable set‑off.

B.            FACTS

            (1)            The parties and the events [2]

[8]               Terrence Pierce married Nancy Pierce in 1984.  He was employed as an investment counsellor.  In late 1997, he began to develop a bi‑polar disorder.  He experienced severe mood swings that affected his marital relationship and business activities.

[9]               In February 1998, Pierce applied for a line of credit with Canada Trustco for $650,000.  He disclosed to Canada Trustco his intent to use the funds to purchase securities.  Nancy Pierce signed the line of credit agreement as an accommodating party and became a co‑debtor.

[10]          By May 1999, the line of credit had been paid down to approximately $500,000.  On May 14, 1999, the Pierces entered into a new loan agreement with Canada Trustco in the principal amount of $775,000 for a five‑year term.  A total of $765,901.84 was advanced under this line of credit.

[11]          Pierce committed suicide on August 20, 1999.  There was a default in the interest payment due on the line of credit on December 3, 1999.

(2)            The litigation

[12]          In August 2000, Canada Trustco commenced an action against the Estate of Terrence Pierce in respect of the outstanding portion of the loan ($765,901.84).  Mrs. Pierce was named as a defendant in her capacity as estate trustee, but she was not named in her personal capacity.

[13]          By way of statement of claim dated December 16, 2003 and amended on March 19, 2004, Nancy Pierce commenced an action against Canada Trustco.  She sought damages for negligence, negligent misrepresentation, breach of contract and breach of fiduciary duty.  She claimed for the loss of support and income provided by her husband and the diminution of the value of his Estate.  She also sought punitive damages.

(3)            The motions

[14]          The parties brought two motions before the motions judge in April 2004.

[15]          The first motion was brought by Canada Trustco seeking an order striking the Amended Statement of Claim issued by Nancy Pierce in her action.  The motions judge granted this order, with leave to amend.  There is no appeal from this order.

[16]          The second motion was brought by the Estate in Canada Trustco’s action, seeking leave to file a Fresh As Amended Statement of Defence to allege damages by way of equitable set‑off as a result of negligence and breach of contract.  The motions judge dismissed this motion on the basis that the pleading fell outside s. 38(3) of the Trustee Act, R.S.O. 1990, c. T. 23, which requires an estate trustee to bring an action on behalf of an estate within two years from the death of the deceased.  The Estate appeals this order.

[17]          Acting pursuant to the permission bestowed by the order in the first motion, Nancy Pierce amended her statement of claim in August 2004.  She abandoned her claims grounded in negligence and breach of fiduciary duty, but advanced claims in negligent misrepresentation and breach of contract.  The essence of her new pleading was assertions that Canada Trustco was under a duty to warn her that her husband did not earn income sufficient to qualify for the line of credit pursuant to its own internal guidelines and, in the circumstances, Canada Trustco’s guidelines required confirmation that Mrs. Pierce had the opportunity to obtain independent legal advice.  These failures, pleads Mrs. Pierce, constitute negligent misrepresentation and breach of contract.

[18]          Canada Trustco brought a motion pursuant to rule 21.01(1)(b) of the Rules of Civil Procedure, R.R.O. 1990, Reg. 194, seeking an order striking the Fresh As Amended Statement of Claim as disclosing no cause of action.

[19]          The motions judge granted the motion.  In his October 5th judgment, he reasoned:

The circumstances pleaded might, if proved, provide in equity a defence on the debt.  On the state of the existing jurisprudence…they cannot in my view give rise to a cause of action for damages.

I have not been referred to a case that has imposed a duty to warn on a bank when it does not follow its internal policies.  To do so would fundamentally change the relationship between bank and borrowers.

There are good reasons why particular facts may provide a defence to a debt on equitable principles, but not give rise to a cause of action.  Such circumstances would not change the basic nature of the banker/customer relationship.  Permitting a cause of action for damages based on the imposition of a duty to warn would change a relationship that has long been recognized and accepted in law.

For the above reasons, I conclude that the Fresh As Amended Statement of Claim is to be struck as disclosing no reasonable cause of action.

[20]          Nancy Pierce appeals the order dismissing her cause of action against Canada Trustco.

C.            ISSUES

[21]          I would frame the issues in the two appeals in this fashion:

(1)       In Nancy Pierce’s action, did the motions judge err by striking out her statement of claim as disclosing no cause of action?

(2)       In Canada Trustco’s action, did the motions judge err by striking out the proposed amendments relating to equitable set‑off in the Estate’s statement of defence for non‑compliance with a statutory limitation period?

(3)       If the answer to (2) is in the affirmative, can the Estate advance a claim relating to equitable set‑off?

D.            ANALYSIS

            (1)            General point

[22]          This appeal relates primarily to pleadings issues.  The test for striking out pleadings is a clear one – “[A]ssuming that the facts as stated in the statement of claim can be proved, is it ‘plain and obvious’ that the plaintiff’s statement of claim discloses no reasonable cause of action?”: see Hunt v. Carey Canada Inc., [1990] 2 S.C.R. 959 at 980 per Wilson J.  In both of the judgments under appeal, the motions judge cited and applied the Hunt test.

(2)            The statement of claim in the Nancy Pierce action

[23]          Nancy Pierce’s Fresh As Amended Statement of Claim asserts two causes of action against Canada Trustco – negligent misrepresentation and breach of contract.  The question is whether the motions judge was correct in determining that it is plain and obvious that these claims cannot succeed.

(a) Negligent misrepresentation

[24]          Nancy Pierce pleads that Canada Trustco did not comply with its own internal loan guidelines in two respects – it did not ensure that she obtained independent legal advice before co‑signing the loan with her husband and it did not advise her that it was making the loan even though her husband did not earn sufficient income to qualify for the loan under the guidelines.  These failures, pleads Mrs. Pierce, amount to negligent misrepresentations.

[25]          The elements of the tort of negligent misrepresentation were enunciated in Queen v. Cognos Inc., [1993] 1 S.C.R. 87 at 110:

(1) there must be a duty of care based on a ‘special relationship’ between the representor and representee; (2) the representation in question must be untrue, inaccurate, or misleading; (3) the representor must have acted negligently in making said misrepresentation; (4) the representee must have relied, in a reasonable manner, on said negligent misrepresentation; and (5) the reliance must have been detrimental to the representee in the sense that damages resulted.

[26]          The motions judge held that Mrs. Pierce’s pleadings did not establish the first element of the tort.  He concluded that there was no special relationship between Canada Trustco and Mrs. Pierce and that the case law did not impose “a duty to warn on a bank when it does not follow its internal policies.” I agree.

[27]          Generally speaking, the relationship between a financial institution lender and its customer borrower is a purely commercial relationship of creditor and debtor.  Absent any special relationship or exceptional circumstances such as would give rise to a fiduciary duty (which is not pleaded by Mrs. Pierce), the courts have consistently held that the lender owes no duty to the borrower in connection with the making of the loan.  In particular, the bank owes no duty to its customer to advise the customer not to undertake the loan: see Bertolo v. Bank of Montreal (1986), 57 O.R. (2d) 577 (C.A.) and Bank of Montreal v. Duguid (2000), 47 O.R. (3d) 737 (C.A.).

[28]          Mrs. Pierce’s specific allegation about the duty owed to her by Canada Trustco – to advise her that it was departing from its internal guidelines in making the loan – is also belied by the case law.  In Bank of Montreal v. Duguid, Feldman J.A. stated, at para. 38, “the fact that a bank does not comply with its own internal lending practices does not, by itself, render its loan unenforceable or give the borrower a right of recourse”.  In the same case, Osborne A.C.J.O. said, at para. 1, that “the Bank’s failure to follow its internal policies (specifically, advising Mrs. Duguid to get independent legal or financial advice) is not a basis upon which to dismiss the Bank’s action on the note.”

[29]          Before concluding on this issue, I note that the motions judge observed that the circumstances pleaded by Mrs. Pierce in her statement of claim “might, if proved, provide in equity a defence on the debt.”  I will return to this point when I consider the appeal from the motions judge’s decision striking out the Estate’s statement of defence in the Canada Trustco action.

(b) Breach of contract

[30]          In her Fresh As Amended Statement of Claim, Nancy Pierce pleaded that Canada Trustco’s alleged misrepresentations constituted a collateral contract with Canada Trustco or, in the alternative, were implied terms of the loan agreement.  In either formulation, Mrs. Pierce pleads that Canada Trustco breached the contract.

[31]          The motions judge did not address this component of Mrs. Pierce’s claim in his reasons, probably because its foundations, and therefore its defects, overlap substantially with the negligent misrepresentation claim.

[32]          In my view, the crucial point is that Canada Trustco made no representations to Mrs. Pierce relating to its decision not to follow its internal guidelines in making the loan.  It was entirely silent about its guidelines.  Nor did Mrs. Pierce say anything to Canada Trustco about its internal guidelines that might have put Canada Trustco on notice that this was a consideration for its customer that warranted a response.  In short, it is difficult to see how silence from both parties on a matter can give rise to the necessary meeting of minds essential to any contract, including a collateral contract, or to the creation of an implied term in the main contract.

(c)              Conclusion

[33]          For these reasons, I would dismiss the appeal from the motions judge’s decision striking out Mrs. Pierce’s Fresh As Amended Statement of Claim as disclosing no cause of action.

(3)            The Estate’s statement of defence in the Canada Trustco action

[34]          The Estate of Terrence Pierce sought leave to amend its statement of defence in Canada Trustco’s action to include a claim for damages by way of equitable set‑off.  The proposed amendment is set out in paragraph 39 of the Fresh As Amended Statement of Defence:

39. The defendant pleads by way of equitable set‑off, the following damages:

(a) Terry Pierce irrationally and improvidently liquidated 126,000 shares of Superior Propane Units at a price of $14.12 per unit.  The said units are currently selling for $26.00 per unit and as a result, the Estate of Terry Pierce has suffered a loss of $1.6 million dollars;

(b) Canada Trustco’s negligent acts, breach of contract and breach of fiduciary duties as particularized above, caused or contributed to Terry Pierce being fired by Scotia Capital and prevented him from being able to earn an income or become gainfully employed.  In addition, the aforesaid acts of Canada Trustco caused or aggravated Terry Pierce’s matrimonial problems;

(c) Canada Trustco’s aforesaid acts caused or contributed to Terry Pierce’s depression and Disorder from which he suffered in 1998 and 1999 before his death; and,

(d) Punitive and exemplary damages in the amount of $1,000,000.00 on the grounds as set out above.  The defendant pleads the aforesaid conduct of Canada Trustco constitutes an independent actionable wrong.

[35]          Section 111 of the Courts of Justice Act, R.S.O. 1990, c. C.43 (the “CJA”), provides for a defence of set‑off:

111(1) In an action for payment of a debt, the defendant may, by way of defence, claim the right to set off against the plaintiff’s claim a debt owed by the plaintiff to the defendant.

(2) Mutual debts may be set off against each other even if they are of a different nature.

(3) Where, on a defence of set off, a larger sum is found to be due from the plaintiff to the defendant than is found to be due from the defendant to the plaintiff, the defendant is entitled to judgment for the balance.

[36]          Section 38(3) of the Trustee Act provides:

38(3) An action under this section shall not be brought after the expiration of two years from the death of the deceased.

[37]          The motions judge refused leave to amend the statement of defence on the basis that the two‑year limitation period prescribed by s. 38(3) of the Trustee Act applied to the proposed pleading and rendered it out‑of‑time.  With respect, I am of the view that the motions judge erred in reaching this conclusion.

[38]          In the law of Canada today, there are two categories of set-off – legal and equitable.  Legal set-off, flowing from 18th century English statutes, is anchored in two requirements – both obligations must be debts and both debts must be mutual cross obligations: see Holt v. Telford, [1987] 2 S.C.R. 193 at 204 per Wilson J.

[39]          Equitable set-off, on the other hand, is quite different.  Its origins are not in statute law.  The claims between the parties do not need to be debts.  Equitable set-off can apply where mutuality is lost or never existed: see Holt v. Telford at pp. 205‑12 and Canada Trustco Mortgage Co. v. Sugarman (1999), 179 D.L.R. (4th) 548 at paras. 17-18 per Charron J.A.

[40]          The requirements for proving equitable set-off in Canada are enunciated in Holt v. Telford at p. 212 and are drawn explicitly from English authorities:

1. The party relying on a set-off must show some equitable ground for being protected against his adversary’s demands: Rawson v. Samuel, [1841] Cr. & Ph. 161, 41 E.R. 451 (L.C.).

2. The equitable ground must go to the very root of the plaintiff’s claim before a set-off will be allowed: … [Br. Anzani (Flexistowe) Ltd. v. Int. Marine Mgmt (U.K.) Ltd, [1980] Q.B. 137, [1979] 3 W.L.R. 451, [1979] 2 All E.R. 1063].

3. A cross-claim must be so clearly connected with the demand of the plaintiff that it would be manifestly unjust to allow the plaintiff to enforce payment without taking into consideration the cross-claim: … [Fed. Commerce and Navigation Co. v. Molena Alpha Inc., [1978] Q.B. 927, [1978] 3 W.L.R. 309, [1978] 3 All E.R. 1066].

4. The plaintiff’s claim and the cross-claim need not arise out of the same contract: Bankes v. Jarvis, [1903] 1 K.B. 549 (Div. Ct); Br. Anzani.

5. Unliquidated claims are on the same footing as liquidated claims: [Nfld. v. Nfld. Ry. Co., [1888] 13 App. C. 199 (P.C.)].

[41]          Based on the analysis and definitions in Holt v. Telford and Canada Trustco Mortgage Co. v. Sugarman, it is clear that the set-off in s. 111 of the CJA corresponds to the statutory definition of legal set-off as further developed by the common law.  On the other hand, the definition in s. 111 is not at all similar to the definition of equitable set‑off articulated by Wilson J. in Holt v. Telford.

[42]          Canada Trustco submits that s. 111(3) of the CJA, which permits a plaintiff to recover an excess amount on a set‑off claim, demonstrates that an independent actionable claim (which the Estate pleads it is making in paragraph 39(d) of its Fresh As Amended Statement of Defence) functions as a counterclaim and not a set‑off and therefore should be subject to the relevant statutory limitation period.  In short, Canada Trustco’s position is that there should be no distinction between legal and equitable set‑off claims in the context of limitation periods.  Both can be defences and both can give rise to claims.  Accordingly, they should be treated the same.

[43]          In oral argument, both counsel conceded that there is no Canadian case directly on point on this issue.  However, there is clear authority in the United Kingdom that equitable set‑off is a substantive defence to which a statutory limitation period is inapplicable.  The leading case is Henriksens Rederi A/S v. PHZ Rolimpex, [1973] 3 All E.R. 589 (C.A.), which involved a claim for recovery of freight charges.  The defendant alleged that the goods came damaged and sought to raise the resulting damages by way of an equitable set‑off defence.  Under the applicable Hague Rules, the claim for damages was statute barred.  However, Denning L.J. held that equitable set‑off is a true substantive defence that is not subject to a limitation period.  He said, at pp. 593 and 595-96:

In point of principle, when applying the law of limitation, a distinction must be drawn between a matter which is in the nature of a defence and one which is in the nature of a cross‑claim.  When a defendant is sued, he can raise any matter which is properly in the nature of a defence, without fear of being met by a period of limitation.  No defence, properly so‑called, is subject to a time‑bar [emphasis in original].

. . .

Although it is often described as an ‘equitable set-off’, it would, I think, be more accurately stated to be an ‘equitable defence’… When the contractor sues for the contract price, the employer can say to him: ‘You are not entitled to that sum because you have yourself broken the very contract on which you sue, and you cannot fairly claim that sum unless you take into account the loss you have occasioned to me.’  It is on a par with the case of a defendant who says that the plaintiff has repudiated the contract by an anticipatory breach, or that the plaintiff has been guilty of a breach going to the root of the contract.  On accepting it, the defendant is discharged from further performance and can set up the breach as a defence.  So also with any breach by the plaintiff of the selfsame contract, the defendant can in equity set up his loss in diminution or extinction of the contract price.  It is in the nature of a defence.  As such it is not subject to time‑bar.

[44]          Lord Denning’s view was approved by the English Court of Appeal in Westdeutsche Landesbank Girozentrale v. Islington London Borough Council, [1994] 4 All E.R. 890 at 945 and applied in Filross Securities Ltd. v. Midgeley, (1999) 31 H.L.R. 465 (C.A.).  This position has also been adopted in Australia in Australian Mutual Provident Society v. Specialist Funding Consultants Pty Ltd. (1991), 24 N.S.W.L.R. 326 (S.C.) and Sidney Raper Pty Ltd. v. Commonwealth Trading Bank of Australia (1975), 2 N.S.W.L.R. 277 (C.A.).

[45]          In my view, the English and Australian authorities are particularly persuasive in light of Wilson J.’s judgment in Holt v. Telford.  In that judgment, Wilson J. synthesized the principles developed by the English courts of equity over the last 200 years.  Thus, it is entirely appropriate to have regard to English cases in order to interpret the law of set‑off in Canada.  Similarly, the Australian case law is drawn directly from the jurisprudence of the English courts and is, therefore, also persuasive in this domain.  Finally, I note that in M. (K.) v. M. (H.), [1992] 3 S.C.R. 6 at 70, La Forest J. cited s. 2 of the Limitations Act, R.S.O. 1980, c. 240, and observed that it “gives rise to the inference that there is a category of equitable claims not subject to the Act at all, and that the equitable defences survive in those cases.”

[46]          For these reasons, I conclude that the proposed defence of equitable set‑off is not subject to the two‑year limitation period in s. 38(3) of the Trustee Act.

[47]          Canada Trustco makes two additional arguments, unrelated to the limitation period issue, in support of the motions judge’s decision striking out the equitable set‑off component of the Fresh As Amended Statement of Defence.

[48]          First, Canada Trustco contends that the motion to amend the Fresh As Amended Statement of Defence should have been dismissed for laches.  This argument was not advanced before the motions judge.  Moreover, I see no basis for it on the merits.

[49]          Second, Canada Trustco points out that the equitable set‑off defence is anchored in several causes of action - breach of fiduciary duty, negligent misrepresentation and breach of contract.  The first of these, breach of fiduciary duty, was struck out as a cause of action in Mrs. Pierce’s action by the motions judge in his decision of May 20, 2004.  This decision was not appealed and breach of fiduciary duty was not pleaded in Mrs. Pierce’s Fresh As Amended Statement of Claim.  The second and third proposed anchors for the equitable set‑off defence, negligent misrepresentation and breach of contract, were struck out as causes of action in Mrs. Pierce’s action by the motions judge in his decision on October 5, 2004.  Mrs. Pierce appealed, but for the reasons set out in the previous section her appeal should fail.  The result of all this, submits Canada Trustco, is that, putting aside the limitation period issue, there is still no substantive basis for the Estate’s equitable set‑off defence.

[50]          I agree with this submission.  Nancy Pierce’s claims under these headings, which have been struck out, cannot be resurrected, with identical content, through a claim for equitable set‑off mounted by the Estate.

[51]          I hasten to add that this does not mean that the Estate’s entire defence must be struck out.  There are legitimate defences which should survive the striking out of the equitable set‑off claim.  Two such defences are premised on a pleaded “social and business relationship” between Pierce and Graham McWaters, Canada Trustco’s representative on the loan transaction.

[52]          One defence advanced by the Estate is that Pierce was suffering from a mental disorder and was not competent to sign the loan documents.  Canada Trustco knew of this situation and proceeded with the loan anyway.

[53]          A second defence advanced by the Estate is the pleading that “McWaters knew or ought to have known…of the continuing undue influence and control exercised by Terry Pierce over Nancy Pierce concerning all financial and business matters between them”.  The Estate pleads that this knowledge or constructive knowledge required Canada Trustco to ensure that Mrs. Pierce received independent legal advice before signing the loan agreement.  I note, parenthetically, that although I am here considering the Estate’s defence to Canada Trustco’s action, Mrs. Pierce’s signature on the loan agreement was required in order for the loan to proceed because the loan was secured by a mortgage on the matrimonial home.  The decision of this court in Bank of Montreal v. Duguid stands for the propositions that a party (in that case, a wife) may set aside a transaction where that party was induced to enter into the transaction by another’s (a husband’s) influence and that equity may then lead to the transaction being set aside against the institution (a bank) that was also a party to the transaction. 

E.            DISPOSITION

[54]          I would dismiss the appeals from both decisions of the motions judge.  In the Canada Trustco action, I would give the Estate leave to further amend its Fresh As Amended Statement of Defence in accordance with these reasons.

[55]          The respondent is entitled to its costs of the appeals which I would fix at $5000, inclusive of disbursements and GST.
