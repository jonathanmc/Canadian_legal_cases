MACPHERSON J.A.:

A.            INTRODUCTION

[1]               At the conclusion of a three‑day trial in Sault Ste. Marie in the early autumn of 2001, Justice Ian McMillan ordered Sydney Archer to pay spousal support of $800 per month to Vickie Archer.  The duration of these payments was limited to three years, concluding in September 2004.

[2]               The Divisional Court allowed an appeal from this judgment on the basis that the trial judge failed to consider relevant uncontradicted evidence and misapprehended other evidence.  The Divisional Court ordered that the spousal support order of $800 per month be continued on an indefinite basis, but subject to “review”, rather than termination, on September 1, 2004.

[3]               Sydney Archer’s appeal of the Divisional Court’s order raises, potentially, several issues, including whether the Divisional Court erred in its analysis of the trial judge’s treatment of the evidence and, if it did not, whether it erred in imposing a spousal support order of indefinite duration, subject to “review” on a specific date.

B.            FACTS

(1)             The parties and the events

[4]               The appellant, Sydney Archer (the “husband”), and the respondent Vickie Archer (the “wife”), began living together in 1987 and married in April 1988.  They separated in March 1999.  On May 27, 1999, the wife obtained a spousal support order of $800 per month.  The parties reconciled in August 1999 and, on consent, spousal support was terminated.  The parties separated, permanently, in October 1999.  There are no children of the marriage.  Through the years of their marriage, the husband had a steady job as a steelworker at Algoma Steel in Sault Ste. Marie.  His gross annual income in 2000 was $43,438.56.

[5]               The wife completed high school in 1985 at Alexander Henry High School in Sault Ste. Marie, a school which accommodated developmentally delayed or challenged students. 

[6]               During her marriage, the wife pursued several educational programs and held a variety of employment positions, mostly part‑time.  These were described by the trial judge in this fashion:

Commencing in or about the autumn of 1993, the Applicant enrolled herself in diploma programs at the local community college, that were intended to increase her potential to secure employment in the areas of home and child support worker.  The courses were funded by the Respondent and by student loans.  The Applicant did not complete any of the programs.

On a recent Resume, (Exhibit 8), the Applicant professes to hold a Canadian Red Cross “Home worker’s” certificate; a St. John Ambulance First Aid & CPR Certificate; a Smart Serve Certificate; an Algoma Health Unit Certificate; computer courses from Sault College of Applied Arts & Technology; and skills pertaining to the care of children and seniors.

The Applicant has, during her marriage, been employed briefly as a fund raiser, taxi service dispatcher, babysitter, housecleaner and residential canvasser.

[7]               After the parties’ initial separation, the wife applied for disability benefits from the Ontario Disability Support Program.  Her application was rejected on November 8, 1999, on the grounds that:

§        the direct and cumulative effect of your impairment on your ability to attend to your personal care, function in the community and function in the workplace does not result in a substantial restriction in one or more of these activities of daily living,

§        you do not have a substantial physical or mental impairment as defined in Section 4.(1) of the Ontario Disability Support Program Act.

[8]               After the final separation, the wife, with the loyal and generous support of her mother, looked for employment, without success.

[9]               On April 20, 1999, the wife made an application for, inter alia, spousal support and an equalization of net family property.

(2)            The legal proceedings

[10]          In relation to the issues of equalization and spousal support, a trial was held before McMillan J. on August 23, 24 and September 18, 2001.

[11]          The trial judge ordered the husband to pay the wife $206 as an equalization payment.  The husband did not appeal this component of the trial judge’s order.

[12]          The trial judge also ordered the husband to pay the wife spousal support of $800 per month, effective October 1, 2001, but terminating three years later on September 1, 2004.  He reasoned:

In the result, the court finds that the circumstances of this case necessitates a fixed term spousal support order.  The Applicant is only 35 years of age and is capable of many productive years in the work place.  She ought not to be encouraged to continue to simply do nothing with her life.  The Applicant previously had an interest in expanding her education and skills as was demonstrated by her participation in various courses at Sault College over an extended period of time.  As a witness, the Applicant was observed to be engaging, responsive to both counsel and the court, articulate and most presentable.  She is a young woman with a long and potentially productive life ahead of her.  She should be aided and encouraged to strive for economic self‑sufficiency by her family and in particular her mother.

It is recognized that the goal of self‑sufficiency will not be accomplished overnight and that a reasonable and realistic time frame should be established and within which, the Applicant can achieve such independence.

. . .

[The combination of the interim support order and a continuing support order limited to three years means that] the Respondent will have paid to the Applicant spousal support of $800.00 a month for almost five and-a-half years, a sufficient period for the Applicant to have achieved self‑sufficiency.

[13]          The wife appealed the time‑limited component of the trial judge’s order relating to spousal support to the Divisional Court.  The appeal was heard on March 25, 2004.  In reasons released the next day, the Divisional Court allowed the appeal.  Central to its disposition was this conclusion:

After carefully reviewing the transcript of the evidence, we conclude that the learned trial judge failed to consider relevant uncontradicted evidence, and with respect to the allegations in paragraph 22 above appears to have misapprehended the evidence.  In our view the conclusions reached by the trial judge that the wife was not making reasonable efforts is not borne out by a review of the evidence.

[14]          These two identified errors led the Divisional Court to conclude that the time‑limited component of the support order was “not appropriate”.  The court made an order of indefinite support, but added a “review” component to the order.  The court reasoned:

In light of the wife’s age, and taking into account that there are no children of the marriage, a reviewable support order is warranted.  The wife still bears an onus of demonstrating all reasonable efforts to achieve economic independence.  A reviewable support order does not mean that the wife is entitled to indeterminate, lifetime support.

The support order made by the learned trial judge is therefore varied to provide for a review of support entitlement on September 1, 2004.

[15]          The husband appeals the Divisional Court’s order.

C.            ISSUES

[16]          The issues are:

(1)       Did the Divisional Court err by concluding that the trial judge failed to consider relevant uncontradicted evidence and misapprehended evidence?

(2)             If the answer to (1) is in the negative, did the Divisional Court err by ordering spousal support for an indefinite duration, but subject to “review” on a specific date?

D.            ANALYSIS

(1)             The evidence issue

[17]          The Divisional Court held that the trial judge committed two errors in his treatment of the evidence – he failed to consider relevant uncontradicted evidence and he misapprehended evidence.

[18]          The “relevant uncontradicted evidence” that the trial judge ignored, according to the Divisional Court, was the testimony of the wife’s mother concerning her efforts with her daughter to find a job for the wife after she and her husband separated and the wife’s testimony that “I wouldn’t be on his support if I could find a job”.

[19]          With respect, I disagree with this conclusion.  In his reasons, the trial judge specifically referred to “evidence from Mrs. Hulko and the Applicant regarding employment applications submitted by the Applicant as well as her circulation of a resume”.  However, the trial judge also looked at other evidence about the wife’s job search efforts and at other related evidence, including the wife’s testimony about a malady which renders her susceptible to seizures, which was not supported by medical evidence, and the fact that the wife’s application for disability benefits was rejected, before concluding that a time‑limited support order was appropriate. In short, the trial judge did consider the relevant evidence.  He identified, balanced and made conclusions concerning the evidence – precisely what a trial judge is supposed to do.

[20]          Turning to the issue of misapprehension of evidence, the Divisional Court identified a specific set of conclusions (“the allegations in paragraph 22” of the trial judge’s reasons) that, in its view, constituted a misapprehension of the evidence.

[21]          In paragraph 22 of his reasons, the trial judge stated:

Notwithstanding evidence from Mrs. Hulko and the Applicant regarding employment applications submitted by the Applicant as well as her circulation of a resume, (Exhibit No. 8), the court must question the sincerity of the Applicant’s purported commitment to seeking out employment.  For example, the Applicant has not applied to any of several local telemarketing firms recently established in this city, and deposed that she “heard it was really hard” work; and in reference to another newly establishing potential employer, that their business premises were “too far” and “I didn't know what bus to take”.  Further, she has not even applied to a former employer where she did maintain employment as a dispatcher.

[22]          Appellate courts must be very careful about overturning factual findings made by trial judges.  Such a result is warranted only if the trial judge has made a palpable and overriding error in his or her factual findings:  see Housen v. Nikolaisen, [2002] 2 S.C.R. 235, and Waxman v. Waxman (2004), 186 O.A.C. 201.

[23]          Unfortunately, other than stating that it had carefully reviewed the transcript of the evidence, the Divisional Court did not indicate why the various facts stated by the trial judge in paragraph 22 of his reasons were inaccurate, let alone whether they rose to the high level of palpable and overriding error.

[24]          I, too, have read the transcript of the evidence relating to the facts stated by the trial judge in paragraph 22 of his reasons.

[25]          The trial judge’s first factual finding in paragraph 22 was that the wife had not applied to any of the several telemarketing firms recently established in Sault Ste. Marie.  The parties agree that there were three such firms.  Two of them were EDS and RMH.  The husband describes the third firm as the one “up on the hill”; the wife characterizes it as “the one that went bankrupt”.

[26]          When she testified at the trial, the wife had clearly not applied to EDS, although she stated that she intended to do so the day after her testimony.

[27]          The wife’s testimony with respect to RMH was confusing.  It is possible that she applied there but was never called in for an interview.  However, it is also possible that she spoke to several employees at RMH who described the work in such a negative fashion that she decided not to apply.

[28]          The evidence with respect to the third firm was very murky indeed.  It was not identified by name.  It was referred to as the firm “on Wellington”, the firm “up on the hill” and the firm that went bankrupt.  The wife testified that she submitted a resumé to the firm on Wellington but was not called and the firm went bankrupt.  However, she also testified that she did not apply to the firm on the hill because another company, RMH, “was closer to me”.

[29]          The trial judge’s second factual finding in paragraph 22 was that the wife testified, with respect to telemarketing, that she “heard it was really hard” work.  This is accurate:

Q. Who told you that it was really hard?  Mr. Lebel?

A. No, six girls, six people I know.  The one of them left town.  She’s at Thunder Bay now.

Q. So six people you know were working there [at RMH], yes?

A. Yeah.

Q. And they all told you that it was hard.

A. Yeah, they moved.

[30]          The trial judge’s third factual finding in paragraph 22 was that the wife complained that the business premises of another potential employer were “too far” and “I didn’t know what bus to take”.  This is an accurate description of the wife’s testimony relating to the telemarketing firm “up on the hill”.

[31]          The trial judge’s fourth factual finding in paragraph 22 was that the wife had “not even applied to a former employer where she did maintain employment as a dispatcher”.  This is also accurate.  Although the wife applied for an advertised position as a dispatcher at Checker Cab, she did not apply to Union Cab where she had previously worked as a dispatcher.

[32]          My analysis of paragraph 22 of the trial judge’s reasons leads to this conclusion – the trial judge did not misapprehend the evidence he considered in that paragraph, so as to warrant appellate intervention, especially when measured against the strict standards enunciated by the Supreme Court of Canada in Housen and by this court in Waxman.

[33]          For these reasons, I conclude that the Divisional Court erred in determining that the trial judge failed to consider relevant uncontradicted evidence and misapprehended evidence.

[34]          The wife makes an additional argument in support of the Divisional Court’s order.  She submits that the trial judge erred by attaching too much significance to the “promote the economic self‑sufficiency” factor in s. 15.2(6) of the Divorce Act, R.S.C. 1985, c.3 (2nd Supp.).

[35]          I disagree.  Section 15.2(6) of the Divorce Act provides:

15.2(6) An order made under subsection (1)…that provides for the support of a spouse should

(a) recognize any economic advantages or disadvantages to the spouses arising from the marriage or its breakdown;

(b) apportion between the spouses any financial consequences arising from the care of any child of the marriage over and above any obligation for the support of any child of the marriage;

(c) relieve any economic hardship of the spouses arising from the breakdown of the marriage; and

(d) in so far as practicable, promote the economic self-sufficiency of each spouse within a reasonable period of time.

[36]          Since there were no children of the marriage, (b) is not applicable.  In my view, the trial judge properly considered (a) and (c).  For example, both of these factors are addressed in this passage from the trial judge’s reasons:

Here, it cannot be said that the Applicant suffered an economic disadvantage as a result of her marriage of 10 years to the Respondent. Nor can it be said that she enjoyed any significant economic advantage. Simply stated, she was maintained by the Respondent. There was no evidence introduced on behalf of the Applicant to demonstrate that, since separation, she has experienced any notable decline in her lifestyle, which continues to be simplistic [emphasis in original].

[37]          The trial judge also addressed (d).  Indeed, he engaged in a fairly comprehensive analysis of this factor.  He considered the wife’s education, health, employment experience, job search efforts and personality (“engaging … articulate and most presentable”) and concluded that economic self‑sufficiency was an important goal.  On a fair reading of his entire reasons, I cannot say that the trial judge overemphasized this factor.

[38]          In summary, the Divisional Court did not determine that the trial judge erred in his application of the factors in s. 15.2(6) of the Divorce Act.  I do not accept the wife’s submission that the trial judge committed such an error.

(2)            The indefinite support/review issue

[39]          In its reasons, the Divisional Court made a direct linkage between its findings of failure to consider relevant uncontradicted evidence and misapprehension of evidence and its remedy of indefinite, but reviewable, spousal support.  Since I have determined that the Divisional Court erred in its conclusions concerning the trial judge’s findings, it follows that its remedy must also fall.  Accordingly, it is neither necessary nor desirable to address the validity of so‑called “review” orders in this appeal.

E.            DISPOSITION

[40]          I would allow the appeal and reinstate the order of the trial judge.  The appellant is entitled to his costs of this appeal and the appeal to the Divisional Court which I would fix at $2500 in total, inclusive of disbursements and GST.
