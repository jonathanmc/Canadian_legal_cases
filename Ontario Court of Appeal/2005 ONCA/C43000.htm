On appeal from the order of the Divisional Court (Justice R. T. Patrick Gravely, Justice James D. Carnwath and Justice Romain W. M. Pitt) dated October 29, 2004.

GILLESE J.A.:

[1]               The Registrar under the Motor Vehicle Dealers Act, R.S.O. 1990, c. M.42 (the “Act”), purported to revoke the respondents’ motor vehicle dealer licences. By order dated October 29, 2004, the Divisional Court declared the purported revocations to be invalid.

[2]               The Registrar appeals, saying that the revocations were lawful because, under the terms of a consent order issued by the Licence Appeal Tribunal (the “Tribunal”), the motor vehicle dealers had consented to revocation for breach of the terms of the order, without a hearing, and the Registrar determined that a breach had occurred. The Attorney General for Ontario intervenes in support of the appeal. The Used Car Dealers Association of Ontario intervenes in support of the respondents.    

[3]               For the reasons that follow, I would dismiss the appeal.

BACKGROUND

[4]               In Ontario, the motor vehicle industry is regulated in accordance with the provisions of the Act. The Act requires that a person be registered under it in order to carry on business as a motor vehicle dealer. The Registrar administers and enforces the Act.

[5]               The respondents are car dealers registered under the Act. On May 7, 2002, the Registrar proposed to revoke their licences to sell cars by issuing a Notice of Proposal to Revoke Registration under s. 7 of the Act (the “2002 proposal”).

[6]               As required by s. 7(2) of the Act, the 2002 proposal stated that the respondents were entitled to a hearing before the Tribunal.

[7]               On receipt of the 2002 proposal, the respondents requested a hearing by the Tribunal. Before the hearing took place, the respondents, who were represented by legal counsel, negotiated with the Registrar and reached a settlement. The terms of the settlement were attached to, and made part of, a consent order dated September 5, 2002, that was issued by the Tribunal. The consent order stated that, with the consent of the parties, the proceedings were “concluded and disposed of without a hearing on the basis of the terms set out” in the settlement.

[8]               Paragraph 24 of the consent order provided that:

The Applicants agree and acknowledge that should they breach any of the terms and conditions set out herein, in whole or in part, the Registrar is directed to and shall carry out the proposal.

[9]               On April 6, 2004, an inspector from the Registrar’s office visited the respondents’ businesses and issued a report that stated that the respondents were in breach of the consent order.

[10]          On April 30, 2004, without giving the respondents an opportunity to respond to the report, the Registrar sent the respondents a document entitled “Final Notice” in which the Registrar purported to revoke the respondents’ registrations as motor vehicle dealers.

[11]          The respondents brought an application for judicial review. The Divisional Court granted the application on the basis that para. 24 of the consent order amounted to a waiver of any prospective hearing and such a waiver was invalid. Thus, the Divisional Court reasoned, the revocation of the respondents’ car dealers registration based on such a waiver was invalid also.

[12]          The Registrar appeals, saying that the Divisional Court erred in:

(1)             holding that the respondents could not waive their right to a hearing under the Act;

(2)             holding that the consent order was inconsistent with the statutory scheme of the Act and, if not, that the Tribunal did not have jurisdiction to make the consent order; and

(3)             failing to give effect to ss. 4 and 4.1 of the Statutory Powers and Procedure Act, R.S.O. 1990, c. S.22 (the “SPPA”).

[13]          Before analyzing these issues, it is helpful to consider the relevant legislative provisions. 

THE RELEVANT PROVISIONS OF THE ACT

[14]          Persons wishing to sell motor vehicles must apply for registration. Pursuant to ss. 5 and 6(1) of the Act, the Registrar must grant the registration unless the Registrar is of the opinion that the applicants’ financial position or conduct, past or present, disentitles them to registration. Registration can be subject to terms and conditions.

5.(1) An applicant is entitled to registration or renewal of registration by the Registrar except where,

(a)  having regard to the financial position of the applicant, the applicant cannot reasonably be expected to be financially responsible in the conduct of business; or

(b)  the past conduct of the applicant affords reasonable grounds for belief that the applicant will not carry on business in accordance with law and with integrity and honesty; or

(c)  the applicant is a corporation and,

(i)  having regard to its financial position, it cannot reasonably be expected to be financially responsible in the conduct of its business, or

(ii)  the past conduct of its officers or directors affords reasonable grounds for belief that its business will not be carried on in accordance with law and with integrity and honesty; or

(d)  the applicant is carrying on activities that are, or will be, if the applicant is registered, in contravention of this Act or the regulations.

   (2) A registration is subject to such terms and conditions to give effect to the purposes of this Act as are consented to by the applicant, imposed by the Tribunal or prescribed by the regulations. 

6.(1) Subject to section 7, the Registrar may refuse to register an applicant where in the Registrar’s opinion the applicant is disentitled to registration under section 5. 

[15]          Section 6(2) empowers the Registrar to revoke a registration for reasons that would have disentitled the registrant to registration or where the registrant is in breach of a term or condition of the registration.

6.(2) Subject to section 7, the Registrar may refuse to renew or may suspend or revoke a registration for any reason that would disentitle the registrant to registration under section 5 if the registrant were an applicant, or where the registrant is in breach of a term or condition of the registration.

[16]          However, s. 7(1) stipulates that if the Registrar proposes to revoke a registration, the Registrar must serve the registrant with notice of the proposal together with written reasons for the proposal. By virtue of s. 7(2), the notice must state that the registrant is entitled to a hearing by the Tribunal.

[17]          If the registrant does not require a hearing, the Registrar may carry out the proposal -- (s. 7(3)). If, however, the registrant requires a hearing, s. 7(4) stipulates that the Tribunal must schedule one. Section 7(4) also spells out the powers of the Tribunal at the hearing. The Tribunal may direct the Registrar to carry out the proposal or to refrain from carrying it out and to take such action as the Tribunal considers appropriate. The Tribunal may attach terms and conditions to its order or to the registration (s. 7(5)). Section 7(6) provides that the Registrar and the registrant are parties to proceedings before the Tribunal. Section 7 is set out in full below.

7.(1) Where the Registrar proposes to refuse to grant or renew a registration or proposes to suspend or revoke a registration, the Registrar shall serve notice of the proposal, together with written reasons therefor, on the applicant or registrant.

(2) A notice under subsection (1) shall state that the applicant or registrant is entitled to a hearing by the Tribunal if the applicant or registrant mails or delivers, within fifteen days after service of the notice under subsection (1), notice in writing requiring a hearing to the Registrar and the Tribunal, and the applicant or registrant may so require a hearing.

(3) Where an applicant or registrant does not require a hearing by the Tribunal in accordance with subsection (2), the Registrar may carry out the proposal stated in the notice under subsection (1).

(4) Where an applicant or registrant requires a hearing by the Tribunal in accordance with subsection (2), the Tribunal shall appoint a time for and hold the hearing and, on the application of the Registrar at the hearing, may by order direct the Registrar to carry out the Registrar’s proposal or refrain from carrying it out and to take such action as the Tribunal considers the Registrar ought to take in accordance with this Act and the regulations, and for such purposes the Tribunal may substitute its opinion for that of the Registrar.

(5) The Tribunal may attach such terms and conditions to its order or to the registration as it considers proper to give effect to the purposes of this Act. 

(6) The Registrar, the applicant or registrant who has required the hearing and such other persons as the Tribunal may specify are parties to proceedings before the Tribunal under this section. 

THE RIGHT TO A HEARING

[18]          The appellant argues that the Divisional Court erred in concluding that the respondents had a right to a hearing under the Act that could not be waived. The argument runs as follows. Section 7 of the Act does not make it mandatory that a hearing be held before a registration can be revoked; a hearing is required only when a registrant requires one. If a registrant does not require a hearing and allows a proposal to go unchallenged, the Registrar is specifically authorized by s. 7(3) to carry out the proposal. As the Act gives the registrant the right to decide whether to require that a hearing be held, it implicitly gives the registrant the right to waive a hearing after having first required one. Given that the respondents waived their right to a hearing, there was no impediment to the Registrar carrying out his 2002 proposal.  

[19]          This argument is based on the Registrar’s view that the issuance of the consent order did not conclude the 2002 proposal. The Registrar says that the consent order had the effect of continuing the respondents’ registrations, albeit subject to conditions, while holding in abeyance the 2002 proposal.  Thus, the Registrar contends, when the conditions were allegedly breached, the consent order effectively amounted to the Tribunal directing the Registrar to carry out the 2002 proposal. The Attorney General holds a similar view, saying that the conditions in the consent order amounted to putting the respondents on probation.

[20]          I reject both the argument and the view of the consent order upon which it is based. In my view, the consent order concluded the proceedings that the 2002 proposal initiated with the result that paragraph 24 amounted to a waiver of the right to a future hearing, a waiver that is impermissible as a matter of law.    

[21]          Recall the chain of events that led up to the Registrar’s purported revocation of the respondents’ registrations. The Registrar served the 2002 notice of proposal to revoke registration upon the respondents. The respondents responded by requiring a hearing before the Tribunal. The respondents and the Registrar settled the matter. The Tribunal concluded the proceeding by issuing the consent order.    

[22]          The consent order itself recognizes that it disposed of the hearing required by the respondents in respect of the 2002 proposal. The third paragraph of the consent order states:

NOW THEREFORE, upon the consent of the parties, this Tribunal orders that the proceedings in this matter are concluded and disposed of without a hearing on the basis of the terms set out in the said [minutes of settlement] [emphasis added].

[23]          Once the Tribunal issued the consent order in which it “concluded and disposed of” the proceeding, it was functus officio in respect of the 2002 proposal;  the 2002 proposal was spent. Therefore, there was no proposal upon which the Registrar could act to revoke the respondents’ registrations.

[24]          The alleged breach by the respondents of the terms and conditions attached to their registrations pursuant to the consent order provided a basis upon which the Registrar could propose to revoke their registrations, but it did not oust the process mandated by s. 7. If the Registrar formed the opinion that the respondents’ licences ought to be revoked for alleged breaches of the consent order, the Registrar had to comply with s. 7 of the Act. That is, the Registrar had to serve a fresh notice of proposal to revoke registration with written reasons, and afford the respondents the opportunity to require a hearing before the Tribunal. As this court has noted, the Registrar, in attempting to curtail the livelihood of a registrant, must comply strictly with the provisions of s. 7. See Hassan v. Ontario (Registrar of Motor Vehicles) (2001), 140 O.A.C. 106. In Hassan, the Registrar revoked Mr. Hassan’s registration. Mr. Hassan then reapplied for registration and the Registrar refused to process the application on the basis that no information had been provided to establish new or changed circumstances. The Divisional Court found that the Registrar’s refusal to process the application amounted to a breach of procedural fairness. This court dismissed the Registrar’s appeal, holding that the Registrar could not refuse to process the application. The court noted, at para. 11 of its reasons, that the Registrar could propose to reject the application but only if the Registrar complied with the requirements of s. 7 which would have included advising Mr. Hassan that he was entitled to a hearing by the Tribunal.   

[25]          A licensed registrant can, in the face of a proposal for revocation from the Registrar, choose to not require a hearing. However, the registrant’s right to choose is absolute under s. 7. If the Registrar issues a notice of proposal to revoke, the registrant has an absolute right to require a hearing.

[26]          In the case at bar, once the respondents required a hearing pursuant to the 2002 proposal, the proceeding had to be decided by the Tribunal. Once the Tribunal concluded that proceeding, there was no proposal upon which the Registrar could act to revoke the respondents’ registrations. Nothing in the Act gives the Registrar the power to interpret or enforce orders made by the Tribunal. The Registrar’s powers are those bestowed by the legislation – to register in accordance with ss. 5 and 6 of the Act and to enforce the Act in accordance with the dictates of s. 7.      

[27]          The Attorney General relies on Meadowglen Mushroom Growers Ltd. v. Ontario (Assessment Review Board) (1988), 28 O.A.C. 53 (H.C.J.); Byrd v. City of Windsor (1990), 71 D.L.R. (4th) 509 (Ont. H.C.J.) and MacCosham Van Lines (Canada) Co. v. Ontario (Minister of Transportation & Communications) (1988), 66 O.R. (2d) 198 (Div. Ct.) to support his position that the waiver was valid. In my view, those cases are of no assistance as they relate to the waiver of an existing proceeding, not to the waiver of a potential future hearing.

THE VALIDITY OF THE CONSENT ORDER

[28]          The Registrar argues that the Divisional Court erred when it held the consent order to be invalid because the consent order was inconsistent with the scheme of the Act. The Registrar says the Tribunal had the power to: impose conditions on the respondents’ registrations in connection with the 2002 proposal that was before it; specify the consequences of a breach of those conditions; direct the Registrar to carry out the proposal; and, accede to the request of the parties to issue a consent order. Accordingly, the argument runs, the Tribunal could issue the consent order in question and the Registrar could revoke the respondents’ registrations without a hearing.

[29]          I do not agree. In addition to the fact that the respondents could not waive their right to a hearing, as explained above, paragraph 24 of the consent order is inconsistent with the scheme of the Act in two ways: it amounts to an improper delegation of authority and a fettering of discretion.

Improper Delegation of Authority

[30]          By virtue of s. 7(4), because the respondents required a hearing, the Tribunal had to schedule one. Also pursuant to s. 7(4), the Tribunal could dispose of the proceeding in one of two ways. First, it could direct the Registrar to carry out the proposal. Second, it could direct the Registrar to refrain from carrying out the proposal and “take such action as the Tribunal considers the Registrar ought to take … and for such purposes the Tribunal may substitute its opinion for that of the Registrar”.

[31]          On the facts of this case, the Tribunal did not direct the Registrar to carry out the proposal. Therefore, the Tribunal must have been acting in accordance with the second alternative when it issued the consent order. On a plain reading of s. 7(4), the relevant portion of which is quoted in the immediately preceding paragraph, if the Tribunal chooses the second alternative, it is the Tribunal that must determine what action ought to be taken and it is the Tribunal that must direct the Registrar accordingly. It is the Tribunal’s obligation to consider the evidence and make factual determinations; the Registrar is an investigator, not a trier of fact, and the Tribunal owes the Registrar’s opinions no deference. See Ontario (Registrar, Motor Vehicle Dealers Act) v. Shine Car Sales (2003), 169 O.A.C. 77 at para. 10. The effect of paragraph 24, however, is to delegate to the Registrar the power to decide whether there was a breach of a condition of an order of the Tribunal. Delegation of that decision-making is impermissible given the wording and intent of s. 7(4).

Fettering of Discretion 

[32]          Moreover, by stipulating that the Registrar could automatically revoke the respondents’ licences for any breach of the order, without regard to individual circumstances, the Tribunal fettered its discretion. It decided what the consequence of a breach would be without the benefit of a hearing to determine whether the breach had actually occurred and, if so, what the appropriate consequence ought to be. While breach of a term or condition of registration may warrant revocation of registration, that can occur only if the procedure mandated by ss. 6 and 7 of the Act is followed.

THE EFFECT OF THE SPPA

[33]          The Registrar submits that the Divisional Court also erred in failing to give effect to ss. 4 and 4.1 of the SPPA. He contends that s. 4 of the SPPA authorized the parties to waive “any procedural requirement” and that a hearing is a procedural requirement. Section 4 of the SPPA reads as follows:

4. (1) Any procedural requirement of this Act, or of another Act or a regulation that applies to a proceeding, may be waived with the consent of the parties and the tribunal.

[34]          Thus, the argument runs, as the respondents and the Registrar consented to waive any hearing under the Act in the event of a breach of the order, the waiver was valid and the Divisional Court was incorrect when it held to the contrary.  

[35]          Moreover, the Registrar argues, s. 4.1 of the SPPA gave the Tribunal the authority to issue the consent order without a hearing. And, as s. 32 of the SPPA makes explicit, in cases of conflict, its provisions prevail over those in the Act. Sections 4.1 and 32 read as follows:

4.1             If the parties consent, a proceeding may be disposed of by a decision of the tribunal given without a hearing, unless another Act or a regulation that applies to the proceeding provides otherwise.

32. Unless it is expressly provided in any other Act that its  provisions and regulations, rules or by-laws made under it apply despite anything in this Act, the provisions of this Act prevail over the provisions of such other Act and over regulations, rules or by-laws made under such other Act which conflict therewith.

[36]          I do not accept that the SPPA authorized the Registrar to revoke the respondents’ registrations in the manner in which he did. For the reasons already given, if the Registrar wished to revoke the respondents’ licences for breach of the conditions of the consent order, the Registrar had to comply with the requirements of s. 7 of the Act and issue a fresh notice of proposal to revoke registration that included a notification to the respondents of their right to require a hearing. Compliance with s. 7 of the Act is a statutory condition precedent to any exercise of power under s. 7; that is, it is a jurisdictional requirement. See Re Don Howson Chevrolet Oldsmobile Ltd. v. Registrar of Motor Vehicle Dealers and Salesmen (1974), 6 O.R. (2d) 39 (Div. Ct.). The parties could not waive a jurisdictional requirement. Thus, in the circumstances of this case, s. 4 of the SPPA could not give the parties the authority to waive the right to a hearing.   

[37]          The appellant also misconceives the scope of s. 4.1 of the SPPA. Section 4.1 provides that, if the parties consent, a proceeding can be disposed of without a hearing. Section 4.1 applies to an existing proceeding, not a possible future proceeding. Section 4.1 does not authorize the Tribunal to dispose of future proceedings without a hearing nor does it authorize the Tribunal to dispose of a proceeding by delegating its power to another. Thus, while the Tribunal had the power, pursuant to s. 4.1 of the SPPA, to dispose of the 2002 proposal by means of the consent order, s. 4.1 did not authorize the Registrar’s revocation of the respondents’ registrations for the alleged breach of the terms of the consent order.

DISPOSITION

[38]          Accordingly, I would dismiss the appeal with costs to the respondents fixed at $15,000.00, inclusive of disbursements and GST. I would make no award of costs for or against the intervenors.
