COURT OF APPEAL FOR ONTARIO

RE:                              HER MAJESTY THE QUEEN (Respondent) – and  IAN STIRLING (Applicant)

BEFORE:                   FELDMAN J.A. (In Chambers)

                                    Amy Alyea for the respondent Crown

HEARD:                     February 9, 2005

ENDORSEMENT

[1]         On April 8, 2002 the applicant was found Not Criminally Responsible (NCR) on a charge of break and enter with intent. On July 12, 2004 he filed an Inmate Notice of Appeal. He seeks to appeal the NCR disposition on the basis that (a) he was malingering in order to serve what he believed was going to be an “easy ride” in a mental institution, and (b) he always intended to appeal if he were transferred to Penetanguishene from St. Thomas, which he eventually was. As the notice of appeal was filed well beyond the thirty days prescribed by Rule 4 of the Criminal Appeal Rules, 1993, the applicant seeks an order extending the time to file his notice of appeal. If the order is granted, the applicant will also need counsel to assist him in prosecuting the appeal, as counsel before me is only appointed as counsel on this motion.

[2]         The Crown opposes the motion on the basis that the proposed appeal is without merit.

[3]         The applicant was born February 20, 1978 and is now twenty-seven years old. He was arrested on November 16, 2001. He had been in attendance at a home along with two other men, one of whom was attempting to enter the home in order to fight with a woman there. The applicant and the third man were present but took no part in the attempt, and they eventually ran away.

[4]         The applicant was originally sent for assessment to determine his fitness to stand trial as well as his criminal responsibility. Two reports were prepared by Dr. Komer. In the first, he determined that the applicant was unfit to stand trial. In the second, he concluded that the applicant was fit to stand trial but was not criminally responsible as a result of mental disorder at the time of the commission of the offence. On April 8, 2002 the applicant elected to be tried by a provincial court judge, pled not guilty to the charge and, following the evidence presented by Dr. Komer and based on the submissions of Crown and defence counsel, was found not criminally responsible. Dr. Komer testified that the applicant suffered from schizophrenia as the “main problem” with regard to the offence, although he also suffered from personality disorder, substance abuse and possible sadistic paraphilia. When asked how long ago the applicant had been diagnosed with schizophrenia, the doctor stated that the applicant had been diagnosed as having a major mental disorder for nine years. The trial judge found that the applicant committed the offence of break and enter as a party to the offence. He also found that at the time the applicant was suffering from a mental disorder that made him NCR, in accordance with s. 16 of the Criminal Code.

[5]         Shortly before the disposition, the applicant told Dr. Komer that during his assessment he had lied about being involved in satanic cults and sacrifices in order to be found NCR and to get “an easy ride,” but that he wanted to serve his time where he was, the Mental Health Center at St. Thomas. Shortly after the disposition, he told Dr. Komer that he would appeal the NCR finding if it were recommended that he be transferred to Penetanguishene. He has maintained this desire from time to time throughout the period of his detention. Ultimately, the applicant was transferred to Penetanguishene. However, he was never assisted with filing an inmate appeal until he spoke to legal counsel who assisted him on his Review Board hearings. Even then, Legal Aid turned down his request for legal assistance to pursue an appeal, and counsel appearing today was only appointed by order of this court.

[6]         The applicant has told several of his treating and assessing physicians and other professionals that he lied about his actions and symptoms in order to be found NCR. Several have noted as part of his diagnosis that he was malingering. Importantly, beginning in the 2003 Administrator’s Annual Report and Recommendation, the applicant’s diagnosis did not include schizophrenia, and the report notes that “[n]o symptoms of major mental disorder have been evident and he receives no psychotropic medication.” Furthermore, the clinical team concluded that since the applicant did not suffer from a major mental disorder, they were supportive of his wish to challenge his NCR status. However, for Review Board purposes, they recommended that there be no change to his current disposition because with his personality disorder and other problems, he continued to represent a significant risk to the safety of the public. The most recent decision of the Review Board confirms that the applicant remains a significant risk to the public and he remains at the Oak Ridge facility at Penetanguishene.

[7]         In R. v. Menear (2002), 162 C.C.C. (3d) 233 (C.A.), this court reviewed, at paras. 20 and 21, the factors that apply to the exercise of its discretion to grant an extension of time to appeal. The three main factors are: (a) whether the applicant has shown a bona fide intention to appeal within the appeal period; (b) whether the applicant has accounted for or explained the delay; and (c) whether there is merit to the proposed appeal. Further, depending on the case, the court may also consider whether the consequences of the conviction are out of all proportion to the penalty imposed, whether the Crown will be prejudiced, and whether the applicant has taken the benefit of the judgment. Above all, the main consideration is whether the applicant has demonstrated that justice requires that an extension of time be granted.

[8]         Dealing with the first two factors, it is obviously very difficult for this court to assess the applicant’s bona fides in terms of his intent to appeal. However, on its face, he has expressed that intent, conditional on where he was to be placed within the mental health system, from the outset. In those circumstances, he has shown a sufficient intent to make a timely appeal. He has also provided a satisfactory explanation for the delay in carrying out his intent, both in terms of his own decision and in terms of obtaining assistance to facilitate the necessary paperwork.

[9]         The third factor is the potential merit of an appeal under s. 675 of the Code. The issue is whether the trial court erred in finding the applicant NCR, the basis of the error being that he manipulated the psychiatrist into believing that he suffered from a major mental disorder, namely, schizophrenia. In considering whether the trial judge erred, I note that at that time, both the Crown and defence were asking the court for this disposition.

[10]          I approach this issue only on a facial reading of the record before the court. This court is not in a position to second-guess Dr. Komer’s assessment or conclusions on this record and without a full hearing. Dr. Komer was alert to the fact that the applicant claimed that he was malingering and he appeared to factor that into his original assessment. However, there is a basis to question the diagnosis of schizophrenia, given both the subsequent findings of the applicant’s professional caregivers and assessors that he was not suffering from a major mental disorder, and their consequent support for his appeal. There was also a previous incident in 1999 where the professionals declined to diagnose the applicant as NCR, although one psychiatrist did diagnose him as schizophrenic in that year.

[11]          The mental health evidence on the record is somewhat confusing, in that the applicant has been consistently diagnosed as having a personality disorder that is causing him to continue to be a significantly dangerous person. Yet that diagnosis does not appear to qualify as a major mental disorder. One thing that is unclear is whether that diagnosis alone would have been sufficient to ground the original NCR finding without the schizophrenia diagnosis.

[12]          In my view, there is enough on the record in this case to suggest that there is potential merit in an appeal. I also note that the consequences of being found not criminally responsible are very significant for any accused because, unlike the case of a definite sentence, there is no time limit on the sentence. The applicant’s involvement in the index offence appears to have been minor, yet he is in indefinite incarceration within the mental health system. Although he remains a dangerous person, who is hopefully benefiting from his current placement, if he is not legally in the right place, it is in the interests of justice that an extension of time for an appeal be granted.

[13]          In the circumstances, the applicant will require legal assistance to perfect and argue the appeal. If the applicant is unable to obtain a legal aid certificate, he can apply to this court for assistance.

[14]          The motion is granted. The time for filing a Notice of Appeal is extended to July 12, 2005.

[15]          I thank both counsel for their assistance on this unusual matter.
