On appeal from the conviction entered by Mr. Justice John McIsaac of The Superior Court of Justice, sitting with a jury, dated September 27, 2003, and on appeal from the sentence imposed by Mr. Justice John McIsaac dated November 4, 2003.

SHARPE J.A.:

[1]               The appellant was tried for attempted murder before a judge and jury.  Ajith Jeyanandakumar and Rajuran Rajagopal, his two co-accused, were charged with aggravated assault of the same victims.  The charges all arose from a gang attack.  Identity was the central issue at trial for all three accused.  The appellant was acquitted of attempted murder but convicted of the included offence of aggravated assault.  Rajagopal, who offered an alibi defence, was acquitted while Jeyanandakumar was convicted of aggravated assault. Jeyanandakumar’s appeal from conviction and sentence was heard together with this appeal and the reasons for judgment in that appeal are being released together with these reasons.

Facts

[2]               On June 16, 2002, a gang of young men wielding swords, axes and machetes attacked two men in a parked car.  Chanthuru Perinpanathan, the passenger and the apparent target of the attack, and the driver, Muraly Balasingam, both sustained serious injuries.

[3]               Perinpanathan testified that a man opened the passenger-side door and kicked him. Perinpanathan testified that he knew the appellant, and that the appellant was not the man who first attacked him.  Perinpanathan lost consciousness in the middle of the attack and could not describe how it ended.

[4]               Balasingam testified that the appellant was the man who first attacked Perinpanathan.  According to Balasingam, the appellant initially kicked Perinpanathan and then attacked him with a long dagger while standing outside the open passenger door.  Balasingam also testified that he later saw the appellant standing at the rear of the car yelling encouragement to the other attackers.

[5]               Balasingam told the police officer who arrived at the scene shortly after the attack that he knew the men who did this and they were “brand new” members of the “Gilder Boys” gang.  At trial, Balasingam acknowledged that the appellant was not a “brand new Gilder Boy” but rather a former member of that gang.

[6]               The Crown’s case rested almost entirely on Balasingam’s evidence and there was no forensic evidence linking the appellant to the crime. Balasingam’s credibility was the subject of a prolonged attack at trial.  Balasingam has a lengthy criminal record including offences of dishonesty.  There were numerous inconsistencies in his various accounts of the attack and the appellant’s involvement.  His evidence relating to the details of his own criminal record and past gang membership was far from candid and on cross-examination he was confronted with transcripts and court records that contradicted his sanitized account of his past misdeeds.  Jeyanandakumar’s uncle, Father Virgil, a Roman Catholic priest, testified that he heard Balasingam offer not to testify if he was paid a bribe.  Balasingam denied this.

[7]               The Crown placed significant reliance on evidence of the appellant’s post-offence conduct. Perinpanathan and Balasingam both testified that appellant telephoned them the before the trial.

[8]               Perinpanathan testified that the appellant called him midway through the preliminary inquiry, after Perinpanathan had already testified that the appellant was not the first assailant.  According to Perinpanathan, the appellant asked him what else he was going to say on the stand.  The appellant also asked Perinpanathan why Balasingam said that he was involved and why Balasingam had “told on” the accused.  The appellant asked Perinpanathan to ask Balasingam not to “come and tell on them”.  Perinpanathan had been charged with attempted murder arising from an earlier incident involving a friend of the appellant and, according to Perinpanathan, the appellant warned that he would make Perinpanathan ’s case “harder” and he would put Perinpanathan  “inside” if Perinpanathan implicated the appellant. Perinpanathan reported the call to the authorities the next day.

[9]               Balasingam testified the appellant had called him from jail to apologize and to ask Balasingam not to testify against him.  According the Balasingam, the appellant explained that he “came for” Perinpanathan with whom he had a “beef”, not to attack Balasingam.  Balasingam also said that the appellant told him that he was “pissed off” about having been charged earlier with assault on a man named Mano.  There was some suggestion that Balasingam had prompted Mano to go to the police.  However, in cross-examination Balasingam admitted that the appellant said he wasn’t upset, but rather it was the appellant’s friends who were upset.  Balasingam was asked on cross-examination if he knew that the appellant had been acquitted of the Mano assault.  He said he did not know.  The trial judge refused to allow the appellant’s trial counsel to pursue this issue by confronting Balasingam with the appellant’s criminal record which contained no entry for the Mano assault.

[10]          The Crown also relied on evidence regarding a possible motive for the attack. Perinpanathan had been charged with assault with a weapon on the appellant six months before the assault.  Those charges were ultimately dropped and Perinpanathan entered into a peace bond.  Two days before the attack, Perinpanathan was charged with attempted murder in respect of a complaint by friends of the appellant.  After the assault the appellant allegedly told Balasingam: “[t] hat’s what you get for chilling with us and backing them.”

[11]          The appellant did not testify.

[12]          The appellant was subsequently charged with attempting to obstruct justice based on Balasingam’s allegations about the telephone call from the jail.  In October 2004, after the trial in the case on appeal, the appellant was acquitted on the attempt to obstruct justice charge after the judge heard essentially the same evidence from Balasingam that was adduced at the trial for attempted murder.  The transcript and judgment from the attempt to obstruct justice trial are the subject of a fresh evidence application.  The appellant argues that his acquittal renders inadmissible evidence of the telephone call to Balasingam and that a new trial on the aggravated assault must be ordered.

Issues

[13]          The appellant raises the following issues:

1. Did the trial judge fail to outline the position of the defence in his jury charge?

2. Did the trial judge err by admitting a statement attributed to the appellant admitting prior misconduct and by excluding evidence capable of dispelling the prejudice?

3. Did the trial judge err in ordering that potential jurors be identified by number only and not by name?

4. Should the appellant’s subsequent acquittal for attempting to obstruct justice be admitted as fresh evidence, and if admitted, does the acquittal render inadmissible the evidence of the telephone call to Balasingam?

Analysis

1.      Did the trial judge fail to outline the position of the defence in his jury charge?

[14]          The trial judge’s instructions to the jury were brief. He did not review the evidence at length.  The instruction occupies a little over fifty pages of transcript and appears to have taken just over one hour to deliver.  This is commendable after a fifteen-day trial involving three accused.  There is much to be said for a concise jury instruction that sets out the issues in plain and simple terms, focusing on the key points and avoiding the obscurity that can be the product of excessive detail and a pointless witness-by-witness recitation of all the evidence: see R .v. Jacquard (1997), 113 C.C.C. (3d) 1 at paras. 1 and 13 (S.C.C.), per Lamer C.J.: “[w] e must strive to avoid the proliferation of very lengthy charges.…I cannot emphasize enough that the role of a trial judge in charging the jury is to decant and simplify.”

[15]          As has been frequently stated, there is no simple template that can be uniformly applied when instructing a jury and no simple formula by which an appellate court can assess the adequacy of a jury charge.  Trial judges must be given considerable latitude in fashioning a jury charge that is appropriate to the particular circumstances of the case. When challenged on appeal, the charge must be read as a whole, not dissected under a microscope and, as Cory J. commented in R. v. Cooper (1993), 78 C.C.C. (3d) 289 at 301 (S.C.C.), appellate courts must be mindful that, “it would be difficult if not impossible to find a perfect charge”.

[16]          The jurisprudence mandates a functional approach whereby the jury instruction is assessed pragmatically with a view to determining whether, “at the end of the day…[the] appellate court is satisfied that the jurors would adequately understand the issues involved, the law relating to the charge the accused is facing, and the evidence they should consider in resolving the issues”: Cooper, supra at 301.  In R. v. MacKinnon (1999), 132 C.C.C. (3d) 545 at para. 27 (Ont. C.A.), Doherty J.A. provided the following helpful description of the fundamental elements to be considered when applying this functional approach:

By the end of the instructions…the jury must understand:

·        the factual issues which had to be resolved;

·        the law to be applied to those issues and the evidence;

·        the positions of the parties; and

·        the evidence relevant to the positions taken by the parties on the various issues.

[17]          The appellant submits that the trial judge’s instructions to the jury failed to provide adequate guidance as to the appellant’s position and the evidence relevant to that position.  Specifically, the appellant alleges that:

(i) the trial judge failed to explain the “second pillar” of the defence, namely, that Perinpanathan’s evidence exculpated the appellant;

(ii) the trial judge erred by instructing the jury that “gang rivalry” was a possible motive without also charging the jury that the same evidence suggested the appellant had no motive to commit the offence;

(iii) the trial judge erred by failing to instruct the jury as to a possible innocent explanation for the “after-the-fact” conduct.

(i) The “second pillar” of the defence

[18]          The defence at trial rested on two propositions.  The first was what trial counsel labelled as the “negative” proposition that Balasingam was not a credible witness and that his identification of the appellant as Perinpanathan’s attacker should not be believed.  The second was the “positive” proposition that Perinpanathan’s evidence positively excluded the appellant as the first attacker.

[19]          The trial judge’s entire outline of the appellant’s position consisted of the following:

[The appellant’s trial counsel], on behalf of her client, suggests to you in the strongest terms, that Mr. Balasingam is just not capable of belief.  He’s totally incredible. He’s totally unreliable because of the circumstances of his record, including the obstruct justice, because of Father Virgil about this attempt to corrupt the trial by seeking money to forget his evidence, and because of the numerous inconsistencies during the progression of this investigation and prosecution.

[20]          The appellant submits that the trial judge erred by mentioning only Balasingam’s lack of credibility and failing to make any reference to the significance of Perinpanathan’s exculpatory evidence.  Trial counsel objected to this omission, but the trial judge refused to recharge on the issue.

[21]          The appellant argues that the problem was compounded by the fact that, apart from Rajagopal’s alibi, the trial judge more or less equated the appellant’s position with that of the other two accused. The appellant says that his position differed significantly as Perinpanathan’s evidence was positive evidence of his innocence, a point not available to his co-accused.  The appellant argues that by leaving the jury with the impression that all three co-accused took the same position, the trial judge obscured and minimized the significance of Perinpanathan’s exculpatory evidence.

[22]          An adequate explanation of the positions of the parties is routinely cited as one of the essential elements of a proper jury charge: MacKinnon, supra. The statement of Taschereau J. in Azoulay v. The Queen, [1952] S.C.R. 495 at 497-498 has been frequently cited on this point:

[t]he rule which has been laid down, and consistently followed is that in a jury trial the presiding judge must, except in rare cases where it would be needless to do so, review the substantial parts of the evidence, and give the jury the theory of the defence, so that they may appreciate the value and effect of that evidence, and how the law is to be applied to the facts as they find them.

[23]          Perinpanathan’s evidence that he knew the appellant and that the appellant was not the man who first attacked him was inconsistent with Balasingam’s evidence that the appellant was Perinpanathan’s initial attacker. In my view, Perinpanathan’s evidence was central to appellant’s defence and the position of the appellant could not fairly be stated without some mention of it.  The trial judge should have referred to it when outlining the appellant’s position to the jury.

[24]          The respondent points out that the trial judge did not ignore Perinpanathan’s evidence that the appellant was not the first attacker.  When instructing the jury with respect to Balasingam’s eyewitness identification of the appellant as Perinpanathan’s attacker, the trial judge stated: “Now, another thing that impacts upon his identification is the fact that Mr. Perinpanathan has said that that was not Mr. Mahalingan who came in – who was the first to come in and the wave of individuals who went after him”.  That, however, is the only reference to Perinpanathan’s exculpatory evidence.

[25]          The trial judge’s brief reference to Perinpanathan’s evidence in connection with his standard caution as to eyewitness identification evidence was, in my view, insufficient to bring home to the jury the essence of the appellant’s defence.  The appellant’s position was not that Balasingam’s evidence should be rejected because of the frailties associated with eyewitness identification evidence.  The appellant’s position was that Balasingam was a liar and that Perinpanathan’s evidence positively exculpated the appellant as the perpetrator of this attack.

[26]          The jury had, of course, heard the appellant’s trial counsel’s closing address, in which she stressed the importance of Perinpanathan’s evidence.  However, counsels’ closing addresses do not absolve the trial judge of the duty to present the jury with a fair and dispassionate summary of the positions of the parties, especially where the position of the Crown is set out but that of the accused is not.  The failure to mention the second pillar of the defence, when combined with the trial judge’s instruction to the jury that the appellant’s position was the same as that of his co-accused, had the effect of at best ignoring, and at worst undercutting a significant element of the appellant’s defence before the jury.

[27]          I hasten to add that there was no need for a lengthy explanation or for a detailed review of Perinpanathan’s evidence.  The task of ensuring that the jury understood the appellant’s position and the evidence relevant to that position could have been accomplished with the same admirable economy demonstrated by the trial judge in the balance of his charge and need not have added significantly to its length.  

[28]          In my view, the trial judge’s failure to explain to the jury the appellant’s position arising from Perinpanathan’s evidence constitutes an error of law. 

(ii) Gang rivalry as a possible motive

[29]          There was evidence that the appellant was a member of the Balasingam’s former gang and that Perinpanathan was friendly with that gang.  By contrast, the other two accused were alleged to be members of rival gangs.  The Crown and defence each sought to rely on gang rivalry or association. The Crown argued that the evidence of gang rivalry provided a motive, while the appellant argued that, so far as he was concerned, the evidence that he was a member of a gang friendly to both Balasingam and Perinpanathan indicated an absence of motive. 

[30]          The trial judge instructed the jury: “gang rivalry could be viewed as a motive for the defendants to commit the offences charged”.  The trial judge noted that both motive and the absence of motive should be considered.  However, he then stated that evidence of gang membership “had been advanced for the limited purpose of potentially explaining why this attack took place and why Mr. Balasingam and Mr. Perinpanathan were targets”. The appellant complains that the trial judge provided the jury with only the Crown’s position with respect to this evidence and not with his position that the evidence tended to exculpate him.

[31]          The appellant’s trial counsel objected to the gang rivalry as motive instruction on the ground that gang association could be taken to demonstrate a lack of motive on the part of her client.  The trial judge refused to recharge on this issue.

[32]          In R. v. Lewis (1979), 47 C.C.C. (2d) 24 at 36 (S.C.C.), the Supreme Court of Canada stated that, “[p]roved absence of motive is always an important fact in favour of the accused and ordinarily worthy of note in a charge to the jury”.  The evidence here falls short of a “proved absence of motive” but it remains the case that it would have been open to the jury to conclude that the evidence of gang associations tended to exclude the appellant as one of the attackers. 

[33]          In my view, having instructed the jury that gang rivalry could be viewed as a motive for the attack, the trial judge should also have explained, however briefly, the appellant’s position with respect to the significance of gang association and left it open to the jury to find that the evidence of gang rivalry could be exculpatory insofar as the appellant was concerned.

(iii) Post-offence conduct

[34]          The trial judge instructed the jury that the calls the appellant made to Perinpanathan and Balasingam were after-the-fact conduct from which an inference of guilt could be drawn.  The trial judge added the standard form qualification:

[t]he words may suggest that the person committed the crime. On the other hand, the words may be those of an innocent person who simply wants to avoid involvement in an investigation or prosecution, or embarrassment for himself or others.

[35]          Trial counsel objected that the “boiler plate” instruction was inadequate as it assumed that the post-offence conduct occurred before the accused was charged, not afterwards.  There was a possible explanation for the call to Perinpanathan consistent with the appellant’s innocence, namely that the call was motivated by stress, panic and confusion.  Perinpanathan had already given evidence at the preliminary inquiry exculpating the appellant and the appellant had no reason to try to influence or dissuade Perinpanathan.

[36]          I agree with the respondent that as the appellant did not testify, it is difficult to fault the trial judge for simply giving the standard instruction without trying to find an innocent explanation for the call.  The explanation that the appellant says the trial judge should have given is speculative and hardly compelling.  Perinpanathan had not completed his evidence, and the appellant could have been worried about what else Perinpanathan might say, not to mention the fact that Perinpanathan testified that the appellant also sought information on Balasingam and urged Perinpanathan to convince Balasingam not to testify against him. I would not give effect to this ground of appeal.

(iv) Conclusion: adequacy of the jury charge

[37]          The trial judge’s failure to instruct the jury on the available competing inferences the jury could draw from the evidence of gang rivalry may not have been sufficient to warrant a new trial. However, it must be considered together with the trial judge’s failure to present the jury with an adequate explanation of the appellant’s position on the significance of Perinpanathan’s exculpatory evidence.  In my view, read as a whole, the instructions to the jury failed to present jury with the position of the defence and this was an error of sufficient gravity to require this court to set aside the conviction and order a new trial.

[38]          While this is sufficient to dispose of the appeal, for the sake of completeness, I offer the following comments on the remaining issues.

2.      Did the trial judge err by admitting a statement attributed to the appellant admitting prior misconduct and by excluding evidence capable of dispelling the prejudice?

[39]          The appellant argues that the trial judge erred in admitting a portion of the phone call between the appellant and Balasingam into evidence.  The contested statement allegedly made by the appellant is that he was “pissed off” at Balasingam because, on an earlier occasion, Balasingam had encouraged a man named Mano to go to the police, causing the appellant to be charged with assault.  The trial judge held that this statement was potentially relevant to the appellant’s motive and therefore refused to exclude this aspect of Balasingam’s evidence as to the phone call.  

[40]          In an attempt to eliminate the prejudice this statement would occasion, the appellant’s trial counsel asked Balasingam on cross-examination whether he was aware that the appellant had been acquitted of the Mano assault. Balasingam answered that he was not, and the appellant’s sought to cross-examine Balasingam by using the appellant’s record to show the acquittal.  The trial judge ruled that the record could not be introduced in this manner.

[41]          The appellant argues that the trial judge erred by admitting the statement as to the Mano charges.  The appellant told Balasingam that he harboured no bad feelings towards him and, submits the appellant, the statement therefore could have no probative value in respect of the motive.  In addition, any probative value was outweighed by the prejudice the statement would cause.  The appellant also submits that a stronger limiting instruction should have been given.  Finally, the appellant argues that the refusal to allow trial counsel to cross examine Balasingam on the appellant’s criminal record was an error

[42]          I am unable to accept these submissions.

[43]          Even if the appellant told Balasingam that he harboured him no ill-will, the fact remains that Balasingam was implicated in the laying of the assault charge.  It was open to the trial judge to conclude as he did that the Mano evidence was “possibly an explanation of why this attack took place either corporately or individually and accordingly it relates to motive”.  The trial judge’s weighing of probative value and prejudicial effect is entitled to deference in this court.

[44]          In keeping with his effort to deliver a concise charge, the trial judge did not give a limiting instruction specifically mentioning the Mano evidence.  However, he did give a general instruction that the verdict must be based upon the evidence and not upon evidence of prior discreditable conduct of gang association and in my view, that instruction was sufficient to overcome any potential prejudice to the appellant

[45]          Nor do I accept the submission that the trial judge erred in refusing to allow the appellant’s trial counsel to cross-examine Balasingam on the appellant’s record.  I reject this submission for two reasons. First, the fact of the acquittal would not affect the motive theory as the appellant was charged as a result of Balasingam’s actions and that, in itself, could provide a possible motive for the attack.  Second, even if the appellant were entitled to prove the fact of his acquittal, having Balasingam interpret his criminal record was plainly not the way to accomplish that end. I would not interfere with the trial judge’s refusal to allow the appellant’s trial counsel to put the appellant’s criminal record to Balasingam in cross examination in order to establish that the appellant was acquitted of the Mano assault. 

3.      Did the trial judge err in ordering that potential jurors be identified by number only and not by name?

[46]          Sections 631(3) and (3.1) of the Criminal Code provide:

631.  (3) The clerk of the court shall, in open court, draw out the cards referred to in subsection (1), one after another, and shall call out the name and number on each card as it is drawn, until the number of persons who have answered is, in the opinion of the judge, sufficient to provide a full jury and any alternate jurors ordered by the judge after allowing for orders to excuse, challenges and directions to stand by where

(a) the array of jurors is not challenged; or

(b) the array of jurors is challenged but the judge does not direct a new panel to be returned.

(3.1) On application by the prosecutor or on its own motion, the court, or a judge of the court, before which the jury trial is to be held, if it is satisfied that it is in the best interest of the administration of justice to do so, including in order to protect the privacy or safety of the members of the jury and alternate jurors, may order that, for the purposes of subsection (3), the clerk of the court shall only call out the number on each card.

[47]          The trial judge invited submissions as to whether prospective jury members should be called by number only rather than by name.  Defence counsel opposed the procedure, submitting that it was unnecessary and prejudicial on the ground that the jury might infer that there was a need for their protection, leading to “a rather pernicious potential inference against these accused”.  She submitted that the order could only be made on the basis of an evidential record establishing the need for adopting the procedure.  Crown counsel did not take a position, but “as a sort of amicus curiae” submitted that it was open to the trial judge to invoke the procedure.  No evidence was called.

[48]          The trial judge ruled, “for formal reasons to be filed”, that “[d]uring jury selection and the trial, the members of the panel and eventually the jury will be referred to by their number only, along with their occupation”.  No further reasons for the order were given.

[49]          The appellant submits that the trial judge improperly exercised his discretion in ordering jury anonymity without evidence that such an order was required and without reasons explaining why it was made.  The appellant suggests that he does not have to establish prejudice stemming from this error, and that, in any event, the error did cause prejudice because the jury may have concluded that something particular to these defendants made the court take special measures for their protection.

[50]          I would not give effect to this ground of appeal.  It certainly would have been preferable had the trial judge given reasons setting out the factual and legal considerations that led him to make the order.  However, this is a discretionary matter and I am not persuaded that, in all the circumstances, the trial judge erred in principle in making the order for juror anonymity.

[51]          I agree that there must be a factual foundation to justify an order under s. 631(3.1). While evidence was not called on the issue, the trial judge did not make this order in the absence of a factual background.  He had heard several pre-trial motions and was apprised of the general circumstances of the offences.  He would have known that the case involved a very serious attack, that there were allegations of gang affiliation as well as allegations of witness tampering.  In my view, this provided a sufficient record for concluding that it was “in the best interest of the administration of justice” to make the order with a view to protecting the privacy or safety of the jurors.  While there was some risk of prejudice, it was open to the trial judge to conclude that the risk of harm to the integrity of the trial process and jury safety was more substantial and that the risk of prejudice to the appellant was one that could be managed through the usual safeguards of appropriate instructions to the jury regarding their duty to render a verdict according to the evidence and according to law.  I would therefore dismiss this ground of appeal.

4.      Should the appellant’s subsequent acquittal for attempting to obstruct justice be admitted as fresh evidence, and if admitted, does the acquittal render inadmissible the evidence of the telephone call to Balasingam?

[52]          Following the trial leading to the conviction under appeal (the “first trial”), the appellant was tried and acquitted of attempting to obstruct justice.  That charge was based upon the alleged telephone call in which the appellant is said to have attempted to dissuade Balasingam from testifying against the appellant at the first trial.  The evidence of this telephone call in the obstruct justice trial was more or less the same as that admitted at the first trial.  In the first trial, this evidence was left with the jury as post-offence conduct from which an inference of guilt could be drawn.  The appellant submits that his subsequent acquittal on the obstruct justice charge conclusively determines his innocence of attempting to obstruct justice and, consequently, the admission of the phone call evidence at the first trial was in error.  For the following reasons, I conclude that on the state of the authorities binding upon this court, the subsequent acquittal has the effect claimed by the appellant and that a new trial is required.

[53]          The seminal case in this area is Grdic v. The Queen (1985), 19 C.C.C. (3d) 289 at 293-294 (S.C.C.), where Lamer J., writing for the majority, stated:

There are not different kinds of acquittals and, on that point, I share the view that “As a matter of fundamental policy in the administration of the criminal law it must be accepted by the Crown in a subsequent criminal proceeding that an acquittal is the equivalent to a finding of innocence”: see Martin L. Friedland, Double Jeopardy (1969), Clarendon Press, Oxford, p. 129…To reach behind the acquittal, to qualify it, is, in effect, to introduce the verdict of “not proven”, which is not, has never been and should not be part of our law.

…

…any issue, the resolution of which had to be in favour of the accused as a prerequisite to the acquittal, is irrevocably deemed to have been found conclusively in favour of the accused…This is so even though the judgment might well be the result of a reasonable doubt on that issue, and even when the judge has said so or expressed views that indicate clearly that his finding, though inuring to the benefit of the accused, had been arrived at with reluctance and the judge has suggested that it is not conclusively in favour of the accused.

See also R. v. Grant (1991), 67 C.C.C. (3d) 268 at 279 (S.C.C.).

[54]          The Grdic issue estoppel principle has been applied to preclude the admission of evidence underlying an acquittal as similar fact evidence.  Where an accused has been acquitted of an offence, with certain exceptions that I will mention below but that do not apply here, the Crown may not lead evidence as to the circumstances of that offence as similar fact evidence at a subsequent trial for a different offence: see R. v. Cullen (1989), 52 C.C.C. (3d) 459 (Ont. C.A.); R. v. M. (A.R.) (1994), 94 C.C.C. (3d) 459 (Man. C.A.), application for leave to appeal dismissed, [1994] S.C.C.A. No. 544; R. v. S.(P.) (2000), 144 C.C.C. (3d) 120 (Ont. C.A.), application for leave to appeal dismissed, [2000] S.C.C.A. No. 486; R. v. Akins (2002), 164 C.C.C. (3d) 289 (Ont. C.A.).  Three decisions of this court have applied issue estoppel even where the acquittal follows the trial at which the similar fact evidence was led: R. v. G. (K.R.) (1991), 68 C.C.C. (3d) 268 (Ont. C.A.); R. v. Verney (1993), 87 C.C.C. (3d) 363 (Ont. C.A.); R. v. Rulli (1999), 134 C.C.C. (3d) 465 (Ont. C.A.), application for leave to appeal dismissed, [1999] S.C.C.A. No. 284.  In these cases, the similar fact evidence was, in effect, retrospectively rendered inadmissible and new trials were ordered.

[55]          Although there appears to be no authority dealing with the Grdic principle in relation to after-the-fact conduct, I agree with the appellant that no meaningful distinction can be drawn between post-offence conduct evidence and similar fact evidence for these purposes.  Both are circumstantial evidence of the accused’s extrinsic discreditable conduct from which an inference of guilt may be drawn. 

[56]          In R. v. Arp (1998), 129 C.C.C. (3d) 321 (S.C.C.), Cory J. considered the application of issue estoppel to trials on multiple count indictments where evidence is admissible across the counts as similar fact evidence.  If the accused is acquitted of one count, what effect does issue estoppel have on a conviction on another count?  Citing R. v. Cullen, R. v. M. (A.R.) and R. v. Verney, supra with apparent approval, Cory J. stated at para. 78, that restricting the use of evidence underlying an acquittal rested on the Grdic principle, “fundamental to our system of justice…that an accused need not repeatedly defend himself against the same allegations.”

[57]          Cory J. qualified the application of issue estoppel to limit the use of similar fact evidence on the basis that “in certain circumstances, the fact of an accused’s prior acquittal may have relevance to an ultimate issue in a subsequent trial”.  The first example was the case of R. v. Ollis, [1900] 2 Q.B. 758 where the accused was charged with false pretences on the basis of obtaining goods in exchange for a cheque that was later dishonoured.  His defence was that he lacked mens rea as he expected to receive funds to cover the cheque.  Evidence was led of a similar incident in which he had raised the same defence and been acquitted.  Evidence of the prior incident was admissible despite the acquittal as, even if the accused was innocent of the first charge, the very fact that he had been charged went to his knowledge of wrongdoing and undermined his explanation for the second offence. Cory J. clearly regarded this as an exception to the rule: “[y]et, in most situations, it will be unfair and inappropriate to admit the evidence underlying the prior acquittal as similar fact evidence in a subsequent trial of the same accused”.  I do not see how the circumstances of the present case can be brought within the Ollis exception.

[58]          The second circumstance described by Cory J. (at para. 79) where the Grdic principle does not apply is that of multiple count indictments:

I cannot accept the proposition that the principle set out in Grdic, supra, applies to verdicts rendered by the same trier of fact in respect of charges tried together in a single proceeding. There is nothing unfair or logically irreconcilable about a jury having reasonable doubt whether the accused committed an act while also finding that it is likely that he committed it. There may very well be good reasons to exclude similar fact evidence underlying a prior acquittal in a subsequent proceeding. However, the principle has no application where the alleged similar acts are the subject of a multi-count indictment.

[59]          I agree with Blair J.A. and with Lee Steusser, “Admitting Acquittals as Similar Fact Evidence” (2002), 45 Crim. L.Q. 488 at 497 that the principle that there is “nothing unfair or logically irreconcilable about a jury having reasonable doubt whether the accused committed an act while also finding that it is likely that he committed it” could equally be applied to acquittals in prior or subsequent trials.  I do not agree, however, that Arp goes that far. The similar fact decisions of this court and other provincial appellate courts applying the Grdic principle to acquittals in prior or subsequent trials were cited in Arp, yet Cory J. carved out a specific exception applicable to multiple count indictments.

[60]          In the face of three prior decisions of this court, including one (Rulli) that was decided after Arp, it is my view that we are bound to follow our three prior decisions which are precisely on point. See R. v. White (1996), 29 O.R. (3d) 577 (C.A.); Bates v. Bates (2000), 49 O.R. (3d) 1 at paras. 31-2 “This court does not lightly depart from its previous decisions…When good reason exists to doubt the correctness of an earlier decision, the usual practice is for the Chief Justice to convene a five-judge panel of the court to reconsider the earlier decision.” The respondent did not request a five-judge panel and we should give the appellant the benefit of the law as it now stands. 

[61]          I would add, however, that I do agree with my colleague and with the respondent that it may well be appropriate to reconsider the application of issue estoppel in this area.  In R. v. Z., [2000] 3 All E.R. 385 (H.L.), the House of Lords rejected the issue estoppel doctrine as it has evolved in Canada in favour of a more limited form of double jeopardy protection.  The appellant was charged with rape and the prosecution adduced as similar fact evidence four incidents of prior sexual misconduct.  The accused had been charged and acquitted in relation to three of the four incidents.  The House of Lords held that as leading similar fact evidence did not put the accused in jeopardy of conviction for the similar fact conduct, the Crown was allowed to introduce the evidence despite the prior acquittals. R. v. Z. was followed by the New Zealand Court of Appeal in R. v. Degnan, [2001] 1 N.Z.L.R. 280 at para. 34 (C.A.) where the decisions of this court, including G.(K.R.), supra, were criticized as “elevating perceived theory over the realities of criminal practice”.

[62]          The exclusion of similar fact evidence on the basis of issue estoppel has been the subject of trenchant criticism by academic and other authors: see Steusser, supra; Wayne Gorman, “Multiple Count Indictments and the Impact of the Accused Being Acquitted on a Count Subsequently Used as Similar Fact Evidence” (1994), 30 C.R. (4th) 222; Jean-Guy Boilard, Guide to Criminal Evidence (Cowansville, Que: Carswell, updated to 2005); David Paciocco and Lee Steusser, The Law of Evidence, 4th ed. (Toronto: Irwin Law, 2005); compare, however Keith Wright, “Similar Fact Multiple Count Indictments – A Reply” (1994), 32 C.R. (4th) 301; Richard Mahoney, “Acquittals as Similar Fact Evidence: Another View” (2003), 47 Crim. L.Q. 265. In R. v. Duguay (2001), 155 C.C.C. (3d) 407 (Que. C.A.), the Quebec Court of Appeal held that on the particular facts of the case, evidence underlying an acquittal could be admitted along the lines of the Ollis exception, discussed in Arp, supra, but at paras. 60 to 65, Chamberland J.A. suggested that the reasoning in R. v. Z, supra, might lead the Supreme Court of Canada to reconsider this area.

[63]          I do not accept the respondent’s submission that we should parse the acquittal and strip it down so as to exclude only that aspect of the evidence embraced by the essential elements of the offence as alleged in the indictment.  This would preserve the admissibility of evidence of all aspects of the call between the appellant and Balasingam, including the appellant’s admission, except for the actual attempt to obstruct justice.  This, it seems to me, would be unduly artificial and contrary to Grant, supra at 278, where the Supreme Court looked not just at the acquittal but considered the trial judge’s reasons.  Balasingam’s version of the call was of one single transaction in which the appellant’s alleged admission was but one aspect.  The trial judge’s reasons for acquittal indicate that he had a reasonable doubt that the call was ever made.  It follows that the entire call must be excluded.

[64]          Nor do I accept the respondent’s submission that, in the face of the authorities to which I have already referred, I should refuse to allow issue estoppel to reach back to retrospectively render inadmissible the phone call evidence on the basis of R. v. Hewson (1978), 42 C.C.C. (2d) 507 (S.C.C.).  Hewson did not deal with issue estoppel. It held that where a prior conviction was otherwise admissible, the mere fact that an appeal against that conviction was pending did not render it inadmissible.  By the time the case reached the Supreme Court, the appeal against the prior conviction had been allowed and a new trial ordered.  However, the effect of the appeal being allowed appears not to have been considered. Ritchie J., for the majority, dealt with the point (at 516) “on the sole ground that a notice of appeal had been entered against” the conviction.  In R. v. Duong (1998), 124 C.C.C. (3d) 392 at paras. 48-50 (Ont. C.A.); R. v. Duong (2001), 160 C.C.C. (3d) 467 (Ont. C.A.), this court considered the effect of Hewson in a case involving the admission at trial of evidence of a prior conviction as an element of the offence. The prior conviction was successfully appealed. R. v. G. (K.R.), supra, was applied and the reversal of the conviction was given the retrospective effect of rendering the prior conviction inadmissible.

[65]          The fresh evidence obviously meets the first and third branches of the test in Palmer and Palmer v. R. (1979), 50 C.C.C. (2d) 193 (S.C.C.): it could not have been adduced at trial through the exercise of due diligence; and, the evidence is credible and reasonably capable of belief. 

[66]          Admission of the fresh evidence renders inadmissible the evidence of the phone call between the appellant and Balasingam. This evidence was a significant part of the Crown’s case against the appellant, and I am satisfied that it does bear upon a decisive or potentially decisive issue in the trial and that in the absence of the phone call evidence, the result of the trial could reasonably be expected to have been different.  The second and fourth branches of Palmer are also therefore met.

[67]          Accordingly, I would admit the fresh evidence, and on that basis as well, set aside the conviction and order a new trial.

Conclusion

[68]          For these reasons, I would allow the appeal, set aside the conviction, and order a new trial.

“Robert J. Sharpe J.A.”

R.A. BLAIR J.A. (dissenting in part):

Overview

[69]          I have had the opportunity to read the draft reasons of my colleague, Sharpe J.A., and I agree that the appeal must be allowed, the appellant’s conviction set aside, and a new trial ordered.  Respectfully, however, I disagree on one ground of appeal. 

[70]          More specifically, I agree with my colleague – for the reasons he articulates – that the trial judge erred in failing to adequately put the defence position to the jury and that a new trial must therefore be ordered.  As Sharpe J.A. also concludes, the trial judge did not err,

(a) by admitting the portion of the phone call between the appellant and Balasingam in which the appellant is alleged to have said he was “pissed off” with Balasingam because he (Balasingam) had encouraged another man, Mano, to cause the police to charge the appellant with assault, without at the same time permitting trial counsel to ask Balasingam whether he knew that the appellant had in fact been acquitted of the Mano assault; and,

(b) by ordering that potential jurors be identified by number only and not by name.

[71]          Where I differ from my colleague is with respect to the appellant’s final ground of appeal, namely that his subsequent acquittal for attempting to obstruct justice should be admitted as fresh evidence and that the acquittal retrospectively renders inadmissible the evidence of the appellant’s telephone call to Balasingam.  My reasons for taking this different approach follow.

 

 

Facts

[72]          The facts on this issue are straightforward. 

[73]          Balasingam testified that he received a telephone call from the appellant just before the trial was scheduled to begin.  Although the caller did not identify himself, Balasingam says he recognized the appellant’s voice.  He testified the conversation went as follows.

[74]          The appellant began by saying “I want to apologize”, and then asked if Balasingam was going to testify at the trial.  Balasingam said he was, as he had been subpoenaed.  The appellant said, “You know, I never came for you.  I never touched you in any way.  So why would you go to court for me?”  He insisted he had come for Mr. Perinpanathan, the other victim, and not for Balasingam, and repeated this several times.  In addition, they had a conversation about the fact that the appellant was “pissed off” with Balasingam because Balasingam had encouraged a Mr. Mano to have the police lay an earlier assault charge against the appellant.  Finally, although there were other statements attributed to the appellant as well, Balasingam testified the appellant said, “Please, just let me out.  Take me out.  Just go to court and say you picked out the wrong guy or just don’t go to court.  Do something.  I’ll come out and you can beat me up.  You can do whatever you want but just let me out.”

[75]          There were several frailties in this evidence, not the least of which was Balasingam’s generally unsavoury and untrustworthy nature, together with a number of inconsistencies in his testimony regarding the conversation.  But the evidence was properly admitted at the time – without objection – as post-offence conduct that might tend to demonstrate a guilty mind on the part of the appellant.

[76]          Following the appellant’s conviction at the trial from which this appeal is taken, the appellant was tried and acquitted by Marchand J. on the charge of attempting to obstruct justice in relation to the telephone conversation outlined above. 

Analysis

[77]          The appellant seeks to have the acquittal entered as fresh evidence on this appeal and argues – on the basis of the fresh evidence -- that this court must reconsider the trial record as if the phone call evidence were excised from it.  In effect he submits that the acquittal must be given retrospective effect on an issue estoppel basis.  Given the likely impact of the phone call evidence, the appellant contends that it cannot be said the jury would have arrived at the same verdict in its absence.  Therefore, a new trial must be ordered.

[78]          I do not accept that the acquittal is admissible and useable for these purposes.  While I agree with my colleague that this is an area of the law that the Supreme Court of Canada may wish to reassess – for the reasons articulated in the literature he canvasses – I do not share his view that we are bound by authority either in the Supreme Court of Canada or in this court to come to a different conclusion.

[79]          As Sharpe J.A. notes, the appellant’s argument is premised on the principle – enunciated in Grdic v. The Queen (1985), 19 C.C.C. (3d) 289 (S.C.C.) and in R. v. Grant (1991), 67 C.C.C. (3d) 268 (S.C.C.) – that an acquittal is equivalent to a finding of innocence, thus precluding the Crown on estoppel grounds from relying, in subsequent criminal proceedings, on facts essentially determined in favour of the accused in the first proceedings.  Grdic involved an attempt by the Crown to use the facts underlying an acquittal in a subsequent proceeding, however.  It did not deal with an attempt to employ the issue estoppel principle retrospectively, and there was of course no analysis of whether it would make sense to do so.  In my view, therefore, Grdic does not stand for the proposition that issue estoppel may be applied retrospectively to undermine the legitimacy of an earlier conviction otherwise rightly entered on evidence properly before the earlier tribunal at the time.  Grdic speaks to the question of issue estoppel prospectively.  As Lamer J. said at 293-294, adopting the words of Professor Friedland in his text, Double Jeopardy,[1]:

As a matter of fundamental policy in the administration of criminal law it must be accepted by the Crown in a subsequent criminal proceeding that an acquittal is the equivalent of a finding of innocence [emphasis added].

[80]          It is in that context that Lamer J’s later remark in Grdic to the effect that the Crown is estopped (in such proceedings) from re-litigating “any issue, the resolution of which had to be in favour of the accused as a prerequisite to the acquittal, is irrevocably deemed to have been found conclusively in favour of the accused”, must be read.

[81]          Indeed, the retrospective application of issue estoppel in the circumstances of this case at least, is meaningless.  It is meaningless because it does not respond to any of the basic principles that underpin the notion of issue estoppel, namely:

a)      to promote finality in litigation (in this case, it accomplishes the opposite, by fostering yet another proceeding);

b)      to prevent double jeopardy and unfairness to the accused, who should not be required to defend himself or herself continuously against the same allegations, once the legal system has determined the issue in his or her favour (that is not the case here); and

c)      to guard against inconsistent verdicts (again, not a concern here, as attempting to obstruct justice was not an issue at the attempt murder/aggravated assault trial, and the two different triers of fact were operating on the basis of a different standard of proof)

See Steusser, Lee “Admitting Acquittals as Similar Fact Evidence”, (2002) 45 CLQ 489, at 490.

[82]          Consequently, I do not view Grdic as precluding us from dismissing this ground of appeal.  Indeed, it seems to me that the Supreme Court’s more recent decision in R. v. Arp (1998), 129 C.C.C. (3d) 321 (S.C.C.) suggests the contrary. 

[83]          Arp involved a multiple count indictment for two murders.  The trial judge concluded there were significant and striking similarities between the two murders and instructed the jurors that, if they were satisfied the two offences were likely committed by the same person, they could consider the evidence on each count in determining whether the accused committed the offences charged on both counts.  The appellant in that case argued that the jurors were only entitled to use the similar facts underlying one count in considering guilt on both counts if they were satisfied beyond a reasonable doubt that the underlying similar facts would give rise to a verdict of guilty on the first count.  In other words, the Grdic principle of issue estoppel applied to prevent the Crown from relying on the similar facts if the jury would acquit on one or both of the counts after considering the underlying similar facts in isolation. 

[84]          Writing on behalf of a unanimous court, Justice Cory rejected this argument.  Although recognizing the importance of the Grdic principle in our system of justice, Cory J. refused to employ the criminal concept of issue estoppel, hitherto applied in a prospective sense (i.e., in subsequent Crown proceedings) in a situation involving simultaneous criminal proceedings (i.e., in a multiple-count indictment).  He did so on the basis (para. 79) that:

There is nothing unfair or logically irreconcilable about a jury having reasonable doubt whether the accused committed an act while also finding that it is likely he committed it.

[85]          In my view, this same approach is equally appropriate in the presence circumstances, in which the appellant attempts to invoke issue estoppel in a way that is even one step more removed from that contemplated in Grdic.  In the subsequent criminal trial on the attempt to obstruct justice charge, Marchand J. found that he had a reasonable doubt as to whether the Crown had established the factual basis to support a finding of guilt.  However, as Cory J. observed, “[t]here is nothing unfair or logically irreconcilable about” the jurors in the case under appeal having found that the appellant had likely made the phone call in question and the remarks attributed to him in it, and in their having used that balance-of-probability finding as part of their consideration in concluding that the appellant was guilty of aggravated assault.

[86]          It is true that the statement of Cory J. was made in the context of a multiple count indictment and in that sense was intended to apply to such a circumstance, as Sharpe J.A. notes.  Respectfully, however, I do not view Justice Cory’s analysis in the limiting fashion that my colleague does.  Indeed, as I have said, it follows from Cory J’s analysis, in my opinion, that the same approach to the application of issue estoppel would apply in a retrospective context, i.e., where we have gone from a prospective situation (use in a subsequent proceeding) to a simultaneous situation (a multiple count indictment) to a retrospective situation (a prior trier having already made findings on the basis of a different standard of proof in relation to a different issue – attempted murder/aggravated assault as opposed to an attempt obstruct justice).  

[87]          Theoretically, there would have been nothing to prevent the Crown from proceeding against the appellant on the attempt to obstruct justice charge, in a multi-count indictment, at the same time as it proceeded with the attempt murder and aggravated assault charges.  Arp would apply directly in such circumstances to permit the admission of the facts relating to the telephone call.  That the appellant may have been acquitted of attempting to obstruct justice while at the same time convicted of aggravated assault, on the basis of the same evidence, would have been of no moment.  Why should it be different if the conviction for aggravated assault preceded the acquittal for attempting to obstruct justice?

[88]          Exactly the same rationale applies:  “There is nothing unfair or logically irreconcilable about a [trier] having reasonable doubt [in the subsequent proceeding] whether the accused committed an act (the attempt to obstruct justice) while [another trier in a previous proceeding has also found] that it is likely he committed it.” 

[89]          Furthermore, none of the principles that underlie the rationale for issue estoppel – as outlined in para. 81 above – apply.  The same issue was not before the two triers.  There is no exposure to double jeopardy.  The jury in this case appropriately weighed the evidence that was properly before it, and found Mr. Mahalingan guilty of aggravated assault.  In doing so they had only to be satisfied on a balance of probabilities that the phone call took place and that the appellant said what was attributed to him.  To convict on the subsequent obstruct justice charge, Justice Marchand had to be satisfied beyond a reasonable doubt that the charge had been made out.  As he said, he was not.[2]  There is nothing inconsistent, unfair, or logically irreconcilable in those verdicts: Arp.   

[90]          While I do not wish to detract altogether from the importance of issue estoppel in our system of criminal jurisprudence, to set aside an otherwise proper jury verdict on the basis that the appellant was subsequently acquitted of a charge that is unrelated to the purposes for which the phone call evidence was tendered in this case, would in my opinion bring the administration of justice into disrepute.  I do not accept that Grdic compels us to do so.

[91]          As Sharpe J.A. also observes, however, this court has applied the Grdic principle to preclude the admission of evidence underlying an acquittal as similar fact evidence.  It has done so even in cases where the acquittal follows the trial at which the similar fact evidence was led – in effect applying issue estoppel retrospectively in the similar fact context:  see R. v. G. (K.R.) (1991), 68 C.C.C. (3d) 268 (Ont. C.A.); R. v. Verney (1993), 87 C.C.C. (3d) 363 (Ont. C.A.); and, R. v. Rulli (1999), 134 C.C.C. (3d) 465 (Ont. C.A.), application for leave to appeal dismissed, [1999] S.C.C.A. No. 284.

[92]          With the exception of Rulli, however, these cases were decided before the decision of Cory J. in Arp.  They should be viewed now in light of that decision.  While Rulli was subsequent to Arp, it purported to follow G. (K.R.) and Verney, and made no reference to Arp on this point. 

[93]          Accordingly, I am not persuaded that we are precluded by the doctrine of stare decisis from rejecting the appellant’s submission that his subsequent acquittal on the charge of attempting to obstruct justice renders the evidence of the phone call between him and Mr. Balasingam retrospectively inadmissible, thereby undermining the validity of his earlier conviction on the unrelated charge of aggravated assault.  For the reasons outlined above, as well as those alluded to in paragraphs 61-62 of the reasons of Sharpe J.A., I reject that argument.

[94]          In these circumstances the test set out in Palmer and Palmer v. R. (1979), 50 C.C.C. (2d) 193 (S.C.C.) for the admission of fresh evidence has not been met since the evidence of the acquittal, even if admitted, would not bear upon a decisive or potentially decisive issue at trial nor could it have been expected to have affected the result at trial since, in my view, it would not have the effect of rendering the Balasingam telephone evidence inadmissible.

Disposition

[95]          Thus, I agree in the result with Sharpe J.A. that the appellant’s conviction must be set aside and a new trial ordered on the ground that the trial judge erred in failing to outline the position of the defence adequately in his charge to the jury.  I also agree that the trial judge did not err in admitting the statement attributed to the appellant admitting prior misconduct, but excluding evidence capable of dispelling the prejudice arising from the statement.  And I agree that the trial judge did not err in ordering that potential jurors be identified by number only and not by name.

[96]          However, I respectfully disagree with the conclusion that the appellant’s subsequent acquittal for attempting to obstruct justice should be admitted as fresh evidence and, when admitted, used to render retrospectively inadmissible the evidence of the telephone call between the appellant and Mr. Balasingam.  I would not give effect to this latter ground of appeal.
