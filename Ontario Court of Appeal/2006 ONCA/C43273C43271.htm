On appeal from the judgment of Justice Robert A. Clark of the Superior Court of Justice dated February 22, 2005, with reasons reported at [2005] O.J. No. 650, dismissing two applications for certiorari in respect of the judgment of Justice Jon‑Jo Douglas of the Ontario Court of Justice dated August 30, 2004.

GILLESE J.A.:

[1]               A motorcycle was stolen.  The ensuing car chase led to the tragic death of a police officer.  The central question raised by these appeals is whether only the driver who actually killed the police officer can be committed to stand trial for criminal negligence causing death.    

OVERVIEW

[2]               On August 21, 2002, Robert Chiovitti went to the home of James Trakas ostensibly to test drive a motorcycle that Trakas was selling.  Chiovitti was driving a late model pick-up truck that bore a stolen licence plate.  Chiovitti falsely identified himself to Trakas. 

[3]               Trakas allowed Chiovitti to take the motorcycle for a test drive on the condition that Chiovitti leave his pick-up truck for security.  Shortly after Chiovitti left on the motorcycle, Trakas noticed the pick-up truck was being driven away by an unknown person.  Trakas got into his Dodge Durango and began chasing the pick-up truck.

[4]               During the car chase, Trakas telephoned the South Simcoe Police and told them that he was chasing a Chevrolet pick-up truck that was involved in the theft of his motorcycle.  P.C. Kuzmich of the South Simcoe Police force was dispatched to investigate.  He decided to place a “stop-stick” at the 10th concession and Yonge Street in order to cause the pick-up truck to stop.

[5]               Trakas chased the pick-up truck over approximately 46 kilometres of major highways and secondary roads.  The two vehicles reached speeds in excess of 140 kph.  The chase ended approximately thirty minutes after it began when Trakas struck and killed P.C. Kuzmich while he was in the process of laying down the “stop-stick”. 

[6]               In the early morning of August 22, 2002, the motorcycle was found in a ditch.  Chiovitti was lying nearby, unconscious and seriously injured.  He died from his injuries shortly after being taken to hospital.  The stolen motorcycle was not involved in the chase and is not directly involved with the matters before the court. 

[7]               Police recovered a cellphone from Chiovitti’s body.  As a result of information gleaned from the cellphone, police made several arrests in relation to other motorcycle thefts in which Chiovitti had been involved.   

[8]               Trakas was charged with criminal negligence causing death. 

[9]               The Crown alleged that the respondent was the driver of the pick-up truck.  The respondent was charged with criminal negligence causing death, theft over $5,000 (in respect of the motorcycle), and theft and possession under $5,000 (in respect of the license plate).

[10]          At the preliminary inquiry, in order to establish that the respondent was the pick-up truck driver, the Crown relied on records of cellphones associated with the respondent and Chiovitti which demonstrated communication between them on the day of the incident.  The Crown also presented the evidence of an eyewitness, Ashley Goodyer, who briefly saw the driver of the pick-up truck as it sped by and who believed the respondent may have been the driver.  The Crown took the position that it relied on Ashley Goodyer’s evidence only for its circumstantial value in establishing that the respondent looked similar to the driver of the pick-up truck. 

[11]          The issues at the preliminary inquiry were two-fold:  the identity of the driver of the pick-up truck; and, the role, if any, that he played in causing the death of P.C. Kuzmich.  For the purposes of the preliminary inquiry, the defence conceded that the driving of both Trakas and the driver of the pick-up truck was criminally negligent.

[12]          After an eight-day preliminary inquiry, Douglas J. found that there was sufficient circumstantial evidence to identify the respondent as the driver of the pick-up truck but that there was no evidence that he “caused” the police officer’s death.  He committed the respondent to trial on the offences of dangerous driving and possession of a stolen license plate.  He discharged the respondent on the offences of criminal negligence causing death and theft under.  It appears that, through oversight, the preliminary inquiry judge neither committed nor discharged the respondent on the count of theft over $5,000 (the motorcycle).

[13]          The Crown and the respondent both brought applications to quash the order of the preliminary inquiry judge.  The Crown sought, by means of its application, to have the respondent ordered to stand trial on criminal negligence causing death and theft over $5,000.

[14]          The respondent sought, by means of his application, to quash the committal on charges of dangerous driving and possession of stolen license plates.  His application was based on the argument that the evidence on identification was too flimsy to support a reasonable inference that the respondent was the driver of the pick‑up truck. 

[15]          On February 22, 2005, Clark J. dismissed both applications.  He upheld the preliminary inquiry judge’s committal based on the sufficiency of circumstantial evidence of identity.  He went further and concluded that the evidence of Ashley Goodyer was sufficient direct evidence of identity to warrant committal on the theft, possession and dangerous driving counts.  He also upheld the preliminary inquiry judge’s discharge of the respondent on the count of criminal negligence causing death.  With the agreement of the parties, he remedied the oversight in respect of the count of theft over $5,000 (the motorcycle) and committed the respondent to stand trial on that charge. 

[16]          The Crown appeals from the dismissal of its application.  This appeal centres on the issue of causation.  The primary issue raised is whether there was some evidence before the preliminary inquiry judge that the respondent “caused” the police officer’s death.  The Crown argues that the reviewing judge erred in failing to find that the preliminary inquiry judge fell into jurisdictional error and asks for an order requiring the preliminary inquiry judge to commit the respondent to stand trial on the count of criminal negligence causing death.   

[17]          The respondent appeals the order of the reviewing judge dismissing his application (“the cross-appeal”).  The issue raised on the cross-appeal is the sufficiency of the evidence of the identification of the respondent as the driver of the pick-up truck.  The respondent asks that the order below be quashed and that he be discharged on all charges.        

[18]          For ease of reference, the Crown is referred to as the appellant and Mr. Shilon is referred to as the respondent throughout these reasons, despite the role reversal in the two appeals. 

[19]          For the reasons that follow, I would allow the Crown’s appeal and make the order as requested.  I would dismiss the respondent’s cross‑appeal.

SHILON’S LIABILITY FOR HIS OWN CRIMINALLY NEGLIGENT BEHAVIOUR

[20]          Section 220 of the Criminal Code states:

Every person who by criminal negligence causes death to another person is guilty of an indictable offence . . .

[21]          In determining whether a person can be held responsible for causing death, it must be determined whether the person caused death both in fact and in law.  Factual causation demands an inquiry into how the victim came to his or her death, in a medical, mechanical or physical sense, and the contribution of the accused to the victim’s death.  Legal (imputable) causation is concerned with the question of whether the accused person should be held responsible in law for the death that occurred.  See R. v. Nette (2001), 158 C.C.C. (3d) 486 at 505 (S.C.C.).

[22]          The preliminary inquiry judge discharged the respondent on the count of criminal negligence causing death saying, at paras. 80 – 81 of his reasons, that Trakas was the cause of the police officer’s death and that while the theft and chase were preconditions to the death, causes and conditions are not the same thing:  Trakas caused the death whereas the theft and escape were “indispensable requisite[s] or necessary condition[s] (Black’s Law Dictionary), not [causes]”. 

[23]          The preliminary inquiry judge noted, in para. 126, that it can be stated with “some significant confidence” that the driver of the pick-up truck actually foresaw that one or another of the vehicles involved in the chase could collide with another vehicle or a person on the road and concluded that the driver of the pick-up truck “very much ought to have foreseen such a consequence”.  Nonetheless, in paras. 127 – 30, he held that, as the driver of the pick-up truck neither caused Trakas to drive as he did nor assisted Trakas in driving as he did, causation could not be imputed to the driver of the pick-up truck as to do so would mock the principle of individual autonomy. 

[24]          The preliminary inquiry judge explained the principle of individual autonomy earlier in his reasons, relying on this quotation from Andrew Ashworth, Principles of Criminal Law, 1st ed. (Oxford: Clarendon Press, 1991) at p. 100:

The principle of individual autonomy presumes that, where … a [capable] individual … has made a sufficient causal contribution to an occurrence, it is inappropriate to trace the causation any further …  The doctrine, then, is that voluntary conduct [by one person] acts as a barrier in any causal inquiry in criminal law: by and large, D’s voluntary conduct will usually be regarded as the cause of an act or omission if it was the closest human conduct to the result …

[25]          The reviewing judge concluded that the preliminary inquiry judge did not commit jurisdictional error in discharging the respondent on the charge of criminal negligence causing death.  Like the preliminary inquiry judge, he was of the view that there was no joint venture, such as is present in racing cases. 

[26]          The reviewing judge was, however, troubled about causation, saying at para. 53:

The problem is not an easy one to resolve.  It seems fair to say, on the one hand, that there was no joint venture, as is omnipresent in the racing cases.  On the other hand, it seems anomalous, to my mind at least, that someone who has stolen property in such circumstances as would cause a reasonable man to conclude that the owner of the property might follow in hot pursuit and who, when actually pursued as he anticipated, drives in a manner that is admitted to be criminally negligent to evade capture, should be less culpable for the death of a third party than a man who decides to race with another motorist.

[27]          The reviewing judge rejected the remoteness argument advanced by the respondent, saying at para. 54 of the reasons:

[T]here is evidence capable of demonstrating that the actions of the driver of the pickup truck were a contributing cause beyond the de minimus, as described in Smithers v. The Queen (1977), 34 C.C.C. (2d) 427 (S.C.C.), or a “significant contribution or cause” as described more recently in R. v. Nette (2001), 158 C.C.C. (3d) 486 (S.C.C.).  I say that because the Trakas vehicle was in hot pursuit of the pickup and because there was evidence from which it could be determined that the drivers were only a short distance apart.  Just as two racers are intent each on beating the other, here, presumably, the driver of the pickup, being pursued, was driving in the fashion he was because he was intent on escaping from Trakas and, equally, Trakas was driving in the same criminally negligent manner in order to prevent the escape of the pickup driver.    

[28]          He concluded his analysis on this issue by rejecting the possibility of party liability stating, in para. 58 of the reasons, that the evidence was not capable of supporting a reasonable inference that the driver of the pick-up truck intended to encourage Trakas to drive as he did.  

[29]          I understand the reviewing judge to have concluded that there was evidence of factual “but-for” causation but that there was no evidence capable of supporting an inference of legal causation.

[30]          For the reasons given by the reviewing judge in relation to remoteness, set out above, I do not accept the respondent’s contention that the actions of the driver of the pick-up truck are too remote to permit the conclusion that there was some evidence that he was a cause of death in fact.  In my view, it is an available inference that the pick-up truck driver’s actions were “a significant contributing cause”[1] of the police officer’s death and that his unlawful driving influenced the actual accident well beyond serving as its backdrop.  In short, I reject the respondent’s argument that Trakas’ driving was so independent of that of the driver of the pick-up truck that the latter’s actions are fairly viewed only as part of the history of the setting in which the death took place. 

[31]          I turn next to legal causation.  In my view, the reviewing judge’s conclusion that Trakas and the driver of the pick-up truck were not co-perpetrators, accomplices or engaged in a joint venture, does not end the legal causation inquiry.  The question remains: is there some evidence on which causation can be legally imputed to the driver of the pick-up truck?     

[32]          Nette offers guidance in considering this question.  At para. 45, Arbour J. comments on legal causation in these terms:

Legal causation, which is also referred to as imputable causation, is concerned with the question of whether the accused person should be held responsible in law for the death that occurred.  It is informed by legal considerations such as the wording of the section creating the offence and principles of interpretation.  These legal considerations, in turn, reflect fundamental principles of criminal justice such as the principle that the morally innocent should not be punished.  In determining whether legal causation is established, the inquiry is directed at the question of whether the accused person should be held criminally responsible for the consequences that occurred.  [Citations omitted, emphasis added.]

[33]          Reasonable foreseeability of harm, it seems to me, is relevant in the analysis of legal causation in negligence based offences.  On this view, the fact that Trakas actually caused the police officer’s death does not preclude an inquiry as to whether the driver of the pick-up truck ought also to be held criminally responsible for the death.  There is nothing in the wording of the offence that constrains the concept of causation to preclude such an inquiry.  The relevant provisions read as follows:

219.(1) Every one is criminally negligent who

(a)    in doing anything, or

(b)    in omitting to do anything that it is his duty to do,

shows wanton or reckless disregard for the lives or safety of other persons.

220.    Every person who by criminal negligence causes death to another person is guilty of an indictable offence …

[34]          Courts in other jurisdictions have used the principle of reasonable foreseeability of harm as informing the causation analysis.  For example, in R. v. Roberts (1972), 56 Cr. App. R. 95 (C.A.), a woman was assaulted while in a moving car.  She jumped from the car, injuring herself.  The English Court of Appeal upheld the accused’s conviction for assault causing bodily harm on the basis that the woman’s act of jumping from the car was not a voluntary act which broke the chain of causation but a natural, foreseeable consequence of the accused’s actions. 

[35]          Some American courts have used reasonable foreseeability as a basis for finding causation.  In State v. Lovelace, 738 N.E. 2d 418 (Ct. App. 1999), a police officer killed a third party while in a high‑speed chase in which he was trying to apprehend the accused.  The majority of the Court of Appeals stated, at p. 427:

The issue is whether any of this evidence made it reasonably unforeseeable, as a matter of law, that Officer Berting would have acted as he did.  We hold that it did not.  Evidence that a police officer involved in a high‑speed pursuit violated departmental policy goes primarily to the reasonableness of the officer’s conduct.  The test of proximate cause, however, is not whether Officer Berting’s response was reasonable, but, rather, whether it was reasonably foreseeable.  People v. Schmies (1996), 44 Cal. App. 4th 38, 51 Cal. Rptr. 2d 185.  Simply because a person’s conduct is unreasonable or even criminal does not make it unforeseeable.  [Footnotes omitted, emphasis in the original].

And further, at p. 428:

We hold that a rational trier of fact could have concluded that the accident that killed Tenhundfeld was within the scope of the risk created by Lovelace as he led the police on an extremely reckless, extremely dangerous car chase.  As noted by the court in Pike, inherent in car chases of this sort is the probability that one or more of the officers in pursuit will lose control of his vehicle and collide with another vehicle.  That is not to say that it happens in every car chase, but when it does, it cannot be considered surprising or extraordinary.

[36]          The parties drew the court’s attention to R. v. Mozylinski (1995), 129 Sask. R. 77 (Q.B.), a case somewhat similar to the case at bar.  In Mozylinski, a police cruiser was chasing the accused at high‑speed.  The police officer failed to observe a “yield” sign and collided with another car.  The officer and two people in the other car were injured.  The accused was charged with dangerous driving causing bodily harm.  The trial judge found, applying the “beyond de minimus” standard, that the accused’s actions did not amount to a “real cause” of the accident, the real cause being the officer’s negligence which was only minimally linked to the unlawful acts of the accused.  Accordingly, the accused was acquitted of dangerous driving causing bodily harm and convicted of dangerous driving.   

[37]          However, as Mozylinski was decided on the issue of factual causation, it is of little relevance because, as previously explained, no issue arises in this case in respect of factual causation.    

[38]          In my view, where conduct is inherently dangerous and carries with it a reasonably foreseeable risk of immediate and substantial harm, the test for legal causation will have been met.  On the facts of this case, there is some evidence of both criteria. 

[39]          As the defence conceded for the purposes of the proceedings below, the driver of the pick-up truck drove in a criminally negligent fashion.  Not only was the driving inherently dangerous, it clearly carried with it the reasonably foreseeable consequence of immediate and substantial harm.  Trakas’ actions were reasonably foreseeable – they were a predictable consequence of the actions of the driver of the pick-up truck.  Indeed, the preliminary inquiry judge stated that the officer’s death was an entirely foreseeable consequence of the conduct of the driver of the pick-up truck. 

[40]          Accordingly, it was an available inference that the police officer’s death occurred in the ambit of the risk created by the actions of the driver of the pick-up truck and that the driver ought reasonably to have foreseen such harm. 

[41]          The respondent maintains that, even if found to be the driver of the pick-up truck, he cannot be found to have caused the police officer’s death because Trakas’ actions amount to an intervening cause.  Because the respondent’s actions were not the actual cause of death, even though the intervening acts of Trakas would not have occurred but for the actions of the driver of the pick-up truck, the police officer’s death cannot be attributable to the respondent because Trakas’ actions were free, deliberate and informed.  That is, the chain of causation leading to the police officer’s death was broken by the free, deliberate and informed intervening acts of Trakas. 

[42]          In making this argument, the respondent relies on the following statement from Glanville Williams, Textbook of Criminal Law, 2d ed. (London: Stevens & Sons Limited, 1983) at 391:

Putting the rule in terms of causation, the new intervening act (novus actus interveniens) of a responsible actor, who has full knowledge of what he is doing, and is not subject to mistake or pressure, will normally operate to relieve the defendant of liability for a further consequence, because it makes the consequence “too remote.”  Underlying this rule there is, undoubtedly, a philosophical attitude.  Moralists and lawyers regard the individual’s will as the autonomous prime cause of his behaviour.  What a person does (if he has reached adult years, is of sound mind and is not acting under mistake, intimidation or other similar pressure) is his own responsibility, and is not regarded as having been caused by other people.  An intervening act of this kind, therefore, breaks the causal connection that would otherwise have been perceived between previous acts and the forbidden consequence.  (An intervening wrongful omission does not break it.)

The rule can be rationalised in psychological terms by saying that the intervention of the responsible actor diverts our retributive wrath from the defendant, the previous actor, who may appear to be so much less culpable than the later actor, and this switching of retributive feeling is expressed in causal language.  Sometimes, too, we may feel that making people responsible for the subsequent behaviour of others, merely because they foresaw or could have foreseen that behaviour, would be too great a restriction upon liberty.  Yet another way of explaining the rule would be to say that part of the object of the criminal trial is to dramatise society’s rejection of the deed, and this is adequately done by prosecuting the immediate author.  There is no pressing necessity to regard more remote authors as responsible for the harm itself, though they may well be prosecuted for other offences, such as attempt, or in appropriate circumstances as accessories.  It is important that the law should not saddle a man with liability for consequences that not only he but also the general public would blame on someone else.  [Emphasis added]

[43]          I accept that independent voluntary human intervention in events started by an accused may break the chain of causation.  However, on any realistic appraisal of the facts, and as was found by both levels of court below, Trakas’ conduct was directly linked to that of the driver of the pick-up truck.  Glanville Williams writes of intervening cause where the responsible actor is not acting under “mistake, intimidation or other similar pressure”.  Was Trakas acting free of “similar pressure”?  It was the conduct of the driver of the pick-up truck that provoked Trakas’ driving.  It was the driver of the pick-up truck who created and continued the highly charged situation in which Trakas, the victim of a premeditated theft, responded predictably to catch the thief and recover his property.  In my view, the question of whether Trakas’ actions are sufficiently independent as to sever the chain of causation is a question best left to a properly instructed jury. 

[44]          Consequently, in my view, the preliminary inquiry judge made a jurisdictional error which the reviewing judge failed to correct.  Because of an erroneous application of the law of causation, the preliminary inquiry judge failed to measure the Crown’s evidence against the elements of the offence of criminal negligence and drew an inference of no causation in the face of a competing inference that would attribute causal blame to the respondent.   

SHILON’S LIABILITY UNDER S. 21 OF THE CRIMINAL CODE

[45]          The Crown contends that the respondent should have been committed to stand trial on the charge of criminal negligence causing death based on party liability pursuant to ss. 21(1)(a) or (c) of the Criminal Code. 

[46]          Sections 21(1)(a) and (c) read as follows:

21. (1)  Every one is a party to an offence who

(a)       actually commits it;

. . .

(c)       abets any person in committing it.

Section 21(1)(a)

[47]          Section 21(1)(a) is directed at the person who actually commits the offence, that is, the perpetrator of the offence.  There can be, however, more than one perpetrator.

[48]          The Crown argues that the car chase in which Trakas and the driver of the pick-up truck engaged had a sufficiently mutual character that a reasonable jury could impute causation to the latter, for harm directly caused by Trakas, on the basis that the two were co‑perpetrators under s. 21(1)(a).  In making this argument, the Crown likens the conduct of Trakas and the driver of the pick-up truck to that of street racing – it is “just racing with a head start”.  Alternatively, the Crown invites the court to view the conduct as akin to acts of road rage in which each driver attempts to cut the other off. 

[49]          The Crown says that cases involving street racing and road rage are best understood as reflecting a mutuality of conduct that creates a foreseeable risk, even where the participants do not share a mutual intent.  In that sense, the Crown maintains, there was evidence that the respondent and Trakas were co-perpetrators, in that each engaged in the same activity even though they acted with opposing intents.  Although Trakas wanted to catch the respondent and the respondent wanted to get away, the Crown submits that their chasing behaviour is more properly analysed as acting in concert because the continuation of the dangerous behaviour depended on the participation of both actors.  There was no reason for the respondent to flee if he were not being chased by Trakas; there was no reason for Trakas to pursue if the respondent did not flee.  Thus, the Crown maintains, there was evidence of mutuality of conduct on which a jury could find that they were engaged as co-perpetrators in the same venture – a chase.  

[50]          In my view, the concept of mutuality in racing cases is referable to a common intention between the two parties to encourage or incite each other to participate in what can be viewed as a “joint venture”, a common activity in which they are jointly, intentionally involved (i.e., racing) and one which creates a common risk.  This requirement of a common intention or intentional encouragement/incitement of the other to commit the offence is absent from the concept of ‘mutuality of conduct’, as described by the Crown. 

[51]          The notion that a joint venture involves a common purpose, mutual incitement or encouragement was expressed by Hill J. in R. v. Menezes (2002), 50 C.R. (5th) 343 at para. 101, (Ont. S.C.J.), in these terms:

By their actions, those who race at excessive speeds on a public roadway assist one another in creating a dangerous risk.  Each encourages and incites the other.  The drivers either deliberately assume the risk of danger to themselves and others or, in the circumstances of the degree of departure from reasonably prudent and lawful driving standards, can be taken as having an unrestrained disregard for the consequences of their actions.  It is the mutuality of their contribution toward the rivalry of speed which creates a singular hazardous situation.  [emphasis added.]

[52]          Glanville Williams expressed the notion in the following way:[2]

Two persons may be guilty as joint perpetrators.  Hence where a body of men beat a constable (some with sticks, some by throwing stones, some with their fists), and the constable died of the aggregate violence, the judge directed the jury that “If several persons act together in pursuance of a common intent, every act done in furtherance of such intent by each of them is, in law, done by all.” [Footnotes omitted, emphasis added.]

[53]          As I am of the view that mutuality of intention between parties to pursue a common unlawful venture is essential to a finding that the parties are co-perpetrators within the scope of s. 21(1)(a), unlike road‑racing behaviour, a driver being chased by another cannot be regarded as a co-perpetrator to an offence committed by the chasing driver.  The driver being chased is not intentionally encouraging or inciting the other.  On the contrary, the driver being chased is, by his actions, likely intending to discourage the other from following.  The two drivers are independent actors, each creating a danger that is separate and distinct from that created by the other.  In my view, absent a common intention, one driver is not responsible for the conduct of the other within the meaning of s. 21(1)(a).   

[54]          The Crown argues on the basis that liability for the offence flows from Trakas to the driver of the pick-up truck.  For the reasons given, I reject the notion that the driver of the pick-up truck is liable as a co-perpetrator.  That, however, does not end the analysis under s. 21(1)(a).  As explained in the preceding section, the conduct of the driver of the pick-up truck may be seen to encompass the driving by Trakas on the basis that Trakas’ actions were a reasonably foreseeable consequence of the conduct of the driver of the pick-up truck.  The driver of the pick-up truck knew he was being pursued by Trakas but he continued his high-speed flight, driving in a fashion that showed a wanton and reckless disregard for the lives or safety of others, knowing that it was likely that Trakas would follow suit.    On that basis, the liability of the driver of the pick-up truck is as the perpetrator, that is, as the person who actually committed the offence of criminal negligence causing death.    

Section 21(1)(c)

[55]          Alternatively, the Crown argues that under s. 21(1)(c), the respondent abetted Trakas and, thus, was a party to the offence of criminal negligence causing death as committed by Trakas. 

[56]          The preliminary inquiry judge rejected liability on this basis.  He reasoned that there must be some evidence that the driver of the pick-up truck “intentionally and purposively” aided or assisted Trakas in his fatal driving.    

[57]          The Crown argues that the preliminary inquiry judge erred by equating the notion of “purpose” with “desire”.  The Crown says that the fact that the respondent did not wish Trakas to pursue him is irrelevant – what matters is whether the respondent intended the consequence (Trakas’ driving) that flowed from his conduct.  The Crown maintains that this requirement is fulfilled by evidence that the respondent subjectively knew that his conduct, as a means of achieving his purpose of escaping, was substantially certain to encourage Trakas, whether or not the respondent viewed this as desirable.   

[58]          In my view, the respondent’s culpability cannot be grounded in s. 21(1)(c), as an abettor.  Although abetting can include encouraging a principal to commit the offence, the trier of fact must be satisfied that the accused’s acts or words encouraged another to commit a crime and that the accused intended his or her actions or words to have that effect.  See R. v. Greyeyes (1997), 116 C.C.C. (3d) 334 at para.38 (S.C.C.), Cory J.  The evidence in this case is not capable of supporting a reasonable inference that the driver of the pick-up intended to encourage Trakas to drive as he did.  The driver of the pick-up truck intended the opposite – to escape or to have Trakas stop pursuing him.  

THE CROSS-APPEAL

[59]          The respondent’s committal was dependent on the preliminary inquiry judge finding some evidence that he was the driver of the pick-up truck.  The cross-appeal is based on the respondent’s contention that there was no such evidence.

[60]          In concluding that there was sufficient circumstantial evidence to commit the respondent, the preliminary trial judge relied on evidence falling into three categories:

i)                   evidence of cellular telephone records respecting two telephones registered to the respondent, a telephone registered to Chiovitti and several other telephones;

ii)                evidence of association between the respondent and Chiovitti; and,

iii)              the evidence of Ashley Goodyer.

[61]          The respondent concedes that, on the evidence, a jury would be entitled to infer that the phone records established communication between Chiovitti and the respondent on the day of the theft.  He argues, however, that such a concession does not permit a reasonable inference that the respondent was the driver of the pick-up truck.

[62]          The cellphone records established that on the day in question, Chiovitti made and received a total of 87 phone calls.  Fourteen of these calls involved Chiovitti phoning the respondent or vice versa.  During the time that the driver of the pick-up truck was being chased by Trakas, Chiovitti made three calls to the respondent’s cellphone.  From these findings, the preliminary inquiry judge concluded that there was some contact among the respondent, Chiovitti and other relevant persons, at relevant times and near relevant places.  

[63]          The respondent argues that there was not a “scintilla” of circumstantial evidence arising from the telephone records to support a finding that the respondent was Chiovitti’s accomplice in the theft and the driver of the pick-up truck.  He contends that the phone records do no more than establish that the respondent and Chiovitti had telephone contact, or attempted telephone contact, on the afternoon and evening of the theft.  He says that the respondent’s failure to telephone Chiovitti for 45 to 55 minutes after the chase ended indicate that he was not the driver of the pick-up truck.  He points also to evidence that Chiovitti had been involved in other thefts committed in a similar way with a third party accomplice (Phil Larizza) and that Chiovitti and Larizza spoke on the telephone numerous times on the day of the theft.  On the other hand, there was no evidence that the respondent had previously been involved with Chiovitti or Larizza in similar thefts and there was no physical or forensic evidence linking the respondent to the pick-up truck.  Thus, he argues, the inference that the respondent was driving the pick-up truck was not reasonably available and, by finding to the contrary, the preliminary inquiry judge exceeded his jurisdiction in committing the respondent to stand trial on any offences. 

[64]          The reviewing judge articulated and applied the correct legal test on an application for certiorari to review committal to stand trial.  As he expressly noted, the reviewing court does not test the evidence against the standard for committal.  Referring to Re Skogman and the Queen (1984), 13 C.C.C. (3d) 161 at 173 (S.C.C.), he emphasized the need for deference to the preliminary inquiry judge’s determination and that a committal was only to be vacated where there was no evidence on an element of the offence.   

[65]          After considering the evidence before the preliminary inquiry judge of the cellular telephone records, the association of the respondent with Chiovitti and others, and the evidence of Ashley Goodyer, in para. 19 of the reasons, the reviewing judge concluded:

Having reviewed the evidence, set out at paragraphs [60] to [76] inclusive of his reasons for committal, upon which the preliminary inquiry judge concluded there was sufficient evidence to commit the accused for trial on the issue of identity, I am of the view that, while the evidence implicating the accused as the driver of the pickup truck was minimal, it cannot be said that there was no proper material upon which the preliminary inquiry judge could form the judicial opinion he did.  Accordingly, the committals must stand.

[66]          In my view, the circumstantial evidence of identity met the “some evidence” standard such that the reviewing judge properly upheld the committal.  A partial summary of that evidence, as drawn from para. 15 of the Crown’s factum, shows:

·        despite inconsistencies, on Ashley Goodyer’s evidence at the preliminary inquiry, the respondent resembled the driver of the pick‑up truck in that he was white, male, and relatively young with light to medium‑coloured hair;

·        the respondent was a known associate of Chiovitti;

·        the cellular telephone records showed the respondent as being in close contact with Chiovitti in the early evening of the day of the theft, but no contact by telephone in the hour-and-a-half before the theft.  There would be no reason for Chiovitti to be in telephone contact with his accomplice while they were in the truck together;

·        immediately before the time of the theft, the respondent’s telephone was communicating with the Innisfil tower in its southern sector.  That tower was 4 km away from and north of the Trakas residence;

·        the respondent did not make or answer any calls on his cellular telephone during the period of the high‑speed chase;

·        of the six calls made to the respondent’s telephone during the time of the chase, three were from Chiovitti;

·        after the time period of the chase (which began at approximately 10:15 p.m. and ended with the accident that killed the officer at approximately 10:47 p.m.), the respondent, in addition to a call to pick up voice mail messages at 11:13 p.m. and a call to a third party, tried three times to contact Chiovitti; and,

·        the respondent was the one and only person who Chiovitti tried to contact after he stole the motorbike and before he crashed.

[67]          As there was some evidence on which the preliminary inquiry judge could conclude that it was a reasonable inference that the respondent was Chiovitti’s accomplice who drove the pick-up truck, I need not go further and consider the alternative analysis of the reviewing judge that Ashley Goodyer’s evidence amounted to direct evidence of identification of the respondent as the driver.

DISPOSITION

[68]          Accordingly, I would allow the appeal and require the preliminary inquiry judge to commit the respondent to stand trial on the count of criminal negligence causing death.  I would dismiss the cross‑appeal for the reasons given.
