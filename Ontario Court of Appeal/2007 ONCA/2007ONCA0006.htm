ENDORSEMENT

[1]                The appellant raises two issues. First, the trial judge reversed the burden of proof and second that she made improper use of the complainant’s prior inconsistent statements. Despite Ms. Trehearne’s very helpful submissions, we have not been persuaded that the trial judge erred. This is not a case like R. v. Maharaj (2004), 186 C.C.C. (3d) 247 (Ont. C.A.), leave to appeal to Supreme Court of Canada dismissed [2004] S.C.C.A. No. 340, where the accused’s evidence was rejected because it conflicted with the complainant’s accepted evidence. The trial judge rejected the appellant’s evidence almost entirely because it was internally inconsistent and inherently implausible.  For example, the appellant claimed a close relationship with the children, yet he was unaware of family and personal matters that would be obvious to anyone involved in that relationship living in that situation.

[2]               The trial judge was entitled and, indeed, required to look at all the evidence in determining the facts including whether the appellant’s version of the events might give rise to a reasonable doubt.

[3]               The trial judge also did not subject the appellant’s evidence to unfair closer scrutiny than the prosecution evidence. She carefully explained why parts of the appellant’s evidence were untrue for reasons that are founded in the record. She closely scrutinized the complainant’s evidence but was satisfied with the explanation for problematic aspects of it, such as the incremental disclosure. She was entitled to make that assessment.

[4]               As to the prior statements, we are satisfied that the trial judge did not err in admitting this evidence nor misuse it. The evidence was properly admitted as part of the narrative and provisionally admissible to rebut recent fabrication. As it turned out, the statements did not fully rebut the allegation of recent fabrication, but there is no reason to believe that the trial judge misapprehended the value of that evidence.

[5]               More importantly, the trial judge referred to the prior statements principally in the context of the attack on the complainant’s evidence because of the incremental disclosure. The defence relied on these prior statements to show inconsistencies in the complainant’s story. It was inevitable that the trial judge would have to refer to the earlier statements to explain why she accepted the complainant’s testimony despite the incremental disclosure. The trial judge spoke of the sister corroborating the complainant’s evidence but this statement was made principally in reference to parts of the sister’s evidence of what happened to her and what she saw. The reference to the complainant’s accusation against the appellant was proper since it preceded the family confrontation concerning the theft allegation.

[6]               Accordingly, the appeal from conviction is dismissed. The appeal from sentence is dismissed as abandoned.
