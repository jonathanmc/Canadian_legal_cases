On appeal from the conviction entered on March 22, 2005 and the sentence imposed on March 24, 2005 by Justice John F. Hamilton of the Superior Court of Justice, sitting with a jury.

ENDORSEMENT

[1]               Following a trial by jury, the appellant was convicted of robbery, abduction of a child under 14 and abandoning a child under the age of 10 years.  He appeals his convictions and alleges that the trial judge erred in his instructions to the jury in two respects.  He also appeals his sentences totaling three years imprisonment in addition to time served of about three years.

[2]               The appellant submits:

1.         The trial judge failed to charge the jury as to the use they could make of prior inconsistent statements.

2.         The trial judge erred by telling the jury that the essential elements of the offences were proven “as a matter of law”.

As a result, the appellant submits that a new trial is necessary.

[3]               We agree with the appellant’s submissions.  Because we would order a new trial, a very brief summary of the evidence pertaining to the issues suffices.

The Evidence

[4]               The complainant, Mohinder Samra, testified that on July 28, 2003, he was driving home from his cousin’s house with his six month old baby in the back seat of his car.  At a red traffic light, a car stopped next to him and the passenger rolled down his window and accused Samra of having cut his car off.  Samra denied this and drove away when the light turned green.  At the next red light, this same individual got out of his car and approached Samra’s car.  He told Samra to get out of the car, which he did.  The individual then took Samra’s cell phone and demanded money.  Samra replied that he had none.  The man then punched Samra, took his keys, and drove off in Samra’s car with the baby still in the back seat.  Samra noted the licence number of the car.  The individual who took Samra’s car had come from a car that was registered to the Appellant’s girlfriend, Samantha Ameerally.    

[5]               About a two-minute drive away, the individual abandoned the car on the side of a residential street.  He left the radio on, the windows down and the hazard lights flashing.  A passerby noticed the baby in the car and contacted the police.  Samra was reunited with his baby, who was unharmed, less than half an hour later. 

[6]               The main issue at trial was identity.  Although Maharaj did not match the description of the driver Samra gave to the police the night of the offence, Samra picked Maharaj out of a photo line-up the following morning.  At trial, Samra conceded that on the day of his arrest, Maharaj did not match the description Samra had given to the police.

[7]               Ameerally was called as a Crown witness.  She testified that on the evening in question, Maharaj had gone out to purchase milk but returned without having bought any.  She also testified that Maharaj’s brother left five minutes later.  She denied, when asked by the Crown, that the two had left together.  After her examination-in-chief, the Crown successfully applied for leave to cross-examine her on two prior statements she had given to the police and on her prior testimony at the preliminary inquiry.  During the cross-examination, Ameerally stated that she had been telling the truth on all three occasions.  However, on crucial elements as to timing or Maharaj’s whereabouts on the night in question, Ameerally stated that she could not recall whether she had been truthful with the police.  She agreed that her preliminary inquiry evidence was “true at the time.”

[8]               On cross-examination by the defence, Ameerally stated that her prior statements to the police were lies, and that she could not remember when Maharaj left her presence or when he returned.  She summarized her evidence at trial as: at some point Maharaj and his brother left her presence, they were gone for some time, that Maharaj had her car keys, and that they returned home some time later.  The position of the defence was that the other person in the car, not the appellant, had committed the alleged acts in question.

The effect of the trial judge’s failure to charge the jury as to the use they could make of Ameerally’s prior inconsistent statements

[9]               The Crown concedes that the trial judge failed to tell the jury that they could only use prior inconsistent statements for the truth of their contents if adopted by the witness.  In addition, to the extent that the witness did not adopt her prior statements they could only consider the statements in assessing the witness’s credibility.

[10]          The Crown’s position is that the trial judge’s instructions to the jury were nevertheless adequate.  We disagree.  The trial judge’s instructions to the jury on how to assess credibility and his instruction that they should try the case only on the evidence they had heard would not have properly equipped the jury to deal with the prior inconsistent statements.

[11]          The Crown further submits that the witness adopted the essential portions of her prior statements.  Whether the witness adopted her prior statements is for the jury to decide.  See R. v. Tat and Long, (1997) 117 C.C.C. (Ont. C.A. ) at para. 21.

[12]          The defence did not object to the trial judge’s omission.  The Crown argues that this was a tactical decision on the part of the defence and that the curative proviso should be applied.  We cannot agree that, having regard to the position taken by counsel at trial, the omission by the defence to object was a tactical one.  The witness clearly and unequivocally adopted her prior statements to the extent it was helpful to the defence and arguably did not adopt other parts that would have helped the Crown. 

[13]          The Crown’s alternate submission for applying the proviso, that the evidence would have been admissible as past recollection recorded or pursuant to a principled exception to the hearsay rule as in R. v. B.(K.G.) 1992, 79 C.C.C. (3d) 257 (S.C.C.) is problematical.  The Crown did not seek to have the evidence admitted on either of these bases at trial and we cannot say that on this record the prior statements would inevitably have been admissible for the truth of their contents.  Once the jury was charged, it was too late for the Crown to seek to introduce the evidence on a different basis. 

[14]          We would allow this ground of appeal and, standing alone, it necessitates that we order a new trial.

The trial judge told the jury that “in law” the offences had been made out

[15]          Before the defence addressed the jury, the trial judge indicated that he was thinking of instructing the jury that the evidence established at law that the offences had been made out and that the issue for the jury was who committed the offence.  The defence agreed that this was the appropriate instruction.  The defence’s position to the jury was that there was no doubt that Maharaj was present when the offences took place, but that the Crown had not proven beyond a reasonable doubt that Maharaj, and not the other person in the car, was the perpetrator.  After the trial judge charged the jury, the jury returned with a question.  It could be inferred from the jury’s question that they were concerned that Maharaj was not aware of the baby in the car and that he did not have the requisite intention to commit the offences of abduction.  However, the trial judge instructed them that “intention is proven,” thereby removing this element of the offence from their consideration.  Likewise, with respect to the abandonment charge, on the facts of the case the jury may have found that the baby’s life was not endangered as required by the wording of the offence.

[16]          Although identity was the central issue at trial, the defence never formally admitted that all essential elements of the offences had been established beyond a reasonable doubt.  While no defence put forward was taken away from the jury, it is apparent from the jury’s question that they were concerned as to whether the Crown had proven the appellant had the necessary intention to commit the crimes of abduction and abandonment.  In the absence of a formal admission, it was for the jury to decide whether the Crown had proven all the elements of the offences.  See R. v. Krieger [2006] S.C.J. No. 47.  While a trial judge is entitled to express his opinion on a question of fact, he must make it clear to the jury that this opinion is given as advice and not as direction.

[17]          Accordingly, we would allow the appeal, set aside the conviction and order a new trial.  In view of our disposition, we need not address the sentence appeal.
