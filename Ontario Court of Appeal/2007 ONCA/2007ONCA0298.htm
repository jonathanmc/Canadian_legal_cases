On appeal from the order of Justice Colin L. Campbell of the Superior Court of Justice, dated March 24, 2006, with reasons reported at (2006), 20 M.P.L.R. (4th) 27.

GOUDGE J.A.:

[1]               The appellant Blandford Square Developments Limited (“Blandford”) owns certain shopping mall lands in Woodstock, Ontario. The appellant Vincorp Financial Ltd. holds a $9 million mortgage on those lands. The respondent Corporation of the County of Oxford (“ Oxford”) wants to expropriate the mall lands and has passed certain by-laws seeking to effect this. Oxford has contracted to then sell the mall lands to Toyota Canada at the expropriation price it paid to Blandford, as part of the land assembly required for the building of a large new Toyota plant in Woodstock.

[2]               The appellants have sued Oxford for a declaration that it does not have the statutory authority or jurisdiction to expropriate in these circumstances. They also seek substantial damages for breach of statutory authority, unlawful interference with economic interest, and conspiracy to unlawfully expropriate.

[3]               This appeal results from three motions heard together, one by each appellant and one by Oxford. A number of different orders were sought in each motion. All were dismissed, save for Oxford’s request for summary judgment, which was granted in part, dismissing the appellants’ claim to set aside the expropriation as “per se” unlawful or without jurisdiction. The appellants appeal this partial granting of summary judgment.

[4]               For the reasons that follow, I would allow the appeal and dismiss the entirety of Oxford’s motion for summary judgment.

THE FACTS

[5]               The respondent Corporation of the City of Woodstock (“ Woodstock”) is an incorporated municipality that lies within the boundaries of Oxford. As between the two respondents, Oxford is an “upper-tier” municipality under the Municipal Act, 2001, S.O. 2001, c. 25, and is the appropriate expropriating authority for the purposes of the Expropriations Act, R.S.O. 1990, c. E.26.

[6]               In February 2005, Oxford commenced the assembly of a parcel of 1,000 acres of land in Woodstock to provide for the development of a new Toyota plant. The parcel was made up of 28 properties, one of which was the mall lands comprising approximately 91.52 acres.

[7]               Oxford acquired 27 of the 28 properties by way of negotiation. However, it was unable to come to terms with Blandford, and therefore took steps to expropriate the mall lands.

[8]               The first step was that on May 11, 2005, Oxford passed By-law 4545-2005 granting it authority to make application for approval to expropriate the mall lands.

[9]               On June 30, 2005, Toyota announced publicly that the Woodstock location had been selected for its new plant. The same day, Oxford requested the Minister of Municipal Affairs and Housing to exercise his authority to issue a Minister’s Zoning Order changing the zoning of all the lands in the parcel to permit industrial uses.

[10]          On July 21, 2005, Oxford entered into an agreement with Toyota providing that Oxford will transfer the mall lands in return for the same amount that Oxford has to pay to acquire them.

[11]          On August 17, 2005, pursuant to s. 47(1) of the Planning Act, R.S.O. 1990, c. P.13, the Minister issued a Minister’s Zoning Order changing the zoning of the lands in the 1,000 acre parcel to permit industrial uses.

[12]          On October 12, 2005, Oxford adopted Official Plan Amendment 99 changing the land use designations within the 1,000 acre parcel to “Traditional Industrial”, but also incorporating the recommendation of its planning department that the mall lands should retain their status as a permitted use and be permitted to operate and expand in accordance with their current “Shopping Centre Commercial” zoning.

[13]          On December 21, 2005, Oxford passed By-law 4638-2005 approving the expropriation of the mall lands.

[14]          The appellants then commenced their actions.

[15]          On March 6, 2006, the three motions were heard by the motion judge. The appellants each sought injunctive relief restraining the respondents until trial from taking any further steps to expropriate the mall lands. Oxford, supported by Woodstock, moved

(a)       to strike the appellants’ statements of claim as disclosing no reasonable cause of action;

(b)       to strike part of those statements of claim as being offensive; and

(c)       for an order granting summary judgment dismissing the appellants’ claims.

[16]          The motion judge dismissed the appellants’ claims for injunctive relief. He also dismissed the respondents’ motion to strike the appellants’ pleadings.

[17]          With respect to Oxford’s motion for summary  judgment to dismiss the appellants’ claims, the motion judge concluded as follows, at para. 69:

Since the factual differences could give rise to different material factual conclusions for the purposes of various tort claims, I have concluded that summary judgment relief (with exception of the one item mentioned below) should not be granted at this time.

[18]          The exception was the appellants’ claim that Oxford’s actions and the resulting expropriation are illegal “per se”. The motion judge stated this claim as follows, at para. 9:

The position of the Plaintiffs is that Oxford’s expropriation of the Mall Lands is illegal per se, being contrary to the provisions of s. 106(1) of the Municipal Act, contrary to s. 24(1) of the Planning Act, and that the lands were taken in bad faith and without statutory authority, as they were not necessary for the success of the Toyota plant development. [Footnotes omitted.]

[19]          He then granted summary judgment dismissing this claim as follows, at paras. 70, 72, and 77:

The stated position of the Plaintiffs on these motions is that there is a valid and important public purpose in the economic benefit to be delivered from the Toyota Plant and they acknowledge that expropriation to permit such development can be lawfully and properly achieved under valid provincial legislation.

…

It does not seem to me that there is any evidence on which (with the above admissions) a Court could conclude that the expropriation itself or the actions of the Province were in the Plaintiffs’ words “per se” illegal or without jurisdiction. The Province would be a necessary party for the purposes of that determination.

…

Absent specific relief to which the Province would of necessity be an essential party, the Court would in my view be without jurisdiction to grant the relief that the expropriation of the Mall Lands is “per se” illegal. To this limited extent summary judgment will issue in favour of the Defendants.

[20]          It is only this order that is under appeal.

ANALYSIS

[21]          The appellants claim that Oxford has acted without lawful authority in expropriating the mall lands. They support this claim with three arguments.

[22]          First, they argue that Oxford has used its expropriation power to acquire the mall lands from Blandford for the benefit of Toyota contrary to ss. 106(1) and (2)(c) of the Municipal Act, by turning them over to Toyota at the same price paid to expropriate them.

[23]          Sections 13 and 14 of the Expropriations Act provide that the appropriate expropriation price is based upon the amount that a willing buyer would pay to a willing seller in the open market, but without taking account of the impact of the development proposed for the lands on that value.

[24]          The appellants say that the expropriation price is less than the fair market value of the lands and therefore contravenes ss. 106(1) and (2)(c) of the Municipal Act. These sections read as follows:

106. (1) Despite any Act, a municipality shall not assist directly or indirectly any manufacturing business or other industrial or commercial enterprise through the granting of bonuses for that purpose.

(2)       Without limiting subsection (1), the municipality shall not grant assistance by,

…

(c)              leasing or selling any property of the municipality at below fair market value; or

[25]          Second, they argue that Oxford’s by-law expropriating the mall lands for Toyota’s plant does not conform to Official Plan Amendment 99, because that industrial use conflicts with the use permitted by the Amendment of operating and expanding within the context of the existing “Shopping Centre Commercial” zoning. They say that this contravenes s. 24(1) of the Planning Act which reads:

24. (1)  Despite any other general or special Act, where an official plan is in effect, no public work shall be undertaken and, except as provided in subsections (2) and (4), no by-law shall be passed for any purpose that does not conform therewith.

[26]          Third, they argue that in the exercise of its expropriation power, Oxford breached its duty to act in good faith, and that this too supports their claim that Oxford has acted without lawful authority in expropriating the mall lands.

[27]          The appellants attack the decision of the motion judge to grant summary judgment dismissing their claim essentially in two ways.

[28]          First, they point to the fact that the motion judge based his decision in part on his view that, to grant the relief sought, the Province of Ontario would have to be a party to the action, and it is not.

[29]          With respect, I do not think that this is an impediment. The appellants make no claim that the Province has acted unlawfully or that any provincial legislation is invalid. Nor do they seek a remedy against the Province. They simply say that Oxford has acted without lawful authority in the ways described. Granting relief that says so does not require that the Province be a party to these actions.

[30]          They then argue that the motion judge erred in finding that there was no evidence on which it could be concluded that Oxford has acted without lawful authority. They say that he erred in concluding that, on this basis, the respondents have established that this claim is not a genuine issue for trial.

[31]          I agree with this submission. In my view, none of the three arguments made by the appellants can properly be resolved at this stage of the proceedings. I would conclude that the respondents have failed to show that this claim is not a genuine issue for trial.

[32]          The appellants’ argument that Oxford has conferred an unlawful bonus on Toyota by expropriating the mall lands in order to turn them over to Toyota at the expropriation price requires evidence that this price is below the fair market value of the mall lands. There is much evidence to support that assertion, just as there is much evidence the other way. Indeed, the motion judge describes this as the most significantly contested factual issue between the parties. It will take a trial to determine what the fair market value of the mall lands was in the spring of 2005.

[33]          It should be said that Oxford’s motion was neither put forward nor decided as a question of law on an agreed statement of fact: namely that, accepting that the actual fair market value of the mall lands exceeds the expropriation price, as a matter of fact, as a matter of statutory interpretation, the “fair market value” in s. 106(2)(c) of the Municipal Act should be equated to the expropriation value as determined under the Expropriations Act. Rather, the respondents continue to assert that, as a matter of fact, the expropriation price is not below the fair market value of the mall lands. As I have said, there is ample evidence on both sides of this factual assertion and it cannot be properly disposed of at this stage of the action.

[34]          The appellants’ second argument in support of its claim also cannot be properly resolved at this point. Whether the expropriation is unlawful because it is for an industrial use contrary to the zoning provided for the mall lands by Official Plan Amendment 99 requires an elucidation of the factual circumstances surrounding the adoption of the Amendment. Only that will permit its true meaning to be determined. There is clearly some evidence from which it could be inferred that the Amendment was intended to continue the zoning of the mall lands as “Shopping Centre Commercial”. The respondents assert the opposite. Which of these prevails would appear to require a trial. At the least, it cannot be said that at this point it is not a genuine issue for trial.

[35]          I reach the same conclusion about the appellants’ third argument, namely that Oxford has acted in bad faith and the resulting expropriation by-law was passed without lawful authority. It cannot be said that there is no evidence on which this argument could succeed. For example, the appellants rely on their evidence of the discussions that took place between the principal of Blandford and the agents of Oxford as an instance of bad faith. Here too, the trial judge acknowledged that the conflicting evidence raised an issue of material fact. This would also require a trial.

[36]          I therefore conclude that there is at least some evidence supporting each of the arguments on which the appellants base their claim that Oxford has acted without lawful authority in expropriating the mall lands. The respondents have thus failed to establish that this claim is not a genuine issue for trial.

[37]          I would therefore allow the appeal and order that the respondents’ motion for summary judgment be dismissed in its entirety.

[38]          The appellants seek no change in the costs order made by the motion judge. On appeal, while the appellants were separately represented, they filed a single factum and a single set of materials and split the argument. Given their success, the appellants are entitled to their costs from the respondents, payable on a partial indemnity basis. We have received draft bills of costs and having considered them together with the length and general nature of this appeal, I would fix those costs at $42,000.00 inclusive of disbursements and G.S.T. If the appellants cannot work out a division of these costs between themselves, the court may be spoken to.
