On appeal from the sending order issued by Justice Michael R. Dambrot of the Superior Court of Justice, dated November 24, 2006, with reasons reported at [2006] O.J. No. 3726 and [2006] O.J. No. 5507.

SHARPE J.A.:

[1]               This appeal involves the Mutual Legal Assistance in Criminal Matters Act, R.S.C. 1985, c. 30 (4th Supp.), s. 15 and whether a judge has the discretion to send to the requesting state materials improperly seized under a defective search warrant.

[2]               At the request of the U.S. Attorney for the Southern District of Illinois, a search warrant was obtained by the Royal Canadian Mounted Police to search the appellants’ business premises and seize business records relating to an alleged fraudulent telemarketing scheme.  The search warrant was facially overbroad and when the search was executed, the seizure exceeded the terms of the warrant.  On the application to send the materials seized to the United States , the application judge found that despite the overbreadth of the warrant and the over-seizure in its execution, the Act gave him the discretion to send to the requesting state those materials that could properly have been seized under a valid warrant.  The appellants contend that the application judge erred and that he should have ordered that the seized materials be returned.

STATUTORY PROVISION

[3]               For convenience, I set out here s. 15 of the Act, the statutory provision at issue on this appeal:

 

15. (1) At the hearing to consider the execution of a warrant issued under section 12, after having considered any representations of the Minister, the competent authority, the person from whom a record or thing was seized in execution of the warrant and any person who claims to have an interest in the record or thing so seized, the judge who issued the warrant or another judge of the same court may

(a) where the judge is not satisfied that the warrant was executed according to its terms and conditions or where the judge is satisfied that an order should not be made under paragraph (b), order that a record or thing seized in execution of the warrant be returned to

(i) the person from whom it was seized, if possession of it by that person is lawful, or

(ii) the lawful owner or the person who is lawfully entitled to its possession, if the owner or that person is known and possession of the record or thing by the person from whom it was seized is unlawful; or

(b) in any other case, order that a record or thing seized in execution of the warrant be sent to the state or entity mentioned in subsection 11(1) and include in the order any terms and conditions that the judge considers desirable, including terms and conditions

(i) necessary to give effect to the request mentioned in that subsection,

(ii) with respect to the preservation and return to Canada of any record or thing seized, and

(iii) with respect to the protection of the interests of third parties.
 

(2) At the hearing mentioned in subsection (1), the judge may require that a record or thing seized in execution of the warrant be brought before him.

FACTS

[4]               At the request of the U.S. Attorney for the Southern District of Illinois, approved by the Minister of Justice of Canada, an RCMP officer applied for and obtained a search warrant in relation to a U.S. investigation into a fraudulent telemarketing scheme allegedly operated by a number of entities, including those known as “Prime One” and “Veritech”, under the direction of the appellants.  The premises of Prime One were identified in the letter of request as being 60 St. Clair Avenue East, Toronto.  Prior to the issuance of the search warrant, but after the RCMP had determined that Prime One was no longer at 60 St. Clair, a subsequent letter from the U.S.Attorney “update[ing] our request for an MLAT search warrant previously approved by [the Minister]” asked for the search to be conducted at 274 Eglinton Avenue West.  The RCMP officer swore that he had attended at 60 St. Clair Avenue and found that Prime One was not a tenant at that building.  He further swore that he had received information from Bell Security that Prime One’s telephone number had been redirected to another location, namely to 274 Eglinton Avenue West.  His affidavit also contained certain observations he made upon visiting the Eglinton premises which tended to show that the appellants were using those premises. 

[5]               Although the list of items contained in the affidavit was broad, the application judge found that it “provided a basis for the limitation of the search within reasonable bounds.”  However, the search warrant, granted ex parte by a judge of the Superior Court of Justice, identified only general categories and did not restrict or limit the scope of the search to the timeframe or nature of the alleged fraud.

[6]               The search warrant was executed and approximately twenty bankers’ boxes of materials were seized from the premises at 274 Eglinton Avenue West.

Proceedings before the Superior Court of Justice

[7]               The appellants initially brought applications to quash the search warrant and secure the return of the seized materials.  These applications were withdrawn when the respondent quite properly took the position that certiorari does not lie as against the order of a justice of the Superior Court.

[8]               The matter subsequently proceeded before the Superior Court on the respondent’s s. 15 application for an order to send the seized material to the requesting state.  Counsel for the respondent conceded that the search warrant was overbroad.  He also conceded that there had been an over-seizure in that some items seized were not identified in the warrant, although he took the position that most of this material was insignificant detritus, having no informational significance, collected because it was entwined with material that did fall within the terms of the warrant.  The respondent further conceded that all but one and one-half of the twenty boxes of seized documents should be returned to the appellants. 

[9]               The application judge found that despite the overbreadth of the warrant and the over-seizure arising from its execution, he had the discretion to make a sending order for the material for which a judge issuing a search warrant could properly conclude that warrant should issue.  His reasons for this decision were released on September 19, 2006, and reported at [2006] O.J. No. 3726 (“the September reasons”).  At the end of those reasons, the application judge directed counsel to review the one and one-half boxes to ensure that they were evidence of the offences in question.  Counsel for the appellants refused to participate in this review to preserve their position for appeal purposes.  A second hearing then took place and on November 24, 2006, the application judge released reasons, reported at [2006] O.J. No. 5507 (“the November reasons”), in which he reviewed the material, on an item-by-item basis, to determine what could properly be sent to the United States.

ISSUES

[10]          The appellants raise the following issues:

(1)  Did the application judge err by finding that the evidence filed in support of the warrant was sufficient to provide a reasonable conclusion that materials found by the search warrant would be found at the Eglinton location?

(2)  Did the application judge err by concluding that he had the discretion to make a sending order despite his finding that the search warrant had not been executed according to its terms and conditions?

(3)  Did the application judge err by concluding that he could make a sending order despite his finding that had this been a domestic search warrant it would have been quashed because it was overly broad?

(4)  Did the application judge err by reviewing each of the seized items seized under the overbroad warrant to determine which items could properly have been seized under a validly drawn warrant and making a sending order with respect to those items?

ANALYSIS

(1) Did the application judge err by finding that the evidence filed in support of the warrant was sufficient to provide a reasonable conclusion that materials found by the search warrant would be found at the Eglinton location?

[11]          The appellants argue that the affidavit evidence supporting the search at 274 Eglinton Avenue West was insufficient to support an inference that evidence of the alleged foreign offences would be found there.  Although this argument was not at the forefront of the appellants’ attack on the sending order, I deal with it first as it stands apart from the other three related issues.

[12]          The application judge dismissed this argument at para. 17 of his September reasons:

I am satisfied that there was material upon which the issuing judge could conclude that the warrant should issue.  In particular, I am of the view that he was entitled to conclude that there were reasonable grounds to believe that the items sought were evidence of the offences described in the affidavit, and were in the named premises.

[13]          I see no basis to interfere with this finding.  A search warrant will be quashed when there were no reasonable grounds for the issuing judge to believe that the items sought were evidence of the offences described in the affidavit or when there were no reasonable grounds to believe that a search of the identified location would find evidence of those offences.  However, the standard is not an exacting one: see Scott C. Hutchison, James C. Morton & Michael P. Bury, Search and Seizure Law in Canada, looseleaf, vol. 1 (Scarborough: Thomson Carswell, 2005) at 16—18.  “A practical, reasonable and non-technical probability that evidence is available, is all that is required.”  The appellants had vacated the St. Clair Avenue address and there was some evidence linking them to the Eglinton address.  I would not give effect to this ground of appeal.

(2) Did the application judge err by concluding that he had the discretion to make a sending order despite his finding that the search warrant had not been executed according to its terms and conditions?

[14]          The appellants submit that where a judge finds that the warrant was not executed according to its terms and conditions, the judge must order that all materials seized be returned to the owner; the judge has no discretion to order that any materials be sent to the requesting state.  The appellants rely on a passage from R. v. Gladwin (1997), 116 C.C.C. (3d) 471 at para. 8 (Ont. C.A.), leave to appeal to S.C.C. refused, [1997] S.C.C.A. No. 325, for the proposition that there is no discretion to make a sending order in the face of a determination that the warrant was not executed according to its terms.  In Gladwin, supra, at para. 8, Finlayson J.A. noted “the awkward language of this section, couched as it is in negatives and coupled with the disjunctive ‘or’ instead of the conjunctive ‘and’” and made the following observation:

[T]his section imposes a double burden upon the judge called upon to make an order under s. 15(1)(b) of the Act sending the material seized to the foreign state.  He or she is obliged under s. 15(1)(a) to review what has transpired to the date of the return of the application.  Before he can order that the record or thing seized be sent to the foreign state, the reviewing judge must be satisfied, first, that the warrant was executed according to its terms and conditions and, second, that he is satisfied that there is no reason why the order should not be made. … [T]he inherent nature of this second condition necessarily bestows discretion on the reviewing judge to consider all relevant factors bearing on the application. [Emphasis added.]

[15]          In his September reasons, at para. 22, the application judge rejected the proposition that s. 15(1) should be interpreted in the manner suggested by the appellants.  He found that the use of the word “may” imported a discretion and that the interpretation advocated by the appellants would make little sense as a matter of policy as it would preclude a sending order even in the case of a minor or inadvertent failure to comply with the terms of the warrant.  As for Gladwin, the application judge pointed out that Finlayson J.A.’s comments were obiter as the issue under consideration here did not arise on the facts of that case.

[16]          I agree with the application judge’s reasoning.  In my view, the passage quoted above from Gladwin should not be read as conclusively determining that on a s. 15 application, the judge has no discretion to make a sending order even where the departure from the terms of the warrant was minor or insignificant.  That was not the point at issue in Gladwin and Finalyson J.A.’s comment must be read in that light.  While the wording of s. 15(1) is not a model of clarity, I fail to see how one can ignore Parliament’s use of the word “may” as the preface to the description of the powers of the judge charged with the responsibility to determine whether it is appropriate to make a sending order.  The word “may” suggests the existence of a discretion.

[17]          I also agree that it would be inappropriate, as a matter of policy, to adopt an interpretation of s. 15(1) that would require the judge to take a purely mechanical approach and refuse a sending order for every minor or trivial failure in the execution of the warrant.  In the domestic context, we do not take that approach when deciding whether or not to exclude evidence under s. 24(2) of the Charter.  To apply a higher standard under the Act would hardly advance its purpose and object.  That purpose was described by Doherty J.A. in Russian Federation v. Pokidyshev (1999), 138 C.C.C. (3d) 321 at para. 15 (Ont. C.A. ) as being to ensure Canada is able to assist in combating the problem of international crime and to fulfil our “international obligations under various treaties and conventions to assist other states in the investigation and detection of [international] crime”.  This assessment of the purpose of the Act was reiterated by Rosenberg J.A. in R. v. Budd (2000), 150 C.C.C. (3d) 108 at para. 28 (Ont. C.A. ), leave to appeal to S.C.C. refused, [2001] S.C.C.A. No. 57, referring to “the need to ensure that Canada ’s international obligations are honoured and [the need] to foster cooperation between investigative authorities in different jurisdictions” when interpreting and applying s. 15.

[18]          That said, a judge hearing the application for a sending order does not, in my view, have an unlimited or untrammelled discretion.  The discretion must be exercised in a manner that comports with the language and the overall purpose of the Act.  The language of s. 15(1)(a) clearly indicates that the lawful execution of the warrant is of primary concern.  The judge must rigorously scrutinize the manner in which the warrant was executed to determine whether there was any significant or meaningful failure to execute the warrant according to its terms.  If there was, the wording of the section requires the judge to refuse to make a sending order.

[19]          In this case, I am satisfied that the application judge fulfilled that duty and that his decision to make a sending order fell within the limits of the discretion allowed under the statute.  While there was what the application judge described as “a significant over-seizure”, he concluded that the over-seizure “flowed largely from the overbreadth of the warrant,” not the failure to follow the terms and conditions of the warrant.  I agree with that assessment.

[20]          Accordingly, I would not give effect to the submission that the application judge erred by finding and exercising a discretion to make a sending order despite the failure of the authorities to execute the warrant according to its precise terms.  I now turn to the issue of overbreadth, the real cause of the over-seizure.

(3) Did the application judge err by concluding that he could make a sending order despite his finding that had this been a domestic search warrant it would have been quashed because it was overly broad?

[21]          The appellants submit that once the application judge had found that he would have quashed the warrant for overbreadth had it been a domestic warrant, he erred by making a sending order.

[22]          The difficulty with this submission is that it assumes that the role of the judge on a s. 15 application is the same as that of a judge asked to quash a search warrant before trial.  At that stage, the issue is simply the validity of the search warrant.  If the warrant is not valid, the applicant is legally entitled to have the warrant quashed and his property returned.

[23]          The proper analogy for a s. 15 application is to that of a trial judge asked to consider the admissibility into evidence of materials seized or their exclusion pursuant to s. 24(2) of the Charter.  In Gladwin, supra, at para. 9, this court analogized the role of a judge in a s. 15 hearing as “akin to that of a trial judge considering the admissibility into evidence of the things seized pursuant to a search warrant.”  Similarly in Budd, supra, at para. 26, this court held that “the impact of alleged Charter violations should be considered on the s. 15 hearing, where the judge is exercising a jurisdiction akin to that of a trial judge”. [Emphasis added.]

[24]          The concern of a trial judge is with the use of the evidence at the trial.  The validity of the warrant is a relevant factor, but there is no automatic exclusion of evidence obtained pursuant to an invalid search warrant; the issue falls to be resolved under the broad discretion of the trial judge.  The role performed by a judge conducting a hearing under s. 24(2) of the Charter is not precisely congruent to the role of a judge under s. 15. The matter is still at the investigative stage and the s. 15 judge is not in a position to assess the impugned evidence in terms of its importance or impact on the fairness of the trial.  However, it remains that just as at trial, the central focus and concern on a s. 15 review is on the use of the evidence against the respondent by the requesting state, rather than the narrower issue of the validity of the warrant and whether or not improperly seized evidence ought to be returned to the respondent.

[25]          In the terms of the Act, this is the second stage of the inquiry, under s.15(1)(b), where the judge “may” 

(b) in any other case, order that a record or thing seized in execution of the warrant be sent to the state or entity mentioned in subsection 11(1) and include in the order any terms and conditions that the judge considers desirable, including terms and conditions

(i) necessary to give effect to the request mentioned in that subsection,

(ii) with respect to the preservation and return to Canada of any record or thing seized, and

(iii) with respect to the protection of the interests of third parties.

[26]          The “inherent nature” of this exercise was characterized in Gladwin, supra, at para. 8, as “necessarily [bestowing] discretion on the reviewing judge to consider all relevant factors bearing on the application”.  In Gladwin, facial deficiencies in the warrant (the failure to name the executing officer) and the lack of specificity in indicating the nature of the offence committed (which was nevertheless apparent from an examination of the information and its appendices) did not preclude the sending judge from ordering the materials sent to the requesting state.  In so holding, Finlayson J.A. also emphasized, at para. 12, the relevance of good faith on the part of Canadian authorities in the execution of the warrant.

[27]          I conclude, accordingly, that the application judge did not err in finding that he had the discretion to make a sending order even though an application to quash the search warrant for overbreadth would have been successful.

(4) Did the application judge err by reviewing each of the seized items seized under the overbroad warrant to determine which items could properly have been seized under a validly drawn warrant and making a sending order with respect to those items?

[28]          The appellants submit that the application judge erred by conducting a detailed item-by-item review of the materials seized and then making a sending order with respect to those items that could have been seized under a properly drawn warrant.  They submit that this process compromises the integrity of the search warrant regime.  Simply put, the authorities were not required to “pay the price” for having proceeded under an improper warrant. By doing the detailed work of identifying the material that could have been properly seized at the sending hearing, the appellants submit that the application judge removed the discipline that should be imposed on the authorities to ensure respect for the constitutional rights of the appellants prior to the search.

[29]          This argument has considerable weight but I am not persuaded that it should carry the day in the circumstances of this case.  I reach that conclusion because I am satisfied that the application judge was fully aware of the need to protect the integrity of the search warrant regime and the constitutional rights of the appellants.  However, as he was entitled to do, he also weighed in the balance other relevant factors that, in the end, persuaded him to make the order he did.

[30]          At para. 23 of his September reasons, the application judge indicated that he took into account “in particular, the seriousness of the overseizure, the facial validity of the warrant…and the conduct of the state generally in this matter [citation omitted].”  He added, however, “I must also take into consideration the need to ensure that Canada ’s international obligations are honoured and to foster co-operation between investigative authorities in different jurisdictions [citation omitted].”  The application judge further provided, at para. 24 of his September reasons, a detailed list of the factors he considered in the exercise of his discretion:

(a)

  

there was a significant overseizure in this case, flowing largely from the overbreadth of the warrant;

  

(b)

  

the authorities have retained possession of the things that ought not to have been seized for almost two years, instead of endeavouring to find a way to return them;

  

(c)

  

the report pursuant to s. 14(1) of the Act was lacking in detail;

  

(d)

  

the warrant is facially defective, because it does not appropriately limit the things to be seized;

  

(e)

  

such a defect is serious, because it could result, and in this case it did result in significant overseizure, and a significant, unjustified intrusion into privacy;

  

(f)

  

this unjustified intrusion into privacy related to a business premises and business documents, not a private residence and personal papers;

  

(g)

  

neither the overbreadth nor the overseizure were intentional, but they must have flowed from negligence or from an inattention to the law;

  

(h)

  

it is possible, on review, to determine if the documents and records seized are evidence of the commission of the offences in question, despite the inadequacy of the warrant, on the basis of the affidavit filed in support of the warrant;

  

(i)

  

the intended beneficiary of the seizure, the United States of America , bears no responsibility for the overbreadth or overseizure;

  

(j)

  

having regard to our international obligations, it is hard to justify denying the United States access to relevant documents seized under this warrant; and

  

(k)

  

if I order the return of the documents and records, nothing prevents the authorities from seizing them again with a proper warrant or order (R. v. Gladwin, supra, at para. 12).

  

[31]          He concluded, at para. 25 of his September reasons, that having “regard in particular to the last three” factors, it would not be appropriate to return the portion of the materials that could have been seized had the search warrant contained the appropriate limitations.

[32]          I certainly do not wish to be taken as saying that a judge entertaining a s. 15 application is required to conduct the kind of item-by-item review that occurred here where a significant quantity of material has been improperly seized.  Indeed, it is my view that such a procedure should be the exception rather than the rule as it could, if routinely used, undermine the integrity of the constitutionally mandated search warrant regime.

[33]          On the other hand, the language of s. 15 is inconsistent with the all-or-nothing approach advocated by the appellants.  The judge is directed to consider whether to order that any “a record or thing seized” should be sent to the requesting state.  Similarly, s. 15(2) provides that “the judge may require that a record or thing seized in execution of the warrant be brought before him.” In my view, this statutory language indicates that the judge is entitled, where appropriate, to consider the material at issue on an item-by-item basis.  

[34]          Drawing on the analogy to s. 24(2) of the Charter and applying the values of the protection against unreasonable search and seizure under s. 8 of the Charter, a judge hearing a s. 15 application is certainly not required to take a mechanical approach and refuse to make a sending order simply because the search warrant was defective.  The core question under s. 8 of the Charter is “whether in a particular situation the public’s interest in being left alone by government must give way to the government’s interest in intruding on the individual’s privacy in order to advance its goals, notably those of law enforcement” (Hunter v. Southam, [1984] 2 S.C.R. 145 at 159-60).  The issue under s. 24(2) is whether allowing the authorities to use improperly seized items would “bring the administration of justice into disrepute”.  As I read his reasons, the application judge in this case carefully identified and weighed the appropriate competing concerns and concluded that, in the circumstances of this case, a sending order of selected items was appropriate despite the defective warrant.  Another judge might well have reached a different conclusion but I am not persuaded that this court should interfere with the exercise of the application judge’s discretion in this case.

CONCLUSION

[35]          For these reasons, I would dismiss the appeal.
