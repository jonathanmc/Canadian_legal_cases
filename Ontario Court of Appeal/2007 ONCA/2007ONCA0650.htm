R.A. BLAIR J.A.:

Introduction

[1]               On September 25, 1996, Chester Lam was injured while sparring with a fellow classmate following formal judo class sessions at the University of Windsor Judo Club.  As a result of the injuries he sustained, he became a quadriplegic.

[2]               The claims of Mr. Lam and his family members were ultimately settled and paid by the appellant, as the University’s insurer.  In this action the appellant seeks contribution to that settlement from the insurer for the Ontario Judo Black Belt Association (known as “Judo Ontario”).  It argues that Judo Ontario’s policy provided overlapping coverage to Mr. Lam’s judo instructors, and that the trial judge erred in concluding otherwise.

[3]               I agree and would allow the appeal for the following reasons.

Facts

[4]               The University of Windsor Judo Club (the “Club”) has existed at the University since 1958.  It was not an incorporated entity at the time of the accident.  Judo Ontario is the governing body for member clubs in Ontario. The Club was a registered member of Judo Ontario – in the words of the witnesses – for the academic year 1994/1995. However, it did not take steps to “re-register” for the academic year 1995/1996, as its numbers did not warrant doing so. While the Club did re-register as a member of Judo Ontario for the academic year 1996/1997, it did not do so until after the accident giving rise to this litigation.[1]

[5]               Ron Hamel was the black belt sponsor of the Club at the time Mr. Lam sustained his injuries.  However, he was ill in September 1996 and asked Jason Temple, a brown belt member of the Club, to take the class for him on the evening in question.  Mr. Hamel was present at the beginning of the evening to greet the judo participants and to sign up new members for the Club and Judo Ontario, but he left early leaving Mr. Temple in charge of the class.  Both Messrs. Hamel and Temple were members in good standing of Judo Ontario at the time.

[6]               After the class had ended, a number of participants, including Mr. Lam and Jeffrey Piesec, continued to spar together informally.  Mr. Lam was injured.  He and Mr. Piesec were not being supervised by Mr. Temple or anyone else at the time of the accident.

[7]               The Lams sued the University, Judo Ontario, Mr. Hamel and Mr. Temple (as the two instructors responsible for the class), and Mr. Piesec.  Their claims were settled for a total sum of $2.75 million. By agreement among the defendants to the Lam action, Canadian Universities Reciprocal Insurance Exchange (“CURIE”) funded the settlement, but each defendant’s liability and each insurer’s responsibility for any such liability were to be determined subsequently.

[8]               MacFarland J. (as she then was) presided over the trial with respect to each defendant’s liability.  She found the University, Ron Hamel and Jason Temple to be equally responsible for the plaintiffs’ damages.  Mr. Hamel was liable for failing to tell Mr. Temple not to leave the class members unsupervised to practice on their own.  Mr. Temple was liable for permitting the students to practice after the class had ended.  The University, as occupier of the premises where the accident occurred, was liable for failing to insist that the activities of the class be properly supervised.  Justice MacFarland found no liability on the part of either Mr. Piesec or Judo Ontario.

[9]               After MacFarland J. apportioned liability, a dispute arose as to each insurer’s responsibility. In particular, three insurers provided overlapping coverage as follows: the appellant, CURIE, provides coverage to the University and to the two judo instructors in that context (the “CURIE Policy”); the respondent, CGU, provides coverage to Judo Ontario and its Member Clubs under a policy originally underwritten by GAN Canada Insurance Company (the “GAN Policy”);[2] and State Farm Fire and Casualty Company, provides separate coverage to Mr. Temple.  CGU acknowledges that the GAN policy, for which it is responsible, constitutes primary coverage and the CURIE Policy excess coverage.  CGU disputes, however, that Messrs. Hamel and Temple were insured under the GAN Policy at the time of the accident.  This issue (which had not been before MacFarland J.) was determined by Justice W. Siegel in the proceedings that are the subject matter of this appeal.[3]  The parties agree that if the GAN Policy provides coverage, CGU is obliged to contribute its limit of $1 million to the settlement.

[10]          The trial judge determined, however, that the GAN Policy did not provide coverage.

The GAN Policy

[11]          GAN has insured Judo Ontario under a commercial general liability policy since 1988.  The declaration page of the GAN Policy identifies the named insured as “Judo Ontario and/or Member Clubs”.  Others may be unnamed insureds.  The GAN Policy states that the word “insured” means “any person or organization qualifying as such under Section II – WHO IS INSURED.”  Section II, paragraph 2 provides the following coverage:

2.      Each of the following is also an insured:

a.           Your employees or your volunteers, other than your executive officers, but only for acts within the scope of their employment by you or, in the case of volunteers, within the scope of their duties assigned by you.…

b.          Any member while participating in or training for a sanctioned sporting or social event. [Emphasis added].

[12]          The GAN Policy states that “[t]hroughout this policy the words ‘you’ and ‘your’ refer to the Named Insured shown in the Declaration.  The words ‘we’, ‘us’ and ‘our’ refer to the Company providing this insurance.”  Thus, the words “your” and “yours” in paragraph 2(a) of the GAN Policy refer to Judo Ontario or to a Member Club.

[13]          A “sanctioned event” is defined in Endorsement No. 2 of the GAN Policy:

It is understood and agreed that the insurance provided by this policy with respect to the playing of or taking part in practicing [sic] or training for sports shall apply only to sanctioned events as described below:

Sanctioned events shall mean all games, competitions or sports demonstrations run by you or by member clubs authorized by you including related training at sites of events and club premises.  Authorization can either be by way of a written procedure manual or specific agreement in writing by your authorized executives.

[14]          CGU acknowledges that training classes are included in the phrase “games, competitions or sports demonstrations” and that training classes by Member Clubs are “authorized” by Judo Ontario for purposes of Endorsement No. 2.  It contends, however, that the Club is not a “Member Club” and, accordingly, that the provisions of Section II, paragraph 2(a) and (b) of the GAN Policy do not apply to Messrs. Hamel and Temple.

[15]          CGU submits that coverage is not extended under Section II, paragraph 2(a) of the GAN Policy because, although Messrs. Hamel and Temple were “volunteers”, they were not acting within the scope of their duties assigned to them by a Member Club or, on the facts, by Judo Ontario.  Whether the Club was a “Member Club” of Judo Ontario is therefore the principal issue for determination on this appeal.  In my opinion, the governing By-Law of Judo Ontario is the primary reference source for that determination.

Judo Ontario’s Governing By-law

[16]          Section II of Judo Ontario’s governing By-law deals with membership.  Section 2.01 states that:

The membership of [Judo Ontario] shall consist of the incorporation applicants and such other persons as are admitted by the board.…

[17]          Section 1.01 defines “person” to include “individuals, bodies corporate, partnerships, syndicates, trusts, unincorporated organizations and any number of aggregate of persons” [emphasis added].  The Club is an unincorporated organization.

[18]          The additional provisions in the By-law that are pertinent to the membership issue are the following:

1.01(g)  “Member in good standing” means a person who is a member of [Judo Ontario] and who is not in arrears with regard to fees.

2.02  Resignation

Any member may resign by submitting his[4] written resignation to the secretary of [Judo Ontario] and it shall become effective upon its acceptance by the board.

2.03    Suspension and Expulsion

The board may suspend and expel any member for any of the following reasons:

(a)   if a member is more than six (6) months in arrears in his payment of fees,

(b)  if a member contravenes the by-laws of [Judo Ontario], or,

(c)  if a member has acted contrary to the standards of behaviour and ethics of Kodokan Judo.

Before this can be done, such member must be served with a written notice stating the alleged offence, and the fact that in the event of non payment of fees or non justification of his actions within thirty (30) days from the date of the notice, he shall be subject to suspension or expulsion upon resolution of the board of directors.

(d) In the event of a member being suspended or expelled from a club, the supervisor of said club shall notify the association.

2.04    Fees

The board of directors may, from time to time, fix fees payable by members.

2.05    Cessation of Membership

Any member who shall resign or be expelled as a member of [Judo Ontario] shall immediately return to [Judo Ontario] all books or other property of [Judo Ontario] which he may have in his care, and he shall remain liable for the payment of all monies due from him at the date of his resignation or expulsion from membership.

2.06    Member in Good Standing

Only a member in good standing who is a holder of a black belt degree shall be entitled to stand for election as an officer or director of [Judo Ontario] or to vote at any annual or general meeting of members.

2.07    Qualification for Membership

(a)   Any member must be a Canadian citizen and/or a resident of Ontario,

(b)  Any applicant who holds a black belt degree must present a certificate of his recognized degree at the time of his application.  The degree must be recognized by a member federation of the International Judo Federation.

2.08    Club Membership

Clubs requiring membership in the association shall submit to the board of directors a completed application for club membership, accompanied by fees, as determined by the board, and shall also be sponsored by a black belt member in good standing in the association.

2.09    Participation in Association Events

Participants in any association event shall be members in good standing of the association.

[19]          The trial judge made the following findings with respect to the organization of the Club, Judo Ontario’s membership practices, and the Club’s “registration” with Judo Ontario:

30        First, the By-laws contemplate registration of judo clubs as well as individuals. A judo club could apply for registration with Judo Ontario provided it was sponsored by a black belt registered with Judo Ontario. The application process consisted of completion of a registration form of Judo Ontario and submission of the requisite fee. It was understood that Judo Ontario expected the black belt sponsor of a judo club to be its head administrator.

31        Second, the only significance of a judo club failing to register with Judo Ontario in any year is that the individual members of the club are unable to attend competitions and other events and programmes held by Judo Ontario or its parent national body in that year, subject to being allowed to participate as a de facto member of another registered club.

32        Third, both Temple and Hamel were individual members in good standing of Judo Ontario at the time of the Accident. Neither was paid for any services they rendered to the Club or to any other judo club registered with Judo Ontario with which they had an association.

33        Fourth, the Club had existed since 1958 and was not an incorporated entity. The University gave the Club access to its facilities to conduct the Club's activities. Hamel, who was a black belt, ran the Club more recently and acted as the head administrator for the Club. While it is possible that the Club had been registered with Judo Ontario prior to the 1994/1995 academic year, there is no evidence to indicate any such registration. Hamel sponsored the Club in its application for membership in Judo Ontario in the 1994/1995 academic year and, accordingly, the Club was a registered member of Judo Ontario in the 1994/1995 academic year.

34        Fifth, while the Club was not registered with Judo Ontario during the 1995/1996 academic year, it continued to conduct its own training activities during that year. There are no legal or other requirements that prevented the Club from carrying on in this manner. It had only a small number of members during that academic year but, based on Hamel's evidence, I find that at least some of those members were registered individually with Judo Ontario during that year.

35        Sixth, university judo clubs were typically organized in September. In the case of the Club, while prospective members of the Club signed a waiver at their first session, they were permitted to attend a few sessions before being required to sign a registration form to become a member of Judo Ontario and to pay the requisite fees to the University and Judo Ontario. Hamel would obtain these fees and registration forms from all continuing members of the Club at one time at the end of September in each year. If there were enough members likely to participate in competitions conducted by Judo Ontario, Hamel would collect a further amount to pay the Judo Ontario Club membership fee and would register the Club with Judo Ontario, as he did in the 1994/1995 academic year and in the 1996/1997 academic year after the date of the Accident.

36        Lastly, Judo Ontario proceeded on the basis that a Club ceased to be a member of Judo Ontario at the end of a registration period if it failed to renew its registration by completing a new registration form and paying the requisite fee. This approach was also reflected in the view of representatives of Judo Ontario that the provisions of section 2.03 of the By-Laws were not applicable to judo clubs and, accordingly, only individual members could fall into the category of a member who was not in good standing. It was therefore not the practice of Judo Ontario to give notice to a member club of its default, or to take any other action, if the member club failed to pay its fees for a new registration period, although there may have been a practice of sending a renewal notice/invoice to each registered club at the end of its registration period or shortly into the following period similar to the notice/invoice sent annually to individuals. Judo Ontario simply treated such clubs as ceasing to be members after a period of time.

Analysis

            Is Coverage Provided Pursuant to Section II, Paragraph 2(a) of the Policy?

[20]          In my opinion, the GAN Policy extends coverage to Messrs. Hamel and Temple.  The key to unravelling this question lies in resolving whether the Windsor Club was a “Member Club” of Judo Ontario. Respectfully, the trial judge erred in concluding that it was not.  Messrs. Hamel and Temple were therefore volunteers acting within the scope of their duties assigned to them by a member club of Judo Ontario (GAN Policy, Section II, 2(a)).  I say this for the following reasons.

[21]          A “Member Club” is a named insured under the GAN Policy. It is therefore encompassed in the references to “you” and “your” in that policy. If Mr. Hamel and Mr. Temple were not volunteers carrying out duties assigned to them by Judo Ontario – I agree with the trial judge that they were not – they were clearly volunteers carrying out duties assigned to them by the Club.  Accordingly, if the Club was a Member Club of Judo Ontario at the relevant time, Section II, paragraph 2(a) of the GAN Policy provides coverage.

[22]          In my opinion, the Club was a Member Club at the time of Mr. Lam’s injury.

[23]          Judo Ontario’s By-law contemplates both individual members and club members.  The only distinction the By-Laws contemplate is that between a “member” and a “member in good standing”. It makes no other distinction. 

[24]          “Members” of Judo Ontario are those admitted to membership by the Judo Ontario board of directors (section 2.01).  I have noted earlier that the By-law makes no reference to “registration,” a term used by the various witnesses and picked up by the trial judge.  Although to lay people the term “registration” may well express the practical effect of filling out “registration” forms and sending in membership fees as a condition of being admitted to membership, members are “admitted” under the By-law; they are not “registered”.  In my view, this distinction between “admission” and the annual practice of “registration” has led to confusion about the circumstances in which a member may cease to be a member of Judo Ontario.

[25]          As I read the provisions of the By-law, members remain members unless they resign (section 2.02), or are suspended or expelled (section 2.03).  Neither happened with respect to the Club, which was admitted to membership in Judo Ontario during the 1994-1995 academic year.  While the Club had not paid its fee for the 1995-1996 academic year, and hence was not “registered” in the eyes of Judo Ontario, it was still a member. The failure of the Club to pay fees provided Judo Ontario with the grounds to suspend or expel the Club (section 2.03(a)); however, notice of neither its default nor its opportunity to cure the default within 30 days was given to the Club, and no suspension or expulsion resolution was passed by the board (section 2.03).  As a result, the Club remained a member of Judo Ontario, albeit not a “member in good standing”.

[26]          The trial judge accepted CGU’s argument – based on the evidence of Judo Ontario representatives and Mr. Hamel – that the requirements of section 2.03 did not apply to member clubs.  He cited two additional factors to buttress his conclusion, namely that section 2.03 did not unambiguously indicate that it included member clubs as well as individuals, and that there are other provisions in Section II that appear to be limited in operation to individual members (sections 2.06 and 2.07).  I disagree.

[27]          With respect to the latter point, I do not see how the fact that section 2.06 limits those who may be elected directors or officers, or those who are entitled to vote at meetings, to members in good standing who are holders of a black belt degree is relevant to whether section 2.03 applies to club members as well as individual members.  It is obvious that only individuals can be officers or directors or hold a black belt, and there may be no good reason for “clubs” to have a vote.  Secondly, I do not agree that the provisions of section 2.07(a), which requires that a member be a Canadian citizen and/or a resident of Ontario, can only apply to individuals; a club may have a residence in Ontario for purposes of membership.  Nor do I agree with the trial judge’s conclusion that section 2.09 (which requires that participants in association events be members in good standing) further buttresses his interpretation because it is intended to have reference only to individual members; I read section 2.09 as applying to all members, including member clubs. Finally, while section 2.06 (Officers and Directors) may be confined to individual members, s. 2.08 specifically deals with club memberships alone. This reinforces the interpretation that, read as a part of Section II as a whole, section 2.03 is intended to apply to both club and individual members.

[28]          This brings me to the evidence of the Judo Ontario representatives and Mr. Hamel – summarized in the trial judge’s findings recited in para. 19 above.  I do not view it as being dispositive of the issue of membership in Judo Ontario.  Rather, as suggested above, I see it as a reflection of the perspective of lay people concerning the practical operations of their organization.  Moreover, that perspective is quite consistent with the governing requirements of the By-law, bearing in mind the significance of the concept of “member in good standing”.

[29]          Judo Ontario and the clubs may have proceeded on the basis that the By-law resulted in automatic termination of clubs without further action of the board in the event of non-registration – as CGU argues the Judo Ontario representatives testified[5] – but what really happened in terms of the governing By-law was that those clubs ceased to be “members in good standing” for that period.  The practical effect was the same.  A club that was not a member in good standing could not participate in Judo Ontario events (section 2.09).  Consequently, its members could not benefit from participating in those events under that club’s banner.

[30]          I see no reason why the language of section 2.03 should not be given its plain and ordinary meaning: to be suspended or expelled from membership, a member – including a member club – must be given notice and an opportunity to cure the default and be subject to suspension or expulsion by resolution of the Judo Ontario board.  That did not happen with respect to the Club.  I am accordingly satisfied that at the time of the accident giving rise to Mr. Lam’s injury the Club remained a member of Judo Ontario.  It is therefore a named insured under the GAN Policy and coverage is provided to Mr. Hamel and Mr. Temple under Section II, paragraph 2(a) because they were volunteers acting within the scope of their duties assigned to them by the named insured at the time.

[31]          Mr. Monaghan raised a secondary argument under Section II, paragraph 2(b) of the GAN Policy, submitting that Mr. Hamel and Mr. Temple were members of Judo Ontario “participating in or training for a sanctioned sporting event”.  I think there is merit in this submission as well, once it has been determined that the Club remained a Member Club of Judo Ontario at the time in question.

[32]          Messrs. Hamel and Temple were members of Judo Ontario at the time of the accident.  CGU acknowledged at trial that training classes are intended to be covered by the phrase “games, competitions or sports demonstrations” in Endorsement No. 2 of the Policy, and that training classes at Member Clubs are “authorized” by Judo Ontario for purposes of the definition of a “sanctioned event” in the Policy.  The event was run by the Club.  While neither Mr. Hamel (who had left for the evening) nor Mr. Temple (who was left in charge) were directly participating in the post-session jousting, they were participating in a broad sense because Mr. Hamel was the person in charge, and on his departure, Mr. Temple became his delegate. 

Equitable Subrogation With Respect to Costs of the Proceedings Before MacFarland J.

[33]          At trial CURIE claimed that it was entitled to contribution from CGU respecting the costs of defending the Lam action and the costs of having Mr. Lam’s counsel participate in the proceedings before MacFarland J.  The parties agree on the quantum of this claim: $22,701.75.  It is based upon an insurer’s duty to defend.

[34]          The trial judge rejected CURIE’s claim in this regard on the basis that it was dependent upon CURIE “establishing that Temple and Hamel were entitled to coverage under the GAN Policy.”  In that regard he was in error since an insurer’s obligation to defend is “wider than, and independent of, its obligation to indemnify”: Broadhurst & Ball and Allport v. American Home Insurance Co. et al. (1990), 1 O.R. (3d) 225 ( C.A. ), at 231.  The test is not whether the insured is entitled to coverage but, rather, whether there might ultimately be coverage under the policy: Nichols v. American Home Assurance Co., [1990] 1 S.C.R. 801, at para. 11.

[35]          For the purposes of this appeal, however, this distinction is not pertinent, as I have concluded that the GAN Policy must respond to the event.  Accordingly, CURIE is entitled to recover CGU’s pro rata share of the expenses relating to the Lam action.

Disposition

[36]          For the foregoing reasons, I would allow the appeal, set aside the judgment of the trial judge, and in its place direct that judgment issue awarding CURIE:

a)         equitable contribution from CGU in the sum of $1 million;

b)         equitable subrogation in relation to the costs and expenses of the Lam action, in the amount of $22,701.75;

c)         pre-judgment and post-judgment interest on these sums pursuant to the provisions of the Courts of Justice Act R.S.O. 1990, C.43; and

d)         its costs of this action.

[37]          Costs of the appeal, payable to the appellant, are fixed in the amount of $25,000.00, inclusive of disbursements and GST, as agreed by counsel.  If the parties are unable to agree on the costs of the trial, those costs are referred back to the trial judge for determination.
