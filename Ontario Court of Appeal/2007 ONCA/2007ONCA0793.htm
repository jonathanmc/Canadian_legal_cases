INTRODUCTION

[1]               The appellant appeals his convictions for trafficking in a controlled substance, possession for the purpose of trafficking, and possession of property obtained by crime on the grounds that the trial judge misapprehended the evidence and improperly admitted hearsay evidence.  This appeal mainly examines the relationship between the traditional exception to the hearsay rule of a co-conspirator’s declaration and the more modern principled exception to hearsay evidence. 

BACKGROUND

[2]               In late October and early November 2002, the police conducted three undercover drug purchases.  Shortly after the completion of the third drug purchase, a search warrant was executed on an apartment where the appellant was found.   

[3]               When he was arrested, the appellant had some of the photocopied police buy money and just over 100 grams of marijuana on his person.  In the apartment bedroom, the police found a quantity of cocaine, a scale, a cell phone, a pager, and mail addressed to the appellant.  The Crown alleged that the appellant was the supplier to the drug dealer, Rhys Williams, who in turn sold cocaine to the undercover officers.

[4]               At trial, the undercover officer who completed the third purchase testified that she had conversations with Mr. Williams in which Mr. Williams indicated that he had to obtain drugs from his “guy” whom she believed to be his supplier.  She also testified that Mr. Williams then phoned the appellant and attended at the premises the appellant rented.  Surveillance officers confirmed that Mr. Williams had to be “buzzed” into the building because he did not have a key.

[5]               Mr. Williams was not called as a witness.  He was initially charged as a co-accused, but resolved his charges.  The trial judge pointed out in her reasons for judgment that there was no explanation given as to why he was not called. 

[6]               The appellant testified that he formerly lived at the residence that was searched but had moved out some time earlier and that Mr. Williams was taking over the lease.[1]  He denied being a drug dealer, and stated that he had only stopped by the apartment for a drink and to smoke a marijuana cigarette.  Mr. Williams, he said, had paid him in cash for expenses related to the apartment, which explained the presence of the drug buy money on his person.

[7]               I will refer to additional aspects of the background below where it is relevant to the issue being considered.  In the end, and for the reasons that follow, I would allow the appeal and order a new trial.

ISSUES

[8]               Two discrete issues were argued on this appeal:

1.         Did the trial judge err when she found that the appellant had powdered cocaine in his pocket when he was arrested?

2.         Did the trial judge err by relying on the common law co-conspirators’ exception to the rule against hearsay when the principled approach would have warranted exclusion because necessity was not established?

1.         Mistakes of Fact:

[9]                In her reasons for judgment and reasons for sentence, the trial judge indicated on several occasions that the appellant had powdered cocaine in his pocket when he was arrested.  The evidence was only that Mr. Williams had cocaine in his pocket upon his arrest.

[10]          Crown counsel acknowledges that the trial judge did err in this respect.  However, he submits that the case was so overwhelming against the appellant that it did not result in any trial unfairness, nor did it have an impact on the ultimate verdict.  Because I would allow the appeal on the hearsay evidence issue, there is no need to discuss this issue further.

2.         Hearsay Evidence:

[11]          The trial judge admitted various hearsay statements made by Mr. Williams to the undercover police officer.  She relied upon R. v. Mapara (2005), 195 C.C.C. (3d) 225 (S.C.C.), to rule that the evidence was admissible.  She found that the statements fell within the co-conspirators’ exception to the hearsay rule and that Mr. Williams’ availability to testify did not warrant their exclusion under the “rare case” exception outlined in R. v. Starr (2000), 147 C.C.C. (3d) 449. 

[12]          On appeal, the appellant challenges the trial judge’s decision to admit the police officer’s hearsay evidence regarding statements allegedly made by Mr. Williams that supported the finding that the appellant was his supplier.  The appellant did not challenge the trial judge’s finding that the hearsay evidence fell within the co-conspirators’ exception to the hearsay rule.  Rather, the appellant argues that since Mr. Williams was a compellable witness, there was no need to rely upon hearsay evidence.  The appellant argues that the trial judge’s analysis ignored this court’s decision in R. v. Chang (2003), 173 C.C.C. (3d) 397 (Ont. C.A. ).      

[13]          I believe it will be of some assistance to briefly first outline the two approaches to the admission of hearsay evidence that are in issue in this case.

(a)       The co-conspirators’ exception to the hearsay rule

[14]          The co-conspirators' exception to the hearsay rule allows the acts and declarations of an accused's alleged co-conspirators, done or made in furtherance of the conspiracy, to be adduced as evidence against the accused: R. v. Carter (1982), 67 C.C.C. (2d) 568 (S.C.C.).  Following Carter, co-conspirators’ statements will be admissible against an accused person if: (i) the trier of fact is satisfied beyond a reasonable doubt that a conspiracy existed; and (ii) if independent evidence, directly admissible against the accused person, establishes on a balance of probabilities that the accused was a member of the conspiracy.

(b)       The principled approach to hearsay evidence

[15]          The basic principle regarding hearsay evidence is that it is generally excluded because it is not the best evidence, as compared to direct evidence, and since it is not given under oath and generally cannot be tested by cross-examination, it may be unreliable.  However, hearsay evidence can be admitted if it falls under one of the traditional exceptions to the hearsay exclusionary rule.  Hearsay evidence can also be admitted if it is shown to be necessary and reliable.   Together, the requirements of necessity and reliability make up the “principled approach” to the admission of hearsay evidence: see R. v. Khan (1990), 59 C.C.C. (3d) 92 (S.C.C.). 

[16]          Later, in Starr, the Supreme Court of Canada held that the traditional categorical exceptions to the general exclusionary rule for hearsay are to be interpreted in a manner consistent with the principled approach to determine the admissibility of hearsay evidence. 

[17]          Starr also held that evidence falling within a traditional exception is presumptively admissible because the exceptions typically have an inherent component of necessity and reliability.  However, when an exception fails to conform to these principles, that is, in a “rare case”, the principled approach can override the exception to exclude the evidence. 

(c)       The effect of the principled approach on the co-conspirators’ exception

[18]          The issue of whether the direction in Starr, to re-evaluate the traditional hearsay exceptions having regard to the principled approach, applied to the rule allowing for the admissibility of co-conspirators' declarations has since been considered by this court in Chang, and by the Supreme Court of Canada in Mapara.  Both courts concluded that if the hearsay dangers are present, then the evidence must conform to the principled approach.   

[19]          As I noted, Starr creates the basic framework for analyzing how the common law exceptions to the rule against hearsay interact with the principled approach.  This framework is summarized succinctly by the majority in Mapara at para. 15:

(i)        Hearsay is presumptively inadmissible unless it falls under an exception to the hearsay rule.  The traditional exceptions to the hearsay rule remain presumptively in place.

(ii)       A hearsay exception can be challenged to determine whether it is supported by indicia of necessity and reliability, required by the principled approach.  The exception can be modified as necessary to bring it into compliance.

(iii)     In “rare cases”, evidence falling within an existing exception may be excluded because the indicia of necessity and reliability are lacking in the particular circumstances of the case.

(iv)      If hearsay evidence does not fall under a hearsay exception, it may still be admitted if indicia of reliability and necessity are established on a voir dire.

[20]          And, as Starr pointed out, the party challenging the admissibility of evidence falling within a traditional exception will bear the burden of showing that the evidence should nevertheless be inadmissible.

[21]          In Mapara, the Supreme Court held that the co-conspirators’ exception generally meets the requirements of the principled approach to the hearsay rule (para. 31).  The Court, at para. 18, described how the necessity requirement is built-in to the co-conspirator exception:

The criterion of necessity poses little difficulty.  As stated in Chang [at para. 105], “necessity will arise from the combined effect of the non-compellability of a co-accused declarant, the undesirability of trying alleged co-conspirators separately, and the evidentiary value of contemporaneous declarations made in furtherance of an alleged conspiracy.” [Emphasis added].

[22]          The requirement of reliability is also satisfied by this exception.  Relying on this court’s decision in Chang, the Chief Justice held at paras. 24, 26:

Proof that a conspiracy existed beyond a reasonable doubt and that the accused probably participated in it does not merely corroborate the statement in issue.  Rather, it attests to a common enterprise that enhances the general reliability of what was said in the course of pursuing that enterprise. …

 [T]he final Carter requirement, i.e., only those hearsay statements made in furtherance of the conspiracy can be considered,  provides guarantees of reliability in the more immediate circumstances under which the statement is made.  “In furtherance” statements “have the reliability-enhancing qualities of spontaneity and contemporaneity to the events to which they relate” (Chang, at paras. 122-23).  They have res gestae-type qualities, being “the very acts by which the conspiracy is formulated or implemented and are made in the course of the commission of the offence” (Chang, at para. 123).  This “minimizes the motive and opportunity for contrivance” (Chang, at para. 124). 

[23]          Nevertheless, evidence admissible under the Carter analysis is by definition hearsay: Chang, para. 79.  Thus, as was held in para. 85 in Chang:

… a statement that becomes admissible under the Carter process is hearsay and concerns about unreliability are very real.  Indeed, the dangers attached to the use of hearsay that give rise to the general exclusionary rule are all present in the case of a statement admitted under the Carter process.  The statement is not made under oath, is not subject to contemporaneous cross-examination, and the trier of fact has no opportunity to observe the declarant at the time the statement was made. [2]

[24]          So while Mapara holds that, in theory, the Carter exception satisfies the principled approach, it is nevertheless important to test the application of the exception to the particular circumstances for the indicia of necessity and reliability.  In “rare cases” where necessity and/or reliability are lacking, the trial judge may exclude the hearsay statements. 

ANALYSIS

[25]          In this case, Mr. Williams’ out-of-court declarations were to an undercover police officer who testified at trial.  The officer testified that Mr. Williams explained how he obtained drugs from his “guy”, phoned the appellant, and attended at the appellant’s residence.  The essence of the hearsay statements of Mr. Williams is reflected in these examples: “I can’t get it.  The guy who has the blow just left before you asked me.”  And, “The guy usually asks for more but when he sees it’s me it will only be $230.”   

[26]          In other words, the hearsay evidence consisted of utterances to an undercover police officer that implicated the appellant in criminal activity.  They were intended to show that Mr. Williams was arranging drug transactions through his supplier, the appellant.  The utterances were not made under oath.  Moreover, in their formal trial they were recollections of the undercover police officer who had later written them down in her notebook.  Further, Mr. Williams did not testify and, therefore, was not subject to any form of cross-examination or observation by the trier of fact. 

[27]          The main question in this appeal is whether these out-of-court statements allegedly made by Mr. Williams, a co-conspirator, fall within the ambit of the “rare case”.  In the “rare case”, as articulated in the third proposition from Mapara, supra, the evidence may be excluded for lacking the indicia of necessity and reliability.   

[28]          It is important to reiterate that it is the “combined effect” of the three justifications offered by the court in Chang and restated in Mapara that explains why the necessity requirement is met when a co-conspirator is being tried with the accused or is otherwise unavailable to testify.  Once again, they are: (i) the non-compellability of a co-accused defendant; (ii) the undesirability of trying alleged co-conspirators separately; and, (iii) the evidentiary value of contemporaneous declarations.  Significantly, only the third has application to the situation where the declarant is available to testify.

[29]          The appellant’s position hinges on the availability of the declarant, Mr. Williams.  The appellant makes a straightforward argument: Where a declarant is available and compellable to testify on behalf of the Crown, it is impossible to hold that the necessity requirement is met so that the declarant’s out-of-court statements may be admitted for their truth.  In other words, the first justification of necessity is not met.   

[30]          The Crown’s argument in response focuses on the third justification, which is the unique character of the evidence, and not on which witness introduces it to the court.  The assumption in this argument is that Mr. Williams — the available declarant in this case — would not be able to replicate the statements he made to the undercover police officer who bought drugs from him, which is sufficient to satisfy the necessity condition. 

[31]          In support of this argument, the Crown cites various non-binding authorities: a decision from the United States Supreme Court[3] and two decisions of the Ontario Superior Court of Justice.[4]  These authorities contemplate fact situations that are different from the facts in this case, however.  In the cited cases, either the statements at issue were direct communications between co-conspirators or the trial judge had found it unlikely that the co-conspirator would be a cooperative witness.    

[32]          Here, the communications were between an undercover police officer and a co-conspirator.  While Mr. Williams no doubt perceived the undercover officer to be a co-conspirator, his statements were recorded later-on by the officer from her perspective as an undercover officer, not as a co-conspirator.  This makes these statements potentially less reliable than the direct communication between two actual co-conspirators whose conversation was recorded contemporaneously.  In addition, there is no evidence that Mr. Williams would be an uncooperative witness.  These differences distinguish our case from the cases cited by the Crown, which did not exclude the hearsay statements.

[33]          I also disagree with the Crown’s submission that Chang and Mapara stand for the principle that the quality or unique character of the evidence admitted under the Carter test, by itself, satisfies the criterion of necessity.  Again, the principle of necessity is defined by a combination of three justifications, not just one alone.  

[34]          As for the appellant’s argument, this court in Chang specifically left open the question of whether the necessity requirement is met when the declarant is available.  In Chang, the appellant was tried jointly with his alleged co-conspirator.  On the issue of necessity, while the court considered the issue of whether the necessity requirement is met when the declarant is available, it explicitly chose to leave the question for another case (para. 110).  This is that case.

[35]          Similarly, it is clear to me that in Mapara the Supreme Court of Canada limited its decision on the issue of necessity to circumstances where the declarant was unavailable.[5]  For example, at para. 14 in Mapara, the Court observed that exceptions to the general rule excluding hearsay evidence evolved in response to the defects inherent in such evidence.  Thus, exceptions emerged when, “the evidence is necessary, in that the person who made the hearsay statement is not readily available.  And it is reliable, in the sense that something about it provides a circumstantial guarantee of trustworthiness.”  And again, at para. 37, the Court concluded that the case was not a “rare exception” because, in part, necessity was established due to the absence of direct evidence from the co-accused declarants.

[36]          There is no doubt in my mind that the availability of the declarant, in some circumstances, can support the “rare exception” in which the Carter test might yield to that required by Starr.  In other words, the availability of the co-conspirator declarant as a witness, may require that the declaration be adduced through the testimony of the declarant.

APPLICTION TO THIS CASE

[37]          The ultimate question to determine on this appeal, therefore, is whether the evidentiary value of contemporaneous declarations outweighs the traditional strong reluctance to admit out-of-court evidence, which has not been made under oath, has not been the subject of contemporaneous cross-examination, and where the trier of fact has no opportunity to observe the declarant at the time the statement was made: see Chang, at para. 85. 

[38]          There are points to be made on either side of this equation.  While other reasons will no doubt come to mind, I will refer to only a few.  

[39]          On the one hand, preventing the admission of out-of-court statements made by a co-conspirator who wishes to negotiate a guilty plea could have a potentially detrimental effect on the Crown’s case, and may make the Crown reluctant to accept such pleas.  This is especially true if refusing to negotiate and maintaining the case against the co-conspirator ensures that the statements are admissible pursuant to Mapara.  The pressure to avoid negotiation is contrary to an efficient administration of the criminal justice system and could penalize co-conspirators who wish to plead guilty in exchange for a more favourable sentencing recommendation from the Crown. 

[40]          On the other hand, the traditional reluctance to admit out-of-court statements is well-placed.  Unlike the other evidence admitted for the trier of fact to consider, the statements will not have been made under oath; they will not have been the subject of contemporaneous cross-examination; and, the trier of fact will have had no opportunity to observe the declarant at the time the statement was made. 

[41]          In R. v. Smith (1992) 75 C.C.C. (3d) 257 at 268, the Supreme Court of Canada stated that, “the circumstances under which the declarant makes a statement may be such as to guarantee its reliability, irrespective of the availability of cross-examination”.  Further, where the hearsay evidence is necessary – the declarant is unavailable and the contemporaneous declarations have evidentiary value – it becomes the best evidence available.

[42]          The trial judge addressed the necessity and reliability of the evidence briefly in her reasons for judgment.  She found the evidence to fall within the hearsay exception.  She also found that this was not a rare case where necessity and reliability were lacking.  On further analysis, I cannot agree with her conclusion.  In this case, the co-conspirator’s out-of-court statements were neither necessary nor reliable.  

Necessity

[43]          This is not a case where the Crown is faced with the situation of either tendering the hearsay evidence or having no evidence at all.  Rather, it appears that the Crown wishes to rely on the Carter hearsay exception because admitting the evidence this way instead of as testimony avoids the risks of unforeseen complications, which might discredit the reliability of the evidence.  For example, Mr. Williams may disagree with the undercover officer’s evidence about what he said; he might retreat from his earlier declarations; his evidence might suffer from an inability to fully recollect; or, he might have an explanation that is exculpatory of the appellant. 

[44]          To be fair, the Crown also says that the hearsay evidence is basically superior to the testimony of Mr. Williams.  In other words, the hearsay evidence is necessary because — without saying why — better evidence is not available.  It argues that this is a case where the circumstances are such that a truthful and accurate statement would naturally be uttered, and there was no reason for Mr. Williams to be deceptive.  I take this to be a reference to the third component in Chang, namely, a contemporaneous declaration made in furtherance of an alleged conspiracy.

[45]          However, as the Supreme Court of Canada observed in Smith at 271, “The criterion of necessity, however, does not have the sense of ‘necessary to the prosecution's case.’”   

[46]          In this case the statement was from a co-conspirator to an undercover police officer.  And, as I have noted, the evidence consisted of utterances implicating the appellant in criminal activity, which were recorded by the officer either hours after the transaction or the next day.  The intent of the evidence was to show that Mr. Williams was arranging drug transactions through his supplier.  At no time, however, does Mr. Williams identify his supplier.

[47]          The fact that Mr. Williams, in this case, resolved his matter before the appellant’s trial suggests that he has reason to lessen his role in the offences he faced and to place a greater role on the appellant, both of which are at least indicative that he might be a cooperative Crown witness.  Importantly, whether or not Mr. Williams would be a cooperative witness, or whether there was any meaningful prospect of obtaining evidence from him, was never established in this case: see R. v. Dionne, 193 C.C.C. (3d) 228 (Alta. C.A.).  Cooperation, however, is just one factor in the analysis.  A declarant’s willingness to be a cooperative witness will not in and of itself determine whether the declarant’s out-of-court statements will be excluded.    

Reliability

[48]          Although my conclusion on the issue of necessity is sufficient to dispose of this appeal, I will nevertheless comment briefly on the issue of the reliability of the statements.  I do so given that the trial judge considered the reliability of the evidence in reaching her decision, and for completeness.

[49]          In my view the hearsay evidence of Mr. Williams is not so reliable that cross-examination would add little to its reliability.  Unlike in Mapara, this is not a case where the out-of-court statements were entirely recorded using a wiretap. 

[50]          In this case, the officer testified that she made notes about her conversation with Mr. Williams later on in the evening or the following day.  Not only would this time lag create the possibility of inaccurate recording, her notes are also questionable insofar as she assumed that when Mr. Williams referred to his “guy” that he was speaking of his supplier.  While this inference may be reasonable, it is important to note that the trial judge disregarded the officer’s testimony where she offered opinions about the behaviour of drug trafficking for the reason that she did not have sufficient expertise in the area. 

[51]          These factors – the time lag and the officer’s insufficient expertise – are appropriate to consider in determining the reliability of the evidence at the admissibility stage.  In  R. v. Khelawon (2006), 215 C.C.C. (3d) 161 the Supreme Court made it clear that reliability need not be analyzed through the categories of threshold reliability and ultimate reliability (para. 4):   

[T]he factors to be considered on the admissibility inquiry cannot be categorized in terms of threshold and ultimate reliability.  Comments to the contrary in previous decisions of this Court should no longer be followed.  Rather, all relevant factors should be considered including, in appropriate cases, the presence of supporting or contradictory evidence.  In each case, the scope of the inquiry must be tailored to the particular dangers presented by the evidence and limited to determining the evidentiary question of admissibility.

[52]          Overall, given the time lag and the police officer’s limited expertise, the statements of Mr. Williams are not such as to guarantee their reliability without the availability of cross-examination.  This is not a case where it has been established that the interests of justice are better served by admitting the statements rather than calling the declarant as a witness.   

[53]          We must, of course, always strive to receive the best evidence available.  Miscarriages of justice must be avoided to the extent possible.  Thus, evidence that can be tested in court, particularly through cross-examination, should generally prevail whenever it is possible to present such evidence, in furtherance of trial fairness.  The unique circumstances of this type of hearsay evidence — where necessity is not met because the declarant is available and where reliability is not met because of the way in which the statements were recorded — merit an application of the principled approach.  I can find no compelling reason why it should not be applicable in this case. 

[54]          If the Crown wishes to rely on this evidence, it cannot do so through the out-of-court statements themselves.  The Crown should, therefore, call the declarant, Mr. Williams, as a witness to tender this evidence.  And, as this court referenced in Chang, if the declarant as a witness recants or gives evidence that is not consistent with the statement that the Crown seeks to have admitted, then the Crown can seek to confront the witness with the earlier statement under s. 9 of the Canada Evidence Act, R.S.C, 1985 c. C-5 or by having the witness declared adverse. 

CONCLUSION

[55]          I conclude, therefore, that the trial judge erred in relying on Mapara to admit the evidence through the undercover police officer without offering the defence an opportunity to cross-examine the declarant, Rhys Williams.   

[56]          For the foregoing reasons, I would allow the appeal, set aside the convictions and order a new trial.
