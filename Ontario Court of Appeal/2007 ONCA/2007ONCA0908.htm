TAXATION OF THE FEES AND DISBURSEMENTS OF COURT APPOINTED COUNSEL

1)      On April 12, 2005, the Sarnia Area Committee of Legal Aid Ontario, refused the appellant’s request for funding on his appeal for conviction and sentence of sexual assault.

2)      On August 25, 2005, the provincial Director of Appeals of Legal Aid Ontario refused the appellant’s appeal from the Area Committee’s decision.

3)      On September 28, 2005, The Honourable Justice Rosenberg, pursuant to s 684 (1) of the Criminal Code, appointed Delmar Doucette to investigate whether there was merit to Mr. George’s appeal.  Mr. Doucette reported his findings to the Court, stating that in his opinion there was “great merit to Mr. George’s conviction appeal.”

4)      On November 4, 2005, the Honourable Justice Rosenberg appointed Delmar Doucette as counsel for the appellant pursuant to s 684 (1) of the Criminal Code of Canada, in Her Majesty the Queen v. Darrell Lee George in reference to his conviction and sentence wherein he was found guilty of sexual assault, by the Honourable Justice J. McGarry. The appellant was convicted on November 25, 2003 and sentenced on March 22, 2004 to five years in prison.

5)      This is a case that involved an allegation of ineffective assistance of trial counsel due to a medical condition.  As a result, it was asserted that counsel was unable to properly prepare for the trial. Mr. Doucette, when appointed by the Court,  proceeded to retain a private investigator to gather the information that should have been gathered by trial counsel or his agent at the time of trial.

6)      An application for bail pending appeal was filed in the interim, and on February 17, 2006 the Honourable Justice Goudge of this Court, heard the application and ordered the appellant to be released on bail pending appeal. Mr. Doucette was successful in this regard, due to the quality of the work submitted by the private investigator, and his expertise in providing appropriate and organized evidence to the court in the documents he prepared and filed. On May 18 and August 22, 2006, Mr. George’s bail pending appeal was extended and varied as necessary.

7)      On September 8, 2006, a panel of this court consisting of Justices Goudge, Juriansz, and Rouleau heard the appeal. The Court allowed the appeal, quashed the conviction, and entered an acquittal.  Mr. Doucette was again successful.

8)      The order appointing Mr. Doucette to act as counsel for the appellant did not fix the rate at which he should be paid for his work on the appeal.  Counsel for the Attorney General of Ontario, submitted that fees and disbursements in such cases are reimbursed in accordance with provincial legal aid rates.  However, there is no statutory or other requirement that such fees be paid at this rate.  Section 684(3) of the Criminal Code provides that the fees are, in effect, negotiable between the Attorney General and counsel. If counsel cannot agree upon the fees, either side is entitled to have them taxed by the registrar.  This provision, in my view, indicates that unless the court itself fixes the counsel fee at the legal aid hourly rate in the order appointing counsel, the registrar is not bound to apply the legal aid rate.

9)      Mr. Doucette has since submitted his account to the Attorney General of Ontario in five interim bills, at a rate of $150.00 per hour, and was paid at legal aid rates of $92.30 per hour as the parties, through negotiation, have failed to agree on the hourly rate at which Mr. Doucette should be paid. Accordingly Mr. Doucette has applied to have this account taxed.

10)  I heard submissions on December 7, 2006 with respect to Mr. Doucette’s application, and reserved my decision.

11)  On December 7, 2006 at the hearing of the taxation of fees and disbursements, Mr. Doucette submitted that considering the criminal record of Mr. George, the seriousness of the conviction and the sentence received, the investigative work carried out at the request of the court, the skill and expertise that was necessary to distill the information from Mr. George in order to give the private investigator proper instructions, and the presentation of the fresh evidence required to properly place the matter before the single judge and the  panel, made this matter a complex, time consuming  and difficult process. However, as a result of such work, Mr. Doucette was placed in a position of strength to proceed on a firm basis with his submissions at the appeal, which was successful as evidenced by the result.

12)  The crown agreed with the chronology of the proceedings, the disbursements and the amount of hours billed for this matter.  However Ms. Crosbie submitted on behalf of the crown, that the case was not complex as trial defence counsel acknowledged his incompetence.  I agree with Mr. Doucette's submissions however, that the subject matter of this appeal was a conviction for a serious offence, and preparing the argument on the fresh evidence application, and on the appeal, was time-consuming, complex and somewhat delicate in nature, given the fact that while asserting the rights of the appellant, the reputation of trial counsel should not unnecessarily or gratuitously be harmed.

13)  I heavily rely on the submissions and references of both parties made at the taxation hearing, the materials filed, and specific reference to R. v. Barton (Court of Appeal File No: C38866) made by Mr. Doucette in which the then Deputy Registrar, Mr. Keith Gauntlett, taxed fees at an hourly rate of $200.00 per hour for a matter dealing with a charge which involved similar issues such as the seriousness of the charge, as well as ineffective assistance of counsel, which required similar processes to argue this appeal.  

14)  Although counsel for the crown submitted that the practice is to pay legal aid rates in the circumstances where the crown is ordered to pay costs, pursuant to an order made by the court, I am not constrained to follow a "practice" that does not result in a fair and just award in a particular case.  Mr. Doucette is a senior counsel whose typical hourly rate for non-legally-aided clients is well above the legal aid rate. The hourly rate that he has requested, is well below the maximum hourly rate at which clients have retained him.  In obtaining an acquittal rather than a new trial, the result was particularly favourable for his client.

15)  Accordingly, I do not feel that the hourly rate of $150.00 is unreasonable in the circumstances, given the general expertise and appellate experience of Mr. Doucette in such matters. I will therefore tax Mr. Doucette’s rate, as requested, at $150.00 per hour.
