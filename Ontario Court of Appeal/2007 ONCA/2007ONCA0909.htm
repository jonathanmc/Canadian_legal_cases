ENDORSEMENT

TAXATION OF THE FEES AND DISBURSEMENTS OF COURT APPOINTED COUNSEL

1)      The appellant filed an Inmate Notice of Appeal on September 15, 2004 against his conviction of two counts of robbery, one count of forcible confinement, and one count of use of an imitation firearm.

2)      At an indeterminate date, Legal Aid Ontario refused funding for the appellant and his appeal therefore proceeded as an Inmate appeal.

3)      On February 14, 2006 the appellant’s inmate appeal was to be heard, however on this day Mr. Delmar Doucette, acting as duty counsel informed the Court that the appellant continued to raise the issue of ineffective assistance of counsel. After hearing Mr. Doucette’s submissions, The Court ordered counsel, pursuant to s 684 of the Criminal Code, to investigate whether there was merit to Mr. McKenzie’s appeal.  Mr. Doucette reported his findings to the Court, stating that in his opinion there was merit to Mr. McKenzie’s appeal.”

4)      On March 8, 2006, the Court appointed Mr. Doucette as counsel for the appellant pursuant to the previously made s 684 (1) order. The appellant was convicted on May 14, 2004 and sentenced on September 13, 2004 to 3 years and 9 months imprisonment (equivalent to six years imprisonment after credit for pre-trial custody).

5)      This is a case that involved an allegation of ineffective assistance of trial counsel which is the basis of the fresh evidence application, and that the learned trial Judge erred in his analysis of the identification evidence led by the Crown. These types of appeals usually increase the amount of hours counsel has to spend in preparation.

6)      On February 7, 2007, a panel of this Court consisting of Justices Goudge, Blair, and McFarland heard the appeal and the Court reserved judgment and Justice Goudge on behalf of the Court also stated that the issues raised made the appeal a very difficult case.

7)      The order appointing Mr. Doucette to act as counsel for the appellant did not fix the rate at which he should be paid for his work on the appeal.  Counsel for the Attorney General of Ontario, submitted that fees and disbursements in such cases are reimbursed in accordance with provincial legal aid rates.  However, there is no statutory or other requirement that such fees be paid at this rate.  Section 684(3) of the Criminal Code provides that the fees are, in effect, negotiable between the Attorney General and counsel. If counsel cannot agree upon the fees, either side is entitled to have them taxed by the registrar.  This provision, in my view, indicates that unless the court itself fixes the counsel fee at the legal aid hourly rate in the order appointing counsel, the registrar is not bound to apply the legal aid rate.

8)      Mr. Doucette submitted his account to the Attorney General of Ontario through a series of 5 accounts, the first 4 accounts at the rate of $150.00 per hour, and the 5th account at a rate of $175.00 per hour, which was increased on January 1, 2007.  Travel time was also billed at the regular rate of $150.00 per hour.  The Crown made partial payment of the first account at the legal aid rate of $92.30 per hour. Both parties have failed to agree on the hourly rate at which Mr. Doucette should be paid. Accordingly Mr. Doucette applied to have this account taxed.

9)      I heard submissions on May 11, 2007 with respect to Mr. Doucette’s application, and reserved my decision.

10)  At the hearing of the taxation of fees and disbursements, Mr. Doucette submitted that there was no dispute with the amount of hours worked or the disbursements on the file. The dispute was the appropriate hourly rate for the hours worked. Mr. Doucette also submitted that his fee for non-legally aided clients for travel time is the same as preparation time.

11)  The crown agreed with the disbursements and the amount of hours billed, after this matter was reviewed by Legal Aid. However, the issue raised by the crown is that the amount of the hourly rate should be paid at the legal aid rate, and that the travel time should not be the same hourly rate as preparation time.

12)  Although counsel for the crown submitted that the practice is to pay legal aid rates in the circumstances where the crown is ordered to pay costs, pursuant to an order made by the court, I am not constrained to follow a "practice" that does not result in a fair and just award in a particular case.  Mr. Doucette is a senior counsel whose typical hourly rate for non-legally-aided clients is well above the legal aid rate. The hourly rate that he has requested is well below the maximum hourly rate at which clients have retained him.  Mr. Doucette has also not charged any interest on these accounts and did not ask for costs as well, in keeping with the interest of public funds

13)  Accordingly, I do not feel that the hourly rates submitted by Mr. Doucette for his counsel work are unreasonable in the circumstances, given his general expertise and appellate experience in such matters and the complex and difficult nature of the appeal.  I do not consider that the modest increase in his hourly rate after January 1, 2007 should not be recognized, given that it no doubt reflects the realities of the increasing costs in the private practice of criminal law, and the fact that considerable work was done on behalf of his client during this year.  I will therefore tax Mr. Doucette’s rate for the first four accounts, reflecting work done in 2006 at the hourly rate of $150.00.  The hourly rate for the final account will be fixed at $175 per hour.

14)  With respect to his bill for travel time, I take a somewhat different view.  Mr. Doucette billed only 1 way for his travel time for 3 of the 4 trips he made to Kingston because he was able to divide the cost of those trips between two clients.  Although Mr. Doucette has submitted a claim for these hours at his hourly rate, given that the cost will be paid out of public funds, I am not prepared to fix his costs for travel time at that rate.  I note that the legal aid plan provides a differential between the hourly rate for preparation and the hourly rate for travel time.  In my view, the rate I fix for travel time should be reduced to reflect a similar differential.  In the circumstances I would fix the rate at $100 per hour for travel time.
