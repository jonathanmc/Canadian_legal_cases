ENDORSEMENT

[1]               The appellant, a police officer, was convicted of criminal harassment and extortion.  He appeals against conviction for criminal harassment on the basis that the elements of the offence had not been proven.  With respect to the conviction for extortion, his appeal against conviction is founded on the basis that the trial judge erred by determining that the appellant had a motive to lie to escape conviction.  See R. v. B.(L.) (1993), 182 C.C.C. (3d) 189 (Ont. C.A. ).  The appellant contends that the trial judge used that determination to erroneously assess his credibility.  Finally, the appellant argues that the sentence was unduly harsh and excessive.

            Criminal Harassment

[2]               To establish harassment under s. 264(2)(d) of the Criminal Code, the Crown had to establish that the appellant engaged in “threatening conduct”.  We accept the definition of threatening conduct given in R. v. George (2002), 162 C.C.C. (3d) 337 (Y.T. C.A. ) at para. 39 that, in order to meet the objectives of s. 264, the threatening conduct must amount to a “tool of intimidation which is designed to instill a sense of fear in the recipient”.  The impugned conduct is to be viewed objectively, with due consideration for the circumstances in which they took place, and with regard to the effects those acts had on the recipient. 

[3]               In brief, the conduct in question is as follows.  The appellant and the complainant knew one another but had virtually no contact in the three years prior to the incident.  The incident took place in broad daylight in downtown North Bay while the complainant was walking down Main Street with her five-year old daughter, after having left a bank.  The appellant was dressed in full police uniform and also on foot on Main Street.  He wolf-whistled at the complainant, said “nice butt” or “nice ass” and then, after the complainant sped up to get away from him, called out “are those pants painted on”. 

[4]               While the appellant’s conduct was clearly inappropriate and unwanted, we do not see the incident as amounting to threatening conduct within the meaning of those words in s. 264(2)(d).  Although the complainant justifiably felt upset and scared by the appellant’s conduct, viewed objectively, we do not see it as rising to the level of a “tool of intimidation designed to instill a sense of fear”.

Extortion

[5]               We note that there has been no suggestion that the appellant’s conduct in relation to the second complainant, as found by the trial judge, could not amount to extortion. 

[6]               We do not accept the argument that the trial judge assessed the appellant’s credibility by referring to his motive to lie at trial.  In the circumstances of this case and reading the reasons as a whole, we understand the trial judge’s reference to a motive related to his view of the appellant’s motive to commit the offence – not to the appellant’s motive to lie in giving his trial testimony.  The trial judge was entitled to make that determination and to reject the appellant’s evidence should he do so.  In any event, there was ample evidence on which the trial judge could find that the appellant had threatened to make Mr. Tiedke’s life a living hell, should Mr. Tiedke not lie for him.   

Sentence

[7]               The offence of extortion is a serious one.  We see no error in the 90-day sentence imposed by the trial judge, particularly as the appellant was a police officer who stood in the position of a mentor to Mr. Tiedke at the relevant time.

DISPOSITION

[8]               Accordingly, we would allow the appeal in part, set aside the conviction for harassment and enter an acquittal.  In all other regards, we would dismiss the appeal.
