ENDORSEMENT

[1]               The appellant, Troy Andrews, an employee of the respondent Air Canada , was briefly suspected by management of being responsible for the improper activation of a fire alarm at Pearson International Airport.  Within weeks, and after an investigation, he was advised that Air Canada had determined that he was not responsible, and he was issued an apology.

[2]               The respondents and employees of Air Canada – including Troy Andrews – were parties to a Collective Agreement.  The Collective Agreement covers virtually all aspects of the employment relationship and includes discipline and grievance procedures.  Troy Andrews commenced a grievance in which he alleged that he had been unjustly accused and treated in the course of an investigation into the fire alarm incident.

[3]               The grievance proceeded to arbitration, and was ultimately dismissed on the merits.

[4]               Troy Andrews then, on his own behalf, and on behalf of his minor children and his wife, commenced an action alleging various torts and Charter breaches by the respondents.  The respondents, under r. 21.01(3)(a) of the Rules of Civil Procedure, moved for an order that the Ontario Superior Court had no jurisdiction over the subject matter of the action.

[5]               The motion judge agreed with the respondents, and in a brief endorsement found that the alleged dispute was within the exclusive jurisdiction of an arbitrator appointed pursuant to the Collective Agreement.  He dismissed the appellants’ action.  We agree with the motion judge and we find no error in his decision.

[6]               Disputes that arise either expressly or implicitly from the interpretation, application, administration or alleged violation of a collective agreement are within the exclusive jurisdiction of an arbitrator empowered under a collective agreement: Weber v. Ontario Hydro, [1995] S.C.J. No. 59, [1995] 2 S.C.R. 929.  Whether a dispute can be litigated or must be arbitrated turns on the nature of the dispute and the terms of the collective agreement, not on the legal characterization of the wrong alleged.

[7]               The appellants’ allegations – as correctly found by the motion judge – are grounded in conduct in the workplace and the disciplinary investigations surrounding that conduct, and are in their essential character, matters governed by the Collective Agreement.

[8]               The fact that the action brought by the appellants sought relief from Air Canada , and from individuals other than union members, does not change the essential character of the subject matter of the dispute.  All of the individual respondents were at all times acting in the scope and course of their employment, and thus, governed by the Collective Agreement.

[9]               Finally, the claims of the appellants under the Family Law Act, R.S.O. 1990, c. F.3 are wholly derivative in nature and do not take this dispute out of the exclusive jurisdiction of the arbitrator: see Giorno v. Pappas, [1999] O.J. No. 168, 42 O.R. (3d) 626 ( C.A. ).

[10]          The appeal is dismissed.  The two groups of respondents — Air Canada and the IAMAW — are each entitled to aggregate costs of the appeal in the amount of $6,000.00, inclusive if G.S.T. and disbursements.
