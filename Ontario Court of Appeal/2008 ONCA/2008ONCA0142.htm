APPEAL BOOK ENDORSEMENT

[1]               The first ground of appeal deals with whether the respondent was in default at the time he moved to dismiss the appellant’s action for delay.  In our view, he was not because the affidavit of documents was effectively served on January 24 and the motion on January 26.

[2]               Even assuming the “PJ” or “Fallon” memorandum should have been included in the respondent’s affidavit of documents, the purpose of production and disclosure was accomplished because there was evidence that the appellants were provided with a copy of the memorandum on March 13, 1995 and the memorandum was again referenced in a letter to appellants’ counsel on June 6, 1995.

[3]               The motion judge referred and applied the correct legal principles canvassed by this court in Armstrong v. McCall, (2006) 213 O.A.C. 229.

[4]               There was not only presumed prejudice but evidence of actual prejudice as found by the motion judge.  The presumed prejudice arises from the fact that a trial would not take place until twenty years after the events that gave rise to the action and, in particular, from the appellants’ delay of nine and a half years during which time they did nothing to move the action forward.  There was also actual prejudice arising from the death of Peter Fallon Sr. who, as the motion judge found, was a key witness.  That finding was amply supported on the record before the motion judge.

[5]               In the end, the motion judge properly balanced the rights of the parties in deciding to dismiss the action for the nine and a half year delay, which he found to be inexcusable and prejudicial to the respondent.  We see no basis on which to interfere with his decision.

[6]               Accordingly, the appeal is dismissed.  Costs to the respondent fixed in the amount of $21,000 inclusive of disbursements plus GST, payable by the appellants severally.
