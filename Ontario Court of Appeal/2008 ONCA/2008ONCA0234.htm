On appeal from the order of Justice Hambly of the Superior Court of Justice dated August 23, 2007.

[1]               Unfortunately the applications judge gave almost no reasons for his decision on access and in our view the decision failed to take into account the dramatic change in access as a result of the mother’s move and the impact on the children’s relationship with their father. The access order was not in the best interests of the children and it was not consistent with the principles laid down in Gordon v. Goertz (1996), CarswellSask 199 (S.C.C.), and the need to maximize access with the non-custodial parent.

[2]               Accordingly, we would allow the appeal and vary paragraph 1 of the order of Hambly J. as follows:

·        Subparagraph (a) is varied to provide that the father will have care of the children for six weeks in July and August.

·        Subparagraphs (b) and (c) are varied to provide that when Easter and March break coincide, in odd numbered years, the father shall have care of the children for the Easter Holiday and March break.

·        Subparagraph (d) is varied to provide that in odd numbered years the father shall have access to the children in Calgary at his own expense on the Thanksgiving holiday. In addition the father shall have access to the children on every Father’s day weekend in Calgary at his own expense.

[3]               In light of our disposition, we consider that success was divided on the application and accordingly there would be no costs of the application.

[4]               We cannot leave this appeal without expressing our concern about the parties’ inability to deal with each other cooperatively in the best interests of the children. We recommend that the parties engage in access counselling to facilitate communication in order to implement a more flexible access regime. Ongoing cooperation is undoubtedly a factor that will be considered on any variation application. In the meantime, we remind the parties of the provisions of the order of Justice Rogers and in particular paragraph 7 of that order.

[5]               Accordingly, the appeal is allowed with costs fixed at $9,000 inclusive of G.S.T. and disbursements.
