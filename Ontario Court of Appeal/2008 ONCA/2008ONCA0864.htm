On appeal and cross appeal from the order of Justice Beth A. Allen of the Superior Court of Justice dated January 17, 2008, and reported at 2008 CanLII 5106.

Gillese J.A.:

[1]               A.C. Concrete Forming Ltd. (hereafter referred to as A.C. Concrete or the appellant) carries on business as a low rise forming contractor in the greater Toronto area.  It is a small contractor with only a small number of employees – between seven and fifteen at the relevant times.  The Association is an accredited employers’ organization within the meaning of the Labour Relations Act, 1995, S.O. 1995, c. 1, Sch. A. (the “Act”).  Piccininni is the General Manager of the Association. 

[2]               A.C. Concrete was a member of the Association from February 20, 1998, until its resignation on April 8, 2004.  The Association is the exclusive statutory bargaining agent for all employers bound by the collective agreement between the Association and the Universal Workers Union, LIUNA, Local 183 (the “Collective Agreement”), even if the employer is not a member of the Association, as in A.C. Concrete’s case.  The Association and the union have been parties to the Collective Agreement at all relevant times; the Collective Agreement covers employees working in the low rise residential formwork industry. 

[3]               Article 10.02 in Schedule A to the Collective Agreement requires all employers bound by it to contribute $1.20 per hour for each hour worked by employees covered by the Collective Agreement.  Thus, even though a non-member of the Association, A.C. Concrete was obliged to remit such monies.  The money is remitted to Local 183 which retains $0.20 per hour for each hour worked by each employee for its training fund.  Local 183 pays the remaining $1.00 per hour to the Association “as each employer’s contribution to the cost of negotiating and administering this [Collective] Agreement.”  The money held by the Association is called the Industry Fund.  Article 10.02 concludes by providing that if the Association determines that it requires more than $0.60 per hour from each employer, it shall notify the union of any change in the amount required and the employers’ contributions shall be increased accordingly.

[4]               In accordance with its constitution and by-laws, the Association established a Board of Directors which manages the Association’s affairs and makes all relevant decisions with respect to representation of the employers and administration of the Collective Agreement, including the amount to be levied for purposes of the Industry Fund.    

[5]               In May 2005, pursuant to s. 96 of the Act, A.C. Concrete filed an unfair labour practice complaint against the Association.  In the complaint, A.C. Concrete alleged that the Association had agreed with Local 183 to eliminate subcontracting, knowing that would have an adverse effect on the appellant and other small contractors.  It also alleged that the Association had discriminated in other ways against small contractors and that it had arbitrarily increased the levy for the Industry Fund and then misused that money.

[6]               On February 9, 2006, at the outset of the hearing before the Ontario Labour Relations Board (the “Board”), A.C. Concrete withdrew the complaint, with leave of the Board. 

[7]               In early March 2006, A.C. Concrete filed a Notice of Arbitration under the Arbitration Act, 1991, S.O. 1991, c. 17, and Article 8 of the constitution and by-laws of the Association.  The issues raised were similar to those in the unfair labour practice complaint with the focus of the Notice being on the alleged misuse of the Industry Fund by the Association.   Article 8 mandates that any disputes arising out of the affairs of the Association shall be referred to arbitration, provided the complainant is a member of the Association or has been a non-member for no more than six months.  The parties agreed to appoint Mr. Surdykowski as the arbitrator. 

[8]               On February 22, 2007, the arbitrator issued an award in which he held that because A.C. Concrete had resigned from the Association and been a non-member for more than six months, it did not have standing to have its claim arbitrated under the Arbitration Act, 1991.

[9]               A.C. Concrete then commenced the present action against the Association and Piccininni, whom it alleges was the “controlling mind” of the Association, claiming damages for breach of contract, breach of fiduciary duty and intentional interference with economic relations.  It also seeks an accounting, by the Association, of all revenues and expenses in relation to the Industry Fund since 1997.

[10]          In its Statement of Claim, A.C. Concrete alleges that it entered into a contract with the Association under the terms of which the Association agreed to use the funds paid by A.C. Concrete into the Industry Fund for the equal benefit of all employer contractors (the “Contract”).  Instead, it is alleged, the Association breached its duties by misusing the Industry Fund. 

[11]          In para. 9 of the Statement of Claim, the particulars of the breaches are said to include:  (i) the Association’s misuse of the Industry Fund in a manner that benefits select employers to the detriment of the other employers who are also required to pay into the Industry Fund; (ii) the Association’s misuse of the Industry Fund in a manner that benefits large employers to the detriment of the other employers who are also required to pay into the Industry Fund; (iii) the Association’s excessive spending on meetings, salaries, benefits, general office expenses and professional fees; (iv) the Association’s arbitrary increase of the amount each employer must submit to the Industry Fund; (v) the Association’s failure to continue to provide contract referrals to A.C. Concrete; and (vi) other actions of the Association that interfere with the economic relations of A.C. Concrete.

[12]          The Association brought a motion to have the action dismissed.

[13]          On January 17, 2008, the motion judge dismissed A.C. Concrete’s action pursuant to rule 21.01(3)(a) of the Rules of Civil Procedure.  She held that the court lacked jurisdiction because A.C. Concrete’s claims fell within the Board’s exclusive jurisdiction.  Having decided the motion based on rule 21.01(3)(a), the motion judge found that there was no need to make a determination under rules 21.01(1)(b) or 21.01(3)(d).

[14]          A.C. Concrete appeals.  It submits that the motion judge erred in dismissing its action for want of jurisdiction.  It argues that the Board’s jurisdiction is limited to matters set out in the Act and that the causes of action in its claim are not matters which the Act empowers the Board to decide.  Further, it maintains that the court has jurisdiction because its claim is based primarily on the Contract and not on the Collective Agreement.  Finally, it says that not all the relief it seeks is available through the Board. 

[15]          The respondents cross-appeal and ask that, in the event the appeal is allowed, the matter be remitted to the motion judge for determination pursuant to rules 21.01(1)(b) and 21.01(3)(d).

ANALYSIS

[16]          To decide whether the Board has exclusive jurisdiction over the appellant’s claims, the essential character of the dispute must be determined.  This is not a matter of semantics nor of the legal labels attached to the claims by one of the parties to the dispute – it is the true substance of the dispute that is determinative:  see Weber v. Ontario Hydro, [1995] 2 S.C.R. 929, at para. 52, and Myrtezaj v. Cintas Canada Ltd. (2008), 90 O.R. (3d) 384 ( C.A. ), at para. 55.  The essential character of the dispute is determined by reference to the factual matrix of the dispute:  see Cintas at para. 55. 

[17]          The appellant argues that the dispute arises from a breach of a contractual or fiduciary relationship that exists between the parties.  I disagree for two reasons.  First, in my view, the relationship between A.C. Concrete and the Association arises from the Act and the Collective Agreement, and not from the Contract.  As a result of an accreditation order made by the Board pursuant to its powers under the Act, the Association is the exclusive bargaining agent with the Universal Workers Union, LIUNA, Local 183.  It represents all employers bound by the Collective Agreement, whether or not the employer is a member of the Association.  Thus, the Association represents the appellant even though the appellant is no longer a member.  Further, the Association funds its activities through the Industry Fund and the Industry Fund is comprised of monies paid by employers, including A.C. Concrete, pursuant to Article 10 in Schedule A to the Collective Agreement.

[18]          Second, the factual essence of the appellant’s claim that the Association misused the Industry Fund relates not to the Contract but to the manner in which the Association has represented employers.  Paragraphs 9(i), (ii), (v) and (vi) of the Statement of Claim are essentially claims that the Association has preferred the interests of large employers over those of small contractors when representing its members.   

[19]          I agree with the motion judge that the substance of those claims is that the Association failed to represent the appellant fairly, without discrimination and in good faith in its representations.  This matter is clearly within the exclusive jurisdiction of the Board.  Section 96(1) of the Act empowers the Board to inquire into a complaint of non-compliance by an employer association with any section of the Act to determine whether there has been a contravention.[1]  Section 114(1) gives the Board exclusive jurisdiction to exercise the powers granted to it under the Act, including the powers conferred by s. 96,[2] and s. 141 prohibits the Association from acting in a manner that is arbitrary, discriminatory or in bad faith in the representation of the appellant.  Section 141 reads as follows:

141.    An accredited employers’ organization, so long as it continues to be entitled to represent employers in a unit of employers, shall not act in a manner that is arbitrary, discriminatory or in bad faith in the representation of any of the employers in the unit, whether members of the accredited employers’ organization or not.  [Emphasis added.]

[20]          Parenthetically, to the extent that the appellant argues that the duties of the Association are owed only to its members, I disagree.  The concluding words of s. 141 clearly provide that the duties are owed to employers in the bargaining unit, including the appellant, regardless of whether they are members of the Association.

[21]          The remaining claims of misuse of the Industry Fund in paras. 9(iii) and (iv) of the Statement of Claim fall within the purview of s. 143 of the Act.  Section 143 gives the Board jurisdiction to consider the amounts paid by A.C. Concrete pursuant to article 10.02 of Schedule A to the Collective Agreement and to determine whether the Association has levied a fee, dues or assessment that is unreasonable or discriminatory. 

Section 143 reads as follows:

143. An accredited employers’ organization shall not charge, levy or prescribe initiation fees, dues or assessments that, in the opinion of the Board, are unreasonable or discriminatory.

[22]          Paragraph 9(iv) of the Statement of Claim (i.e. the alleged arbitrary increase in amounts required to be paid into the Industry Fund) falls within the plain meaning of s. 143.  The Board may examine the amounts that A.C. Concrete has been required to pay to determine whether they are unreasonable or discriminatory, as between large and small contractors.  The allegation in para. (iii) – that the Association spent excessive amounts on meetings, salaries and the like – can be examined, as well, because if such excesses have taken place, presumably the fees have been increased as a result and such increases would, therefore, be unreasonable. 

[23]          Accordingly, I am of the view that the Board has exclusive jurisdiction over the appellant’s claims and the motion judge made no error in dismissing the action for want of jurisdiction.

[24]          I turn next to the appellant’s argument that the court has jurisdiction because, in a proceeding before the Board, it would be unable to obtain the relief it seeks in the court action.  I reject this submission.  Section 96(4) of the Act grants the Board very broad remedial powers.[3]  In the event that the Board finds a contravention of the Act, the Board is empowered to order the Association to rectify the act or acts that are complained of. 

[25]          Nor do I accept the appellant’s assertion that if the action is dismissed, it will have been denied the opportunity to have its grievances in respect of the Association properly decided.  The appellant had the opportunity to have these matters dealt with by the Board but, by withdrawing its complaint, chose to forego that opportunity.  It cannot now complain about the consequence of that decision.

[26]          Finally, I wish to comment on the appellant’s submission in oral argument that even if those parts of its pleading which relate to the collection of fees or dues are within the Board’s exclusive jurisdiction, the motion judge ought to have struck only those parts and allowed the balance of the claim – which it says relates to enforcement – to proceed.  As this suggestion was not put to the motion judge, it ought not to be raised here on appeal for the first time.  Furthermore, the unnecessary bifurcation of proceedings flowing out of the same factual matrix is to be avoided – where related issues arise from a dispute, they should be heard by a single adjudicative body to the extent jurisdictionally possible.[4]  If courts fail to defer completely to the Board’s jurisdiction in circumstances such as these, violence is done to the comprehensive statutory scheme designed to govern the relationship between the parties – the assumption of jurisdiction, even over a limited part of the claim, would fly in the face of the demonstrated legislative intent to provide an exclusive and comprehensive mechanism for labour dispute resolution.[5]  

DISPOSITION

[27]          Accordingly, I would dismiss the appeal with the result that the cross-appeal falls away.  I would order costs of the appeal and cross-appeal to the respondents, fixed in the amount of $12,600, inclusive of disbursements and GST.  Costs of the motion were awarded to the respondents and, in light of the result on appeal, that order remains.  The suggestion was made that this court should set the quantum of those costs.  I would decline to do so as, in my view, that matter is best decided by the motion judge.  Thus, in accordance with her decision, if the parties are unable to resolve the matter of costs of the motion, they shall return to the motion judge for a determination of that matter.
