On appeal from the conviction entered by Justice Frank J.C. Newbould of the Superior Court of Justice on May 30, 2008, and the sentence imposed on June 6, 2008.

ENDORSEMENT

[1]              The appellant was convicted of breaking and entering and committing theft, breach of probation and breach of recognizance.  He was sentenced to three and a half years for the break and enter; one year consecutive for the breach of probation; and one year, concurrent with the latter, for the breach of recognizance.  He was given ten months credit for eight and a half months of pre-trial custody, resulting in a global sentence of 44 months.

[2]              At the time of the break and enter, the appellant was subject to a probation order that required him to keep the peace and be of good behaviour.  He was also subject to a recognizance that required him to remain in his residence at all times. 

[3]              The appellant appeals from both conviction and sentence. 

[4]              The break and enter occurred on September 10, 2007 at an Etobicoke residence.  The owner and other residents of the house were not home at the time of the break-in.  The rear door of the house was damaged and the premises were ransacked.  Several items, including $2,500 USD, were taken from the house. 

[5]              A postman noticed two people behaving suspiciously and called 911.  He provided descriptions of both suspects and specified that one of them was wearing a red hooded sweatshirt.  P.C. Walker and P.C. Barnes attended at the scene.  They gave chase but could not apprehend the suspects. 

[6]              On September 13, 2007, the postman was shown a photo line-up.  He identified the appellant as the person wearing a red sweatshirt. 

[7]              On February 22, 2008, five months after the break and enter, P.C. Walker identified the appellant in a photo line-up.  P.C. Barnes had earlier identified a person who was not a suspect.

[8]              The appellant raises the following grounds of appeal:

(1) The trial judge erred in his charge to the jury by failing to emphasize the frailties of the identification evidence, especially as they related to the appellant;

(2) The trial judge erred by failing to permit defence counsel to cross-examine a police officer on the recommendations of The Hon. Peter deCarteret Cory in The Inquiry Regarding Thomas Sophonow:  The Investigation, Prosecution and Consideration of Entitlement to Compensation (Winnipeg: Manitoba Justice, 2001) [Sophonow report];

(3) The trial judge erred in his treatment of the evidence concerning a cell phone call made on the morning of the break and enter to the appellant's residence; and

(4) The trial judge erred in failing to give two for one credit for the appellant’s pre-trial custody.

[9]              In respect of the first ground of appeal, duty counsel submitted that the trial judge should have placed greater emphasis on the five-month delay in showing P.C. Walker the photo line-up.  Duty counsel also submitted that the appellant is the only person in the photo line-up with a significant bare patch in his eyebrow and the trial judge should have emphasized this in his charge to the jury.

[10]         In our view, a review of the charge to the jury on the weaknesses of eyewitness identification evidence, both generally and in the particular circumstances of this case, does not suggest reversible error.  Further, a review of the photo line-up reveals no unfairness to the appellant. 

[11]         In respect of the second ground of appeal, duty counsel submitted that the trial judge erred by refusing to permit cross-examination on the recommendations of Commissioner Cory in the Sophonow report.  Defence counsel wished to make the point that the photo line-up should have been videotaped. 

[12]         In our view, cross-examination on Commissioner Cory’s recommendations in the Sophonow report would have been acceptable.  However, defence counsel clearly made the desired point in his subsequent cross-examination.  We find no reversible error on the part of the trial judge. 

[13]         The third ground of appeal concerns a cell phone call made to the appellant’s residence on the morning of the break and enter.  The relevant facts are as follows.  A second person, Mr. Antunes, was apprehended by the police near the scene of the break and enter.  He had in his possession a stolen cell phone.  The police investigation revealed that sometime between 6:03 a.m. and 9:44 a.m., the cell phone was used to place a call to the appellant’s residence in Brampton.   It was the position of defence counsel that the call was made by Mr. Antunes, who was ultimately convicted of break and enter and had no reason to make the call if the appellant had participated in the offence.  In his charge to the jury, the trial judge reviewed the relevant evidence and advised the jury of the following:

There’s also an issue as to who placed the call.  The stolen cell phone was seized from Mr. Antunes.  There is no direct evidence, however, as to who made the call.  One inference is that Mr. Antunes made it, that would be because the cell phone was on his person.  It could have been made by someone else, even Mr. Alves or anyone else that the cell phone had been used by someone else, however, this is all speculation and as I said earlier you’re not to speculate.  You may or may not conclude that it would be speculative to conclude that the call on the stolen phone was made by Mr. Antunes to Mr. Alves at the time of the break-ins.  However, as I stated to you, it’s your view of the evidence that counts and you’re free to disregard what I say.  You are not free to, however, speculate.

[14]         Defence counsel objected to the charge on the basis that there was nothing speculative about finding that Mr. Antunes made the phone call; instead, it would be speculative to find that anyone else made the call.  In our view, the evidence went no further than to show that someone made a call on the stolen cell phone to the appellant’s residence in Brampton.  Duty counsel submitted that it was open to the jury to infer from this evidence that the appellant was at home in Brampton at the time of the offence.  To draw such an inference would be speculative.  We find no error in the trial judge’s charge to the jury on this issue.

[15]         Turning to the sentence appeal, the appellant submits that he was entitled to two-for-one credit for his pre-trial custody.  The trial judge considered this issue thoroughly.  He emphasized that the decision to give credit on a two for one basis was discretionary and not subject to a mechanical formula: see R. v. Wust, [2000] 1 S.C.R. 455.  Among the factors he considered was the absence of evidence that the appellant would have availed himself of any rehabilitative programs.  He noted that the offence took place less than two months after the appellant’s release from jail on several convictions for break and enter, and about one month after he entered into a recognizance requiring him to remain in his residence.  He also noted that the appellant’s pre-trial detention was of his own making; namely, the appellant had a history of disobeying court orders.    In the result, the trial judge gave the appellant ten months credit for eight and a half months pre-trial custody.  We see no error in the trial judge’s exercise of discretion in not giving two for one credit. 

[16]         Accordingly, the appeal from conviction is dismissed.  Leave to appeal sentence is granted but the sentence appeal is dismissed.
