ENDORSEMENT

[1]              Mr. Weber submits that the trial judge erred in five respects.  One, Mr. Weber contends that the trial judge erred in averaging his income over the three years preceding trial for the purpose of determining support.  We do not accept this submission.

[2]              The trial judge was of the view that averaging would produce the fairest income number.  As Mr. Weber was self employed and was not fully forthcoming in his disclosure, we think that the trial judge exercise of discretion to average was reasonable. 

[3]              Two, Mr. Weber contends that, in ordering child support, the trial judge erred because he did not give effect to the reality that two of the five children were living with Mr. Weber.  Again, we disagree. 

[4]              That two of the children were living with Mr. Weber was first raised after Mrs. Weber had put in her case and after both parties had consented to an order incorporating the parenting plan.  When apprised of the new information, the trial judge gave Mr. Weber a choice: a mistrial or agree to the parenting plan.  Mr. Weber said that he did not want a mistrial.  However, the trial judge expressly told Mr. Weber that he could bring a motion to vary.  We note that even as of today, he has not brought that motion. 

[5]              Three, Mr. Weber contends that the trial judge erred in valuing the long-term debt of $355,000 at zero.  There were some factors, referred to by Mr. Joseph, that supported giving that debt some value.  However, there were a large number of factors, referred to at paras. 28 to 32 of the trial judge’s reasons, that led the trial judge to value the debt at zero.  That zero valuation is, therefore, reasonably supported by the evidence and not reviewable in this court. 

[6]              Four, Mr. Weber further contends that the trial judge failed to exclude from the net family property calculation the proceeds derived from the sale of the Puslinch property.  That property was a gift to Mr. Weber, but he did not segregate the proceeds or lead any evidence from which they could fairly be traced either into his bank account or his company.  Accordingly, the trial judge did not err in finding that the proceeds were untraceable and, therefore, did not err in refusing to expressly exclude the proceeds from the NFP calculation.

[7]              Five, finally Mr. Weber seeks leave to appeal the partial indemnity costs award in favour of Mrs. Weber.  He argues that the trial judge’s award fails to recognize that Mr. Weber succeeded on some issues.  Presumptively, under the Family Law Rules, Mrs. Weber was entitled to her costs.  The trial judge in exercising his discretion recognized that Mr. Weber achieved success on some issues but overall was of the view that this success did not displace the presumption or warrant a reduction in costs.  We are not persuaded that the trial judge erred in principle in his costs award.

[8]              Accordingly, for these brief reasons, the appeal is dismissed, with costs fixed at $18,000, inclusive of disbursements and GST.
