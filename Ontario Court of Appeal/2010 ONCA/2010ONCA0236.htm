I.                   FACTS

Background Facts and Procedural History on the Underlying Murder Charge

[1]        Robert Badgerow, the applicant, is charged with the first degree murder of Diane Werendowicz.  On June 20, 1981, the morning after friends had last seen her, Ms. Werendowicz was found lying face down in a creek with a tire over her head and her purse strap wrapped around her neck.  Police found what forensic testing revealed was semen on her jeans and in her vagina.  With the murder still unsolved in 1998, the Centre for Forensic Sciences was able to employ scientific advances to extract a DNA profile from the semen.  It matched a DNA sample from Mr. Badgerow.  Police arrested Mr. Badgerow on December 1, 1998.

[2]        At trial before Lofchik J. of the Superior Court of Justice sitting with a jury, the Crown alleged that Mr. Badgerow sexually assaulted and strangled Ms Werendowicz to death.  As part of the evidence on which its prosecution relied, the Crown submitted a statement that Mr. Badgerow had given to police after arrest – but before he was aware of the DNA evidence – in which he denied knowing or recognizing Ms Werendowicz.  The jury convicted Mr. Badgerow on April 13, 2001.

[3]        Mr. Badgerow appealed his conviction to this court in 2008.  Though four grounds of appeal were submitted, Simmons J.A. needed only to consider one that she found dispositive: she found that the police breached Mr. Badgerow’s s. 10(b) Charter rights in obtaining the statement and concluded that the statement should have been excluded under s. 24(2).  In reasons released on September 5, 2008, she set aside the conviction and ordered a new trial.

[4]              The retrial commenced on February 22, 2010, and the parties expect it to continue until June 2010.

Prior Unsuccessful Bail Applications

[5]        Mr. Badgerow first applied for bail several months after his arrest, in March 1999.  Yates J. of the Ontario Court (General Division) denied his application.  He noted evidence before the court that described Mr. Badgerow’s personality as “aggressive, pugnacious, troublemaker, weird, bizarre, crude, vindictive [and] prone to violent behaviour both at work and elsewhere.”  He also noted that Mr. Badgerow, as a shift worker, “experiences sleep problems, which would cause him to go out walking or bike riding late at night and into the early morning, and during this period he was known to have prowled into people’s yards and peeped into their windows.”  In light of this evidence, Yates J. decided that Mr. Badgerow had failed to show cause why his detention in custody would not have been justified under s. 515(10); indeed, he was not persuaded on any of the three grounds therein.

[6]        After failing in a subsequent bail review at trial, Mr. Badgerow remained in custody through his trial, through the interim between his conviction at trial and his appeal, and through his appeal.  This totalled over ten years in custody.

Successful Bail Application and Conditions of Release

[7]        After this court set aside Mr. Badgerow’s conviction, he applied for bail pending the new trial.  This application came before Crane J. of the Superior Court of Justice.  As well as the evidence as to Mr. Badgerow’s personality that was before Yates J., Crane J. also heard viva voce evidence from a correctional guard who described Mr. Badgerow as a “model inmate” and from Mr. Badgerow’s five proposed sureties: his father, his mother, his brother, his sister, and his brother-in-law.

[8]        On July 24, 2009, Crane J. granted bail to Mr. Badgerow pending his retrial.  Strict conditions, he reasoned, could address the concerns underlying each of the three grounds for justifying ongoing detention in s. 515(10).  In reviewing the grounds, Crane J. further noted:

(1)       that Mr. Badgerow “has had the opportunity to be matured by ten years of imprisonment” and that he “would not appear to have access to the financial means for flight”;

(2)       that the evidence against Mr. Badgerow on his murder charge is not susceptible to interference and that the risk to the sureties’ “life savings” would supply substantial motivation to prevent “any physical separation from a surety [as] a breach of a recognizance”; and

(3)       that, while the offence is “most grave” and subject to a lengthy term of imprisonment, the case against him, though strong, is not certain and the 30 years elapsed suggest that the “community and its media have gone on to many other alarms since then”.

As to the sureties, who pledged a total of $450,500 representing their life savings, Crane J. was “impressed with each”, describing them as “good people who are in a position to know [Mr. Badgerow]”.

[9]        Among other conditions of release, Crane J. required: that alarm systems be installed in each surety’s residence where Mr. Badgerow would sleep to prevent his leaving at night undetected; that Mr. Badgerow be in the presence of a surety at all times during his waking hours; and that Mr. Badgerow report to the Hamilton police every Thursday between 9:00 a.m. and 9:00 p.m.

The Failure to Report

[10]         On December 24, 2009, a Thursday, Mr. Badgerow spent Christmas Eve at his sister’s home with four of his sureties: his mother and siblings, but not his father.  For the first time, Mr. Badgerow did not report to the police during the set window that day and the police contacted Mr. Badgerow’s father.  Mr. Badgerow’s father passed the information on to Mr. Badgerow, whose sister drove him to the police station where he reported at 9:40 p.m.  Mr. Badgerow explained and continues to maintain that he and his sureties simply forgot that it was a Thursday in light of the emotion and excitement of the first Christmas Eve family gathering since Mr Badgerow’s release after over ten years in prison.

[11]         The police allowed Mr. Badgerow to sign in and to leave with his surety.  However, five days later, on December 29, a police officer attended at Mr. Badgerow’s father’s residence and told the father that Mr. Badgerow would be “breached for being late.”  According to the officer, Mr. Badgerow’s father replied: “For being 40 minutes late?”  The officer arrested Mr. Badgerow, who cooperated with the arrest, and charged him with failing to comply with his recognizance under s. 145(3). 

Bail Revocation under Appeal

[12]         In the interim before trial on the s. 145(3) charge, the Crown also applied pursuant to s. 524(4) to revoke Mr. Badgerow’s bail on his murder charge.  Ramsay J. of the Superior Court of Justice heard the application on January 4, 2010.  The Crown and Mr. Badgerow’s counsel jointly agreed to proceed on a paper record of both police notes and reports and written statements.  These included statements from all of Mr. Badgerow’s sureties except his mother.  Each of the sureties’ statements supported Mr. Badgerow’s explanation for his failing to report on time.

[13]         Ramsay J. first looked to the Crown’s onus to show that Mr. Badgerow contravened his recognizance.  Noting that Mr. Badgerow admitted to failing to report as required, Ramsay J. framed Mr. Badgerow’s position on this point as follows: “He says that in the excitement of his first Christmas Eve with his family in eight years, he forgot that it was Thursday.  He does not allege an excuse.  Rather, he says that he lacked the mens rea for the offence.”

[14]         Ramsay J. noted that Mr. Badgerow’s father, when asked what had happened on December 24 in a witness statement to police, answered, in part: “Robert’s mother decided to take him to sign in instead of myself.  He was given the OK to stay at his sister’s home for Christmas Eve and Christmas day.”  Ramsay J. inferred, from this: “I take from the father’s statement that it was [Mr. Badgerow’s] mother who had adverted to the fact that it was Robert’s sign-in day, and she who was supposed to drive him to the police station.”  He continued:

I find it implausible that a man in the accused person’s position, having reported every Thursday for some months, would forget what day of the week it was, even if it was Christmas Eve.  It is also inconsistent with the evidence that his mother, the bondsman who was that day responsible for driving him to the police station, adverted to the fact that it was Thursday and must have communicated with his father about it.  I do not believe that the accused forgot that it was Thursday.  I do not believe him and I do not believe his bondsmen….  I do infer that the father and mother communicated with each other about the need to report on Christmas Eve.  I am satisfied that the accused did not forget in the excitement about spending Christmas with his family.  I find that for whatever motive, he decided to push his luck.

[15]         Ramsay J. then addressed whether Mr. Badgerow nevertheless showed cause why he should not be detained.  On this point, Ramsay J. stated that while “Crane J. did trust the sureties”, “[g]iven the circumstances as they now exist, I do not.  Either they were negligent or they colluded with the accused.”  For Ramsay J., the breach undermined the order’s ability to address the primary and secondary grounds for justifying detention: “whether … a minor breach or not”, “it casts doubt on the feasibility of the existing release order to ensure the appearance of the accused and the protection of the public”.

[16]         Ramsay J. granted the application, revoked Mr. Badgerow’s bail and ordered that he be detained in custody.  That is the order from which Mr. Badgerow now appeals.

Fresh Evidence: Trial and Acquittal on the s. 145(3) Charge

[17]         On January 20, 2010, after the revocation decision of Ramsay J., Mr. Badgerow was tried before Takach J. in the Ontario Court of Justice for failure to comply with his reporting condition.  Takach J. heard viva voce evidence on the failure to report on December 24, 2009.  Mr. Badgerow now seeks to introduce the transcript of the proceedings before Takach J. as fresh evidence, which the Crown opposes.

[18]         In particular, Mr. Badgerow’s father testified that the discussion between Mr. Badgerow’s parents regarding responsibility for taking Mr. Badgerow to report on Christmas Eve had taken place two or three days earlier and the two did not discuss it again.  For Mr. Badgerow, as appellant, this counters any possible inference by Ramsay J. that this conversation took place on the day of the breach.

[19]         Takach J. acquitted Mr. Badgerow.  Looking to the necessary mens rea of the offence, which required the Crown to show at least wilful blindness or recklessness, Takach J. found:

At the very least, his testimony raises a reasonable doubt on the critical issue.  He stated simply that he forgot.  It is apparent that, by inference, from the evidence, that his mother also forgot…  In my view, it’s not unreasonable that he forget, having regard to the time of the year and the juxtaposition of Thursdays in connection with Christmas.

[20]         After so ruling, the Crown asked for a certificate of default under s. 770 to note the actus reus of failing to report.  Accepting the request, Takach J. endorsed the recognizance “with the indication that there was a default, that the reason for the default was forgetfulness on the part of the accused”.  Moreover, Takach J. stated: “Clearly, as I have said, my finding should be clear that I accept his evidence, at least on the basis of raising a reasonable doubt, that he simply forgot about it, forgot about the requirement to appear on the day in question because he did not think it was a Thursday.”  Mr. Badgerow also seeks to submit Takach J.’s ruling and these subsequent statements as fresh evidence on this appeal.

Fresh Evidence: Bail Application before Trial Judge

[21]         On February 3, 2010, Mr. Badgerow applied to the assigned trial judge on his murder charge, Taylor J., for an order to vacate Ramsay J.’s detention order pursuant to s. 523(2)(a).  He sought to put viva voce evidence from the sureties before Taylor J.  However, Taylor J. determined, based on R. v. Robinson (2009), 95 O.R. (3d) 309 (C.A.), that the hearing was not de novo and that he could not substitute his view for Ramsay J.’s view; rather, he could only consider evidence of a significant or material change in circumstances subsequent to Ramsay J.’s order.  He determined:

It should not be open to Robert Badgerow, having decided to proceed before Ramsay J. on the basis of written material, to now seek to have me consider, in the form of viva voce evidence, the same evidence that was before Ramsay J.  Similarly, it should not be open to Robert Badgerow to now seek to present evidence before me that was available to be called before Ramsay J.

Accordingly, Taylor J. ruled that Mr. Badgerow could only lead evidence of changed circumstances subsequent to Ramsay J.’s ruling.

[22]         However, before so ruling, Taylor J. heard viva voce evidence from Mr. Badgerow’s sister, mainly confirming Mr. Badgerow’s explanation that the sureties present at the family gathering simply forgot.  Again, Mr. Badgerow seeks to have the transcript before and ruling of Taylor J. admitted as fresh evidence on appeal.

Procedure for this Appeal

[23]         After the unsuccessful application before Taylor J., Mr. Badgerow applied to this court to review Ramsay J.’s ruling pursuant to s. 680.  Pursuant to s. 680(1), Blair J.A., sitting as the Chief Justice’s designate, directed that this court consider the application.  The parties consented to putting the s. 680 application before a single judge of this court, pursuant to s. 680(2).

II.               ISSUES

[24]         This appeal under s. 680(1) of the Code involves two issues: (1) Should the decision of Ramsay J. that the appellant and his sureties were deliberate in his failure to report on Christmas Eve and that his bail should be revoked on that basis be set aside? (2) If so, has the appellant shown cause why he should be granted bail?

III.            ANALYSIS

[25]         Section 680(1) of the Code provides:

680(1)              A decision made by a judge under s. 522 or subsection 524(4) or (5) or a decision made by a judge of the court of appeal under s. 261 or 679 may, on the direction of the chief justice or acting chief justice of the court of appeal, be reviewed by that court and that court may, if it does not confirm the decision,

            a) vary the decision; or

b) substitute such other decision as, in its opinion, should have been made.

Section 524(4) provides:

524(4)              Where an accused described in paragraph 3(a) is taken before a judge and the judge finds

a)     that the accused has contravened or had been about to contravene his … recognizance,

...

he shall cancel the …recognizance and order that the accused be detained in custody unless the accused, having been given a reasonable opportunity to do so, shows cause why his detention in custody is not justified within the meaning of subsection 515(10).

[26]         In R. v. Daniels (1997), 35 O.R. (3d) 737, this court considered the proper operation and application of s. 680, albeit in the context of a Crown appeal from an order of this court granting bail pending appeal. Doherty J.A. described the “broad remedial powers” of the court of appeal under this section at para. 26:

The broad remedial powers found in s. 680(1) do not suggest that the review provided by s. 680 is other than an appellate review. The power to “vary” in s. 680(1)(a) refers to the reviewing court's power to make the same type of order as was made at the initial hearing but to vary the specifics of that order. For example, the reviewing court could hold that an appellant was properly released on bail but alter the conditions of that release. The power to “substitute” found in s. 680(1)(b) recognizes that the reviewing court may make an entirely different order than that made on the initial bail application. For example, when the reviewing court determines that bail should have been refused, it substitutes an order dismissing the application for bail for the initial order granting bail. It is noteworthy that while s. 680(1)(b) gives the reviewing court a wide authority, it still directs that the court make the order which should have been made on the s. 679 application. The broad remedial powers found in s. 680(1) arm the reviewing court with the authority to make any order which could have been made on the initial application. Arguably those with powers imply a correctness standard on review. They do not, however, alter the appellate nature of the process contemplated by s. 680: R. v. M. (S.H.), [1989] 2 S.C.R. 446 at pp. 464-66, 50 C.C.C. (3d) 503 at pp. 548-49.

[27]         He also held at para. 29 that when exercising its appellate jurisdiction under this section, the court can hear fresh evidence of a change in circumstances and use that evidence in determining the appropriate order, even though the court on review is making the order that should have been made on the original application.

[28]         In this case, following the s. 524(4) proceeding before Ramsay J., a breach hearing was held where Takach J. dismissed the charge on the basis that he accepted the appellant’s evidence, at least on the basis of a reasonable doubt, that he forgot to report on Christmas Eve, forgetting that it was a Thursday. On that hearing, there was also evidence from the appellant’s father, one of his sureties, that the conversation he had with the appellant’s mother where they agreed that she would be the one to take the appellant to report on Christmas Eve did not occur, as Ramsay J. may have inferred, on that day, but a few days earlier.

[29]         In my view, the findings of Takach J., made based on viva voce testimony and not only on the paper record before Ramsay J., are properly admitted as fresh evidence on the s. 680 application.

[30]         Having admitted the fresh evidence, in my view, the conclusion of Ramsay J. that the appellant and his sureties acted deliberately or in collusion when they failed to ensure that he report on Christmas Eve cannot stand. The appellant and his sureties gave an explanation for what occurred. That explanation was un-contradicted and was further explained before Takach J. Furthermore, the appellant had reported faithfully every Thursday until that day. His sureties have a very significant amount to lose in the event of a breach where a judge finds mens rea, including recklessness or wilful blindness.

[31]         It is clear that the sole basis for Ramsay J.’s finding that the appellant had not shown cause was  his conclusion that the appellant had acted deliberately in failing to appear and that his sureties could no longer be trusted to supervise him.

[32]         In my view, in light of the fresh evidence and my conclusions based on it, the correct result is that I do not confirm the decision of Ramsay J. Under subsection (b), I can then substitute the decision that should have been made.     

[33]         This is an unusual case from a procedure and timing point of view, because this appeal was heard after the new trial commenced, and therefore, while the appellant is before the trial judge and under his jurisdiction. In those circumstances, I raised with counsel whether the appropriate order for this court would be to refer the show cause issue to the trial judge. This would certainly be consistent with this court’s preferred approach, as expressed in cases such as R. v. Ranger (2003), 180 O.A.C. 138, and R. v. Durrani (2008), 94 O.R. (3d) 350, when the trial has not yet begun.

[34]         Counsel for the appellant took the position that under s. 680, this court does not have the option as one of the allowed substitutions, to remit the show cause decision to the trial judge. He also argued that in the circumstances of this case, such an order would not serve the interests of justice. He relies on the recent procedural history where following the decision of Takach J., the appellant first moved before Taylor J., the trial judge (but before the trial had started) for a new bail order. On that motion, the Crown opposed the jurisdiction of Taylor J. on the basis that the appellant should have pursued a s. 680 application for review of Ramsay J.’s order or concede the correctness of that decision. Taylor J. accepted that position. The appellant submits that having then obtained leave from the Chief Justice’s designate, and proceeded with this review, it is in the interests of justice that he now receive the decision from this court. He also points to the fact that the trial is proceeding from Monday to Thursday each week and it would possibly cause the trial judge to have to disrupt that schedule to conduct a show cause hearing.

[35]         I am persuaded by the appellant that in the circumstances of this case, it is appropriate that this court make the decision under s. 680(1)(b) and not refer it to the trial judge. Besides the arguments made by counsel, another reason that has persuaded me is that in this case, there are no new circumstances that may require a show cause hearing with viva voce or other new evidence. Rather, the decision is to be made on the record subject to one matter raised by the Crown which I will refer to below. This does not in any way preclude the bail issue from being revisited before the trial judge if new circumstances arise. Because of this decision, it is unnecessary for me to decide whether this court has the power to refer the show cause decision to the trial judge in the appropriate case.

[36]         As I indicated above, the sole basis for Ramsay J.’s conclusion that the appellant had not shown cause was his view that the appellant’s conduct in failing to report was deliberate and that the sureties were complicit and could no longer be trusted. There was no other change from the record before Crane J. Based on the fresh evidence, I accept that the appellant forgot to report on Christmas Eve, as he testified, as did the sureties. The question before me therefore is: is the appellant’s negligent failure to report on Christmas Eve sufficient cause to revoke his bail? In my view, based on the record before Crane J., the appellant has shown cause to be entitled to continue his previous bail. Having only once forgotten to report on a special occasion, and having reported upon being reminded, and being under the very stringent conditions imposed by Crane J. as well as the surety bonds posted or pledged by the sureties along with the alarm system that was installed to ensure that the appellant could not leave the home where he was staying without a surety, in my view, the appellant has shown cause why he should not be detained in accordance with the reasons of Crane J. granting him bail pending his new trial. Before reaching a final conclusion, however, I must address the fresh evidence application of the respondent.

[37]         The respondent seeks to introduce fresh evidence following the argument on this review motion. It is new evidence that the Crown just learned from a man who says he agreed to assist the appellant following his arrest in 1999 by removing what appeared to be store-bought sex video tapes from the appellant’s attic and throwing them in the garbage. I agree with the appellant that on its face, this proposed fresh evidence, without more, is not relevant to my decision on the show cause. However, I also agree with the appellant that the Crown is not precluded from relying on this new evidence and seeking to elaborate on it if it wishes to bring a s. 523(2)(a) application for detention before the trial judge.

IV.             CONCLUSION

[38]         On this s. 680(1) review application, the decision of Ramsay J. is set aside and an order shall go for bail in accordance with the conditions contained in the original recognizance ordered by Crane J. on July 24, 2009, together with any additional conditions or variations that may be properly requested by the Crown, in which event, I may be spoken to.
