ENDORSEMENT

[1]              Each of the parties has filed a separate motion to this court.  Both were heard together.  Some brief background will be beneficial to understanding the nature of both motions, and my disposition of them.

BRIEF BACKGROUND

[2]              Mr. Gilbert and Ms. Warren met in 1994; they decided in March of 1996 that they would live together; and, in April 1996, the disputed property (the “farm property”) was purchased.  The farm property was purchased for $125,000, with a $10,000 deposit, and financed by a first and second mortgage.  Apparently it now has a value of approximately $1,500,000 to $2,000,000. 

[3]              At the time the farm property was purchased, title to it was in the name of Mr. Gilbert and his aunt, Elizabeth Gilbert, as joint tenants.  Elizabeth Gilbert died in July of 2005.  As a result of her death, Mr. Gilbert held the legal title to the property in his name alone.  

[4]              On February 5, 2003, Mr. Gilbert was arrested and charged with assaulting Ms. Warren. Ms. Warren and her son and the parties’ daughter remained on the farm property until November 2003, at which time they vacated pursuant to a court order.  Mr. Gilbert then went into possession and continues to reside on the farm property.

[5]              Also in February 2003, Ms. Warren commenced proceedings in which she claimed a beneficial interest in the property.  This was vigorously opposed by Mr. Gilbert.  After a lengthy trial that occupied days in October 2005 and February and March 2006, MacDougall J. – in reasons released May 19, 2006 – found that Ms. Warren holds a one-half interest in the property by way of resulting trust and constructive trust.

[6]              Mr. Gilbert appealed MacDougall J’s decision which, on December 18, 2006, this court dismissed in its entirety, with costs.  

[7]              Not satisfied, the Estate of Elizabeth Gilbert moved to set aside the judgment of MacDougall J.  On July 18, 2007 the motion was dismissed, and the appeal of that decision to this court was dismissed on October 10, 2008.  There are two more proceedings in this matter, which seems to bring us to these motions.

[8]              First, in his decision of May 19, 2006, MacDougall J. provided that an accounting hearing was to take place to settle matters such as income, expenditures and liabilities as between Mr. Gilbert and Ms. Warren.  That hearing took place on July 21, 2009 and MacDougall J’s decision was released on August 21, 2009 (the “accounting decision”).  Second, MacDougall J. rendered a decision on costs dated August 21, 2009 (the “costs decision”).  I am told that it is Mr. Gilbert’s intention to appeal both of those decisions to this court.

THE MOTIONS

[9]              David Gilbert’s motion (M38594) – as argued by his counsel – is said to involve two discrete matters.  First, he seeks to re-open the appeal decision of this court dated December 18, 2006.  Second, he requests directions in connection with his intended appeal of the “accounting” and “costs” decisions of MacDougal J referred to above. 

[10]         Kellie Warren’s motion (M38717) also seeks different forms of relief.  First, she asks this court to dismiss Mr. Gilbert’s motion.  Second, she requests an order requiring Mr. Gilbert to immediately pay the costs of prior proceedings that remain outstanding.  Third, she requests an order that no further proceedings be instituted by Mr. Gilbert without leave of this Court.  And, finally, she seeks an order for security for costs.

David Gilbert’s motion

[11]         Although he frames his motion as a request to re-open the appeal decided by this court on December 18, 2006, what he really seeks to do is to amend, set aside or vary the May 19, 2006 judgment of MacDougall J. on the basis of newly discovered documents.  To obtain this relief he must make a motion to the Superior Court of Justice pursuant to r. 59.06(2).  This court has no jurisdiction to grant such relief: Aristocrat v. Aristocrat (2004), 73 O.R. (3d) 275 (CA).

[12]         This court confirmed in Aristocrat that even where a judgment of the Superior Court of Justice has been affirmed on appeal, the proper venue for a r. 59.06(2) motion is the Superior Court of Justice.  As that court is the proper venue for the hearing of the motion, it is also the proper venue for any request for directions concerning the motion.  Moreover, issues arising out of newly discovered documents should also be submitted to the Superior Court of Justice pursuant to a r. 59.06(2) motion. 

[13]         Regarding the intention to appeal the costs and accounting decisions of MacDougall J.; they are separate from the original judgment.  Each appeal has its own record and the contents of the record are determined with reference to the Rules of Civil Procedure and Practice Guidelines.  And, as I understand the oral submissions, it is in connection with this intended appeal that Mr. Gilbert seeks directions.  In this regard, he poses a series of question in his Notice of Motion, which I will comment on very briefly:

·     Directions are not necessary as to the constitution of the court for the purposes of the intended appeal. 

·     The court cannot give directions on matters such as what materials are necessary for the resolution of the questions under appeal.  Those are matters for counsel to decide when the grounds of appeal are known. 

·     It will be necessary to file the documents and evidence for the accounting hearings by the parties other than their own affidavits. 

[14]         I would conclude this part of my analysis with this observation.  A motion for directions is not, nor should it be, a substitute for counsel researching legal questions.  It seems to me that counsel should have been able to answer the questions posed without recourse to this court.

Kellie Warren’s motion

[15]         First, with respect to Ms. Warren’s request for an order that no further proceedings may be instituted without leave of this court; that request must be made by application to the Superior Court of Justice: see Courts of Justice Act, s. 140(1). 

[16]         Second, Ms. Warren seeks an order requiring Mr. Gilbert to immediately pay the costs of prior proceedings.  Given the history of this matter, I am inclined to grant this relief.  The record demonstrates that the present motion is part of an ongoing and continuous campaign to simply delay payment of previous judgments.  I note that in her book of authorities, Ms. Warren lists 12 separate decisions in this dispute, two of which were from this court. 

[17]         For these reasons, pursuant to r. 56.01(c), I make such an order.   Mr. Gilbert should not be permitted to proceed with any further appeals until the previous costs awards are paid, or unless this court otherwise orders. 

[18]         Finally, Ms. Warren’s requests related to security for costs, in my view, should be made when and if the intention of Mr. Gilbert to file another appeal actually materializes.  

CONCLUSION

[19]         Even though I did briefly respond to the questions posed in Mr. Gilbert’s Notice of Motion, I also observed that it was wholly unnecessary to have asked them.  I view this as amounting to a dismissal of his motion.  That coupled with Ms. Warren’s success in obtaining an order pursuant to r. 56.01(c), entitles her to her costs of these motions.  Accordingly, I award her such costs in the aggregate amount of $5,000 inclusive of disbursements and GST.
