On appeal from the order of Justice Patricia C. Hennessy of the Superior Court of Justice, dated July 23, 2008, with reasons reported at [2008] O.J. No. 3397.

Rouleau J.A.:

[1]              The appellant, Manon Gauthier, was a graduate student in the Faculty of Education at the University of Ottawa. In 1998, she undertook the necessary steps to begin her doctoral studies. This case stems from a disagreement between the appellant and the respondents, the University of Ottawa and professors Michel Saint-Germain and Pierre Boudreau. 

[2]              The statement of claim alleges the following causes of action:

(a)       breach of contract;

(b)       negligent misrepresentation;

(c)       breach of the duty of care;

(d)       intentional infliction of mental suffering; and

(e)       intimidation.

[3]              The appellant claims general damages in the amount of $1,000,000, special damages, punitive damages in the amount of $500,000, and pre- and post-judgment interest, as well as costs.

[4]              The respondents filed a motion to strike the appellant’s statement of claim. The motions judge granted the respondents’ motion, concluding that the court had no jurisdiction over the subject matter of the action (rule 21.01(3)(a) of the Rules of Civil Procedure, R.R.O. 1990, Reg. 194 (“Rules”)).

[5]              Alternatively, the judge ordered that, should this matter proceed, the statement of claim should be entirely revised because a significant portion of it, in its current form, contains evidence rather than material facts (rule 25.06(1)). She also ordered that the proceeding be transferred from Sudbury to Ottawa. 

[6]              The appellant appeals this decision.

[7]              For the following reasons, I would uphold the decision of the judge striking out the statement of claim and ordering the transfer from Sudbury to Ottawa, but I would grant the appellant leave to amend the statement of claim in accordance with these reasons.

THE FACTS

1)     Overview of the facts

[8]              The facts stated below are drawn from the statement of claim. When dealing with a motion to strike a pleading pursuant to rule 21.01(3), the court must consider the facts alleged in the statement of claim as being true unless they are manifestly ridiculous or incapable of being proven.

[9]              In 1998, encouraged by Michel Saint-Germain, a professor in the Faculty of Education who would become Ms. Gauthier’s first thesis supervisor, the appellant decided to do her doctorate at the University of Ottawa. The appellant claims that Mr. Saint-Germain made two representations: first, that it would take Ms. Gauthier only four years to complete her studies, and second, that the University would give her a $50,000 scholarship and guarantee her a research assistantship as soon as she registered for full-time doctoral studies. Because she had not written a thesis for her Master’s, her registration for her doctorate would be contingent upon her completing and submitting an interim report.

[10]         The appellant maintains that from the beginning of her studies, she was harassed and intimidated by Mr. Saint-Germain. She accuses her supervisor of having invited her to spend a week at his cottage, and of having asked her questions of a personal and sexual nature. She also claims that the intentional intimidation by Mr. Saint-Germain set back her progress in her doctoral studies. 

[11]         In May 2002, Mr. Saint-Germain was replaced by Pierre Boudreau as her thesis supervisor. The appellant maintains that Mr. Boudreau had never before served as a thesis supervisor, that he was incompetent, and that his incompetence created several additional delays. In particular, the appellant maintains that he gave her contradictory instructions, and that he did not remember the recommendations that he had previously made. She also claims that, on two occasions, the University’s administration admitted that Mr. Boudreau was not competent. Moreover, the appellant notes that further delays were generated by the resignation of several members of her thesis committee, as well as the fact that the University took a long time to replace them.

2)     Delays and intimidation

[12]         The appellant maintains that her studies were delayed on several occasions due to the negligence or incompetence of the University and her thesis supervisors. For example, the appellant submitted her interim report in the summer of 1998, but Mr. Saint-Germain waited more than a year before accepting it. Moreover, Mr. Saint-Germain never provided a valid reason for that year-long delay. 

[13]         In April 2002, Mr. Saint-Germain indicated that the thesis proposal was ready to be defended, and the appellant therefore submitted it to the committee. The committee deemed the proposal to be generally satisfactory, and recommended that the appellant make a few changes that would not take more than a month to complete. Mr. Saint-Germain, who had previously indicated that he would support the proposal, then told the appellant that major changes were necessary, and that she would need four to six years to complete her doctorate. The appellant took this change of heart regarding the thesis proposal, along with the time required to complete the doctorate, as part of Mr. Saint-Germain’s attempt to harass and intimidate her because she had rebuffed his inappropriate advances. 

[14]         In early May 2002, after the appellant had informed the University of Mr. Saint-Germain’s behaviour, the University’s administration recognized that the appellant’s contact with Mr. Saint-Germain should cease, and gave her the option of changing thesis supervisors, which she decided to do. From May 2002 onwards, the appellant therefore pursued her doctorate under Mr. Boudreau’s supervision.

[15]         In September 2003, the appellant submitted her thesis proposal to the committee. She maintains that over the following three years, she suffered several unreasonable delays, several members of her committee resigned, Mr. Boudreau and two of the committee members lost their respective copies of her thesis proposal, and Mr. Boudreau and the board members gave her advice and suggested changes that were inconsistent and contradictory. She also maintains that the University was at all times aware of these developments. Moreover, Mr. Slater, the vice-dean of graduate studies, allegedly acknowledged Mr. Saint-Germain’s negligence. In addition, some time after Mr. Boudreau was appointed to replace Mr. Saint-Germain as her thesis supervisor, Mr. Slater allegedly admitted that Mr. Boudreau was equally incompetent, and that the appellant would do well to find a new supervisor.

[16]         In August 2006, the appellant served her statement of claim.

3)     Alleged causes of action

[17]         The appellant’s case is based on several different causes of action. Each of these is summarized in the following paragraphs.

Breach of contract

[18]         The appellant argues that there was a contractual relationship between her and the University, and that the University breached this contract. In general, she claims that the University’s contractual obligation included the duty to provide her with the necessary support with which to meet the requirements of her program, namely in the form of competent faculty members with the appropriate training, and a competent thesis supervisor and committee. She does not claim to be entitled to resources that would guarantee her success, only to reasonable resources arising under the contract. 

Negligence

Negligent misrepresentation

[19]         The appellant maintains that she suffered prejudice by relying on the negligent statements made by Mr. Saint-Germain. According to the appellant, Mr. Saint-Germain had told her that the University would automatically grant her a $50,000 graduate admission scholarship as well as a research assistantship, and that her studies would only last four years. These statements proved to be false. Mr. Saint-Germain’s claims prompted the appellant to move from Sudbury to Ottawa, and to give up her full-time job to dedicate herself entirely to her doctoral studies.

Breach of the duty of care

[20]         The appellant maintains that the respondents were bound by a duty of care. According to her, the agreement she reached with the University required that it, among other things, ensure that its faculty members have the ability to properly carry out their responsibilities as professors and supervisors. She maintains that the University breached this duty by agreeing to have Mr. Saint-Germain, and later Mr. Boudreau, serve as the appellant’s thesis supervisors. In addition to the fact that the University acknowledged the incompetence of these two professors, Mr. Saint-Germain allegedly harassed and intimidated the appellant. 

[21]         In addition, the appellant maintains that Mr. Saint-Germain and Mr. Boudreau had a duty to behave in a competent and professional manner, which required professionalism and diligence in the performance of their duties. She claims that they breached this duty.

Intentional infliction of mental suffering

[22]         The appellant maintains that Mr. Saint-Germain harassed her, thereby causing her mental suffering, pursuant to which she claims damages. In addition, she accuses Mr. Saint-Germain of having invited her to his home and to his cottage, of having asked her inappropriate questions, and of having greeted her with “salut beauté”[1] in the University’s hallways. She maintains that Mr. Saint-Germain’s advances were part of a campaign directed at her and aimed at exploiting her vulnerable position in an effort to intimidate her. She claims that Mr. Saint-Germain had told her that her work was satisfactory, but that he changed his mind after his sexual advances were rebuffed. According to the appellant, Mr. Saint-Germain aimed to thereby demonstrate the extent of his influence over the appellant. She maintains that Mr. Saint-Germain’s advances caused her tremendous stress and significantly affected her health.

Damages

Damages

[23]         The appellant claims damages for the lost opportunity to take classes during the fall 2000 semester, the loss of her guaranteed research assistantship, the loss of her admission scholarship, and the lost opportunity to obtain her doctoral degree within a reasonable period, which also affected her reintegration into the labour market, her prospects of being hired and her access to positions.

Punitive damages

[24]         The appellant claims punitive damages because of the harm and mental suffering resulting from the gross negligence of the respondents.

ANALYSIS

The issues

[25]         The appellant appeals the orders of the Superior Court staying the action and changing the venue for the action from Sudbury to Ottawa. In particular, she claims that the motions judge erred by:

1. concluding that the court did not have jurisdiction due to the scholastic nature of the dispute; and

2. failing to consider the factor of “serious prejudice” when she changed the venue for the action from Sudbury to Ottawa.

1)        Does the Superior Court have jurisdiction over the subject matter of the action?

[26]         The motions judge granted the motion to stay the proceedings pursuant to rule 21.01(3), on the grounds that the Superior Court does not have jurisdiction over disagreements of an academic nature.

[27]         The appellant maintains that the motions judge’s decision was vitiated by errors. According to her, the causes of action pleaded are based in tort law and contract law. These issues therefore do not arise from the scholastic or academic domain. 

[28]         In contrast, the respondents maintain that the motions judge correctly understood the jurisdiction of the Superior Court, and that the latter should not involve itself in the internal affairs of universities. According to the respondents, the jurisprudence establishes that if the true nature of the dispute is scholastic, that is to say, arising from university responsibilities related to the granting of diplomas, then the Superior Court has no jurisdiction. 

a)                 Analysis of the case law

[29]         I am of the opinion that the case law relied upon does not support the principle advanced by the respondents. The starting point for the analysis revolves around the fact that the Superior Court is a court of inherent jurisdiction. As our court explained in TeleZone Inc. v. Canada (Attorney General) (2008), 94 O.R. (3d) 19, this jurisdiction can only be limited by clear and express legislative or contractual provisions. Barring any such provision, the court is deemed to have jurisdiction to rule on a dispute. 

[30]         However, when the judicial remedy sought aims to alter an internal academic decision made by university officials, the appropriate route remains judicial review. Abundant case law supports this. Under such circumstances, the court is justifiably reluctant to involve itself in the University’s internal affairs, respecting as it does the independence and discretion associated with the decision-making process in place in the academic sector. In this regard, see: Re Polten and Governing Council of the University of Toronto et. al. (1976), 8 O.R. (2d) 749 (Div. Crt.); F. v. University of Toronto (1997), 143 D.L.R. (4th) 574 (Div. Crt.); Paine v. University of Toronto et al. (1982), 34 O.R. (2d) 770 (Ont. C.A.); Ward v. University of Prince Edward Island (1997), 157 Nfld. & P.E.I.R. 129 (P.E.I. S.C. (T.D.)).

[31]         In Warraich v. University of Manitoba (2003), 173 Man. R. (2d) 202 (C.A.), a case in which a student suspended an application for judicial review in order to bring an action aiming to induce the university to let him continue his studies, the court concluded that the case concerned an internal academic decision, and was therefore outside its jurisdiction. 

[32]         In addition, in this case we know that the link between Ms. Gauthier and the University of Ottawa is of a contractual nature, thereby giving rise to obligations in both contract and tort. In this regard, see: Young v. Bella, [2006] 1 S.C.R. 108, at para. 31. Thus, when a case alleges civil offences or breach of contract in an effort to recover damages, it follows that the court has jurisdiction to hear the case. For instance in Mohl v. University of British Columbia (2006), 52 B.C.L.R. (4th) 89 (C.A.), and Hoizaima v. Perry (2008), 232 Man. R. (2d) 85 (Q.B.), and in light of the fact that the plaintiffs in these cases had not put in issue their grades or the decision by university officials to not grant them their diplomas, the courts held that an action in negligence or breach of contract fell within their jurisdiction.

[33]         The respondents, on the other hand, claim that this court’s decisions in Wong v. University of Toronto (1992), 4 Admin. L.R. (2d) 95, Dawson v. University of Toronto, 2007 ONCA 875, and Zabo v. University of Ottawa, [2005] O.J. No. 2664, leave to appeal to S.C.C. refused, [2005] S.C.C.A. No. 354, support the notion that the Superior Court would not have jurisdiction over a cause of action against a university if it involved issues of an academic nature. Thus, if the core of the dispute concerns a school matter, the court has no jurisdiction, even if the underlying cause of action is based on tort and breach of contract and the remedy sought aims to recover damages.

[34]         In my opinion, the above-mentioned cases do not establish such a broad principle. 

Wong

[35]         In Wong, a doctoral student brought an action against his university after his thesis supervisor resigned. At first instance, in Wong v. University of Toronto (1989), 79 D.L.R. (4th) 652 (Ont. Dist. Ct.), the Superior Court held that the dispute was outside its jurisdiction. In so doing, the Court based itself on two premises. The first, set out at pp. 659-664, centred on a broad interpretation of the University of Toronto Act, 1971, S.O. 1971, c. 56, confirming the grant of “visitatorial powers” to the relevant institution. Such powers are a vestige of the English common law and grant universities a certain discretionary power, one that is not subject to review by the courts: Lazar Sarna and Noah Sarna, The Law of Schools and Universities (Markham: LexisNexis, 2007) at pp. 83-94.  It is worth noting that the notion of “visitatorial powers” was not raised in this case. The second premise invoked by the Superior Court in Wong, at pp. 666-67, presumed that by registering at a university, a student agrees to address any dispute involving the university through the institution’s internal mechanisms, without resort to the courts.

[36]         In the appeal to this court, the jurisdictional issue was not debated because the parties accepted the Superior Court’s conclusions in this regard. Nonetheless, the Court of Appeal noted that if the agreement between the student and the university, whether it was explicit or implicit, had provided that supervision by a specific professor was guaranteed, then the resignation of that professor would have resulted in a cause of action for breach of the contract for the student against the university. In short, the relevant issue was not the nature of the dispute, but the existence and the content of the contract.

Dawson

[37]         In Dawson, the plaintiff, who was behind in preparing her doctoral thesis, took leave from the university on the advice of her thesis supervisor in order to complete her thesis outside of the program. However, when she applied for readmission to the doctoral program, her application was denied based on a negative evaluation of her thesis by two members of the graduate studies faculty. When her application for readmission was rejected, she appealed the decision through the institution’s internal channels. After obtaining the advice of two external experts who reached the same conclusion as the initial evaluation, the university committee upheld the decision not to readmit the former doctoral student. Five years later, the plaintiff commenced an action based on what was, essentially, the same factual situation. In this context, she claimed that the advice provided by her thesis supervisor had been negligent. In that case, however, the request to strike the statement of claim was not based on rule 21.01(3), but rather on rule 21.01(1). Hence the key issue did not involve the court’s jurisdiction, but sought instead to determine whether the statement of claim disclosed a reasonable cause of action. 

[38]         In Dawson, the statement of claim had already been amended three times. In Dawson v. University of Toronto, [2007] O.J. No. 591 (Ont. S.C.), the trial judge’s analysis of the latest version of the statement of claim led him to conclude, at para. 19, that the dispute concerned academic matters related to the student’s completion of work in connection with her doctoral program.

[39]         I acknowledge that the trial judge’s reasons could be interpreted quite broadly, so as to bolster the respondents’ position in this case. However, the treatment of the Dawson case on its appeal to this court was more restrained. In effect, the Court of Appeal upheld the decision and rejected the appellant’s claim that the cause of action pleaded was founded in negligence, which therefore allowed it to remain independent and distinct from the academic dispute that arose between Dawson and the university. The true nature of the dispute put in issue the criteria for readmission to the doctoral program, a dispute that Dawson had already appealed through the university’s internal procedures to no avail. In regard to the allegations that her thesis supervisor had improperly advised her, all the while failing to warn her about the possible consequences of withdrawing from the doctoral program, the court emphasized that, in so doing, the professor had referred to the calendar, a document that contained all of the pertinent details and options available to the appellant.

[40]         The court was thus right to conclude that the appellant’s statement of claim did not disclose a reasonable cause of action. Moreover, there was no claim that the decision to deny the appellant’s readmission to the doctoral program was negligent or even incorrect. Thus, given that her application for readmission was denied because of the unsatisfactory quality of her doctoral work, the appellant did not suffer any prejudice in this regard, even if the advice provided by her thesis supervisor was negligent. Moreover, the fact that the thesis supervisor referred to the calendar in his exchanges with the appellant, a fact that was uncontested in this case, would counter the claim that the advice provided by the professor was inadequate and negligent. 

[41]         The principle to be drawn from Dawson is therefore not that the court does not have jurisdiction to rule on disputes of an academic nature, but rather that once her internal appeal was dismissed by the university, the plaintiff in that case could not take what was essentially the same complaint before the courts by claiming that, this time, it was a tort matter.

Zabo

[42]         Zabo, like Dawson, concerned a doctoral student who had filed an appeal internally as a result of certain decisions made by his committee. The plaintiff had even applied for a judicial review, but had withdrawn the application before it was heard. The plaintiff only filed his lawsuit against the university afterwards, claiming that the revisions required by his committee constituted a breach of contract. He also claimed to have been the victim of a malicious conspiracy involving various university officials.

[43]         The trial judge refused to strike out the cause of action based on malicious conspiracy, instead striking it on the grounds of breach of contract after concluding that it clearly could not succeed in this regard. Thus, it was not a matter of the court’s jurisdiction. In fact, the portion of the cause of action that concerned civil torts was kept. In sum, the court recognized, in light of the facts in the case, that the plaintiff could not succeed in his cause of action based on breach of contract. 

[44]         On appeal, the court expressed doubt about whether the remedy obtained could be based on rule 21.01(3)(a), i.e. for lack of jurisdiction. It then indicated that, given the exceptional circumstances of the case before it, the trial judge had not erred by striking the portion of the statement of claim based on breach of contract, namely because this aspect was untenable, and could be struck out separately pursuant to rule 25.11. 

b)                 Principles stemming from the case law

[45]         It would therefore appear that no precedent dictates that the court does not have jurisdiction to hear cases for the sole reason that the tort or related breach of contract arises from a scholastic dispute.

[46]         In my opinion, in order to determine whether the court has jurisdiction, it is more helpful to focus on the remedy being claimed by the plaintiff. When a party seeks to overturn an internal academic decision made by a university, the appropriate route is judicial review. However, if the plaintiff alleges the constituent elements of a cause of action based in tort or breach of contract, while claiming damages, the court will have jurisdiction even if the dispute stems from the scholastic or academic activities of the university in question. 

[47]         Moreover, when a student enrols in a university, it is understood that the student will be subject to the discretion of that institution when it comes to resolving academic issues, whether in the evaluation of the quality of the student’s work, the structure and implementation of university programs, or the identification of the skills required to serve as a professor or thesis supervisor. This discretion is very broad. Thus, claiming that a grade is incorrect or that a professor is incompetent, without more, will not normally be sufficient grounds on which to base a cause of action in breach of contract or tort.

[48]         In order to establish a cause of action for breach of contract, the student must demonstrate that the university failed to fulfil an express or tacit obligation to which this institution had committed by accepting the student’s registration. In Wong, the statement of claim was struck because the plaintiff was unable to demonstrate that the university had agreed to assign him a specific supervisor. In the same vein, Zabo shows that a university can require changes to a thesis unless the relationship extant between the university and the student implied otherwise. 

[49]         In regard to allegations of negligence, Young confirms that a university indeed owes a duty of care to its students. Nonetheless, in order to establish a cause of action based on negligence, the student must do more than merely state that a professor was too exacting in his evaluations or demonstrate his or her incompetence. In order to establish that the university breached its duty of care, the student must plead specific facts tending to show that the conduct of the university or professor in question constituted an intentional tort, as in Zabo, or was outside the broad margin of discretion that the university and its professors enjoy.

[50]         Thus, even if the court has jurisdiction, Dawson and Zabo nonetheless demonstrate that it is prepared to strike out the cause of action under rule 21.01(1) or, in exceptional circumstances, under rule 25.11 when it appears that the cause of action is untenable or could not succeed. The circumstances in which the court will exercise its discretion to strike a cause of action fall into one of two categories. First, if the action in tort or breach of contract is but an indirect attempt to appeal an internal academic decision when the appropriate approach would be judicial review (for example, the decision to give a certain grade, to require certain work, to refuse admission to a program or to not award a diploma), the court can strike it out. Second, if the pleadings do not provide the necessary information to demonstrate that the university or its employees have exceeded their broad discretion, the court could also strike out the cause of action. 

c)                 Principles applied to this case

[51]         In this case, the appellant makes several allegations against the respondents. She claims, among other things, to have suffered pecuniary damage by leaving her position and moving to Ottawa as a result of the incorrect and negligent statements made by Professor Saint-Germain. She also maintains that her supervisor deliberately caused her mental suffering. She alleges that the University was bound by a duty of care, and that it breached this duty by assigning her an incompetent professor who harassed her, and another one who was simply incompetent. In this regard, she provides a number of examples of what she considers incompetence. She also claims that these actions breached the contract that existed between her and the University. Finally, she maintains that the negligence and incompetence of several members of the faculty and of the administration caused her unreasonable and excessive delays in the completion of her program of study. In short, she claims damages from the respondents because of their negligence and breach of contract. She is neither seeking a reversal of decisions made by the professors or by the members of her committee, nor to force the University to award her a diploma. 

[52]         Given that it was filed under rule 21.01(3) and not under rule 21.01(1)(b), the motion ought to have been dismissed because the Superior Court has jurisdiction in matters of tort and breach of contract. The respondents did not identify any legislative or contractual provision that would deprive the appellant of her access to the court. 

[53]         Alternatively, the respondents invoked rule 25.11 in order to secure recognition that the statement of claim constitutes an abuse of the process of the court. As our court stated in Zabo, it is only under exceptional circumstances that a statement of claim of this type will be struck out pursuant to rule 25.11. The respondents did not in any way demonstrate that such circumstances exist in this case. 

2)        Should the statement of claim be struck without leave to amend?

[54]         The statement of claim contains 154 paragraphs, most of which plead evidence rather than material facts. It is thus not a concise statement of claim as envisaged by the Rules. Moreover, some of the allegations concerning negligence are lacking in precision. I therefore share the opinion of the motions judge, who concluded in the alternative that the statement of claim should be entirely rewritten, paying particular attention to the elimination of evidence and repetitive claims. 

[55]         Although rule 21.01(1)(b) was not invoked in the original notice of motion, in light of the parties’ arguments, I believe that it is nevertheless important to raise it because, if the statement of claim discloses no reasonable cause of action under this provision, it should be struck and the appellant may not be granted leave to amend it.

[56]         To my mind, this is the trickiest issue. To what extent should the appellant be granted leave to amend the statement of claim or, in other words, do the allegations contained in the statement of claim disclose one or more causes of action and, if so, what are these causes of action?

[57]         In my opinion, at this stage and based on the record before us, it is impossible to conclude that the appellant did not raise any cause of action. From my reading of the record, this action is not an indirect attempt to appeal an internal academic decision, and the allegations advanced by the appellant do not concern behaviour within what I have previously described as the broad discretion granted to universities in the design and implementation of their academic programs. 

[58]         Some of the allegations raised in this case appear to me defensible and, at the pleadings stage, can therefore be retained. By way of example, the allegations that Mr. Saint-Germain harassed the appellant and made promises to her that he did not keep are of a contractual or tortious nature, and do not fall within the purview of the University’s academic discretion.

[59]         On the other hand, certain allegations against the University, such as the one that the University failed to ensure the presence of an organization or committee that could provide the appellant with judicious advice concerning her problems and her future, are more tenuous. In order to maintain this kind of allegation, the plaintiff would normally have to indicate to what extent the university’s behaviour constituted a breach of the agreement reached with the student, or was inconsistent with the objective criteria established or accepted by this post-secondary institution to address such situations. Similarly, a bare allegation that a professor is not competent is insufficient to support a valid cause of action. The academic standards and expectations regarding the competence of the university staff are set by the university, and this institution is responsible for the evaluation and performance of its staff. It is therefore necessary for a plaintiff to indicate the extent to which the conduct of a professor fails to meet the accepted standard of conduct governing such a profession, while taking into consideration the broad discretion held by universities in terms of how they operate and implement their programs. 

[60]         Under the circumstances, given that the claim does not possess the level of precision required by the Rules, and without overlooking the fact that the parties’ submissions on this aspect of the appeal were very limited, I am of the opinion that a detailed analysis of all of the contested paragraphs and the different allegations contained therein is not desirable. It is not up to this court to make the necessary amendments. In my opinion, it is preferable to give the appellant the opportunity to reformulate the greater part of her statement of claim, and to rule on the viable causes of action once this has been done.

[61]         In conclusion on this issue, I would modify almost the entire statement of claim by striking out paragraphs 6 to 141 and granting leave to amend them. 

3)        Did the motions judge err in failing to consider the factor of “serious prejudice” when she transferred the proceeding from Sudbury to Ottawa?

[62]         The motions judge granted the respondents’ motion to have this case transferred from Sudbury to Ottawa.  In her reasons, she noted that the majority of the events underlying the dispute had occurred in Ottawa, and that most of the witnesses lived there. Moreover, she rejected the argument that there was a better chance of empanelling an impartial jury in Sudbury.

[63]         In this regard, the appellant maintains that the motions judge erred. She points out that it was incumbent upon the respondents to demonstrate that serious prejudice would result from hearing the action in Sudbury, a burden that they did not discharge. In contrast, the respondents maintain that the motions judge took all of the relevant factors into consideration. They also note that the decision to transfer the case is discretionary and, consequently, this court should not overturn it unless the motions judge made a palpable and overriding error: Visic v. University of Windsor (2007), 227 O.A.C. 129 (Div. Ct.), aff’d 2008 ONCA 731, leave to appeal to S.C.C. refused, [2008] S.C.C.A. No 534. 

[64]         I am not persuaded that the motions judge made an error in transferring the proceeding from Sudbury to Ottawa.  I agree with the respondents that this decision is discretionary. In making it, the motions judge weighed all of the relevant considerations. In particular, she noted that Ottawa had an interest in, and close ties with, the proceeding, that the events in dispute occurred in Ottawa, and that most of the witnesses are located there.

CONCLUSION

[65]         For these reasons, I would overturn the decision striking out the appellant’s statement of claim, and would replace it with an order striking out paragraphs 6 to 141, with leave to amend them. I would uphold the decision to transfer the proceeding to Ottawa. If the parties are unable to agree on the issue of costs, the appellant shall file written submissions, not to exceed five pages, within 20 days following the release of this decision, and the respondents shall file written submissions, not to exceed five pages, within 10 days following the receipt of the appellant’s submissions.
