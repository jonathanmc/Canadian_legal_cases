Feldman J.A.:

[1]              The issues on this appeal arise out of the trial judge’s findings that the appellant committed numerous breaches of an agreement made on August 2003 for the breeding of a thoroughbred stallion, named Bold N’ Flashy.

Facts

[2]              The corporate appellant operates a stud farm and is owned by the appellant Michael Byrne. The respondent owns race horses and is owned by a business man, Domenic Romeo. Both men have extensive involvement and experience in the horse breeding and horse racing industries.

[3]              These actions involve the thoroughbred stallion, Bold N’ Flashy, which was owned by the respondent, Terra Farm Ltd., and after its racing career, stood stud at the farm of the appellant, Park Stud Inc. The relationship began with a four-year Breeding Agreement dated December 15, 1995, the essential terms of which were:

·        Terra would supply the stallion for breeding at no charge;

·        Park would absorb all boarding, veterinarian, advertising and other expenses;

·        Terra would pay insurance premiums;

·        Ontario sire fees and breeder’s cup fees were to be paid jointly;

·        all revenues from stud fees were to be shared equally;

·        at the end of the agreement or earlier at Park’s option, the stallion would be syndicated with Park as the syndicate manager;

·        upon syndication, Park could purchase 12 out of 40 shares in the stallion at $4,000 per share.

[4]              This agreement was extended by the parties in October 1999 to December 12, 2001.

[5]              The stallion began to breed in the 1996 breeding season, which ran from February to early June, and continued every year thereafter. In December 2001, Park exercised the option to syndicate and purchased its 12 out of 40 shares at that time. Thereafter, the expenses of the horse were shared, with Park charging Terra 70% of the expenses. However, problems developed in the relationship. Terra complained about Park’s reporting and accounting format, resulting in a procedural agreement of May 2002 that obliged Park to provide monthly reports of stud fees together with payment of fees less expenses. Terra also refused to agree to one point in Park’s proposed syndication agreement regarding the percentage approval vote required to permit major changes in management of the horse. Park had the exclusive right to manage the horse, and wanted that to be subject to a 75% ownership vote, while Terra would only agree if the percentage required was 66%.

[6]              This latter disagreement resulted in an application by Terra in 2003 to wind up the partnership. While this litigation was pending, Mr. Byrne refused to pay the stud fees owed to Terra for the 2002 breeding season.[1] The parties and their lawyers met to resolve the issues between them on August 13, 2003, when the parties entered into a new written agreement. Mr. Byrne’s understanding of their agreement was that they were rolling back or undoing the steps they had taken toward syndication and joint ownership and reverting back to the essential terms of their original agreement except that instead of a 50/50 split of stud fee revenue, the split was to be 55/45 in favour of Terra.

[7]              The main terms of the August 13, 2003 agreement by which Terra’s action was dismissed were:

·        The parties recited that their respective interests in the stallion were set forth in the original Breeding Agreement dated December 15, 1995.

·        Terra would provide the stallion to Park for breeding purposes at no charge to Terra except for 55% of the Breeder’s Cup fees and 100% of the insurance;

·        the stallion would stand stud at Park for the rest of his life subject to Park’s option to terminate the arrangement on 3 months’ notice;

·        all “revenues from stud fees” would be shared 55% Terra and 45% Park, which would be applied retroactively for the 2002 breeding season;

·        on August 1st of each year, Park would provide to Terra a list of mares bred, including stud fees and owners’ names and, beginning in March each year, on the 15th of the month following (except August which would be provided in October), Park would provide a monthly report of stud fees paid together with a cheque for Terra’s share of revenue;

·        Terra would repay the share price to Park, Park would repay the expenses paid by Terra since December 2001 and Breeder’s Cup and insurance obligations would be adjusted back to December 2001;

·        Park would employ the customary degree of skill and care in the management of the stallion but would not be responsible for injury, disease or death.

[8]              Before this settlement meeting and agreement, in July 2003, Mr. Byrne, as syndicate manager, had entered the stallion in a newly created stallion awards program operated by the Canadian Thoroughbred Horse Society (“CTHS”), which program had been approved by the Ontario Racing Commission in March 2003. In the application form, Mr. Byrne named Park Stud as the party to which the award should be issued. The stallion qualified for the program because it was standing stud in Ontario and was enrolled in the Ontario Sire Stakes (“OSS”) program. That enrolment had allowed the stallion to race in OSS races before its retirement, making it more desirable for breeding. The $100 cost of enrolment in the OSS was shared between Terra and Park.

[9]              Although the rules of the new award provided that in the event of a change in ownership, the nominator was obliged to advise the CTHS, following the August 2003 agreement Mr. Byrne did not notify the CTHS that the stallion was now owned exclusively by Terra. Consequently, in January 2004, Park received the cheque for $14,529.90 representing the stallion award for the previous year, based on the racing success of the stallion’s progeny. Park forwarded half the amount to Terra.

[10]         Thereafter, Mr. Romeo made his own application to the CTHS in respect of the stallion and asserted Terra’s right to receive the whole amount of the award, copying Mr. Byrne in the correspondence. After learning of this dispute regarding the rights of the parties to this stallion award in November 2004, Park discontinued sending the monthly report of stud fee revenues to Terra. When the Ontario Racing Commission advised the parties in March 2005 that it would not settle their dispute over the right to the stallion award, Terra commenced a lawsuit seeking a determination of entitlement to the award.

[11]         Park responded with a letter from Mr. Byrne dated July 26, 2005. In that letter, Mr. Byrne took the position that the stallion awards constituted revenue that should be shared and that if the parties could not come to an agreement, then Park would only comply with paragraph 10 of the August 2003 agreement, that is to care for the stallion, but would not advertise or promote the horse or attempt to attract outside mares for breeding “to create stud fee revenue.” If they came to a resolution, then Park would use a percentage of the stallion awards for advertising and promotion of the stallion.

[12]         Both before and after August 13, 2003, as part of its management of the stallion for breeding, Park regularly advertised the stallion in thoroughbred magazines, periodicals and the Sire Books that were published in Canada and the United States . Following this letter, Park discontinued all advertising and promotion of the stallion.

[13]         Following the July 26, 2005 letter, Park also failed to make any payment of stud fees including the fees for the 2004 breeding season and all subsequent fees right up to the time of trial and continued not to report as required by the 2003 agreement. On November 8, 2005, Terra commenced the second of the two actions under appeal, claiming relief as a result of Park’s breach of the August 2003 agreement.  On the third day of trial, Park paid $97,500 into court although 55% of the stud fees actually collected for 2004, 2005 and 2006 was $107,800.

Findings of the Trial Judge

[14]         The trial judge identified four issues raised by the appellants’ trial counsel: (1) who owned the stallion after August 13, 2003; (2) who was entitled to the stallion award money; (3) whether the August 2003 agreement was terminated by Terra or repudiated by breach by Park; and (4) did the withholding of the stud fee revenue by Park constitute a breach of fiduciary duty or breach of trust by Park?

[15]         Dealing first with the ownership issue, the trial judge found that, except for the period when Mr. Byrne (Park) owned 12 out of 40 shares in the stallion between December 2001 and August 2003, the stallion was always owned by Mr. Romeo (Terra). The trial judge found that Mr. Byrne, a man with sophisticated business acumen in the stud farm business, made a business decision in August 2003 to exchange a 30% ownership interest in the stallion for the right to earn 45% of the stud fees for the life of the horse. As the stallion was doing very well at the time, it appeared to provide a very profitable arrangement.

[16]         On the second issue, he found that “revenue from stud fees” referred to in the August 2003 agreement, did not include the stallion award monies, which could have been specifically included in the agreement by Mr. Byrne, who knew about the new award, having just recently applied for it for the stallion. As Mr. Byrne (Park) had no contractual right to receive any share of the stallion award monies, Terra was entitled to that total revenue. Further, Mr. Byrne was obliged to indicate correctly the respective ownership interests in the horse on the original application and to advise of the change in ownership wholly to Terra after the August 2003 agreement.

[17]         On the third issue, the trial judge found that by fundamentally breaching the August 2003 agreement by failing to report as required and by failing to pay over any of the stud fees from the 2004, 2005, 2006 and 2007 breeding seasons, Park repudiated the agreement, thereby depriving Terra of virtually the whole benefit of the agreement and entitling Terra to terminate the agreement. Terra accepted Park’s repudiation by commencing the lawsuit before the court.

[18]         The trial judge also found that although the August 2003 agreement did not specifically state that Park had an obligation to advertise and promote the stallion, advertising and promotion was clearly part of the practice between the parties. In fact, the letter of July 26, 2005, indicated Mr. Byrne’s understanding that that was his obligation. The trial judge concluded that advertising was part of the management of the stallion for breeding and failure to advertise constituted a breach of Park’s fiduciary duty as the caretaker and manager of the stallion.

[19]         Based on these findings and evidence that the trial judge accepted regarding what the stallion would have earned had it been advertised and promoted after July 2005, the trial judge made the following orders against Park: (1)  to return the stallion to Terra; (2) to pay the stud fees owed for the 2004 and 2005 breeding seasons plus prejudgment interest; (3) to pay damages for breach of contract for 2006-2008 of $206,250, based on the amount that the trial judge found Terra would have made from its 55% share in stud fee revenues had the contract been performed according to its terms; and (4) to pay damages of $100,000 for future losses for the following three years that the stallion would be expected to breed. The trial judge also ordered both Park and Mr. Byrne jointly to repay $7,265, representing one half of the first stallion award that was not paid over to Terra. Finally, the trial judge accepted the respondent’s argument that because Mr. Byrne was the directing mind of Park and therefore responsible for the positions it took in these proceedings, he should be jointly liable with Park for costs, which he fixed at $98,520.83.

Issues on Appeal

[20]         The appellants submit that the trial judge made five errors: (1) he misapplied the doctrine of repudiation; (2) he misconstrued the August 2003 agreement and relied on inadmissible evidence; (3) he erred in finding that Terra owed Park a fiduciary duty to advertise the stallion; (4) he erred in his calculation of damages and failed to require Terra to mitigate; and (5) he erred by holding Mr. Byrne personally liable for the payment of one half of the first stallion award and for the costs of the action.

Analysis

[21]         The appellants submit that although the trial judge referred to the correct five-part test from 968703 Ontario Ltd. v. Vernon (2002), 58 O.R. (3d) 215 (C.A.), for fundamental breach of contract amounting to repudiation, he failed to properly apply the test to the facts. The test, at para. 16, sets out five-factors as guidance in finding a fundamental breach:

(a) the ratio of the party's obligation not performed to the obligation as a whole; (b) the seriousness of the breach to the innocent party; (c) the likelihood of repetition of the breach; (d) the seriousness of the consequences of the breach; and (e) the relationship of the part of the obligation performed to the whole obligation.

[22]         The appellants state first that the respondent did not appropriately pursue its repudiation claim, because it did not attempt to retrieve possession of the stallion and because its original claim was for rescission of the agreement, which claim was only amended shortly before trial to claim repudiation. Park also relies on the fact that it continued to feed and care for the horse according to the agreement as evidence that Terra was not deprived of the whole benefit of the agreement.

[23]         I do not accept these arguments. With respect to retrieving possession of the horse, the trial judge found that Park consistently resisted Terra’s claim for an order to return the stallion. On the pleading point, Park argues that by the time the pleading was amended, Park was entitled to believe that the August 2003 agreement was still in effect. However, Park could not have believed the agreement continued in effect when as found by the trial judge, it refused to carry out its critical obligations under the agreement: to report on the breeding activity of the stallion and to pay the resulting stud fees. It certainly does not lie with Park to rely on the fact that it continued to feed and care for the horse when it would not return the horse to Terra, its rightful owner, once it was no longer abiding by the terms of the agreement by which the stallion was standing stud at the Park Stud farm.

[24]         The second issue raised on appeal relates to entitlement to the stallion award monies. The appellant’s first objection is to the trial judge’s interpretation of the phrase “revenue from stud fees” in the August 2003 agreement as meaning only stud fees and no other type of revenue that might be generated by the stallion including award monies. The appellants object that the trial judge erred by looking to the prior dealings of the parties to assist in the interpretation of the phrase. In my view, the trial judge made no error either in his analysis or in his conclusion regarding the meaning of the phrase within the agreement.

[25]         In interpreting the phrase, the trial judge looked to the agreement as a whole as well as to the plain language of the phrase. To the extent that there could be any argument of ambiguity, he examined the past dealings between the parties as an indication of how they interpreted the phrase in practice. The appellants submit that the trial judge gave no meaning to the words “revenue from” and say that revenue from stud fees includes any revenue “derived from stud fees”, by which it means generated by the breeding of the horse including but not limited to stud fees.

[26]         I reject this submission. First, the trial judge made no error in his approach to the interpretation of the phrase within the context of the agreement as a whole. Second, his conclusion was not only reasonable, it was in accordance with the practice of the parties as well as the language they used. If the parties had intended to include all revenues generated as a result of the breeding of the stallion including revenues earned by its progeny, they could have said so. As the trial judge correctly noted, Mr. Byrne was the only one of the two men who was aware of the new stallion award, so that if he had intended to include any award monies, he was the one to ensure that the August 2003 agreement referred to such monies. The term “stud fees” is unambiguous. Revenue from stud fees is essentially redundant verbiage, but this does not detract from the meaning of the phrase. I find no error in the approach or the interpretation determined by the trial judge.

[27]         Finally, as the appellants point out in their factum, the purpose of interpreting the phrase was to assist in determining which party was entitled under their agreement to the stallion award money. The trial judge found on that issue that it was the owner of the horse who was entitled to receive the award monies and that was the respondent. This finding was not moot as the appellants contend, but necessary to determine entitlement, once it was clear that the issue was not addressed in the August 2003 agreement. The appellants also point to the fact that the trial judge failed to consider that because the parties shared the $100 OSS fee, they intended to share the stallion award. However, the OSS fee was not the application fee for the stallion award. The trial judge made no error on this issue.

[28]         The appellant next submits that the trial judge erred by attributing to the parties a fiduciary relationship, when their relationship is merely governed by the contract between them, and, as horse breeder and horse owner, is not one of the traditionally recognized fiduciary relationships. The context for this submission is the trial judge’s finding that Park breached a fiduciary duty when it threatened to and then did discontinue advertising the stallion for breeding in the trade magazines.

[29]         The appellant relies on the principle from Hodgkinson v. Simms, [1994] 3 S.C.R. 377, at pp. 409-10, that where the relationship is not within one of the recognized categories of fiduciary relationship, then “what is required is evidence of a mutual understanding that one party has relinquished its own self-interest and agreed to act solely on behalf of the other party.” In this case, the contract between the parties set out obligations that Park was to carry out for the benefit of both parties.

[30]         In my view, it is unnecessary in this case to find a fiduciary relationship. The trial judge found that although the obligation to advertise and promote the stallion was not specifically listed in the August 2003 agreement, it was “clearly part of the practice between the parties and was anticipated to carry on”. Although the trial judge uses this analysis to conclude that Park owed Terra a fiduciary duty to advertise and promote the stallion, the effect of his finding is that Park’s obligation to advertise and promote was an implied term of the August 2003 agreement as part of its management of the stallion for breeding. The trial judge made this finding based on the intentions of the parties, demonstrated by their conduct, by their practice and the practice in the industry and by the evidence of Mr. Byrne. Park advertised and promoted all of the stallions that stood stud at its farm including Bold N’ Flashy. It advertised Bold N’ Flashy both before and after the August 2003 agreement until Byrne unilaterally decided not to do so following his July 2005 letter. As the whole purpose of the arrangement was to breed the stallion to as many mares as possible, advertising and promoting the stallion was a necessary component of the breeder’s obligations. Finally, the 1995 written agreement between the parties did refer to advertising as one of Park’s obligations. In his evidence regarding the August 2003 settlement agreement, Mr. Byrne stated that the intention was to revert to the previous arrangement. In my view, the trial judge was correct in the findings he made that advertising and promotion were part of Park’s obligations as breeder under the August 2003 agreement. However, those findings lead to the legal conclusion of an implied term in the August 2003 agreement rather than the imposition of a fiduciary duty.

[31]         On the issue of damages, the appellants submit that the trial judge did not give effect to the evidence of their expert, Mr. McCormack, in contrast to the respondent’s expert report of Mr. Hogan. I would not give effect to this submission. The trial judge is the one who assesses and weighs the evidence and decides what evidence to accept or reject. This court accords deference to his decision on these issues. The appellants also argue that the respondent failed to mitigate by not repossessing the horse. I have already addressed this submission in the context of the repudiation argument.

[32]         The final issue is whether the trial judge erred by holding Mr. Byrne personally liable for the costs of the litigation and for the half of the stallion award from 2003. Dealing first with the stallion award money from 2003, the respondent’s action in respect of the award was brought against both Park Stud and Byrne. However, on the evidence, although Mr. Byrne was the actor through which Park Stud conducted its affairs, there is no evidence that he was the personal recipient of the award money.

[33]         On the issue of costs, the trial judge in effect pierced the corporate veil and found that as Mr. Byrne was the directing mind of Park, all of its actions including in the litigation, were really Mr. Byrne’s actions and taken for Mr. Byrne’s benefit. In awarding the costs of both actions, he made one award, although Mr. Byrne was only a party to the stallion award claim and not to the second action regarding the repudiation of the agreement.

[34]          The test for when a court may pierce the corporate veil of a one shareholder corporation requires not only that the shareholder controls the actions of the corporation but that that control is exercised to allow the corporation to be used for a fraudulent or improper purpose: see 642947 Ontario Ltd. v. Fleischer (2001), 56 O.R. (3d) 417 (C.A.), at paras. 67-68. In this case, Park’s actions, taken by Mr. Byrne, were in breach of the agreement. The trial judge made adverse findings regarding Mr. Byrne’s credibility and found that his conduct in failing to advertise the stallion after July 2005 was deliberate and vindictive. He also found that by retaining the horse, and not allowing the respondent to retake possession, he prevented the respondent from obtaining the full benefit from the horse.

[35]         Although these actions by Mr. Byrne on behalf of Park constituted a hardball strategic approach, the trial judge made no finding of a fraudulent or improper purpose, which is a very high test. I would therefore set aside the award of costs against Mr. Byrne in his personal capacity.

Conclusion

[36]         In the result, I would allow the appeal only in respect of the orders against Mr. Byrne, but dismiss the appeal in respect of the orders against Park.

[37]         As the respondent was substantially successful, I would award costs of the appeals to the respondent, but at a reduced amount to reflect the partial success of Mr. Byrne. The respondent claimed $19,970 on a partial indemnity basis. I would award $15,000 inclusive of disbursements and G.S.T.

[1] A stud fee is generally only paid after a foal is born alive. After mares are bred to the stallions in the late winter and spring, an 11-month gestation period ensues. Mare owners will pay the stud fee between this live birth and the following autumn.  Therefore, stud fees will generally not be received until the calendar year subsequent to the breeding season.
